Indices

When a number 'P' is multiplied by itself 'n' times, then the product is called nth power of 'P' and is written as ' \( P^n \) '. Here, P is called the basis and 'n' is known as the index of the power (here, plural of index is called indices).

Laws Of Indices

Let a and b be any real number and let m, n be positive integers, then

  1. \( a^m \times a^n = a^{m + n} \)
  2. \( a^m \div a^n = a^{m - n} \)
  3. \( (a^m)^n = a^{mn} \)
  4. \( (ab)^n = a^n b^n \)
  5. \( \left ( \frac{a}{b} \right )^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n} \)
Remarks

The same laws can also be proved from negative integers as exponents as shown below

  1. \( a^{-m} \times a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^m} \times \frac{1}{a^n} = \frac{1}{a^m \times a^n} = \frac{1}{a^{m + n}} = a^{-(m + n)} \)
  2. \( \frac{1}{a^m} \div \frac{1}{a^n} = \left ( \frac{1}{a^m} \times \frac{a^n}{1} \right ) = \frac{a^n}{a^m} = a^{n - m} = a^{-m -(-n)} \)
  3. \( (a^{-m})^{-n} = \left [ \frac{1}{(a^{-m})} \right ]^n = (a^m)^n = a^{mn} = a^{(-m)(-n)} \)
  4. \( (ab)^{-n} = \frac{1}{(ab)^n} = \frac{1}{a^n \times b^n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \times \frac{1}{b^n} = a^{-n} \times b^{-n} \)
  5. \( \left ( \frac{a}{b} \right )^{-n} = \frac{1}{\left ( \frac{a}{b} \right )^{n}} = \frac{1}{ \frac{a^n}{b^n}} = \frac{b^n}{a^n} = \frac{a^{-n}}{b^{-n}} \)

Surds or Radicals

If \( \sqrt[n]{a} \) is irrational, where a is a rational number and n is a positive integer, then \( \sqrt[n]{a} \) or \( a^{ \frac{1}{n}} \) is called a surd or radical of order n and a is called the radicand.

Important facts

Let n be a positive integer and a be a real number.

  • If a is an irrational, then \( \sqrt[n]{a} \) is not a surd.
  • If a is rational, then \( \sqrt[n]{a} \) is a surd.

Laws of Radicals

As surds can be expressed with fractional exponents, the laws of indices are therefore, applicable to surds

  1. \( \left ( \sqrt[n]{a} \right )^n = a \)
  2. \( \sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a} \sqrt[n]{b} \)
  3. \( \sqrt[m]{ \sqrt[n]{a}} = \sqrt[mn]{a} = \sqrt[n]{ \sqrt[m]{a}} \)
  4. \( \sqrt[n]{ \frac{a}{b}} = \frac{ \sqrt[n]{a}}{ \sqrt[n]{b}} \)
  5. \( \left ( \sqrt[n]{a} \right )^m = \sqrt[n]{a^m} \)

Types Of Surds

  1. Pure Surds A surd which has unity only as rational factor, the other factor being irrational is called a pure surd.
  2. Mixed Surds A surd which has a rational factor other than unity, the other factor being irrational, is called a mixed surd.

Comparison of Two Surds

If two surds are of same order, then the one whose radicand is larger, is the larger of the two. If two surds of different order are to be compared, then we reduce them to the same but smallest order and then compare.

Rationalizing Factors

When the product of two surds is rational, then each one of them is called the rationalizing factor of the other.

Rationalizing of Surds

The process of converting a surd to a rational number by multiplying it to a suitable rationalizing factor is called the rationalization of the surd.

Solved Examples

Example 1:

The value of \( (16)^{\frac{5}{2}} + (16)^{- \frac{3}{2}} \) is equal to

Solution:

\( \begin{align} (4^2)^{ \frac{5}{2}} + (4^2)^{ - \frac{3}{2}} = (4)^{2 \times \frac{5}{2}} + (4)^{2 \times \frac{-3}{2}} \\\\[6pt] = (4)^5 + (4)^{-3} = 1024 + \frac{1}{64} = \frac{1024 \times 64 + 1}{64} = \frac{65536 + 1}{64} = \frac{65537}{64} \end{align} \)

Example 2:

\( 3^{2n - 1} = \frac{1}{27^{n - 3}} \) , then the value of n is

Solution:

\( \begin{align} 3^{2n - 1} = \frac{1}{27^{n - 3}} \Rightarrow 3^{2n - 1} = \frac{1}{(3)^{3(n - 3)}} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow 3^{2n - 1} = \frac{1}{(3)^{3n - 9}} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow 3^{2n - 1} = (3)^{(9 - 3n)} \Rightarrow 2n - 1 = 9 - 3n \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow 5n = 10 \Rightarrow n = 2 \end{align} \)

Example 3:

If \( 2^x = 5^y = 10^{-z} \) , then the value of \( \left ( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z} \right ) \) is

Solution:

Let \( 2^x = 5^y = 10^{-z} = k \)
\( \therefore 2 = k^{ \frac{1}{x}}, \ 5 = k^{ \frac{1}{y}}, \ 10 = k^{ \frac{1}{z}} \)
Since, 2 \( \times \) 5 = 10
\( \begin{align} \therefore k^{ \frac{1}{x}} \times k^{ \frac{1}{y}} = k^{ - \frac{1}{z}} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow k^{ \left ( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} \right )} = k^{- \frac{1}{z}} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} = - \frac{1}{z} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z} = 0 \end{align} \)
