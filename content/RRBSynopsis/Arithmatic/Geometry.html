Line

A line segment \( \overrightarrow{PQ} \) , when extended indefinitely in both the direction is called line \( \overrightarrow{PQ} \) and a line has no end points.

Types of lines

Intersecting Lines

Two lines having a common point are called intersecting lines. This common point is point of intersection.

Concurrent Lines

Three or more lines intersecting at the same point are said to be concurrent.

Parallel Lines

Two lines l and m are said to be parallel, if they have no common point and it can be written as \( l \shortparallel m. \)

Angle

An angle is the union of two non-collinear rays with a common initial point.

Types of Angles

Complementary Angles

Two angles, the sum of whose measure is \( 90^{\circ} \) , are called complementary angles.

Supplementary Angles

Two angles are said to be supplementary, if the sum of their measure is \( 180^{\circ} \) .
When the sum of two adjacent angles is \( 180^{\circ} \) , then they are called linear pair of angles.

  • Acute angle \( 0^{\circ} \leq \theta < 90^{\circ} \)
  • Right angle \( \theta = 90^{\circ} \)
  • Obtuse angle \( 90^{\circ} < \theta < 180^{\circ} \)
  • Straight angle \( \theta = 180^{\circ} \)
  • Reflex angle \( 180^{\circ} < \theta < 360^{\circ} \)

Transversal Line

A line which intersects two or more given lines at distinct points is called a transversal line of the given lines.

Here, line P is a transversal of the lines l and m, respectively.

Angle Made by Transversal Line

Here, we introduce some new angles made by a transversal with two given lines

  1. Vertically Opposite Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} \) and \( \angle{3} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} \) and \( \angle{4} \)
    3. \( \angle{5} \) and \( \angle{7} \)
    4. \( \angle{6} \) and \( \angle{8} \)
  2. Corresponding Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} \) and \( \angle{5} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} \) and \( \angle{6} \)
    3. \( \angle{3} \) and \( \angle{7} \)
    4. \( \angle{4} \) and \( \angle{8} \)
  3. Alternate Interior Angles
    1. \( \angle{4} \) and \( \angle{6} \)
    2. \( \angle{3} \) and \( \angle{5} \)
  4. Alternate Exterior Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} \) and \( \angle{7} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} \) and \( \angle{8} \)

If a Transversal Intersects Two Parallel Lines

  1. Vertically Opposite Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} = \angle{3} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} = \angle{4} \)
    3. \( \angle{5} = \angle{7} \)
    4. \( \angle{6} = \angle{8} \)
  2. Corresponding Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} = \angle{5} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} = \angle{6} \)
    3. \( \angle{3} = \angle{7} \)
    4. \( \angle{4} = \angle{8} \)
  3. Alternate Interior Angles
    1. \( \angle{4} = \angle{6} \)
    2. \( \angle{3} = \angle{5} \)
  4. Alternate Exterior Angles
    1. \( \angle{1} = \angle{7} \)
    2. \( \angle{2} = \angle{8} \)
  5. Same Side Transversal Angles
    1. \( \angle{4} + \angle{5} = 180^{\circ} \)
    2. \( \angle{3} + \angle{6} = 180^{\circ} \)
    3. \( \angle{1} + \angle{8} = 180^{\circ} \)
    4. \( \angle{2} + \angle{7} = 180^{\circ} \)
note

If anyone of the vertically opposite angles, corresponding angles, alternate interior angles or alternate exterior angles are equal, then the two lines l and m are parallel.

Triangle

A plane figure closed by three line segment is called a triangle.

Types Of Triangles on the Basis Of Angles

  • Right Angled Triangle A triangle with one angle at a right angle is called a right angled triangle.
  • Acute Angled Triangle A triangle each of whose angle is acute is called an acute angled triangle.
  • Obtuse Angled Triangle A triangle with one angle is an obtuse angle, is known obtuse angled triangle.

Types Of Triangles on the Basis Of Sides

  • Scalene Triangle A triangle, no two of whose sides are equal, is called a scalene triangle.
  • Isosceles Triangle A triangle, two of whose sides are equal in length is called an isosceles triangle.
  • Equilateral Triangle A triangle, all of whose sides are equal is called an equilateral triangle.

Mid-point Theorem

The line segment joining the mid-points of any two sides of triangle is parallel to the third side and equal to half it.

\( DE || BC, \ DE = \frac{1}{2} BC \)

Converse of Mid-point Theorem

The line drawn through the mid-point of one side of a triangle parallel to another side bisects the third side.

Congruence of Two Triangles

Two triangles are congruent if and only if one of them can be made to superpose on the other, so as to cover it exactly.

Some Important Points of Congruence of Two Angles
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side) Two triangles are congruent, if two sides and the included angle of one are equal to the corresponding sides and the included angle of the other triangle.
  • AAS (Angle-Angle-Side) If any two angles and a non-included side of one triangle are equal to the corresponding angles and side of another triangle, then the two triangles are congruent.
  • ASA (Angle-Side-Angle) Two triangles are congruent, if two angles and included side of one triangle are equal to the corresponding two angles and the included side of the other triangle.
  • RHS (Right Angle-Hypotenuse Side) Two right angled triangles are congruent, if the hypotenuse and one side of one triangle are respectively equal to the hypotenuse and one side of the other triangle.

Similar Triangles Two triangles are said to be similar, if their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are proportional.

Basic Proportionality Theorem In a triangle, a line drawn parallel to one side to intersect the other sides in distinct points, divides the two sides in the same ratio.

Circle

A circle is a set of points which lie in a plane and at a constant distance from a fixed point in the plane. Here, the fixed point 'O' is called the centre and the constant distance is called radius of circle, denoted by r.

Some Important Definitions and Theorem about Circles

  • Chord A line segment whose end points belong to the circle is a chord. Here, AB is a chord.
  • Diameter The chord which passes through the centre of a circle is called a diameter of the circle. Here, PQ is diameter.
    \( \therefore Diameter = 2 \times Radius = 2r \)
  • Circumference It is a distance travelled, if you will go once around a circle.
    \( Circumference = 2 \pi r \)
  • Theorem 1 The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord.
    Here, OD \( \perp \) AB, then AD = DB.
  • Theorem 2 If two arcs of a circle are congruent, then the corresponding chords are equal.
    Here, AB = CD (arcs)
    Then, AB = CD (chords)
  • Theorem 3 The line joining the centre to the mid-point of a chord is perpendicular to the chord.
  • Theorem 4 The perpendicular bisectors of two chords of a circle intersect at its centre.
  • Theorem 5 There is one and only one circle passing through three non-collinear points.
  • Theorem 6 Equal chords of congruent circles are equidistant from the corresponding centres.
  • Theorem 7 Chords which are equidistant from the corresponding centres are equal.
  • Theorem 8 The angle in semi-circle is a right angle.
  • Theorem 9 Angles in the same segment of the circle are equal.
    Here, \( \angle{ACB} = \angle{ADB} \)

Secant A line which intersects a circle in two distinct points is called a secant of the circle.

Tangent A line meeting a circle only in one point is called a tangent to the circle and the point, where tangent meets is called point of contact.

Properties Of Circle

  • Equal chords of a circle are equidistant from the centre.
    If PQ = RS
    Then, OM = ON
  • Equal chords of a circle subtend equal angles at the centre.
    If AB = PQ
    Then, \( \angle{AOB} = \angle{POQ} \)
  • If two chords of a circle bisects one another, then they must be diameters.
  • The angle subtended by an arc of a circle at the centre is double the angle subtended by it at any point on the remaining part of circle.
    \( \angle{AOB} = 2 \angle{ACB} \)

Regular Polygon

A polygon is a figure bounded by three or more straight lines. If all sides and interior angles of a polygon are equal then it is called a regular polygon
Let a be the each side of regular polygon, then
Area of regular pentagon (Polygon with 5 - side) = \( 5a^2 \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \)
Area of regular hexagon (Polygon with 6 - side) = \( 6a^2 \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \)
Area of regular Octagon (Polygon with 8 - side) = \( 2a^2 (\sqrt{2} + 1) \)
Each exterior angle = \( \frac{360^{\circ}}{n} \) (n = number of side)
Each interior angle = \( 180^{\circ} \) - Exterior angle
Number of diagonals = \( \left \lbrace \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} - n \right \rbrace \) or \( \left \lbrace \frac{n(n - 3)}{2} \right \rbrace \)
{Where n is number of sides.}

Solved Examples

Example 1:

In figure, chord ED is parallel to the diameter AC of the circle and \( \angle{CBE} = 65^{\circ} \) . Then, \( \angle{DEC} \) is equal to

Solution:

\( \angle{CAE} = \angle{CBE} \) (angles in the same segment)
\( \angle{CAE} = 65^{\circ} \)
\( \angle{AEC} = 90^{\circ} \) (angle in a semi - circle)
In \( \triangle{ACE} \) ,
\( \begin{align} \angle{ACE} + \angle{AEC} + \angle{CAE} = 180^{\circ} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow \angle{ACE} + 90^{\circ} + 65^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \\\\[6pt] \therefore \angle{ACE} = 25^{\circ} \end{align} \)
given that, AC \( \shortparallel \) DE
\( \therefore \angle{DEC} = \angle{ACE} = 25^{\circ} \) (alternate angles)

Example 2:

Consider \( \triangle{ABD} \) such that \( \angle{ADB} = 20^{\circ} \) and C is a point on BD such that AB = AC and CD = CA. Then, the measure of \( \angle{ABC} \) is

Solution:

Given, CD = CA
\( \therefore \angle{CAD} = \angle{CDA} = 20^{\circ} \)
In \( \triangle{CDA} \) ,
\( \begin{align} \angle{ACD} = 180^{\circ} - (20^{\circ} + 20^{\circ}) = 140^{\circ} \\\\[6pt] \therefore \angle{ACB} = 180^{\circ} - \angle{ACD} \\\\[6pt] = 180^{\circ} - 140^{\circ} = 40^{\circ} \\\\[6pt] \because AC = AB \\\\[6pt] \therefore \angle{ABC} = \angle{ACB} = 40^{\circ} \end{align} \)

Example 3:

ABC is a triangle. The bisectors of the internal angle \( \angle{B} \) and external angle \( \angle{C} \) intersect at D. If \( \angle{BDC} = 50^{\circ} \) then \( \angle{A} \) is

Solution:

In \( \triangle{BDC} \) ,
\( \begin{align} y + 180^{\circ} - 2x + x + 50^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \Rightarrow y - x + 50^{\circ} = 0 \\\\[6pt] x - y = 50^{\circ} \end{align} \)
In \( \triangle{ABC} \) ,
\( \begin{align} 2y + 180^{\circ} - 2x - \angle{A} = 180^{\circ} \Rightarrow 2(y - x) + \angle{A} = 0 \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow 2(-50) + \angle{A} = 0 \Rightarrow \angle{A} = 100 \end{align} \)
