Logarithm

Introduction

The logarithm of a number x with respect to base b is the exponent by which b must be raised to yield x. Thus, the logarithm of x to the base b is the solution y to the equation \( b^y = x \) . The logarithm is denoted as \( 'log_b (x)' \) .

Properties of logarithms

  • If \( a^y = x \) , then \( y = log_a x \)
  • In \( log_a x \) both x and a are positive, then x \( \neq \) 0 and a \( \neq \) 1.
  • Exponential function is always positive.
  • \( log_a a = 1 \)
  • \( log_a 1 = 0 \)
  • \( log_b a . log_a b = 1 \)
  • \( log_c a = log_b a . log_c b \) , where a, b > 0 \( \neq \) 1
  • Another form \( log_c a = \frac{log_b a}{log_b c} \)
    1. \( log_a (m.n) = log_a m + log_a n \)
    2. \( log_a \left ( \frac{m}{n} \right ) = log_a m - log_a n \)
    3. \( log_a m^n = n log_a m \)
    4. \( log_a b^m = \frac{1}{b} log_a m \)
  • \( log nq(m^p) = \frac{p}{q} log_n m \)
  • \( a^{log_a n} = n \)
  • If p > 1, then \( a > b \Rightarrow log_p a > log_p b \)
  • If 0 < p < 1, then \( a > b \Rightarrow log_p a < log_p b \)
  • \( a^{log_e x} = x^{log_e a} \) , when a > 0, e > 0, a \( \neq \) 0, x > 0
Note:
  • The logarithm to the base, b = 10 is called common logarithm.
  • The natural logarithm has the constant \( ( \approx 2.718) \) as its base.

Matrices

Matrices

A set of mn numbers (real or complex) in the form of m horizontal lines (called rows) and n vertical lines (called columns), is called an m \( \times \) n matrix or rectangular matrix (to be read as m by n matrix).
A order of m \( \times \) n matrix is usually written as
\( \begin{align} \left [ \begin{array}{cccc} a_{11} & a_{12} & ... & a_{1n} \\\\ a_{21} & a_{22} & ... & a_{2n} \\\\ ... & ... & ... & ... \\\\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & ... & a_{mn} \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
A matrix may be represented by the symbols \( [a_{ij}], \ (a_{ij}), \ |a_{ij}| \) or by a capital letter A.
i.e., \( A = [a_{ij}]_{m \times n} \) or \( (a_{ij})_{m \times n} \) or \( || a_{ij} ||_{m \times n} \)
A matrix is not a number. It has no numerical value.

Types of Matrices

  1. Row Matrix A matrix having one row and n columns of the form
    \( A = \left [ \begin{array}{cccc} a_{11} & a_{12} & ..... & a_{1n} \end{array} \right ] \)
    or \( A = \left [ \begin{array}{cccc} a_1 & a_2 & ..... & a_n \end{array} \right ] \)
    is called a row matrix of order n. It is also called a row vector.
  2. Column Matrix A matrix having m rows and one column of the form
    \( \begin{align} A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} a_{11} \\\\ a_{21} \\\\ ..... \\\\ a_{m1} \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
    or \( \begin{align} A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} a_1 \\\\ a_2 \\\\ ..... \\\\ a_m \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
    is called a column matrix of order m. It is also called a column vector.
  3. Zero Matrix A matrix in which all the elements are equal to zero is called zero matrix. It is also called a null matrix and is denoted by O.
    e.g., The matrix \( \begin{align} A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) is a zero matrix of order 2 \( \times \) 3.
  4. Real or Complex Matrix A matrix in which all the elements are real or complex is called a real matrix.
  5. Equal Matrices Two matrices \( A = (a_{ij})_{m \times n} \) and \( B = (b_{ij})_{p \times q} \) are said to be equal, if
    1. m = p and n = q,
    2. \( a_{ij} = b_{ij}, \ \forall \) i and j

    Thus, two matrices are said to be equal if they are of the same order and the corresponding elements are same.
  6. Square Matrix A matrix in which the number of rows is equal to the number of columns is called a square matrix. i.e., m = n.
  7. Diagonal Matrix A square matrix in which all its elements are zero except those in the leading diagonal is called a diagonal matrix.
    e.g. The matrices \( \begin{align} C = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} d_1 & 0 \\\\ 0 & d_2 \end{array} \right ], \ D = \left [ \begin{array}{cccc} d_1 & 0 & 0 \\\\ 0 & d_2 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0 & d_3 \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) are diagonal matrices, of order 2 and 3. Or it can be written as
    \( C = diag(d_1, \ d_2); \ D = diag(d_1, \ d_2, \ d_3) \)
  8. Identity Matrix A diagonal matrix in which all the diagonal elements are equal to 1 is called an identity matrix. It is also called a unit matrix. An identity matrix of order n is denoted by I or \( I_n \) .
    e.g., The matrix \( \begin{align} I = \left [ \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) is an identity matrix of order 3.

Properties of Symmetric and Skew-symmetric Matrices

  1. In a skew-symmetric matrix A, all its diagonal elements are zero, i.e., \( a_{ii} = 0, \ \forall i. \)
  2. The matrix which is both symmetric and skew-symmetric is a null matrix.
  3. For any real square matrix A; \( A + A^T, \ AA^T \) is a symmetric matrix and \( A - A^T \) is a skew-symmetric matrix.
  4. A real square matrix A can be expressed as the sum of a symmetric matrix and a skew-symmetric matrix.
    i.e., \( A = \frac{1}{2} (A + A^T ) + \frac{1}{2} (A - A^T) \)
  5. If A and B are two symmetric matrices of same order, then
    1. A \( \pm \) B is symmetric matrix.
    2. AB + BA is symmetric matrix.
    3. AB - BA is anti-symmetric matrix.
    4. AB is symmetric matrix when AB = BA
  6. If A and B are two skew-symmetric matrices, then
    1. A \( \pm \) B, AB - BA are skew-symmetric matrices.
    2. AB + BA is a symmetric matrix.

Solved Examples

Example 1:

Find x, y, z and w, if
\( \begin{align} \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 3x & y \\\\ 2z & 3w \end{array} \right ] = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} x + 4 & x - y \\\\ z + w & z - w + 3x \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)

Solution:

The matrix on each side is of order 2 \( \times \) 2.
On equating the corresponding elements, we get
3x = x + 4 \( \Rightarrow \) 2x = 4 \( \Rightarrow \) x = 2
y = x - y \( \Rightarrow \) 2y = x = 2 \( \Rightarrow \) y = 1
2z = z + w \( \Rightarrow \) z = w
3w = z - w + 3x \( \Rightarrow \) z = 4w - 6
On substituting w in terms of z, we get
z = 4z - 6 \( \Rightarrow \) 3z = 6 \( \Rightarrow \) z = 2
Hence, we obtain x = 2, y = 1, z = 2 and w = 2

Example 2:

If the least number of zeroes in a lower triangular matrix is 10, then what is the order of the matrix?

Solution:

If the least number of zeroes in a lower triangular matrix is 10, then order of the matrix is 4 \( \times \) 4.

Example 3:

If \( \begin{align} A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} x & 0 \\\\ y & 0 \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) and \( \begin{align} B = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 \\\\ a & b \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) are the two matrices, then find AB.

Solution:

\( \begin{align} AB = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} x & 0 \\\\ y & 0 \end{array} \right ] \ \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 \\\\ a & b \end{array} \right ] \\\\[6pt] = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} x \times 0 + 0 \times a & x \times 0 + 0 \times b \\\\ y \times 0 + 0 \times a & y \times 0 + 0 \times b \end{array} \right ] = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0 \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)

Example 4:

If \( \begin{align} A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} cos \alpha & - sin \alpha \\\\ sin \alpha & cos \alpha \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \) , then find the value of \( A^T A \) .

Solution:

we have \( \begin{align} A^T = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} cos \alpha & sin \alpha \\\\ - sin \alpha & cos \alpha \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
Therefore, \( \begin{align} A^T A = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} cos \alpha & sin \alpha \\\\ - sin \alpha & cos \alpha \end{array} \right ] \ \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} cos \alpha & - sin \alpha \\\\ sin \alpha & cos \alpha \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
\( \begin{align} = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} cos^2 \alpha + sin^2 \alpha & 0 \\\\ 0 & cos^2 \alpha + sin^2 \alpha \end{array} \right ] = \left [ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right ] = I \end{align} \)

Probability

Probability is used to indicate a possibility of an event to be occurred. It is often used synonymously with chance.

Definition

If in a random experiment there are n mutually exclusive and equally likely elementary events in which m elementary events are favourable to a particular event E, then the probability of the event E is defined as
\( \begin{align} P(E) = \frac{Favourable \ Events}{Total number of Events} \\\\[6pt] = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{m}{n} \end{align} \)

Essential Facts

  1. When a dice is rolled six events occur. They are {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
  2. When two dice are rolled 36 events occur. They are
    \( \begin{align} \left [ \begin{array}{cccccc} (1, \ 1), & (1, \ 2), & (1, \ 3), & (1, \ 4), & (1, \ 5), & (1, \ 6) \\\\ (2, \ 1), & (2, \ 2), & (2, \ 3), & (2, \ 4), & (2, \ 5), & (2, \ 6) \\\\ (3, \ 1), & (3, \ 2), & (3, \ 3), & (3, \ 4), & (3, \ 5), & (3, \ 6) \\\\ (4, \ 1), & (4, \ 2), & (4, \ 3), & (4, \ 4), & (4, \ 5), & (4, \ 6) \\\\ (5, \ 1), & (5, \ 2), & (5, \ 3), & (5, \ 4), & (5, \ 5), & (5, \ 6) \\\\ (6, \ 1), & (6, \ 2), & (6, \ 3), & (6, \ 4), & (6, \ 5), & (6, \ 6) \end{array} \right ] \end{align} \)
  3. When a coin is tossed 2 events occur. They are {H, T}.
  4. When two coins are tossed 4 events occur. The {HH, HT, TH, TT}.
  5. When there coins are tossed 8 events occur. The are {HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT}.
  6. In a pack of 52 cards, there are 26 red cards and 26 black cards. The 26 red cards are divided into 13 heart cards and 13 diamond cards. The 26 black cards are divided into 13 club cards and 13 spade cards. Each of the colours hearts, diamonds, club and spades is called a suit.
    In a suit, we have 13 cards (i. e. , A, K, Q, J, 10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3 and 2).

Solved Examples

Example 1:

Two unbiased coins are tossed. What is probability of getting both heads.

Solution:

Here, S = {HH, HT, TH, TT} and E = {HH}
\( \Rightarrow P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{1}{4} \)

Example 2:

An unbiased dice is tossed. Find the probability of getting an even number.

Solution:

Here, S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} and E = {2, 4, 6}
\( \therefore P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \)

Example 3:

In a simultaneous throw of a pair of dice, find the probability of getting a total more than 9.

Solution:

Here, n(S) = 6 \( \times \) 6 = 36
Let E = Event of getting a total more than 9.
= {(4, 6), (5, 5), (5, 6), (6, 4), (6, 5), (6, 6)}
\( \therefore P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6} \)

Example 4:

In a simultaneous throw of a pair of dice. Find the probability that the sum of the numbers shown on the two faces is divisible by 5 or 6.

Solution:

Here, n(S) = 6 \( \times \) 6 = 36
Let E = Event of getting a sum of numbers shown on the two faces divisible by 5 or 6.
= {(1, 4), (1, 5), (2, 3), (2, 4), (3, 2), (3, 3), (4, 1), (4, 2), (5, 1), (5, 5), (6, 4), (6, 6)}

Example 5:

A card is drawn from a well shuffled pack of cards. Find the probability that it is a queen

Solution:

The total number of ways of selecting one card out of 52 cards is \( ^{52}C_1 \) ways.
Let E be the event of drawing a queen card.
Then,
1 queen card can be drawn from 4 queens in \( ^4C_1 \) ways
\( \therefore P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{^4C_1}{^{52}C_1} = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13} \)

Complex Numbers

Introduction

The number of the form (a + ib), where a and b are real numbers and \( i = \sqrt{-1} \) , are known as complex numbers. The set of all complex numbers is denoted by C.
e.g., Each of the numbers (2 + 5i), \( (- 3 + \sqrt{2} i) \) , \( \left ( \frac{2}{3} - \frac{5}{7} i \right ) \) is a complex number.
For a complex number, z = (a + ib), we have,
a = real part of z, written as Re(z).
and b = imaginary part of z, written as Im (z).
e.g., If z = (5 + 9i), then Re(z) = 5 and Im(z) = 9

Purely Real and Purely Imaginary Numbers

A complex number z is said to be

  1. purely real, if Im(z) = 0,
  2. purely imaginary, if Re(z) = 0

Thus, each of the numbers 2, -7, \( \sqrt{3} \) is purely real.
and each of the numbers 2i, \( ( \sqrt{3i}), \ \left ( \frac{-3}{2} i \right ) \) is purely imaginary.

Conjugate of a Complex Number

Conjugate of a complex number z = (a + ib) is defined as \( \overline{z} = (a - ib) \) .
e.g.,

  1. \( \overline{(3 + 8i)} = (3 - 8i) \)
  2. \( \overline{(-6 - 2i)} = (- 6 + 2i) \)
  3. \( - \overline{7} = - 7 \)

Modulus of a Complex Number

Modulus of a complex number z = (a + ib), denoted by |z|, is defined as \( |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \) .
e.g.,

  1. If z = (2 + 3i), then \( |z| = \sqrt{2^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{13} \)
  2. If z = (- 5 - 4i), then \( |z| = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{41} \)

Equality of Complex Numbers

If \( z_1 = a_1 + ib_1 \) and \( Z_2 = a_2 + ib_2 \) , then \( z_1 = z_2 \Leftrightarrow a_1 = a_2 \) and \( b_1 = b_2 \) .

Sum and Difference of Complex Numbers

If \( z_1 = (a + ib) \) and \( z_2 = (c + id) \) , we define

  1. \( z_1 + z_2 = (a + ib) + (c + id) = (a + c) + i(b + d) \)
  2. \( z_1 - z_2 = (a + ib) - (c + id) = (a - c) + i(b - d) \)
    e.g.,
    1. \( z_1 = (3 + 5i) \) and \( z_2 = (-5 + 2i) \) , then
      \( z_1 + z_2 = {3 + (-5)} + i(5 + 2) = (-2 + 7i) \)
      and \( z_1 - z_2 = {3 - (-5)} + i (5 - 2) = (8 + 3i) \)
    2. \( z_1 = (-6 - 2i) \) and \( z_2 = (- 3 - 5i) \) , then
      \( z_1 + z_2 = {(-6) + (-3)} + i{(-2) + (-5)} = (- 9 - 7i) \) and
      \( z_1 - z_2 = {-6 - (-3)} + i{(-2) - (-5)} = (- 3 + 3i) \)

Express \( (4 - 3i)^3 \) in the form of (a + ib).

We have \( (4 - 3i)^3 = 4^3 - (3i)^3 - 3 \times 4 \times 3i \times (4 - 3i) \)
\( \begin{align} = 64 - 27i^3 - 36i(4 - 3i) = (64 - 108) + i(27 - 144) \\\\[6pt] = (- 44 - 117i) \ \therefore (4 - 3i)^3 = (- 44 - 117i) \end{align} \)

Example 3:

If \( z = (\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{-3}) \) , then find |z|

Solution:

\( \begin{align} z = (\sqrt{2} - i \sqrt{-3}) \\\\[6pt] |z| = \sqrt{(\sqrt{2})^2 + (- \sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{2 + 3} = \sqrt{5} \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Find the least positive integer value of m for which
\( \left ( \frac{1 + i}{1 - i} \right )^m = 1 \)

Solution:

We have
\( \begin{align} \frac{1 + i}{1 - i} = \left ( \frac{1 + i}{1 - i} \right ) \times \left ( \frac{1 + i}{1 + i} \right ) \\\\[6pt] = \left ( \frac{1 + i}{1^2 - i^2} \right )^2 = \frac{1 + i^2 + 2i}{2} = \frac{2i}{2} = i \\\\[6pt] \therefore \left ( \frac{1 + i}{1 - i} \right )^m = 1 \Rightarrow i^m = 1 \\\\[6pt] clearly, \ m = 4 \end{align} \)

    \( \begin{align} m_1 : m_2 \ (m_1, \ m_2 > 0), \ then \ x = \frac{m_1 x_2 + m_2 x_1}{m_1 + m_2}, \ y = \frac{m_1 y_2 + m_2 y_1} {m_1 + m_2} \ (internally) \\\\[3pt] and x = \frac{m_1 x_2 - m_2 x_1}{m_1 - m_2}, \ y = \frac{m_1 y_2 - m_2 y_1} {m_1 - m_2}\ (externally) \\\\[3pt] so, \ the \ mid-point \ of \ AB \ is \ \left ( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2} , \ \frac {y_1 + y_2}{2} \right ) \end{align} \)
  5. Angle between Two Lines
    Angle between two lines, if their slopes are \( m_1 \) and \( m_2 \) is given by \( \theta = tan^{-1} \left | \frac{m_1 - m_2}{1 + m_1 m_2} \right| \)
    note
    • if \( m_1 = m_2, \) then lines are parallel.
    • if \( m_1 m_2 = -1 \) , , then lines are perpendicular
  6. Area of a Triangle
    Area of a \( \triangle{ABC} \) whose vertices are \( A(x_1, \ y_1), B (x_2, \ y_2 ) \) and \( C(x_3, \ y_3) \) is given by
    \( \begin{align} \triangle = \frac{1}{2} \left| \begin{array}{ccc} x_1 & y_1 & 1 \\\\ x_2 & y_2 & 1 \\\\ x_3 & y_3 & 1 \end{array} \right| \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2}[x_1(y_2-y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)] \end{align} \)
    Centroid of a \( \triangle {ABC} \)
    Centroid of a \( \triangle {ABC} \) whose vertices are \( A(x_1, \ y_1), B(x_2, \ y_2 ) \) and \( C(x_3, \ y_3) \) is given by
    \( \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2 + x_3}{3} , \frac{y_1 + y_2 + y_3}{3} \right) \)
    Incentre of a \( \triangle {ABC} \)
    Incentre of a \( \triangle {ABC} \) is given by
    \( \left( \frac{ax_1 + b x_2 + c x_3}{a + b + c}, \ \frac{a y_1 + by_2 + c y_3}{a + b + c} \right) \)
  7. Various Forms of Straight Lines
    • General linear equation in x and y, say ax + by + c = 0 represent equation of line.
    • Equation of line passing through \( (x_1, \ y_1) \) having slope m is given by \( y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \)
    • Equation of line, if its slope is m and y intercept is c, is given by y = mx + c
      where, \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)
    • Equation of line passing through two points, is given by \( \frac{y - y_1}{y_2 - y_1} = \frac{x - x_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)
    • Equation of line, if their intercept on the axes are a and b, is given by \( \frac{x}{b} + {y}{b} = 1 \)
    • Equation of line, if perpendicular distance of origin from line is p and perpendicular drawn from origin to the line is making an angle a from the positive direction of x-axis, then equation of line is
      \( x cos \alpha + y sin \alpha = p \)

note

  • Perpendicular distance of a point \( (x_1, \ y_1) \) from the line ax + by + c = 0, is given by \( \left | \frac{ax_1 + by_1 + c}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}} \right| \)
    and length p of the perpendicular from the origin to the ax + by + c = 0, is given by \( p = \frac{|C|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}} \)
  • Conditions for two lines to be coincident, parallel, perpendicular or intersecting two lines
    \( a_1x + b_1y + c_1 = 0 \) and \( a_2x + b_2y + c_2 = 0 \) are
    • coincident, if \( \frac{a_1}{a_2} = \frac{b_1}{b_2} = \frac{c_1}{c_2} \)
    • parallel,if \( \frac{a_1}{a_2} = \frac{b_1}{b_2} \neq \frac{c_1}{c_2} \)
    • Perpendicular, if \( a_1 a_2 + b_1 b_2 = 0 \)
    • intersecting,if \( a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1 \neq 0 \ i.e., \ \frac{a_1}{a_2} \neq \frac{b_1}{b_2} \)
  • Perpendicular distance between two parallel lines \( ax + by + c_1 = 0 \) and \( ax + by + c_2 = 0, \) is given by \( p = \left | \frac{c_1 - c_2}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}} \right | \)
    the line \( a_ix + b_iy + c_i = 0 \) for i = 1,2,3 will be concurrent \( \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{ccc} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 \\\\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 \\\\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \end{array} \right | = 0 \end{align} \)

Example 2

The locus of a point which is equidistant from point (4, 2) and x-axis is

Sol.
