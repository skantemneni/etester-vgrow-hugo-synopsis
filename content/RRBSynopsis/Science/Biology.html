Living World

In 18th Century, Carolus Linnaeus developed binomial nomenclature for living organisms i.e., scientific name consists of genus and species.

  • Whittaker (1969) classified living organisms into five kingdoms-Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae and Animalia.
  • Monera includes bacteria and Mycoplasma, while Protista includes Protozoa (unicellular Eukaryotes).
  • Viruses are sub-microscopic, obligate, intracellular parasite consisting of nucleoprotein. WM Stanley Firsthy crystallised TMV (Tobacco Mosaic Virus).
  • Viroids are smallest infectious single stranded RNA molecules discovered by TO Diener.

Cell

  • All living beings are made up of one or more units called cell.
  • Cell is the structural and functional unit of life.
  • If the organism consists only one cell, it is called unicellular. Example; Nostoc, yeast.
  • If the organism consists of many cells, it is called multicellular. Example; Algae, fungi, animals, plants.
  • If the organism has no cell, it is called acellular. Example; Virus
  • Cell was discovered by Robert Hooke.
    \( Cell \rightarrow Tissue \rightarrow Organ \rightarrow Organism \rightarrow Community \rightarrow Universe \)
  • Plant cell consists a cell wall, made up of cellulose.
  • Animal cell does not have cell wall.
  • Despite their complexity and variety, all living cells can be classified as prokaryotic or eukaryotic.
  • Prokaryotic cells lack nucleur and membrane bound cell organelles, e.g., Bacteria, Blue-green algae, Mycoplasm'a etc.
  • Eukaryotic cells have a nucleus with elaborate nuclear envelope and membrane bound cell organelles. e.g., Algae, fungi, mammals.

Cell Organelles

Mitochondria

  • Discovered by Altman (1890).
  • Having double membrane envelope, composed of lipid and protein.
  • It is known as the 'power house of cell' because the energy is released in it through the cellular respiration.

Endoplasmic Reticulum

  • Discovered by Porter (1945).
  • It is of two types
    1. Rough Endoplasmic Recticulum (presence of ribosomes on the surface).
    2. Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum.
  • It's functions. are mechanical support, exchang of molecules, protein synthesis etc.

Golgi Complex

  • Discovered by Camillo Golgi in 1898 and got Nobel Prize 1906.
  • Its functions are secretion, providing storage place for proteins, cell wall formation, plasma membrane formation and lysosome formation.

Lysosomes

  • Discovered by de Duve in 1949.
  • Single membrane bound organelle containing hydrolytic enzymes.
  • Also called suicidal bags.
  • Help in digestion.

Ribosomes

  • Discovered by Palade in 1955.
  • Ribosome is the smallest organelle in the cell.
  • Ribosomes are attached to endoplasmic reticulum.
  • Ribosomes help in protein synthesis.

Nucleus

  • Discovered by Robert Brown in 1831.
  • Largest and most easily seen cell organelle within the eukaryotic cell.
  • Nucleus is the primary carrier of heriditary material in he cell.

Nucleic Acids

  • These contain the genetic instrnctions used in the development and functioning of all know living organisms. These are of two typer DNA and RNA. Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) It is a long polymer made from repeating units called nucleotider. It has four bases, adenine, gnanine, cytosine and thymine.
  • Ribonucleaie Acid (RNA) It is also made up of a long chain of nucleotider. It contains uracil is plase of thymine.

Circulatory System

  • Circulatory system is concerned with the circulation of body fluids to distribute various substances to various body parts.
  • It is a transport system that uses a fluid (blood) to move substances such as nutrients. wastes and gases throughout the body of an organism.
  • William Harvey in 1628, discovered circulation of blood in the body. He is regarded as 'Father of circulatory system.'
  • Malpighi in 1661, described capillaries.
  • The flow of circulation and the structures concerned, constiutes the circulatory system.
    • Single celled organisms such as bacteria, Paramecium, Amoeba, Euglena do not have circulatory system.
    Circulatory system is of two types
    1. Open circulatory system, in which circulating fluid bathes internal organs and tissues directly.
    2. Closed circulatory system, consisting three components-blood vessels, blood and heart. Blood always flows in blood vessels and never comes out thus. there is no direct contact of blood and tissues.

Human Heart

  • Heart is pumping organ that circulates the blood throughout the body via blood vessels.
  • It consists of four chambers: the right and left auricles and the right and left ventricles.
  • The heart sound lubb-dupp is produced by the opening and closing of the valves in heart.
  • Sphygmomanometer is the instrument used to measure the blood pressure. Normal \( BP \ is \ \frac{120}{80} mm \ of \ Hg. \)
  • Normal heart beat is 72 per minute.

Blood Vessels

  • Blood vessels form a network of tube-like vessels which permeate the body.
  • Blood circulates in blood vessels.
  • Arteries are the blood vessels, which carry oxygenated or pure blood away from the heart, except pulmonary artery, which carries impure blood from right ventricle to lungs.
  • Veins are the blood vessels, which always drain deoxygenated or impure blood from the organs towards the heart except pulmonary vein which carries oxygenated blood from lungs to left auricle.
  • Capillaries are thin-walled, smallest blood vessels, which form a dense network in the organs.
  • These join artery on one hand and vein on the other.
  • Bldod flows from arterioles to venules via capillary.

Blood

  • Blood is a fluid connective tissue.
  • Its quantity is 5-6 L in man and 500 mL less in woman.
  • 6-8% of body weight and has a pH of 7.4 (i.e., slightly alkaline).

Blood Cell

They are of three types

Red Blood Corpuscles (RBCs)

  • Also called erythrocytes, disc-shaped, no nucleus, contains a pigment called haemoglobin, which gives blood its red colour and transports oxygen and carbon dioxide.
  • In mammals, RBCs are circular and non-nucleated except those of family- Comilladae (camels, llamas, etc), which have nucleated RBCs. In rest of the vertebrates, oval and nucleated RBCs are present.
  • Manufactured in red bone marrow.
  • Life span is of 120 days.
  • Number of RBCs is \( 4.5-5 \ \frac{million}{cubic} mm \) of blood.

White Blood Corpuscles (WBCs)

  • Also called leucocytes, rounded with a nucleus and far less numberous than RBCs(average of 8,000 per cubic mm of blood.)
  • Manufactured in red bone marrow.
  • Act as the soldiers of body's defence system.

Platelets

  • Also called thrombocytes and are about 2,50,000 per cubic mm of blood.
  • Manufactured in red bone marrow.
  • Help the blood to clot.

Rh Factor

  • It is a blood antigen found in RBC.
  • A person can be \( {Rh}^+ \ or \ {Rh}^- \) depending upon the presence of Rh factor in RBCs.
  • A very important point is \( {Rh}^+ \) can receive blood from both \( {Rh}^+ \ and {Rh}^- \ but \ {Rh}^- \) can receive blood from \( {Rh}^- \) only.
  • In world population, \( {Rh}^+ \) are 85% and \( {Rh}^- \) are 15%.
  • Blood transfusion technique was developed by James Blundell.

Nervous System

Nervous system controls and regulates the activities of all the other systems of the body.
In man, nervous system can be divided into

  • Central Nervous System (CNS) Brain and spinal cord.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) Nerves from brain (cranial nerves) and spinal cord.
  • Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) Nerves and ganglions.

Brain

  • Brain is the main organ of the nervous system.
  • Human brain weighs from 1220 to 1400 g and has 100 billion neuron.
    Human brain is divisible into three parts
    1. Fore brain includes olfactory lobes, cerebrum and diencephalon. Cerebrum
      • It controls the voluntary actions and is the seat of intelligence.
      • Its outer grey matter is the most important part.
    2. Mid brain includes corpora quadrigemina and crura cerebri.
    3. Hind brain consists of cerebellum, pons Varolii and medulla oblongata.

Cerebellum

It is concerned with equilibrium of the body and co-ordination of muscles.

Medulla Oblongata

  • Lowest part of the brain and is connected with the spinal cord.
  • It controls the involuntary actions. Also works as the passage for impulses between spinal cord and rest of brain.

Reflex Action

  • It can be defined as the spontaneous response to the external stimuli.
  • It is not co-ordinated by the brain but by the spinal cord.

Sense Organs

There are several organs in the body that receive the external and internal stimuli and convey it to the brain and spinal cord. The main sense organs are eye, ear, skin, tongue and nose.

Digestive System

  • The process of converting food into energy giving substances is carried out by this system.
  • The digestive system comprises the alimentary canal and the associated digestive glands like liver and pancreas.

Alimentary Canal

It comprises following parts

Buccal Cavity

  • In humans, teeth are produced in two sets : Milk teeth and permanent teeth. The teeth are of four types : incisors, canines, premolars and molars.
  • Dental Formula : in adults
    \( I \frac{2}{2}, C \frac{1}{1}, Pm \frac{2}{2}, m \frac{3}{3}, = \frac{8}{8} \times 2 = 32 \)
  • The buccal cavity contains teeth, tongue and palate.
  • In the mouth, salivary glands secrete saliva.
  • Food is broken down, masticated and mixed with saliva.
  • Salivary amylase converts starch into maltose. Another enzyme maltase converts maltose into glucose.

Stomach

  • It is a warehouse, where food can be stored to await the main process of digestion.
  • Digestive gastric juice contains water, mucin salt, HC1 and three enzymes :
    1. \( Pepsin \rightarrow Converts \ protein \rightarrow Peptones \)
    2. \( Rennin \rightarrow Converts \ milk protein(Casein) \rightarrow Calcium \ para \ casesinate. \)
    3. \( Lipase \rightarrow Converts \ fats \rightarrow Glycerol \ and \ fatty \ acids. \)
  • Here, various enzymes are secreted, which act upon various nutrients of food and digest them.
  • Also contains HC1, which kills bacteria apart from other things.

Small Intestine

  • Here, the food from stomach is mixed with bile (from liver) and pancreatic juice (from pancreas).
  • Bile doesn't take direct part in digestion while pancreatic juice has several enzymes to help in digestion.
  • Food is absorbed after digestion in small intestine only.

Large Intestine

  • It receives undigested material from the small intestine and absorbs water.
  • The undigested material is finally discharged through anus.

Excretory System

Excretion is the removal of toxic waste products of metabolism from the body.

  • Waste materials in animals are of many kinds : nitrogenous substances, carbon dioxide, pigments, excess water, etc.
  • The nitrogenous waste material can be excreted in the form of ammonia (in aquatic animals), urea (in mammals) or uric acid (in birds, insects and reptiles).
  • Humans, being mammals are ureotelic, i.e., they excrete urea.
  • The pH of urine is between 5 - 8.
  • Kidney stones are basically made of calcium oxalate.

Kidney

  • The chief excretory organ in humans is kidney only.
  • There are two kidneys in the human body.
  • Each kidney is made up of numerous microscopic functional units called nephrons or uriniferous tubules.
  • The kidneys collect the excretory products and eliminate them in the form of urine.
  • The urine is released periodically to the outside through urethra.
  • Dialysis is a technique used in the person whose kidney doesn't perform well.

Food and Nutrition

Balanced Diet

  • A diet, which has various nutritional materials like protein, carbohydrate, fats, mineral, vitamins, roughage and water in sufficient amount and proper proportions.
  • Balanced diet of each individual can be determined according to his or her needs.
  • A pregnant lady requires more diet.

Carbohydrates

  • Constitute three elements : Carbon, hydrogen and oxygen.
  • Daily requirement 500 g. 1 g gives 17 kJ energy.
  • The carbohydrates of the food eaten, after being processed in the alimentary canal and liver, are supplied to the tissues mainly as glucose, often called blood sugar.
  • Sources three main cereals (wheat, rice and maize), sugarcane, milk, banana, honey, beet, etc.
  • Examples Cellulose, starch and sugar (glucose, fructose, sucrose, etc).
  • Structurally, carbohydrates are of three types : Monosaccharides, Disaccharides and polysaccharides. Excess carbohydrate is stored in the liver in the form of glycogen.

Fats

  • Provides double the energy of carbohydrate (1 g provides 37 kJ of energy).
  • Act as the reserve food material because excess fat is stored in the liver and as adipose tissue.
  • An enzyme called Lipase digests fats. It breaks down into fatty acids and glycerol.
  • Daily requirement : 50 g.

Proteins

  • Composition : Carbon, hydrogen, oxygen and nitrogen.
  • Important for growth and repair of the body.
  • Made up of amino acids.

Minerals

  • Some of the important minerals which form a part of our food are :
  • Iron Needed to prepare haemoglobin, which carries oxygen in blood and gives blood its red colour. It is present in green vegetables.
  • Iodine For preparation of the hormone thyroxin. It is present in sea food and iodised salt.
  • Fluorine Component of enamel of teeth and bones. Present in milk and drinking water.
  • Calcium Required in making bones and teeth, nerve functioning and muscle contraction. Needed more in children.
  • Phosphorus For formation of bones and teeth. Present in milk.
  • Sodium and Potassium Sodium is needed to maintain contraction of heart. Potassium is required in carbohydrate and protein metabolism.

Vitamins

  • Necessary for normal growth, good health, good vision, proper digestion of the body, etc. They do not provide energy to our body.
  • Vitamins can be divided into two categories :
    1. Water - soluble Vitamin B - complex, vitamin - C.
    2. Fat - soluble Vitamin - A, vitamin - D, vitamin - E, vitamin - K.

Roughages

  • These are fibrous material present in food items.
  • They do not take part in growth because these are not digested by human.
  • These should be added in bulk in food to prevent constipation.
  • Sources are salads, outer layers of grains, cereals, vegetables and porridge (dalia).

Water

  • Human body contains about 65 per cent water.
  • It regulates body temperature by sweating and evaporation.
  • Main sources are metabolic water, liquid food and drinking water.
  • Abnormal depletion of body fluid leads to dehydration.

Malnutrition

  • Inadequate or under nourishment or over nutrition or unbalanced diet are the cause of malnutrition.
  • Protein malnutrition leads to kwashiorkor and marasmus in growing child.
  • Goitre is caused due to deficiency of iodine in diet.

Disease

It is a condition of the body or a part of it in which functions are disturbed.

Communicable Diseases

Infectious as spread from infected person to health one.
They are of further two types

Contagious

  • Spread through direct contact.
  • Examples; Chickenpox, mumps, syphilis, meales, dermatitis, eczema, etc.

Non - Contagious

  • Spread through without coming in direct contact.
  • Examples; Influenza, tetanus, malaria, tuberculosis, etc.
    Non - communicable Diseases
  • Non - spreadable or non-infectious
  • Deficiency diseases (hormonal and vitamin deficiency)
  • Degenerative diseases (diseases due to inactively working body organs)
  • Allergic diseases (diseases due to colour, dust, etc).
  • Addiction

Common Body Disorders

Allergy A special reaction to a certain substance such as pollen or certain foods (causing sneeze, skin rashes, etc).
Allergy can be from any material, even by colours, etc.
Arthritis Inflammation of joints.
Asthma A respiratory disorder caused by narrowing of bronchial tubes. It can be caused by infection or due to allergy.
Bronchitis Inflammation of bronchial tubes caused by bacteria or virus.
Cancer An abnormal growth of body cells, often resulting in a malignant tumour.
Diabetes Mellitus Excess sugar in the body, when the body is not able to control the level of blood sugar due to malfunctioning of pancreas (inadequate production of insulin).
Epilepsy Unwarned and periodic loss of consciousness alongwith convulsions, due to nervous disorders.
Mumps An acute infection particularly in children and young adults in which there is swelling of neck and high fever.
Hepatitis An infectious or inflammatory disease of the liver commonly identified by its primary symptoms of jaundice.
Hernia A weakness of the muscle surrounding an organ allowing it to bulge through, often found in the groin.
Jaundice Excessive bilirubin (present in bile juice secreted by liver) in the blood, sign yellowing of eyes, skin and even urine, can lead to death.
Leukaemia Blood cancer.
Measles A contagious disease caused by virus, red rashes appear on the body alongwith fever.
Migraine A type of a headache followed by disturbed vision and no speech accompanied by nausea.
Pellagra A disease caused by the deficiency of vitamin - 13, (niacin). Its symptoms are 3D's : Diarrhoea, Dermatosis, Dementia.
Osteomalacia A disease caused by shortage of vitamin - D (calciferol), which results in softening of bones, frequent fractures and bending of the backbone.
Pleurisy Inflammation of the membrane that covers the lungs and lines the chest cavity.
Rabies A viral disease transmitted by the saliva of infected animals. Symptoms include convulsions and repulsion to water (hydrophobia).
Ringworm A skin disease causing circular swellings on the skin. Transmitted through air - borne spores and contact with infected person.
Slipped Disc A painful condition in which a cartilage disc in the spine is displaced putting pressure on the nearest nerve.
Small Pox A contagious viral disease, common among children. Symptoms are rashes on skin.
Thrombosis Formation of blood clot in a blood vessel or in the heart causing death.
Ulcer An inflammed open sore on the skin, or on the membrane of a body cavity. Peptic ulcer is a condition in which ulcer is there in the food pipe causing burning sensations.

Medical Equipments and Aids

Cystoscope An instrument for examining the inside of the urinary bladder.
Cat Scanner The Computerized Axial Tomography (CAT) scanner is a machine for photographing a selected part of the human body using X-rays. It is a ring - shaped X-ray machine that rotates through \( 180^{\circ} \) around the patient, who lies on a horizontal plane, making numerous X-rays measurements at every few degrees. The data is processed by a computer to produce the final image.
\( Artificial Kidney \) A device containing tubes or chambers of a special membrane immersed in a bath of dialysing solution, used to remove waste material from the blood of patient whose kidneys do not work properly.
\( Branchioscope \) An instrument with a lighted tube which is passed dawn the trachea (wind pipe) for examination of the air tubes of the lungs.
\( Eectro encephalograph \) (EEG) Device used to obtain graphic resentation of the electrical activity of the brain cells (neurons) that are picked up by the electrodes placed on the scalp.
\( Electrocardiograph \) (ECG) Device used to record the electric activity of the heart to detect abnormal electric impulses through the muscles.
\( Endoscope \) Used for internal examination by direct vision through a lighted tube fitted with a system of lenses.
\( Gastroscope \) An instrument fitted with a lighted tube passed down the gullet (oesophagus) to inspect the lining of the stomach.
\( Heart - Lung Machine \) A machine comprising a system of pumps, which takes over the function of the heart during open-heart surgery and circulates the blood around the body. The machine also takes over the function of the lungs in supplying fresh oxygen to the blood.
\( Laproscope \) An instrument used for the examination of the abdominal cavity.
\( Sphygmomanometer \) An instrument used to measure blood pressure.
\( Stethoscope \) An instrument used to measure pulse rate.
