Introduction

Identifying the x-axis, y-axis and origin on a Cartesian plane

  1. The Cartesian system is used to determine the pcsition of a point on Cartesian plane. A Cartesian plane consists of two number lines which intersect at right angles

  2. The horizontal number line is the x-axis and the vertical number line is the y-axis.

  3. The point of intersection between the x-axis and y-axis is called the origin.

Stating the coordinates of a given point on a Cartesian plane

  1. The position of a point on a Cartesian plane is represented by an ordered pair (x, y), which are the coordinates of the point.
    x-coordinate This means the point is x units from the y-axis. \( \longrightarrow \) (x, y)
    x-coordinate This means the point is y units from the x-axis. \( \longrightarrow \) (x, y)
    Example

    The coordinates of points A and B are (3, 4) and (-6, 2) respectively. This means that point A is located 3 units from the y-axis and 4 units from the x-axis, whereas point B is located 6 units on the left from the y-axis and 2 units from the x-axis.

  2. The coordinate of the origin 0 is (0, 0).

Plotting points on a Cartesian plane

  1. When the coordinates of a point are given, we can plot the point on the Cartesian plane.

  2. To plot a point with coordinates (m, n):
    Step 1: Move m units from the y-axis.
    Step 2: Then, move n units from the x-axis
    Step 3: Mark the point with a cross (x) sign.

Plotting points on a Cartesian plane

  1. A Cartesian plane is made up of 4 quadrants. We can plot any point on the Cartesian plane by first determining the quadrant in which the point is:

  2. All x-coordinates to the the left of the y-axis are positive, while all x-coordinates to the left of the y-axis are negative.

  3. All y-coordinates above x-axis are positive, while all y-coordinates below the x-axis are negative. Example

    The coordinates of point B are (5, -3)

Scales of the Coordinate Axes:

  1. The scale for any axis is written in the form of a ratio.
    Example : A scale of 1 : 2 means one unit on the graph represents 2 units of the actual length.

  2. Both coordinate axes on the Cartesian plane may have:

    1. the same scalers or

    2. different scales.

Locus

A two dimensional locus is the path along which a point moves in a plane so as to satisfy given crditions.
Example :

  1. The locus of the tip of the minute hand of a clock as it moves between 12 noon and 1.00 p.m. is a circle. Locus of the minute hand.

  2. The locus of a football being kicked by a player is a curve.

Symmetry

Line symmetry

A geometrical figure is said to have line symmetry if it can be divided into two identical halves by a line drawn through it, i.e., if the figure is folded about this line, the two parts of the figure will coincide exactly. The line dividing the figure into two congruent parts, i.e., two symmetric halves, is known as the line of symmetry or the axis of symmetry.

Point symmetry

A figure is said to have point symmetry about a point O, if every line segment joining two points on the boundary of the figure and also passing through O gets bisected at O. The point O is known as the point of symmetry or centre of symmetry.

Reflection of a Point in a Line:

The reflection of the point (object) P in the line AB is the point PI such that

  1. \( PP' \perp AB \) , and

  2. AB bisects PP' , i.e., PQ = QP' .

P' is called the image of the point P in the line AB, called axis of reflection. We also say that on reflection in the line AB, the point P is mapped ontoP' .
Clearly, AB is the perpendicular bisector of the line segment PP' , and P and P'. are on the opposite sides of AB.

Reflection of a Point in the x-axis

The image of the point (x, y) in the x-axis is the point (x, -y)

Reflection of a Point in the y-axis

The image of the point (x, y) in the y-axis is the point (-x, y).

Reflection of a Point in a Line Parallel to the x-axis

The image of the point (x, y) in the line parallel to the x-axis at a distance a from the x-axis (i.e., y = a) is the point (x, -y + 2a),
where a is taken positive if the line is on the positive side of the y-axis and a is taken negative if the line is on the negative side of the y-axis.

Reflection of a Point in a Line Parallel to the y-axis

The image of the point (x, y) in the line parallel to the y-axis at a distance a from the y-axis (i.e., x = a) is the point (-x + 2a, y)
where a is taken positive if the line is on the positive side of the x-axis and a is taken negative if the line is on the negative side of the x-axis.

invariant Points for Reflection in a Line

If the point P is on the line AB then clearly its image in AB is P itself.

We say P is an invariant point for the axis of reflection AB.
Thus, all the point lying on a line are invariant points for reflection in that line, and no point lying outside the line will be an invariant point.

Reflection of a Point in a Point

Let A be a fixed point and P be any other point.

If PA is produced to a point P' such that PA = AP' then the point P' is the image of the point P in the point A.
Clearly, A is the middle point of PP.
It is also obvious that the point A is the only invariant point for reflection in the point A.

Reflection of a Point in the Origin

Let the coordinates of the point P be (x, y) and O be the origin. Join P and O, and produce PO to the point P' such that OP = P'O. Thus, P is the image of P in the origin O.
From the congruent triangles OAP and OAP' we find that the coordinates of P' are (-x, -y).

Some Geometrical Properties of Reflection in a Point or a Line

  1. If P' is the image of P in a line AB then AB is the perpendicular bisector of PP'.

  2. If P' is the image of P in the point A then P, A and P' collinear and PA = AP'.

  3. The shape and size of a geometrical object does not change on reflection in a line or a point.
    However, the orientation of the image changes

The diagonals of a quadrilateral bisects each other at right angles. Show that the quadrilateral is a rhombus.

Solution:

To prove: Quadrilateral ABCD is a rhombus.
Proof: Since AC bisects BD at right angles (given)
\( \therefore \) AC is the perpendicular bisector of line segment BD.
and
\( \begin{align} \therefore \left. \begin{array}{cc} AB = CD \\\\ CB = CD \end{array} \right\rbrace \end{align} \) ........(1) Again, BD bisects AC at right angles (given)
\( \therefore \) BA = BC and DA = DC ... (2) From (1) and (2), AB = BC = CD = DA
\( \therefore \) Quadrilateral ABCD is a rhombus.

Example 2:

What is the locus of points which are equidistant from three given non-collinear points A, B and C ? Justify your answer

Solution:
Let A, B and C be three non-collinear points.
Join AB and BC.
Draw the perpendicular bisectors of AB and BC.
Let they meet each other at O.
Since, O lies on the right bisector of AB. \( \therefore \) OA = OB
Similarly, OB = OC.
\( \therefore \) OA = OB = OC Hence the point 0 is equidistant from A, B and C.

Now since the right bisectors of AB and BC are two non-parallel lines, hence they have only one point in common and the point is O.
\( \therefore \) O is the only point equidistant from A, B and C.
Hence the required locus is the centre of the circle through three given non-collinear points.

Example 14:

Find the locus of the centre of a circle of radius r touching externally a circle of radius R.

Solution:
Let a circle of radius r (with centre B) touch a circle of radius R (with centre A) at C. Then ACB is a straight line and AB = AC + CB = R + r.
Thus, B moves such that its distance from fixed point A remain constant and is equal to R + r. Hence the locus of B is a circle whose centre is A and radius equal to R + r.
