Triangle

Equilateral Triangle

\( \begin{align} Area \ A = \frac{1}{2} \times base \times height \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2} \times b \times h \\\\[3pt] but \ h = \frac{ \sqrt{3}}{2} a \\\\[3pt] \therefore A = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{ \sqrt{3}}{2} a \times a = \frac{ \sqrt{3}}{4} a^2 \end{align} \)

Right-Angled Triangle

\( \begin{align} Area \ A = \frac{1}{2} \times base \times height \\\\[3pt] (or) \ = \frac{1}{2} \ (product \ of \ sides \ making \ 90^{\circ}) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2} bh \\\\[3pt] but \ h = \sqrt{d^2 - b^2} \\\\[3pt] \therefore A = \frac{1}{2} b \left( \sqrt{d^2 - b^2} \right) \end{align} \)

Acute-Angled Triangle

\( \begin{align} Area (A) = \sqrt{s(s - a) (s - b) (s - c)} \\\\[3pt] Where \ S = \frac{a + b + c}{2} \\\\[3pt] \text{S is the semi-perimeter of the triangle} \\\\[3pt] A = \frac{1}{2} bh = \frac{b}{2}. \sqrt{a^2 - \left( \frac{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}{2b} \right)} \end{align} \)

Obtuse Angled Triangle

\( \begin{align} Area (A) = \sqrt{s(s - a)(s - b)(s - c)} \\\\[3pt] where \ S = \frac{a + b + c}{2} \\\\[3pt] A = \frac{1}{2} bh = \frac{b}{2}. \sqrt{a^2 - \left( \frac{c^2 - a^2 - b^2}{2b} \right)^2} \end{align} \)

Quadrilaterals

Square

  1. \( Area = (side)^2 = a^2 \)

  2. \( Side (a) = \sqrt{area} \)

  3. \( Diagonal (D) = \sqrt{2 (side)^2} = \sqrt{2 \times Area} = 1.414 \sqrt{Area} \)

Rectangle

  1. \( Area (A) = length \times breadth = l \times b \)

  2. \( Area (A) = l \sqrt{d^2 - l^2} = b. \sqrt{d^2 - b^2} \)

  3. Perimeter (S) = 2(length + breadth)
    = 2(l + b)

  4. \( Diagonal (D) = \sqrt{l^2 + b^2} \)

Parallelogram

  1. \( Area (A) = base \times height = bh \)

  2. \( b = \frac{A}{h} \)

  3. \( h = \frac{A}{b} \)

Rhombus

\( Area (A) = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Product of its diagonals} \)
\( = \frac{1}{2} \times d_1 \times d_2 \)

Trapezium

\( Area (A) = \frac{1}{2} (sum \ of \ parallel \ sides) \times height \)
\( = \frac{1}{2} (a + b) h \)

Trapeziod

\( Area (A) = \frac{(H + h) a + bh + CH}{2} \)
A trapezoid can be divided into two triangles as indicated by dotted line. The area of each of these triangles is calculated, and the result added to find the area of trapezoid

Circle

  1. Area of circle (A) = \( \pi r^2 = \frac{ \pi d^2}{4} \)
    where r = radius
    d = diameter of a circle

  2. Circumference = \( 2 \pi r = \pi d = 3.14 d \)

  3. \( Radius = \sqrt{ \frac{area}{ \pi}} \)

  4. \( Diameter = \frac{circumference}{ \pi} \)

Semi Circle

  1. Area of semi circle (A) = \( \frac{1}{2} \times \pi r^2 \)

  2. Perimeter of semi circle (S) = \( 2r + \pi r = r(2 + \pi) \)

Circular Sector

l = length of arc
\( \alpha \) = angle in degrees
A = Area
\( \begin{align} l = \frac{ \alpha}{360^{ \circ}} \times 2 \pi r \ units \\\\[3pt] = \frac{ \alpha \times pi \times r}{180^{ \circ}} \\\\[3pt] A = \frac{ \alpha}{360^{ \circ}} \times \pi r^2 \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2} \times l \times r \end{align} \)

Circular Ring

\( \begin{align} D = 2r, \ d = 2r \\\\[3pt] Area (A) = \pi(R^2 - r^2) \\\\[3pt] = \pi (R + r) (R - r) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{ \pi}{4} (D + d) (D - d) \end{align} \)

Ellipse

Length of semi major axis is 'a'.
Length of major axis is '2a'.
Length of semi minor axis is 'b'.
Length of minor axis is '2b'.
2a \( \rightarrow \) length of major axis
2b \( \rightarrow \) length of minor axis

  1. Area (A) = \( \pi ab \)

  2. Perimeter (or) Circumference \( C = \pi \sqrt{2 (a^2 + b^2)} \ (approx) \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

The difference of the area of the circumscribed and the inscribed squares of a circle is 35 sq.cm. Find the area of the circle.

Solution:
Let the radius of the circle be x cm
\therefore diameter = 2x cm
Now, diagonal of the inscribed square = 2x cm
\therefore Area of the inscribed square = \( \frac{diagonal^2}{2} \)
\( = \frac{(2x)^2}{2} = 2x^2 \ sq.cm \)
Again side of the circumscribed square = 2x cm
\therefore Area circumscribed square = \( (2x)^2 sq.cm \)
\( = 4x^2 \ sq.cm \)
So, by the problem,
\( \begin{align} 4x^2 - 2x^2 = 35 \\\\[3pt] or \ 2x^2 = 35 \\\\[3pt] x^2 = \frac{35}{2} \end{align} \)
Area of circle = \( \pi x^2 = \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{35}{2} = 55 \ sq.cm \)

Example 2:

The sides of a triangle are a = 13 cm, b = 14 cm, c = 15 cm, the sides a and b are the tangents to a circle , whose centre lies on the third side. Find the circumference of the circle.
Note :

  1. Tangent is a line touching the circle at one point

  2. Radius is perpendicular to the tangent

Solution:
The centre O of the circle lies an AB and let r cm be the be the radius of the cirlce.
Since radius is perpendicular to the tangent at the point of contact.
The area of \( \Delta{BOC} = \frac{1}{2} \times 13 \times r \ sq.cm \ and \ \Delta{AOC} = \frac{1}{2} \times 14 \times r \ sq.cm \)
Hence the total area of the \( \Delta{ABC} = \Delta{BOC} + \Delta{AOC} \)
\( = \left( \frac{1}{2}.13.r + \frac{1}{2}.14.r \right) \ sq.cm \)
\( = \frac{1}{2} \times r \times 27 \ sq.cm \)
Again if S = semi-perimeter = \( \frac{13 + 14 + 15}{2} \ cm \)
= 21 cm
The area of \( \Delta{ABC} = \sqrt{s(s - a)(s - b)(s - c)} = \sqrt{21(8)(7)(6)} = 84 \ sq.cm \)
\therefore Comparing these two,
\( \frac{1}{2} \times r \times 27 = 84 \)
or \( r = \frac{56}{9} \ cm \)
\therefore Cirumference of the circle = \( 2 \pi r = 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{56}{9} = 39 \frac{1}{9} \ cm \)

Example 3:

The two adjacent sides of a parallelogram are 5 cm and 4 cm respectively and if the respective diagonal is 7 cm, then find the area of the parallelogram ?

Solution:
Required area = \( 2 \sqrt{s(s - a)(s - b)(s - D)} \)
\( \begin{align} where \ s = \frac{a + b + D}{2} = \frac{5 + 4 + 7}{2} = 8 \\\\[3pt] = 2 \sqrt{8(8 - 5)(8 - 4)(8 - 7)} \\\\[3pt] = 2 \sqrt{8 \times 3 \times 4} = 8 \sqrt{6} = 19.6 \ sq.cm \end{align} \)

Example 4:

A 5100 sq.cm trapezium has the perpendicular distance between the two parallel sides 60 cm. If one of the parallel sides be 40 cm then find the length of the other parallel sides.

Solution:
Let the length of the unknown parallel side be 'x' cm.
\( A = \frac{1}{2} (a + b) h \)
\( or \ 5100 = \frac{1}{2} (40 + x) \times 60 \)
or 170 = 40 + x
\therefore Required other parallel side = 170 - 40 = 130 cm

Example 5:

How many metres of a carpet 75 cm wide will be required to cover the floor of a room which is 20 metres long and 12 metres broad?

\( Length \ required = \frac{length \ of \ room \times breadth \ of \ room}{width \ of \ carpet} \)
\( \therefore Length \ required = \frac{20 \times 12}{0.75} = 320 \ m \)

Example 10:

A square field of area 31684 sq.mts is to be enclosed with wire placed at heights 1, 2, 3, 4 mts above the ground. What length of the wire will be required, if its length required for each circuit is 5% greater than the perimeter of the field ?

Solution:
Area of the field = 31684 sq.mts
\( \therefore Perimeter = \sqrt{31684} \times 4 \ mts = 178 \times 4 \ mts \)
\therefore Length of each circuit = \( 178 \times 4 \times \frac{105}{100} \ mts \)
Since the wire goes round 4 times,
\therefore Total length of wire required = \( 178 \times 4 \times \frac{105}{100} \times 4 \ m = 2990.4 m \)

Example 11:

Find the area of the triangle whose sides are 50 m, 78 m, 112 m respectively and also find the perpendicular from the opposite angle on the side 112 m.

Solution:
Here a = 50 m, b = 78 m, c = 112 m
\( \begin{align} \therefore S = \frac{1}{2} (50 + 78 + 112) m = \frac{1}{2} \times 240 \ m = 120 \ cm \\\\[3pt] \therefore S - a = (120 - 50) m = 70 m \\\\[3pt] S - b = (120 - 78) m = 42 m \\\\[3pt] S - c = (120 - 112) m = 8 m \\\\[3pt] \therefore Area = \sqrt{120 \times 70 \times 42 \times 8} sq.cm = 1680 \ sq.cm \\\\[3pt] Perpendicular = \frac{2 \ Area}{Base} = \frac{1680 \times 2}{112} m = 30 \ m \end{align} \)

Example 12:

The perimeter of a rhombus is 146 cm and one of its diagonal is 55 cm. Find the other diagonal and the area of the rhombus

Solution:
Other diagonal = \( 2 \times \sqrt{ (side)^2 - \left( \frac{diagonal}{2} \right)^2} \)
Now one side of a rhombus = \( \frac{146}{4} = 36.5 cm \)
\( Other diagonal = 2 \times \sqrt{(36.5)^2 - \left( \frac{55}{2} \right)^2} = 48 \ cm \)
Now, area = \( \frac{1}{2} \) (product of diagonals) = \( \frac{1}{2} \times 48 \times 55 = 1320 \ sq.cm \)

Example 14:

The length of a rectangle is increased by 60%. By what percent should the width be decreased to maintain the same area?

Solution:
Let the length and breadth be x and y then its area = xy
New length = \( x \left( \frac{160}{100} \right) = \frac{8x}{5} \)
As the area remains the same, the new breadth of the rectangle = \( \frac{xy}{ \frac{8x}{5}} = \frac{5y}{8} \)
\therefore Decrease in breadth = \( y - \frac{5y}{8} = \frac{3y}{8} \)
\therefore % decrease in breadth = \( \frac{3y \times 100}{8 \times y} = \frac{75}{2} = 37 \frac{1}{2} \% \)

Example 15:

If the length of a rectangle increases by 10% and the breadth of the rectangle decreases by 12% then find the % change in area.

New length = 110 units
New breadth = 88 units

Example 16:

  1. What is the relation between a circle and an equilateral triangle which is inscribed in the circle?

  2. What is the relation between an equilateral triangle and a circle inscribed in a circle ?

  3. An equilateral triangle is circumscribed by a circle and another circle is inscribed in that triangle. Find the ratio of the areas of two circles?

  1. The area of a circle circumscribing an equilateral triangle of side x is \( \frac{ \pi}{3} x^2 \)

  2. The area of a circle inscribed in an equilateral triangle of side x is \( \frac{ \pi}{12} x^2 \)

Example 17:

The front wheels of a wagon are \( 2 \pi \) m in circumference and the back wheels are \( 3 \pi \) m feet in circumference. When the front wheels have made 10 more revolutions than the back wheels, how many metres has the wagon travelled?

Solution:
Suppose the back wheel has made x revolutions.
\therefore Front wheel has made (10 + x) revolutions
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow 3 \pi x = 2 \pi (10 + x) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \pi x = 2 \pi \times 10 \Rightarrow x = 20 \end{align} \)
\therefore The wagon has travelled \( 3 \pi x = 60 \pi \)

Example 27:

If one leg of an isosceles right-angled triangle is increased by 6 cm and that of the other leg decreased by 4 cm, then the area of the triangle decreases by 24 sq cm. Find the length of the leg of the original triangle.

Solution:
Let x be the length of the leg of the right-angled isosceles triangle, originally.

Its area = \( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \)
The area of the new triangle = \( \frac{1}{2} (x - 4) (x + 6) \)

\therefore \frac{1}{2} x^2 = \frac{1}{2} (x - 4) (x + 6) + 24
\( \Rightarrow x^2 = x^2 + 2x - 24 + 48 \Rightarrow x = - 12 \)

Example 28:

What is the ratio of the heights of two isosceles triangles which have equal vertical angles, and of which the areas are in the ratio of 9 : 16?

Solution:
Let the heights be \( h_1 \) and \( h_2 \) . Let the bases be \( B_1 \) and \( B_1 \) respectively.
\( \begin{align} \therefore \frac{ \frac{1}{2} \times B_1 \times h_1}{ \frac{1}{2} \times B_2 \times h_2} = \frac{9}{16} \\\\[3pt] Let \ \frac{B_1}{h_1} = \frac{B_2}{h_2} = k, \ say \\\\[3pt] \therefore \frac{h_1^2}{h_2^2} = \frac{9}{16} \Rightarrow \frac{h_1}{h_2} = \frac{3}{4} \end{align} \)

Example 29:

Four horses are tethered at four corners of a square plot of side 14 metres (m) so that the adjacent horses can just reach one another. There is a small circular pond of area \( 20 \ m^2 \) at the centre. Find the ungrazed area.

Solution:
Total area = \( 14 \times 14 = 196 \ m^2 \)
Grazed area = \( \left( \frac{ \pi \times r^2}{4} \right) \times 4 = \pi r^2 = 22 \times 7( r = 7) = 154 \ m^2 \)
Ungrazed area = (196 - 154) = \( 42 \ m^2 \)

Example 30:

A square, whose side is 2 metres, has its corners cut away so as to form an octagon with all sides equal. Then find the length of each side of the octagon, in metres.

Solution:
Let the position of the edge cut at each corner be x m. Since the resulting figure is a regular octagon.

\( \begin{align} \therefore \sqrt{x^2 + x^2} = 2 - 2x \Rightarrow x \sqrt{2} = 2 - 2x \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \sqrt{2} x(1 + \sqrt{2}) = 2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = \frac{ \sqrt{2}}{ \sqrt{2} + 1} \end{align} \)
