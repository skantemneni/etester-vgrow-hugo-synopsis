Common misconception Fact
1 Coal and petroleum are used mainly as sources of fuel and nothing else. And we get all kinds of clothing material from plants and animals only. Most of the synthetic fibres are obtained from coal, petroleum and natural gas.
2 Natural fibres are short fibres where as synthetic fibres are continous filament fibres. Silk is a natural fibre whose continuous filaments can stretch sometimes upto 1 kilometer length.
3 Plastics are biodegradable, i.e., decomposed by micro-organisms. Plastics are non-biodegradable, they stay on the earth for some thousands of years.

Introduction

A fibre is a thread or a filament from which cloth is made. If synthesized by humans, it is known as synthetic fibre and if obtained from plants and animals it is known as natural fibre. The materials which are obtained from natural or artificial sources, by weaving or knitting the fibres are called fabrics. For example, the clothes which we wear are made up of fabrics. Eg: cotton, wool etc., [natural fabrics]. Most of the synthetic fabrics are also made from coal and petrol. The components of coal or natural gas are heated to very high temperature under high pressure, which results in conversion of these components as starting materials for many fabrics.

Types of synthetic fibres - Their properties and Uses

Nylon

  1. It is a man-made fibre prepared from coal, water and air.

  2. Nylon fibre is strong, elastic and light.

  3. It is lustrous and easy to wash.

  4. It is used in preparing socks, ropes, tents, tooth brushes, car seat belts, parachutes, curtains, sleeping bags, ropes for rock climbing etc.

Rayon

  1. It is obtained by the chemical treatment of wood pulp or cotton.

  2. As this fibre resembles silk in properties it is also known as artificial silk.

  3. It is mixed with cotton to make bed-sheets or mixed with wool to make carpets.

Note
Silk is a natural fibre which can stretch upto 1 km.

Polyester

  1. Made from organic compounds, named as esters.

  2. Esters are the chemicals which give fruity smell.

  3. Single unit (monomer) of ester is repeated forming units called polymers.
    Example
    Polycot, polywool, terrycot etc.,

  4. Polycot is a mixture of polyester and cotton.

  5. Polywool is a mixture of polyester and wool.

  6. Terylene is a type of polyester if mixed with cotton it becomes terricot, like wise with wool, it becomes terrywool.

  7. Terrycot \( \rightarrow \) in making shirts, skirts etc.,

  8. Terrywool for making suits.

  9. It is strong, light weight and elastic, resists wrinkling and goes back to the normal shape after wrinkles too.

  10. Tensile strength is very high.

  11. Under the name 'Mylar' is used in making magnetic recording tapes in audio, video cassettes and floppy disks.

Acrylic

  1. Artificially prepared synthetic fibre which resembles wool is known as acrylic.

  2. They are also known as orlon and acrilan fabrics.

  3. It is warm, light and flexible.

  4. It can be easily knitted to make sweaters, socks and shawls.

Note

  1. Yarn is a spun thread used for sewing, weaving or knitting.

  2. Weaving and knitting are the ways of combining threads to form cloth.

  3. In some cases, rayon is not considered as a synthetic fibre literally, as it involves regeneration of cellulose from natural sources.

Spandex

  1. It is also known as lycra.

  2. spandex is mixed with other cotton fabrics, so that the fabric attains high elasticity.

  3. It is used in making T-shirts and caps.

Note
A single monomer unit combining to form a large unit, is called a polymer and the process is known as polymerisation.
Example

  1. Synthetic fibre is a chain of small units joined together.

  2. In cotton, a polymer called cellulose is made up of a large number of glucose units. (monomer unit = glucose)

Pet bottles and jars which we use to store grossary items are made up of polyesters.

Charecterristics of Synthetic fibre

  1. They dry up quickly.

  2. Durable.

  3. Less expensive.

  4. Readily available.

  5. Easy to maintain.

Advantages of synthetic fibres

  1. Some cotton fabrics like rayon, absorb moisture or sweat, hence keep light weight.

  2. Most of them are elastic, due to which they retain their original shape when compressed or stretched.

  3. Do not wrinkle easily, retain their original shape.

  4. Can handle heavy loads without breaking.

Disadvantage

The synthetic fibres melt on heating. If catches fire it sticks to the body of the person, wearing it.

Plastics

  1. Just as synthetic fibres, plastics are also polymers.

  2. As plastics can be moulded and cast into various shapes and sizes, we can prepare number of items.

  3. Plastics can be melted, recycled, reused, coloured, rolled into sheets or made into wires.

  4. The monomer units of plastics differ in the arrangement, where in certain cases, they are linear and sometimes cross-linked.

Note
Polymer means many units. Poly = many; mer = units in

Types of plastics

Basing on the reaction to heat, plastics may be classified as thermosetting plastics and thermo plastics.

Thermo plastics

They can be melted by heating and thereafter moulded into desired shapes and sizes.
Example
PVC (polyvinyl chloride) and polystryene; polythene.

Thermosetting plastics

Thermosetting plastics are harder and stronger than thermo plastics. They can retain their shape and size even at high temperatures.
Example

  1. Bakelite is created by Leo Backeland in 1907.

  2. Melamine

Note
First completely synthetic substance - Bakelite
As Bakelite is poor conductor of electricity, it is used for making electrical switches, handles of utensils etc.,
Melamine resists fire and tolerates heat better than other plastics. Hence, it is used in making floor tiles, kitchen ware and fabrics.

Advantages

Plastics are non reactive and do not conduct electricity.
Plastics are light, strong and durable.
They are light, price is low, good strength and easy to handle.
Hence used in aircraft, cars, spacecrafts, making slippers, furniture, decoration pieces etc.,

Plastics and their Environment

  1. Plastics cause health hazards and are very harmful to the environment, as they are NON - BIODEGRADABLE. cannot be decomposed by micro-organisms.

  2. Hence they prevent rain water from seeping into the earth, affect plant growth.

  3. Plastics lying on roadsides and floating in lakes and streams provide shelter for germs and animals causing diseases.

  4. Toxic to aquatic life when placed in water.

  5. Produce toxic gases when burnt, hence causing air pollution.

  6. Take several years to decompose.

Prevention or solution

  1. By recyling w involves collecting, sorting and processing the plastic.

  2. Follow 4R principle
    Reduce, Reuse, Recycle and Recover.

  3. Making use of cotton or jute bags while shopping.

  4. By dividing the garbage into biodegradable and non-biodegradable wastes and hence recycling the non - biodegredable wastes.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Ram wants to buy cotton shirts for summer. The whole family is happy and wanted to buy the same. Are they correct in their choice? Give reason.
Answer:
Cotton absorbs moisture and sweat. As a result, when we sweat more in summer the Sweat is not trapped between the fabric and the skin. Sweat keeps on evaporating from the fabric and we feel our skin can breath. Hence their choice of clothes is absolutely right.

Example 2:

Rayon is not considered as a synthetic fibre completely. Justify the statement.
Answer:
In the true sense or literally rayon is not considered as a synthetic fibre completely because, it involves the regeneration of cellulose from natural resources, in the synthesis, to improve the quality of fibre. Hence it is also called as modified natural fibre.

Example 3:

Is terylene a type of polyester? Explain.
Answer:
Polyester is light weight and strong. Terylene, as it is very elastic, used in making conveyor belts. Polyester under the name 'mylar' is used in making recording tapes in audio, video cassettes and floppy disks.

Example 4:

The thermal conductivity of plastics is very poor. Justify the statement giving examples.
Answer:
APlastics are very poor conductors of heat. Hence, they are used for making handles of cooking containers. In refrigerators (as plastic foam core) to prevent heat from outside from entering inside. As containers in micro waves.

Example 5:

Radha takes a plastic bucket to fill water. Would not plastic soluble in water?
Answer:
Plastics are insoluble in water and for this reason bottles, buckets containers made of plastic are used for storing Water, as plastic cups and tumblers to drink water.
