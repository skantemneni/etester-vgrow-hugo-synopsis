Common misconception Fact
1 Atoms are indivisible Atom can be divided into its sub-atomic particles mainly into electrons, protons and neutrons.
2 All the stable atoms follow and obey octet configuration i.e. Having 8 electrons in valence shell. 'He' atom shows duplet configuration i.e.2 electrons in valence shell and stable element.

Introduction

Atoms are the building blocks of all matter. The word atom is coined by Greek philosopher meaning indivisible they assumed that these are the smallest units of matter. The first theory about atom was put forward by Dalton. John Dalton put forward his theory in 1808 which is also known as Dalton's atomic theory.

Daltons atomic theory

It is proposed on the basis of law of conservation of mass and law of definite proportions. The main postulates of this theory are :

  1. Each element is composed of extremely small particles called atoms.

  2. Atoms of one element differ from the atoms of the other elements.

  3. Atoms are indestructible i.e., they can be neither created nor destroyed.

  4. Atom is the smallest unit of matter which takes part in a chemical reaction.

  5. Atoms of similar or dissimilar element combine in a fixed ratio by weight.

Merits

It provides a basic idea about the matter. It also embodies several simple laws of chemical combination, which were known at that time.

Demerits

  1. Discovery of subatomic particles concluded that atom is divisible.

  2. It could not explain why like atoms of element differ in their atomic masses

  3. The only convincing statement about Dalton's theory was that atoms of elements tak part in a chemical reaction in a simple integral ratio to form molecule.

  4. Further investigations on study of atom, led to the discovery of three fundamental particles and they are electron, proton and neutron.

Discovery of fundamental particles

  1. Electron was discovered in cathode ray experiment.

  2. The term electron was coined by G.J stoney.

  3. The study of electric discharge through partially evacuted tubes; at a high voltage (about 10,000 V) produces radiations within the tube.

Properties of cathode rays

  1. They travel in straight lines.

  2. They effect ZnS screen.

  3. They are negatively charged and are effected by magnetic field and electric field.

  4. They generate heating effect.

  5. They ionize the gases.

  6. They have penetrating power.

  7. They produce X-rays when a metal target is placed in their way.

  8. They cause mechanical motion.

Discovery of Protons

Atomic models

Atom contains fundamental particles electrons, protons and neutrons. But the systematic arrangement of there particles in an atom depicts the atomic model of an atom. J.J.Thomson.

Thomson model of an atom

  1. It is also known as water melon model or plum pudding model.

  2. According to Thomson, electrons are embedded in the positively charged mass distributed uniformly thoughout the atomic sphere.

Demerits

It was discarded because he could not explain how both positive and negatively charged particles protect their identity being together.
Later Rutherford conducted \( \alpha \) ray scattering experiment to test Thomson's model of an atom.

Rutherford's alpha ray scattering experiment

Rutherford bombarded thin 'Au' foil of \( 10^{-4} \) mm thickness with high speed \( \alpha \) - particles. An \( \alpha \) - particle is doubly ionised 'He' nuclei ( \( He^{++} \) ).

Observations Conclusions
(a) Most of the alpha particles passed without any deflection Most of the atom is hollow (empty) in space.
(b) Some of them were deflected away from their path. Due to repulsions.
(c) Only a few (1 in 20,000) returned back to the original direction of propagation. Most of the mass is present at the centre of the atom, which is positive called as nucleus.

Rutherford model or planetary model

  1. The entire positively charged particles are present in the centre of atom called as nucleus.

  2. According to Rutherford, electrons revolve around the nucleus just as planets revolve around the sun. Hence it is called as planetary model or Solar model.

  3. Electrons revolve around the nucleus with a high speed to overcome the electro static force of attractions between protons and electrons.

The size of nucleus = \( 10^{-15} m \ or \ 10^{-13} \ cm = 10^{-6} \ nm \)
1 fermi = \( 10^{-15} m \)
The size of the atom = \( 10^{-10} \ m \ or \ 10^{-8} \ cm = 10^{-1} \ nm \) (nm = nanometer)
The total positive charge = total negative charge of an atom. Hence atom is neutral as a whole.

Drawbacks of Rutherford theory

  1. It doesn't obey the Maxwell law of electrodynamics. According to this theory, a small charged particle like electron, while moving around the oppositely charged particles should lose energy continuously and finally fall down into the nucleus. But it is not happening.

  2. He could not specify the number of electrons orbiting in each orbit.

Plancks Quantum theory

Max Planck proposed quantum theory which states that any body either radiates or absorbs energy only in fixed packets of energy (called quanta) such that this energy (E) is directly proportional to its frequency ( \( \nu \) ).
i.e., \( E \propto \nu \)
\( E = h \nu \)
where h = Planck's constant = \( 6.623 \times 10^{-34} \ joule \ s \)

Bohr's model of atom

Neils Bohr proposed his atomic model based on Planck's quantum theory by overcoming the difficulties faced with Rutherford's model. It explained the origin of stationary orbits which also led to an entirely new concept of atomic structure.
This theory is based on classical physics and based on the postulates of Planck's quantum theory.

  1. Electrons revolve around the nucleus in definite, circular paths known as orbits or shells.

  2. Each orbit is associated with definite amount of energy. Hence they are known as stationary orbits.

  3. These shells are designated as K, L, M, N..... or 1, 2, 3, 4......

  4. As the orbit number increases, energy of the orbit increases.

  5. When electron jumps from lower to higher energy orbit, energy is absorbed. Whereas when it jumps from higher energy orbit to lower orbit, energy is released.
    This is given by \( \triangle{E} = E_1 - E_2 = \triangle{h \nu} \)
    Where \( E_2 \) = higher energy orbit
    \( E_1 \) = lower energy orbit
    h = planck's constant = \( 6.623 \times 10^{-34} \ joule \ s \)
    = \( 6.623 \times 10^{-27} \ erg \ s \)
    \( \nu \) = frequency

  6. The angular momentum of the orbit is equal to the integral multiple of \( \frac{h}{2 \pi} \)
    \( i.e., \ mvr = \frac{nh}{2 \pi} \) n = 1, 2, 3 ......
    m = mass of electron
    v = velocity of electron
    r = radius of orbit
    \therefore The angular momentums for K shell is = \( \frac{h}{2 \pi} \)
    L shell is = \( \frac{h}{ \pi} \)
    M shell is = \( \frac{3h}{2 \pi} \)
    N shell is = \( \frac{2h}{ \pi} \)

Merits

Bohr could explain about

  1. spectrum of one electron species like \( H, \ He^{+}, \ Li^{+2} \)

  2. quantisation of angular momentum.

Atomic number

  1. It is denoted by 'Z'

  2. The number of protons in an atom is atomic number.

  3. It is equal to the number of electrons in the atom.

Representation

\( _ZX^A \)
A = Mass number
Z = Atomic number
X = Element

Mass number

The sum of protons and neutrons present in the nucleus of an atom is mass number.

  1. It is denoted by 'A'

  2. Mass number (A) = number of protons + neutrons
    = Z + number of neutrons
    \therefore Number of neutrons in an atom = A - Z

Atomic Mass

The atomic mass of an element gives us the mass of the one atom of that element in amu.
Atomic mass of hydrogen = 1 a.m.u and Helium = 4.00150 a.m.u. Carbon - 12 isotope was chosen as standard reference for measuring atomic masses. The atomic mass corrected to the nearest whole number is given by the mass number.
Example
He atomic weight = 4.00150 amu, Mass number = 4

Isotopes

Atoms of the same element having same atomic number but different mass number are called isotopes.
Example

  1. \( \begin{array}{ccc} \underset{Protium}{_1H^1} & \underset{Deuterium}{_1H^2} & \underset{Tritium}{_1H^3} \end{array} \)

  2. \( ^{17}_C^{35}, \ ^{17}_C^{37} \)

  3. \( ^{6}_C^{12}, \ ^{6}_C^{14} \)

Note
Due to the isotopes, we obtain fractional values in atomic weights. Species having same number of electrons are known as isoelectronic species.
eg ;
\( \begin{align} \begin{array}{ccc} Na^{+} \ \ \ \ \ F^{-} \\\\ \underbrace{(11 - 1) \ \ (9 + 1)} \\\\ 10 e^- \end{array} \\\\[3pt] \begin{array}{ccc} CO \ \ \ \ \ CN^{-} \\\\ \underbrace{(6 + 8) \ \ (6 + 7 + 1)} \\\\ 14 e^- \end{array} \end{align} \)

Isobars

Atoms of different elements having same atomic mass.
eg: \( _{18}{Ar}^{40}, \ _{19}P^{40} \)

Isotones

Atoms of different elements with different mass numbers, but have same number of neutrons .
eg: \( _{16}S^{32}, \ _{15}P^{31} \)

Bohr-Bury Scheme

  1. The outer most orbit or ultimate shell or valence shell of an atom cannot have more than 8 electrons. Having \( 8e^- \) in the outermost orbit is known to have octet configuration. It is in accordance with octet rule.

  2. The penultimate shell [ \( 2^{nd} \) orbit from the last shell] of an atom cannot have more than 18 electrons.

  3. The antipenultimate shell [3rd orbit from the last shell] of an atom cannot have more than 32 electrons.

  4. The filling up of electrons in atom would be according to the Bohr - Bury principle.

  5. The total number of electrons in an orbit is given by the formula \( 2n^2 \) where n - orbit number.

Example

  1. Helium has electrons
    Z = 2 \( \Rightarrow He = 2 \times 1^2 \) = 2
    Here n = 1

  2. No.of electrons in the valence shell of 'Ne'
    Z = 10 \( \Rightarrow 2,\ 8 \rightarrow \) K, L
    Since in \( 2^{nd} \ shell = 2 \times 2^2 \)
    Here n = 2 = 8

Valence electrons

  1. The electrons present in the valence shell i.e., outer most orbit are called valence electrons.

  2. Generally this valence number gives the group number of that element.

Solved examples

Example 1:

The atomic masses of two isotopes of oxygen are 15.9936 and 17.0036 calculate in each atom.

  1. Number of neutrons

  2. Number of protons

  3. Number of electrons

  4. Mass Number

Answer:
\( _ZX^A = _8O^{16} \ and \ _8O_{17} \) ( let us consider 15.9936 = 16, 17.0036 = 17)

  1. Number of neutrons = A - Z
    in \( _8O^{16} \) = 16 - 8 = 8
    = 17 - 8 = 9 (8, 9)

  2. Number of protons \( _8O^{16} \) = 8 = Z atomic number
    \( _8O^{17} \) = 8 = Z = atomic number (8, 8)

  3. Number of electrons \( _8O^{16} \) = Z = 8 Number of electrons
    \( _8O^{17} \) = Z = 8 = number of electrons number (8, 8)

  4. Mass Number \( _8O^{16} \) = A = 16 Mass number
    \( _8O^{17} \) = A = 17 = Mass number (16, 17)

Example 2:

An atom of an element has 13 electrons. Its nucleus has 14 neutrons. Find out atomic number and approximate atomic weight. Indicate the arrangement of electrons and electrovalency.

Answer:
\( _ZX^A = _{13}X^{13 + 14} = _{13}X^{27} \)
Atomic number = 13
Approximate atomic weight = 13 + 14 = 27
= No. of electrons + No.of neutrons
Electronic configuration \Rightarrow 2, 8 , 3
Z = 13
The no.of electrons present in the outer most orbit are called valence electrons.
The combining capacity of elements is called valency. In the above case, outer most orbit shows 3 electrons, hence electrovalency is 3.

Example 3:

Calculate the number of electrons in the given ions.

  1. \( Na^+ \)

  2. \( Cl^- \)

  3. \( O^{2-} \)

Answer:
(a) \( Na^+ : Na \rightarrow \) atomic no : Z = 11
\( \therefore \ Na^+ = 11 - 1 = 10e^- \)
Note
When positive charge is present remove the electrons.
Here one positive charge is present So remove 1 electron.

  1. \( Cl^-: Cl^- rightarrow \) atomic no : Cl = 17 \( \therefore Cl^- \) = 17 + 1 = 18 electrons

  2. \( O^{2-} \) : O = Z = atomic number = 8
    \( \therefore O^{2-} \) = 8 + 2 = 10 electrons

Example 4:

An oil drop has \( 6.39 \times 10^{-19} \) coulombs charge. Find out the number of electrons in this oil drop.

Answer:
Charge on the oil drop \( \frac{6.39 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \) = 4 electrons.

Example 5:

If the average atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5 then find the percentage abundance of the two isotopes of chlorine which have the mass number 35 and 37.

Answer:
Let the percentage abundance of isotopic chlorine of mass number 35 be x
Average atomic mass \( \frac{(35x) + (37(100 - x))}{100} \)
\( 35.5 = \frac{ 35x + 3700 - 37x}{100} \)
3550 = 3700 - 2x
2x = 150 = x = 75
\( ^{17}Cl^{35} \) the percentage abundance is 75% whereas that of \( ^{17}Cl^{37} \) is 25%.

Example 6:

Three isotopes of an element have the mass numbers. A, A + 1 and A + 2. If they are present in 3 : 2 : 1 ratio, what is the average atomic mass of the element?
Answer:
Average atomic mass \( \frac{3(A) + 2(A + 1) + (A + 2)}{6} \)
\( \frac{6A + 4}{6} = \frac{(3A + 2)}{3} \)
