Introduction

Carbon is a universally occurring element. It is found in free as well as combined state. In the free state, carbon occurs as graphite, diamond and coal. Compounds of carbon on the other hand make up the structure of all living things. Carbon forms a large number of compounds with other elements.
The carbon compounds are studied under a separate section called organic chemistry. However, some carbon compounds like carbonates, bicarbonates, carbides, cyanides and oxides are studied under inorganic chemistry
Symbol : C
Atomic number : 6
Mass number : 12
Electronic configuration : 2, 4 (K, L)
\( C \Rightarrow 1s^2 \ 2s^2 \ 2p^2 \)
After oxygen and hydrogen, it is the third most important element. It occurs both in free state and in the combined states.
In the free state it occurs as diamond, graphite and coal.
In the combined state, it occurs in all solid, liquid and gaseous state.

Unique Characteristic of Carbon

Element carbon exhibits allotropy, shows catenation and the number of its compounds is very large. Hence a separate branch of chemistry namely organic chemistry is studied. It also has isotope \( C^{14} \) , which is used in carbon dating to know the age of plants and vegetable origin.

Allotropy

The existence of an element in two or more physical forms, is called allotropy. And the forms are called allotropes.
Allotropy is mainly due to the different atomic arrangement.
arbon has both crystalline and amorphous forms of allotropes.

Crystalline forms of Carbon

Diamond

It is found in a rock called kimperlite.

Preparation

Principle

Pure carbon is subjected to high temperature \( 3000^{\circ} C \) and very high pressure.

Process

Pure carbon obtained from cane sugar or graphite is added to molten iron in an electric furnace at a temperature above \( 3000^{\circ} C \)
By cooling this suddenly to \( 300^{\circ} C \) , solidification of iron takes place. Due to very high pressure on the dissolved carbon, it crystallizes out in the form of diamond.

Structure

The four valence electrons in the carbon atom are bonded strongly with the other four neighboring carbon atoms. These carbon atoms are again further bonded to four other carbons and so on in a regular tetrahedron pattern.
It forms a giant three dimensional structure of tetrahedron giving crystalline form.

\( O \Rightarrow Carbon \)

Physical properties

  1. Colour - Pure diamonds are colourless and transparent and others can be coloured blue, yellow, pink, red or black. Due to the presence of impurities, they are coloured.

  2. Nature - Due to the carbon - carbon covalent bonding, it is the hardest substance known.

  3. Density - The density of diamond is \( 3.5 g cm^{-3} \) . The distance between each C - C in diamond is \( 1.53 ^{\circ} A \) , which makes the crystal compact and makes it denser.

  4. Conductivity - Due to the absence of free electrons, it is a bad conductor of electricity.

  5. Transmission of X-ray - Diamonds are transparent to X-rays. Artificial ones can be distinguished by this property.

  6. Solubility - Insoluble in all the solvents.

  7. Effect of heat - When exposed to high temperature, it is destroyed.

Structure

  1. The carbon atoms in graphite are arranged in hexagonal layer like structures.

  2. Here the structure is two dimensional.

  3. Out of the four valence electrons in carbon, only three are involved in bonding one is free.

  4. Hexagonal layers are held by weak attractive forces, known as vanderwaal's for The layers can slide over the other, and are slippery to touch.

Physical Properties

  1. Dark grey crystalline solid with metallic lustre (shining nature).

  2. Its density is \( 2.3 \ g \ cm^{-3} \) .

  3. Due to weak vanderwaal's forces between the hexagonal layers, the layers are slippery to touch hence used as a lubricant.

  4. It is good conductor of electricity due to the presence of free electrons.

  5. Transmission of light - It is opaque to x-rays.

  6. Insoluble in solvents normally.

  7. The melting point of graphite is above \( 3500^{\circ} C \)
    \( graphite \xrightarrow{2500^{\circ} C} diamond \)

As it is a good conductor of electricity, used as electrode in batteries and electric ft naces. Due to its slippery nature, it is used as a lubricant.

Fullerenes

In 1985, a third allotropic form of carbon known as buckminister fullerene was discovered \( [C_{60}] \) .
The other fullerenes are \( C_{32}, \ C_{50}, \ C_{70}, \ C_{76}, \ C_{84} \) .
The molecules have the structure of tiny soccer balls and are known as buckyball.

Preparation

When an electric spark is stuck between graphite electrodes placed in argon gas, soot is produced.
This soot consists of carbon black fullerenes.
By chromatography technique, fullerenes are separated from soot, by dissolving in benzene.

Structure

One molecule of buckminister fullerene has 60 atoms arranged in hexagons and pentagons which are joined by double covalent bonds.

Properties

  1. It is yellow powder.

  2. Soluble in organic solvent.

  3. Density ranges from 1.8 to 2.1.

  4. It is a bad conductor of electricity (insulator).

  5. It is more active than diamond and graphite chemically.

Oxides of carbon

Carbon forms two oxides carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide.

Carbon monoxide (CO)

Preparation

  1. Carbon monoxide is prepared by incomplete combustion of carbon or carbon containing compounds in a limited supply of oxygen.
    \( 2 C + O_2 \rightarrow 2 CO \)
    It is also present in exhaust gases from automobiles due to incomplete combustion of carbon or carbon compounds during burning of petrol or diesel.

  2. Pure carbon monoxide in the laboratory is prepared by heating formic acid (HCOOH) with \( H_2SO_4 \) at about 373 to 413 K. \( H_2SO_4 \) is a dehydrating agent.
    \( HCOOH \xrightarrow[-H_2O]{H_2SO_4} CO \)

physical Properties

  1. It is colourless, odourless and sparingly soluble in water.

  2. Toxic Nature It is highly toxic in nature. CO combines with haemoglobin in human red blood cells and forms carboxy-haemoglobin by a reversible reaction. This destroys the oxygen carrying capability of the blood causing suffocation and even death in some cases

Chemical Properties

  1. Combustibility
    Carbon monoxide burns in air with a pale blue flame forming carbon dioxide. aiV
    \( 2 CO + O_2 \rightarrow 2 CO_2 + heat \)

  2. Reducing properties
    It is a powerful reducing agent and reduces many metal oxides to metals.
    \( \begin{align} ZnO + CO \rightarrow Zn + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] CuO + CO \rightarrow Cu + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] 2 Fe_2 O_3 + 3 CO \rightarrow 4 Fe + 3 CO_2 \end{align} \)

  3. Formation of Metal carbonyls
    It combines with many transition metals such as nickel, iron, cobalt, etc., forming a. variety of compounds known as metal carbonyls.
    \( \begin{align} Ni + 4CO \xrightarrow{60 - 70^{\circ} C} Ni(CO)_4 (Nickel \ carbonyl) \\\\[3pt] Fe + 5CO \rightarrow Fe(CO)_5 (Iron \ carbonyl) \end{align} \)

  4. Manufacture of producer gas
    Producer gas is a mixture of two parts of nitrogen and one part of carbon monoxide by volume. It is produced by passing controlled amount of air through red hot coke. It is used as a fuel.

  5. Water gas
    Water gas is a mixture of equal volume of CO and hydrogen. It is prepared by passing super heated steam through white hot coke.
    \( \underset{(Red \ hot \ coke)} {C} + \underset{\text{(Superheated steam)}}{H_2O} \rightarrow \underset{\text{(water gas)}}{CO + H_2} \)

Air Pollution by Carbon monoxide

Carbon monoxide is one of the pollutants of air.

  1. When coal or wood is burnt in less supply of air, carbon monoxide is formed and pollutes the air.

  2. With increasing number of vehicles, the exhaust gases coming out of the engine with CO, are some of the major pollutants of air.

  3. When natural wastes and dry leaves are burnt, incomplete combustion takes place and CO is released into air.

  4. Smoking produces CO which pollutes air.

Harmful Effects of Carbon Monoxide

Carbon monoxide is one of the pollutants of air.

  1. Causes severe headache and nausea

  2. Excess amount of CO can make a person unconscious within minutes and cause death due to carbon monoxide poisoning.

Carbon dioxide \( (CO_2) \)

Occurrence

About 0.03 % by volume of air contains carbon dioxide. It occurs in nature both in free and combined state. In air it is present due to the oxidation of organic matter during combustion and decay. Plants use carbon dioxide during photosynthesis. These molecules are the basic raw materials for living organisms. During respiration, organisms give carbon dioxide back to air. Thus the optimum balance of carbon dioxide is maintained. It is estimated that about 40 litres of \( (CO_2) \) is exhaled by a human being per day. In the combined state, \( (CO_2) \) , occurs as carbonates, for example, limestone \( (CaCO_3) \) , dolomite \( (CaCO_3.MgCO_3) \) and magnesite \( (MgCO_3) \) .

Preparation

Carbon dioxide can be prepared by the following methods

  1. Carbon dioxide can be prepared by the following methods
    \( \begin{align} carbon + Oxygen \rightarrow Carbondioxide \\\\[3pt] C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 \\\\[3pt] Methane + Oxygen \rightarrow carbon \ dioxide + Water \\\\[3pt] CH_4 + 2 O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2 H_2O \end{align} \)

  2. By the action of heat on certain metallic carbonates and bicarbonates
    \( \begin{align} Calcium \ carbonate \rightarrow Calcium \ oxide + Carbon \ dioxide \\\\[3pt] CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] Sodium \ bicarbonate + Hydrochloric \ acid \rightarrow Calcium \ chloride + Water + Carbondioxide \\\\[3pt] 2 Na HCO_3 \rightarrow Na_2 CO_3 + H_2O + CO_2 \end{align} \)

  3. By the action of dilute acids on carbonates and bicarbonates
    \( \begin{align} Calcium \ carbonate + Hydrochloric \ acid \rightarrow Calcium \ chloride + water + Carbon \ dioxide \\\\[3pt] CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2 O + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] Sodium \ bicarbonate + Hydrochloric \ acid \rightarrow Sodium \ chloride + Water + Carbondioxide \\\\[3pt] 2 Na HCO_3 + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O + CO_2 \end{align} \)

Physical properties of carbon dioxide

It is a colourless, odourless and tasteless gas.

Solubility of carbon dioxide in water

At lower temperature, the solubility of carbon dioxide is large. At \( 0^{\circ}C \) and 1 atm pressure, 180 ml of carbon dioxide can be dissolved in 100 ml of water.
The solubility of carbon dioxide in water decreases with increase of temperature.
The solubility of carbon dioxide in water increases with increase of pressure. Aerated soft drinks contain carbondioxide dissolved in water under pressure. When the cap of a bottle of a cold drink is removed, \( CO_2 \) rushes out because of the release of pressure.
Carbon dioxide dissolved in water is used by marine plants for photosynthesis.

Density of carbon dioxide

Carbon dioxide is 1.5 times as dense as air. It can be poured from one container to another container.

Chemical properties of carbon dioxide

Non-combustible and non-supporter of combustion

Carbon dioxide is very stable and inert. It is neither combustile nor a supporter of combustion. However, some active metals like magnesium continue to burn in it.
Introduce burning magnesium into a jar of carbon dioxide. It continues to burn forming white ash.
\( 2 Mg + CO_2 \rightarrow 2MgO + C \)

Reduction

Carbon dioxide is reduced to carbon monoxide when passed over red hot coke.
\( C + CO_2 \rightarrow 2CO \)

Acidic nature

  1. Carbon dioxide dissolves in water forming carbonic acid.
    \( CO_2 + H_2 O \rightarrow \underset{(Carbonic \ acid)}{H_2CO_3} \)
    Introduce a piece of moist blue and red litmus into a jar of carbondioxide. Blue litmus turns red due to the formation of carbonic acid.

  2. \( CO_2 \) neutralises alkalis to give two salts - normal salts (carbonates) and acid salts (bicarbonates).
    \( \begin{align} 2 NaOH + CO_2 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 + H_2 O \\\\[3pt] Na_2 CO_3 + H_2 O + CO_2 \rightarrow 2 Na HCO_3 \end{align} \)

Action with lime water

Bubble carbon dioxide through lime water \( [Ca(OH)_2] \) . It turns milky or turbid. Continue to pass carbon dioxide. Turbity disappears. It turns milky due to the formation of insoluble calcium carbonate.
\( \underset{(Milky)}{Ca(OH)_2} + CO_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + H_2O \)
When more carbon dioxide is bubbled through it, the milkiness disappears due to the formation of water - soluble calcium bicarbonate.
\( \underset{(Milky)}{CaCO_3} + H_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow \underset{(Water \ soluble)}{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \)
If the calcium bicarbonate solution is heated, the milkiness reappears because calcium bicarbonate decomposes to form calcium carbonate.
\( Ca(HCO_3)_2 \xrightarrow{heat} CaCO_3 + H_2O + CO_2 \)

Action with basic oxides

Being an acidic oxide, carbon dioxide reacts with basic oxides like sodium oxide and calcium oxide to form salts.
\( Na_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 \)

photosynthesis

Carbon dioxide present in the air reacts with water in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll, the green colouring matter in plants. It is converted into glucose, starch and cellulose. This process is known as photosynthesis.
\( 6 CO_2 + 6H_2 O \xrightarrow[sunlight]{Chlorophyll} C_6H_{12}{O_6} + 6CO_2 \)
This process purifies air by removing carbon dioxide and giving out oxygen or it can be said that it is the reverse process of respiration where oxygen is taken in and carbon dioxide is given out.

  5. It is used in preserving fruits.

Uses

  1. It extinguishes the burning splinter or candle.

  2. It turns lime water milky.

Preparation of aerated water

Aerated water is prepared by dissolving carbon dioxide gas in water under pressure. This mixture of carbon dioxide and water has a pleasant taste.
Various types of effervescent drinks, are obtained when sugar, flavour and colours are added to soda water. When pressure is released by opening the bottle, carbon dioxide is released.

Dry ice

When carbon dioxide is subjected to a pressure of 60 atmospheres and cooled, it is liquifies to a colourless liquid. The colourless liquid \( CO_2 \) , on cooling further freezes like white snow. This is called dry ice.

Uses

  1. Dry ice is a very good refrigerant and easy to handle. It is used to preserve meat, fish and butter.

  2. It is also used in creating exceedingly low temperature required for research work laboratories.

  3. It is used as a local anaesthetic in surgical operations.

  4. Dry ice is used to produce artificial rain.

Advantages of using dry ice as a refrigerant

  1. It freezes food faster.

  2. It is not poisonous.

  3. It can provide a temperature as low as \( 75^{ \circ} C \) . Hence bacterial growth is controlled.

Carbon cycle

\( CO_2 \) is produced and added into the atmosphere by :

  1. Burning of fossil fuels (by combustion).

  2. Respiration of plants and animals.

  3. Decay of dead animals, plants or burning of dead plants.

A large proportion of this carbon dioxide is reabsorbed by the plants during the process of photosynthesis when carbohydrates are formed with the release of oxygen.
Rest of \( CO_2 \) is absorbed by sea water to form carbonates. Because of this cycle the concentration of \( CO_2 \) in air remains constant.
Note:
On combustion of fuels, many pollutants are given out. The minimum temperature a which a fuel catches fire is called ignition temperature.

Solved examples

Example 1:

What is lead in pencil made of ?

Answer:
Lead in pencil is made of graphite and clays but not lead metal. Pencils which are soft have graphite in high quantities and clay in low quantities where hard pencils have low graphite and high clay quantities.

Example 2:

Why is diamond so hard?

Answer:
The carbon atom has tetravalency. Hence each carbon is bounded to four neighbouring atoms in tetrahedron fashion. These units are repeated forming a giant three dimensional structure making it so hard.

Example 3:

What is dry ice? Write its uses.

Answer:
When carbon dioxide gas is subjected to a pressure of 60 atm and cooled, it gets liquified. It is a colourless liquid, which further cooling freezes like snow. This is called dry ice.

Example 4:

What products are obtained when a fuel is burnt or in oxygen?

Answer:
On combustion, the fuel is oxidised to form carbondioxide and water. Combustion is burning of fuel in the presence of oxygen with evolution of heat and light.

Example 5:

Increase in 1% of CO carbon monoxide in air leads to death. Explain.

Answer:
CO is extremely poisonous gas. it has high affinity towards oxygen. It reacts with haemoglobin which carries oxygen in the blood, and forms carboxy haemoglobin. This prevents haemoglobin from carrying oxygen, hence tissues i.e., blood cells do not get oxygen and death occurs.

Example 6:

Diamond is used as precious stone. Explain.

Answer:
Diamond has a very high refractive index and possesses the ability of reflection .and refraction. This refraction of colours of light, are not equal, when light is passed glues brilliant rays of colours which make diamond a valuable jem.
