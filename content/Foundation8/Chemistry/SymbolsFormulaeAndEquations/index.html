Common misconception Fact
1 CO is the symbol of cobalt In case of a symbol the second letter used in symbol is a lower case letter. In case of a formula where there is a combination of two elements, the two letters are written as upper case. For example CO stands for formula of carbon monoxide.
2 A balanced chemical equation tells about the physical conditions which bring about the chemical reaction. The physical conditions of a chemical reaction are heat energy, light energy, pressure, catalyst etc., These conditions are not told by a balanced chemical equation but however can be written on the reaction.

Introduction

Around 118 elements were discovered so far. Most of these elements have been found to occur in nature. Some of the elements are man made. It is possible that scientists will continue to discover some more elements. The scientists who suggested a method of representing elements using the English letters (Capitals as well as small) is J.J. Berzelius.
A symbol is defined as an abbreviation or short hand sign for the full name of an element.
Most of these elements take part in a variety of reactions giving rise to a large number of new substances. Writing their names using the names of elements is cumbersome. Hence there is a need to write the names of elements and compounds in a short form. Chemical symbols and formulae are introduced to represent the elements and compounds respectively. Chemical changes can be conveniently written using chemical symbols and formulae. Written in this manner, they are called chemical equations.
The method of naming the substance is called chemical nomenclature. The representation of a substance with the help of symbol is called chemical notation.

Symbols and formulae

Significance of a symbol

A symbol of an element has a qualitative and quantitative significance.
Qualitative significance
Qualitatively, it represents the name of the element.
Example
For example O stands for oxygen, N stands for nitrogen.
Qualitative significance
Quantitatively it represents,

  1. One atom of an element.

  2. The number of parts by weight (Atomic weight) of an element.

  3. One gram atom of an element.

Example
The symbol O represents

  1. One atom of oxygen.

  2. 16 parts by weight of oxygen.

  3. One gram atom of oxygen i.e., 16 grams

Chemical formula

The representation of a molecule of a substance (element or compound) interms of symbols and subscript numbers is known as a formula.
The symbol of an element represents one individual atom of the element. Some of the elements no doubt exist independently.
Example
He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Fe, Hg, Co etc.
However many elements occur in combination with one or more atoms of its own kind or with one or more atoms of other elements as molecules.
According to the molecular concept of matter a molecule is the smallest unit of matter capable of independent existence. Molecules containing 1, 2 or 3 atoms are called monatomic, diatomic or triatomic molecules respectively. If they contain more than 3 atoms they may be described as polyatomic. Thus the representation of a molecule of an element or a compound in terms of symbols and figures is defined as chemical formula.
Example
\( H_2, \ H_2O, \ SO_2, \ NH_3, \ O_3, \ H_2SO_4 \) .
\( H_2 \) stands for a molecule of hydrogen consisting of two hydrogen atoms. \( H_2O \) stands for a molecule of water consisting of 2 atoms of hydrogen and one atom of oxygen. Thus the number of atoms of each present in the molecule is indicated by the number on its right hand corner as subscript.
Thus \( H_2SO_4 \) stands for one molecule of sulphuric acid which contains 2 atoms of hydrogen, one atom of sulphur and 4 atoms of oxygen.

Significance of a formula

Like the symbols, a formula has also qualitative as well as quantitative significance.

Qualitative significance

Qualitatively, it represents the name of the substance and the names of various elements present in the substance.

Qualitative significance

Quantitatively, it represents one molecule of the substance, the actual number of atoms of each element present in one molecule of the substance, the number of parts by weight of the substance (molecular weight) and the number of parts by weight of each element.

Valency (or) combining capacity of elements

Old concept

Valency of an element may be defined as the number of hydrogen atoms or the number of chlorine atoms or double the number of oxygen atoms with which one atom of that element combines.
Example

  1. One atom of nitrogen combines with three atoms of hydrogen to form \( NH_3 \) . Thus, the valency of nitrogen is 3.

  2. \( AlCl_3 \) is formed when one atom of aluminium combines with three atoms of chlorine. Thus,the valency of Al is 3.

  3. ZnO is formed when one atom of Zn combines with one atom of oxygen. Thus, double the number of oxygen. Thus, double the number of oxygen atoms is the valency of Zn. The valency of zinc is 2.

Modern concept

According to the modern concept, the valency may be defined as the number of electrons lost, gained or shared with one atom of the element in order to acquire the stable configuration of the nearest inert gas element

Radicals or ions

An atom or a group of atoms can exist independently with charge(s). These are formed by loss or gain of electron(s). They are called radicals or more commonly as ions. Radicals formed by the loss of electron or electrons, such radicals are described as electrons positive and they are called basic radicals or cations.
\( \begin{align} \underset{sodium \ atom}{Na} \xrightarrow{on \ loosing \ an \ electron} \ \underset{sodium \ ion}{Na^+} \\\\[3pt] \underset{magnesium \ atom}{Na} \xrightarrow{on \ loosing \ 2 \ electrons} \ \underset{magnesium \ ion}{Mg^{++} \ or \ Mg^{2+}} \end{align} \)
Radicals can also be formed by gain of electrons. These are described as electro negative and are called acidic radicals or anions.
\( \begin{align} \underset{Chlorine \ atom}{Cl} \xrightarrow{gaining \ an \ electron} \ \underset{Chloride \ radical \ or \ ion}{Cl^-} \\\\[3pt] \underset{Oxygen \ atom}{O} \xrightarrow{gaining \ 2 \ electron} \ \underset{Oxide \ radical \ or \ ion}{O^{--} \ or \ O^{2-}} \end{align} \)
Example
A group of atoms of different elements bearing positive or negative charge(s) are
\( S{O_4}^{2-}, \ N{H_4}^{+}, \ C{O_3}^{2-}, \ N{O_3}^{3-}, \ P{O_4}^{3-}, \ H_3 {O^+}, \ N{O_3}^- \)
The number of charges indicate the number of electrons lost or gained by the atom or group of atoms. Depending on the number of charges viz., 1, 2, 3 or 4 the ions or radicals are described as monovalent, divalent, trivalent respectively.

Writing the chemical formula from the knowledge of valency

One of the most important point to remember while writing the formula of a chemical compound is that, the compound is always electrically neutral. In other words, the positive and negative valencies of the ions or radicals present in the chemical compound add up to zero. To write a formula follow the steps given below. This method of writing formula is called criss-cross method.
Step I
Write the symbol of the positive ion or the radical to the left and the negative ion or the radical to the right.
Step II
Put the valency number of each of the radical or ion on its top right. Divide the valency number by the highest common factor, if any, to get simple ratio. Now ignore the (+) and (-) symbols. Interchange the valency numbers of the radicals.
Step III
Shift the valency number to the lower right of the ion or the radical. If radical receives a number more than 1, enclose it within brackets. Do not enclose single atom within brackets.
Example
Step I
Write the symbols of the combining units side by side
Cation on the L.H.S. and the anion on R.H.S
\( AlSO_4 \)
Step II
Enclose the compound ion, in a bracket, eg. in case of \( AlSO_4, \ SO_4 \ as \ (SO_4) \)
Step III
Write the numerical value of the charge on top
\( Al^3(SO_4)^2 \)
Step IV
If the charges of the two ions are divisible by a common factor, then reduce the numerals.
Step V
Finally criss-cross the numerical values of their charges to obtain the formula.

Chemical equations

The chemical equation is a statement that describes a chemical change in terms of symbols and formule.
The term "equation" is used, because in a chemical reaction the atoms are neither created nor destroyed. Thus, the number of atoms of each element on the right hand side of an equation must be equal to number of atoms of each element on the left hand side of an equation.
Before we start interpreting the meaning of a chemical equation, let us try to know few terms used in writing a chemical equation.

  1. Reactants
    The substance/substances which take part in a chemical reaction are called reactants. The reactants are always written on the left hand side in a chemical equation.
    Products
    The substances formed as a result of chemical change during a chemical reaction are called products.
    The products are always written on the right hand side in a chemical

  2. A chemical equation consists of formulae of reactants connected by plus sign (+) and an arrow ( \( \rightarrow \) ) followed by the formulae of products, connected by plus sign (+).

  3. The sign of an arrow ( \( \rightarrow \) ) is read "to yield" or "to form". It also shows the direction in which reaction is predominant.

Pre-knowledge Before Writing a Balanced Chemical Equation

Following knowledge is necessary before one starts writing a balanced chemical equation:

  1. Whether or not a chemical reaction takes place between two chemical substances.

  2. One must know all the reactants and the products formed during a chemical change.

  3. One must know the correct symbols and formulae of all the reactants and products.

A chemical equation in which number of atoms of each element is same on the side of reactants and products is called balanced equation.

Rules of balancing a chemical equation

  1. Write the frequency numbers of all the elements in a given chemical equation.

  2. Start balancing equation from that element which has least frequency number.

  3. Other elements should be balanced in the order of increasing frequency numbers.

  4. If two or more elements have same frequency number, then balance the metallic element first.

  5. If there are two or more metallic elements with same frequency number, first balance the metal with highest atomic number. Then balance the next metallic elements with lower atomic number and so on.

  6. If there are two or more non-metallic elements, with same frequency number, first balance the non-metallic element with highest atomic number. Other non-metallic element should be balanced in the decreasing order of atomic numbers.

Note
To balance an equation is its f-number.
However, if f-numbers are same, then first metallic elements should be balanced in the order of decreasing atomic number, followed by non-metals in the order of decreasing atomic numbers.

Condition

The element which is being balanced must be present in equation in its elementary form.
Example
\( Pb(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow PbO + 2NO_2 + O_2 \)
In the above equation the element oxygen is present in the form of compound in \( Pb(NO_3)_2, \ PbO \ and \ NO_2 \) . However, it exists in elementary form in \( O_2 \)

.

Rule

If there is a difference of one atom in reactants and products or the number of atoms in the reactants and the products are even or odd numbers, and that particular element exists in pure elementary state in the equation, then without disturbing the atom in elementary form, multiply the whole equation by 2 (do not multiply element atom in elementary form by 2).
Thus, multiplying with 2, the equation is written as
\( 2Pb(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2PbO + 4NO_2 + O_2 \)
In the above, the number of oxygen atoms towards the reactants side are 12. Similarly, number of atoms towards the products side are 12. Thus, the above equation is fully balanced.

Limitations a balanced chemical equation

  1. It does not give information about the physical state of reactants and products. For example, the equation given below does not tell whether the substances involved in chemical reaction are in solids, liquids or gaseous state.
    \( CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2 O + CO_2 \)
    This difficulty is overcome by putting symbols like(s) for solids, (l) for liquids and (g) for gases. For chemicals, which react in solution form, a symbol (aq) is used. Following examples will illustrate the point :

    1. \( CaCO_3(s) + 2HCl(aq) \rightarrow CaCl_2(aq) + H_2 O(l) + CO_2(g) \)

    2. \( AgNO_2(aq) + NaCl(aq) \rightarrow AgCl(s) + NaNO_3(aq) \)

    3. \( 2Pb(NO_3)_2(s) \rightarrow 2PbO(s) + 4NO_2(s) + O_2(g) \)

  2. A balanced chemical equation does not tell whether a chemical reaction will come to completion or not.

  3. A balanced chemical equation does not tell anything about the speed of a chemical reaction.
    For example, the reaction between silver nitrate solution and sodium chloride solution completes in few seconds. However, decomposition of lead nitrate crystals takes place in few minutes.

  4. A balanced chemical equation does not tell about the physical conditions which bring about the chemical reaction, e.g., it does not tell whether heat energy, light energy, pressure, catalyst, etc., are required for a chemical reaction or not.
    The problem is partly solved by writing the conditions of reaction on the arrow head as illustrated by a balanced equation.
    \( N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \xrightarrow[900 atm - 450^{\circ} c]{Fe - Mo} 2NH_3(g) \)
    From the above equation we can say that 1 volume of nitrogen gas, reacts with 3 volumes of hydrogen gas, under a pressure of 900 atmospheres, at a temperature \( 450^{\circ} c \) , in presence of catalyst iron containing molybdenum, when a reversible reaction takes place, with the formation of 2 volumes of ammonia gas.

  5. A balanced chemical equation does not tell about changes such as precipitation, change in colour, evolution of heat, light and sound energy during the chemical change

Example 6:

What is the valency of Cu in CuO and \( Cu_2O \) ?

Solution:
Copper atom combines with one oxygen atom, to give CuO and hence, the valency of copper in CuO is 2.
Let us consider \( Cu_2O \) . Two Cu atoms combine with one 'O' atom.
\( \therefore \) One Cu atom combines with \( \frac{1}{2} \) 'O' atoms.
The valency of an atom or a radical, is also defined as double the numbers of oxygen atoms, with which, it combines.
\( \therefore \) The valency of Cu is twice the number of oxygen atoms, with which, one Cu atom
combines = \( 2 \frac{1}{2} \) = 1
\( \therefore \) Valency of Cu in \( Cu_2 O \) is one.

Example 7:

Write the equation for preparation of oxygen, by heating potassium chlorate.

Solution:
Step 1:
Write the equation in words
Potassium Chlorate \( \xrightarrow{on \ heating} \) Potassium Chloride + Oxygen
Step 2:
Write the unbalanced equation, using symbols and formulae.
\( KClO_3 \xrightarrow{\triangle} \ KC1 + O_2 (unbalanced) \)
Step 3:
Balance the chemical equation, by determining the coefficients, that provide equal number of each type of atoms, on each side of the equation.
\( 2KClO_3 \rightarrow 2KC1 + 3O_2 \) (balanced equation)
Step 4:
Indicate the physical state of each chemical, by using the symbols (g),(l), and (s) (for gas), liquid and solid respectively).
\( 2KClO_3(s) \rightarrow 2KC1(s) + 3O_2 (g) \)

3 in front of \( Cu(NO_3)_2 \) ;
2 in front of NO to balance the number of N atoms
\( Cu + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 + 2NO + H_2O \)
write 4 in front of H2O to balance the number of 0 and H atoms

write 3 in front of Cu to balance the number of Cu atoms

Write physical states of each chemical
\( 3Cu(s) + 8HNO_3(aq) \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 (aq) + 2NO(g) + 4H_2O(1) \)
