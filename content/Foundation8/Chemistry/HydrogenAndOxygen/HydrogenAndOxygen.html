1 Hydrogen can be used as a fuel as it is very lighter and has got high calorific value. The hydrogen gas does n't burn in small quantities, but it explodes at a time.
2 We can transport hydrogen very easily as it is a gas. The gas cannot be liquefied easily and has to be transported under pressure in steel cylinders having almost negligible mass of gas.
3 Oxygen is one of constituents of water. Hence is readily soluble in water. Oxygen is sparingly soluble in water. If it is higly soluble the aquatic life cannot survive because of high reactivity.

Introduction

Hydrogen was the first element to be formed, after the universe was formed millions of years ago. It is the lightest and simplest atom known to man. It is a colourless, odourless and tasteless gas that weighs about one-fourteenth as much as an equal volume of air. Hydrogen is the element that makes the sun shine. A huge amount of energy is given out when the nuclei of atoms of hydrogen join up or fuse together in the Sun's interior.
It may be possible for a person to live without water, food, shelter etc., for a few hours or days but no living being can survive without taking oxygen. The whole life on earth is revolving around oxygen. Knowingly or unknowingly, every living organism on this earth owes its life to oxygen.
Oxygen is essential for life and it takes part in processes of combustion, its biological functions in respiration make it important. Oxygen is sparingly soluble in water and in this dissolved form, oxygen is available to aquatic beings and thus plays an important role in their survival. It is the most abundant element in earth's crust and constitutes about one-fifth of the atmosphere.

Dual Nature of hydrogen

Hydrogen has the simplest electronic configuration of 'one' and hence it:

  1. Either loses one electron, behaving like electropositive alkali metals.
    Example
    \( \begin{align} H + e^{\ominus} \rightarrow H^{\ominus} \\\\[3pt] F + e^{\ominus} \rightarrow F^{\ominus} \\\\[3pt] Cl + 1 e^{\ominus} \rightarrow Cl^{\ominus} \end{align} \)

  2. Gains one electron, behaving like electronegative halogens.
    Example
    \( H - 1 e^- \rightarrow H^-, \ F - 1 e^- \rightarrow F^- , \ Cl - 1 e^- \rightarrow Cl^- \)

Discovery of hydrogen

Robert Boyle prepared hydrogen in 1672, by treating iron with sulphuric acid, but was unable to establish its elementary character.
The credit of discovery of hydrogen goes to Henry Cavendish, who prepared it by the action of dilute hydrochloric acid, with zinc in 1766. He not only established its elemen- tary character, but also proved that, when hydrogen is burnt in air it forms water.
Because of its property of water formation, it was named hydrogen by Lavoisier in 1783, as hydrogen means water former in Greek language.

Occurance of hydrogen

Hydrogen occurs in free as well as in combined state:

Free state

  1. Hydrogen does not occur in the free state on planet Earth.

  2. It is however, found in minute traces in the earth's crust and the earth's atmosphere, in ,volcanic gases and to a higher extent around the sun and the stars.

Combined state

  1. In plant and animal tissues - which are made of compounds of hydrogen along with carbon, oxygen and nitrogen.

  2. In water - about one ninth by mass of water is Hydrogen.

  3. As a constituent of - acids, alkalis, petroleum products and organic substances.
    Example : Proteins.

Chemical properties of hydrogen

Combustibility

Hydrogen is a combustible gas, but it does not support combustion. In air or oxygen, it burns silently, with a very pale blue flame. However, if hydrogen is premixed with air or oxygen, it explodes with a pop sound, because of spontaneous combustion.

Action with litmus

Hydrogen is neutral towards litmus solution.

Action with non-metal

oxygen

Hydrogen burns silently in oxygen with a pale blue flame.
\( 2H_2 O_2 \rightarrow 2HCl \)

Chlorine

Equal volumes of \( H_2 \) and \( C1_2 \) react slowly in diffused sunlight and explosively in direct sunlight .
\( H_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2HCl \)

Nitrogen

Nitrogen reacts with hydrogen to form ammonia.
\( N_2 + 3H_2 \ \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3 \) ;Temp - 450 - \( 500^{\circ}C \) , pressure: 200 - 900 atm, catalyst - finely divided iron and promoter is molybdenum.

Sulphur

\( H_2 + S \rightarrow H_2 S \)

Action with metals

Most of the metals do not react with hydrogen. However, when dry hydrogen gas is passed over heated Na, K or Ca, they react to form their respective hydrides.
\( \begin{align} 2 K + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2KH \\\\[3pt] 2Na + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2NaH \\\\[3pt] Ca + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} CaH_2 \end{align} \)
The hydrides further react with cold water forming hydrogen.
\( CaH_2 + 2 H_2 O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + 2 H_2 \)

Reducing properties of hydrogen

When hydrogen gas is passed over heated oxides of the less active metals such as zinc, iron, lead and copper, it reduces them to free metals and forms water.
\( \begin{align} Metal \ oxide + Hydrogen \xrightarrow{\Delta} Free \ metal + Water \\\\[3pt] ZnO + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Zn + H_2O \\\\[3pt] Fe_2O_3 + 3H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2Fe + 3 H_2O \\\\[3pt] PbO + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Pb + H_2O \\\\[3pt] CuO + H_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Cu + H_2O \end{align} \)
The hydrides further react with cold water forming hydrogen.
Note
In the above reactions, the hydrogen is a reducing agent and hence, is oxidized to water, In the above reactions, the metallic oxides are oxidizing agents and hence, are reduced to free metals.
Hydrogenation - involves addition of hydrogen to organic compounds: Addition of hydrogen to organic compounds in the presence of a catalysts like platinum or nickel is called hydrogenation
\( H_2C = CH_2 + H_2 \rightarrow C_2H_6 \)

Uses of hydrogen

As a fuel

It is present in coal gas ( 45 %) and water gas ( 50%) by volume. It produces a large ; amount of energy with the formation of water only and no other gases. It is an excellent non - polluting fuel.

For cutting and welding purposes

The flame of hydrogen and oxygen can produce a temperature of \( 2800^{\circ}C \) . This flame is used for cutting and welding metals.

For meteorological purposes

To study weather conditions.

For the manufacture of industrial chemicals

Used extensively in the manufacture of \( NH_3 \) and HCl.

In hydrogenation reactions

Used in hydrogenation of oil [ghee preparation] and hydrogenation of coal.

In extraction of metals

Hydrogen acts as a reducing agent and reduces metallic oxides to metals.

For making artificial petroleum

The artificial petroleum can be fractionally distilled so as to obtain various useful fractions like petrol, kerosene oil, diesel, etc.

Laboratory Methods

From Hydrogen peroxide \( (H_2O_2) \) :

Principle
Decomposition of \( H_2O_2 \) in the presence of manganese dioxide as catalyst evolves oxygen gas.

Procedure

  1. A clean conical flask is taken and about 10 g of manganese dioxide is placed in it.

  2. The conical flask is closed with a two holed rubber stopper. Through one hole, a tap funnel and through another hole, a delivery tube are inserted. The other end of the delivery tube is connected to beehive shelf, placed in a trough of water.

  3. Hydrogen peroxide is poured through the thistle funnel.

  4. Hydrogen peroxide is allowed to trickle drop by drop, on manganese dioxide.

  5. It rapidly decomposes to form water and oxygen gases. The equation involved in the process is as follows:
    \( \underset{(Hydrogen \ peroxide)}{(2H_2O_2)} \xrightarrow{MnO_2} \underset{(Water)} {2H_2O} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \downarrow \)

Precaution
Decomposition of \( H_2O_2 \) results in brisk effervescence due to the oxygen evolved. By regulating the flow of hydrogen peroxide, using the tap attached to the thistle funnel the flow of oxygen may be controlled.

From Potassium chlorate \( (KClO_3) \) :

Principle
Decomposition of potassium chlorate on heating evolves oxygen gas.

Procedure

  1. Four parts of potassium chlorate \( (KClO_3) \) and one part of manganese dioxide \( (MnO_2) \) are taken in a hard glass test tube. This mixture is called oxygen mixture.

  2. When the test tube is heated strongly, chemical decomposition takes place, which yields potassium chloride and oxygen gas. The reaction involved in the process is as follows:
    \( \underset{(potassium \ chlorate)}{2 KClO_3(s)} \xrightarrow[Catalyst]{MnO_2} \underset{(Potassium \ chloride)} {2KCl} + \underset{Oxygen}{3 O_2} \)

Note
Manganese dioxide acts as a catalyst without taking part in the reaction.

From Potassium permanganate \( (KMnO_4) \) :

Principle
Potassium permanganate on heating evolves oxygen gas.
Procedure
This method employs the same setup as above. Potassium permanganate crystals are taken in the hard glass test tube and heated. They decompose to form potassium manganate, manganese dioxide and oxygen. The reaction involved is as follows:
\( \underset{(Potassium \ permanganate)}{2 KMnO_4} \rightarrow \underset{(Potassium \ manganate)} {K_2 MnO_4} + \underset{(Potassium \ dioxide)}{MnO_2} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \)
Precaution

  1. \( MnO_2 \) should be free of charcoal in order to prevent explosion which may cause heating it with \( KClO_3 \) .

  2. The delivery tube must be removed carefully from the trough before the heating stops, or the water will enter the tube on cooling due to reduced pressure.

Collection of oxygen

In laboratory methods, oxygen is collected by downward displacement of water but not air, since it is almost as heavy as air and is very slightly soluble in water. The oxygen gas is then passed through the drying agents such as fused calcium chloride \( CaCl_2 \) or concentrated sulphuric acid \( (H_2SO_4) \) and is collected over mercury.
Note
The oxygen so obtained has a fishy smell as it contains some impurities.

industrial method

From liquid air:

Principle (Joule-Thompson effect)
When a gas, under pressure is allowed to expand suddenly through a small orifice, into a region of low pressure, its temperature falls and converts into liquid. This, on distillation, gives oxygen.
Procedure

Step - I: Removal of dust particles and carbon dioxide
Carbon dioxide present in air is removed by passing it through caustic potash (KOH) and the dust particles are removed by passing through an electric precipitator.
Step - II: Liquefaction of air
The purified air is compressed to 200 atm pressure and is cooled by passing through cooling chambers and finally the gas is allowed to escape through a small aperture (opening). The process is repeated. It results in the temperature of the air being continuously lowered, until the air becomes a liquid, at - \( 200^{\circ}C \) .

Step - III: Fractional distillation (evaporation) of liquid air:

  1. Liquid air thus obtained is mainly a mixture of liquid nitrogen and liquid oxygen.

  2. The liquid air is then led to a fractionating column, where it is subjected to fractional evaporation.

  3. On distillation, nitrogen with a lower b.p. \( (-196^{\circ}C) \) is evolved first, leaving behind liquid oxygen, which further on evaporation, turns into gaseous oxygen.

  4. The oxygen gas is then dried, compressed and stored in steel cylinders under pressure.

By the Electrolysis of water

Principle (Joule-Thompson effect)
Acidified water, when subjected to electrolysis, hemical decomposition takes place, evolving \( O_2 \) .
Procedure

The electrolysis cell consists of two electrodes (usually an inert metal such as platinum) submerged in acidified water and connected to opposite poles of a source of direct current.

The electric current dissociates water molecule into hydroxide \( (OH^-) \) , and hydrogen \( (H^+) \) ions.
\( \underset{(Acidified \ water)}{2H_2O} \xrightarrow[current]{Electric} \underset{(Hydrogen)} 2 H_2 \uparrow + \underset{(Oxygen)}{O_2} \uparrow \)
Hydrogen gas accumulates in the limb which is connected to the negative terminal and oxygen gas accumulates in the limb which is connected to the positive terminal.
Note
Pure water does not conduct electricity because of its low ionization ability. However, it becomes a good conductor when dilute sulphuric acid is added to it. This water is called acidified water or acidulated water.

Physical Properties

Nature

Oxygen is colourless, odourless and tasteless gas.

Density

Oxygen gas is heavier than air. It is for this reason that there is more oxygen near the earth and the amount of oxygen decreases as we go higher.

Solubility

Oxygen is sparingly soluble in water and its solubility decreases with increase of temperature

Chemical Properties

Combustibility: Oxygen is non-combustible (i.e., itself does not burn) but supports combustion (helps other substances to burn in it).
To confirm this, introduce a smoking wooden splinter in a jar full of oxygen. You will see that the splinter bursts into a flame. This proves that oxygen supports combustion aud helps in burning.

Controlling burning due to oxygen

IThe percentage of oxygen in atmosphere is about 21%. With such a high concentration of oxygen, the burning process should be very rapid and it should be difficult to control fires. But the greater percentage of inert nitrogen (78%) dilutes the effect of oxygen, preventing the burning process.

Nature

oxygen has no effect on either moist blue litmus or moist red litmus. Hence, it is neither acidic nor basic. It is neutral gas.

Reactivity

It is chemically a very reactive element.

Oxygen reaction

Oxygen reacts with all substances and oxidises them to their respective oxides. This phenomenon is known as oxidation.

Solved examples

Example 1:

How can we say that hydrogen is prepared by displacement reaction?
Answer:
Metals like sodium (Na), potassium (K), Calcium (Ca) and magnesium (Mg) displace hydrogen from compounds like dilute acids or water and liberate hydrogen gas.
Eg: \( \underset{Sodium}{2Na} + \underset{Sulphuric \ acid}{H_2SO_4} \rightarrow \underset{Sodium \ Sulphate}{Na_2SO_4} + \underset{hydrogen}{H_2} \)

Example 2:

What is oxy - hydrogen flame ?
Answer:
Hydrogen gas on combustion with oxygen produces a blue colour flame with a very high temperatures of above \( 2800^{\circ} C \) is called oxy hydrogen flame. It is used for welding, cutting etc.,

Example 3:

'Cu' copper, cannot liberate hydrogen from dil. \( H_2SO_4 \) . Explain.
Answer:
A displacement reaction is that in which a more active element displaces a less active element from a compound.
Elements like copper, silver, gold etc., are less active than \( H_2 \) . Hence they cannot displace \( H_2 \) from dil. \( H_2SO_4 \) .

Example 4:

What happens in the reaction of hydrogen with oxygen?
Answer:
\( \underset{hydrogen}{2 H_2} + \underset{oxygen}{O_2} \rightarrow \underset{water}{2 H_2 O} \)
The above reaction can be termed as oxidation reaction because hydrogen is oxidised.(gain of \( O_2 \) ). It is also termed a reduction reaction because oxygen is reduced (gain of \( H_2 \) ).
As both oxidation and reduction takes place, it can named as redox reaction.

Example 5:

How is ammonia prepared from hydrogen?
Answer:
Nitrogen reacts with hydrogen in the presence of a catalyst, 'Fe', iron (Fe) to give ammonia, where this is further used in the preparation of urea fertilizer.
\( \underset{nitrogen}{N_2} + \underset{hydrogen}{3H_2} \rightleftarrows \underset{ammonia}{2 NH_3} \)

Example 6:

Ammonium nitrate doesn't produce oxygen on heating. What are the products given here?
Answer:
Ammonium nitrate decompose into dinitrogen monoxide, i.e., nitrous oxide also called as laughing gas.
\( NH_4 NO_3 \xrightarrow{heat} 2 H_2 O + N_2 O \ (laughing \ gas) \)
