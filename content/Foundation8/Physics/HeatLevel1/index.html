Common misconception Fact
1 Heat can only be produced by burning things. All forms of energy can be changed to heat energy. For example, rubbing the hands together produces heat energy.
2 \( 100 \ cm^3 \) of water at \( 100^{\circ} \ C \) contain the same amount of heat as \( 10 \ cm^3 \) of water at \( 100^{\circ} \ C \) . Both \( 100 \ cm^3 \) of water and the \( 10 \ cm^3 \) of water are equally hot. But the \( 100 \ cm^3 \) of water contains 10 times more heat than the \( 10 \ cm^3 \) of water.

Introduction

In our day-to-day life, we feel cool, when we touch an ice piece. But we feel hot when we accidentally touch a vessel kept on a stove. The feeling of hotness or coldness is due to the relative flow of heat between our body and the object touched.
In the above example, in the case of a hot body, heat flows into our body while in case of a cold body, heat flows out of our body.
Definition
Heat is defined as the energy that flows from one body to another because of a difference in temperature. Heat flows from a body at higher temperature to a body at lower temperature by itself represented by Q.

Temperature

In certain cases, by touching two bodies it is not possible to say which body is relatively hotter. However, it is not possible to tell exactly how hot it is. The degree of hotness or coldness of a body is called temperature. When a body is heated ,generally its temperature rises, Thus, heat is a cause while temperature is an effect.

Thermal equilibrium

When two bodies at different temperatures are brought in contact with each other, heat energy flows from a body at higher temperature to a body at lower temperature. The net flow of heat energy ceases, when the temperatures of the two bodies become equal At this stage the two bodies are said to be in thermal equilibrium with each other.
Thus, the two bodies are said to be in the state of thermal equilibrium, when net exchange of heat energy between them is nil. In the state of thermal equilibrium, the two bodies have equal temperatures.

Calorimetry

The branch of physics which deals with the measurement of heat energy is called calorimetry. It is evident that heat always flows from a body at higher temperature to the body at lower temperature till the bodies attain the same temperature (equilibrium temperature)
At equilibrium temperature, there is no flow of energy between two bodies. But the rate of flow is same from one body to another. The quantity of heat is measured in calories. In SI system, it is measured in joule.
Definition
One calorie is the quantity of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one gram of water through \( 1^{\circ} C \) .
The bigger units of heat are kilocalorie (kcal), kilojoule (kJ), megajoule (MJ).

Relation between units of heat

  1. 1 calorie = 4.186 joule = 4.2 J

  2. 1 J = 107 erg

  3. 1 kcal = 1000 cal

  4. 1 kJ = 1000 J

  5. \( 1 MJ = 10^6 \ J \)

Principle of Calorimetry

When two bodies having different temperatures are brought into contact with each other, heat is transferred from the body at higher temperature to the body at lower temperature, till thermal equilibrium is attained.
i.e. Heat lost by the body at higher temperature = Heat gained by the body at lower temperature.
When heat is supplied to a body, then the amount of heat absorbed by a body is

  1. proportional to the mass of the body i.e. Q \( \propto \) m

  2. proportional to rise in its temperature i.e. \( Q \propto \triangle{T} \)
    Hence \( Q \propto m \triangle{T} \) or \( Q = mc \triangle{T} \)
    Where the constant 'C' is called the specific heat of the body. The value of 'C' depends on the nature of material.

Principle of Calorimetry

The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of unit mass of a substance through \( 1 ^{\circ} C \) or 1 K is called specific heat.
Mathematically
\( \begin{align} Specific \ heat = \frac{Quantity \ of \ heat}{\text{Mass of the substance } \times \text{change in temperature}} \\\\[3pt] C = \frac{Q}{m \triangle{T}} \end{align} \)

Units of specific heat

SI unit is \( J \ kg^{-1} \ K^{-1} \)
C.G.S unit is cal \( g^{-1} \ (^{\circ} c^{-1}) \)
Among all gases hydrogen will have maximum specific heat.
Among all liquids water will have maximum specific heat and among all solids ice will have maximum specific heat.

Heat capacity or Thermal capacity (H)

Heat capacity or thermal capacity of a body is defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of the body through \( 1^{\circ} C \) or 1K.
Heat capacity = Heat required to raise the temperature of a body \( 1^{\circ} C \)
Mathematically, heat capacity = \( \frac{amount \ of \ heat}{\text{rise in temperature}} = \frac{Q}{\triangle{T}} \)
\( \therefore \ H = \frac{Q}{\triangle{T}} = mC \)
Hence, Thermal capacity = mass \( \times \) specific heat.

Units of heat capacity

Heat capacity = \( = \frac{Q}{\triangle{T}} = mC \)
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ C.G.S. unit = \frac{calorie}{degree \ centigrade} = cal {^{\circ}}C^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ S.I \ unit = \frac{joule}{kelvin} = J K^{-1} \end{align} \)

Conductors of heat

Substances which allows heat to pass through them rapidly are called good conductors of heat.

  1. Metals are good conductors of heat.

  2. Non-metals are usually bad conductors of heat, except graphite.

Applications of good conductors

  1. Cooking vessels are made of metals so that heat is conducted through them and passes onto the food.

  2. Mercury is used as thermometric liquid because it is a good conductor of heat.

  3. Automobile radiators use tubes made of copper as it is a good conductor of heat, Being a good conductor, it absorbs the heat from the hot water in the engine and transmit it to the surroundings.
    For the same reason, air conditioners and refrigerators use copper tubes.

  4. The heat is passed onto the solder through the tip of soldering iron which is made of copper as copper is a good conductor of heat.

Insulators of heat

Substances which does not allow heat to pass through them readily.
Non-metals like plastic, wood and rubber together with liquids and gases are good insulators.

Applications of bad conductors

  1. We wear woolen clothes and use blankets in winter as they contain large amount of trapped air which is a bad conductor of heat and therefore does not allow heat energy to flow outward from our body. Thus, our body stops losing heat and we feel warm.

  2. The gap between double walls of an ice box is filled with glass, wool, which is a bad conductor of heat. It prevents the heat from flowing in so that ice does not melt.

  3. The handles of appliances like pressure cooker, electric iron, electric ovens, etc., are made of bad conductors of heat such as wood or plastic or ebonite so that while handling them, the heat is not conducted from the hot vessels to our hands.

  4. The pipes carrying steam from boiler are covered with asbestos orlass wool to prevent loss of heat due to conduction.

Change of State

Matter exists in three states viz, solid, liquid or gas. The physical state of matter can be changed by adding or removing heat energy from it. For example, when heat is added to ice, which is in solid state, it changes to water which is in liquid state. By adding heat further, water can be vaporized so as to change it into gaseous state. Similarly, a substance in gaseous state can be brought into liquid and then solid state by successively removing heat energy from it. Terms associated with change of state

Fusion or Melting:

The process during which a solid changes to liquid state at some fixed temperature by the absorption of heat energy (or heating) is called fusion or melting
Example
Conversion of ice into water is fusion.
\( \underset{(Solid)}{Ice} \xrightarrow [(melting)]{fusion} \underset{(liquid)}{Water} \)

Melting point

The fixed temperature at which a solid substance gets converted into a liquid is called its fusion point or melting point.
Example
Conversion of ice into water is fusion.
The melting point of ice is \( 0 ^{\circ}C \) .

Vaporization or Boiling

The process during which a liquid on heating changes to vapour state at some fixed temperature is called vaporization or boiling.
Example
Conversion of water into steam is vaporization.
\( \underset{(liquid)}{Water} \xrightarrow[(boiling)] {vaporization} \underset{(gas)}{steam} \)

Boiling point

The fixed temperature at which a liquid gets converted into vapour state is called its vaporization point or boiling point.
Example
The boiling point of water is \( 100^{\circ}C \) .

Condensation or Liquefaction

The process during which vapours on cooling change into liquid state at some fixed temperature is called condensation.
Example
Conversion of steam into water is condensation.
\( \underset{(gas)}{Steam} \xrightarrow[(cooling )] {condensation} \underset{(liquid)}{water} \)

Condensation point

The fixed temperature at which the vapour state of substance gets converted into liquid state is called its condensation point or liquefaction point.

Freezing or Solidification:

The process during which a liquid changes to solid state at some fixed temperature with the liberation of heat energy (or cooling) is called solidification or freezing.
Example
Conversion of water into ice is solidification.
\( \underset{(liquid)}{water} \xrightarrow[(solidification )]{freesing} \underset{(soildl)}{ice} \)

Freezing point

The fixed temperature at which a liquid substance gets converted into a solid is called its freezing point.

Evaporation and sublimation:

The conversion of a liquid into vapour, at temperatures below the boiling point is called evaporation.

The difference between evaporation and boiling are listed again below

Evaporation Boiling
1 It takes place at all temperatures. It takes place at a definite temperature.
2 Temperature may change during evaporation. Temperature does not change during boiling.
3 The evaporation of liquid is confined only to the surface of liquid. Boiling takes place in every region of liquid
4 The rate of evaporation depends on the free surface area of the liquid The rate of boiling is independent of the surface area of the liquid.

A few substances change directly from solid to gas on heating. This process is called sublimation and such substances are called sublimates. Iodine, camphor, ammonium chloride and naphthalene are some examples of sublimates. The sublimates on sublimation convert to gas and this gas on cooling can be reconverted to solid.

Latent heat

During the change in the state of a substance, its temperature remains constant even though heat is being added (or removed) continuously. The heat energy supplied during the change in the state is used to overcome forces of attraction among the molecules so as to increase their potential energy. The potential energy increases when distance between molecules increases. The heat energy absorbed during change in the state is called latent heat.
The amount of heat needed to change the state of a given substance without any change in its temperature is called the latent heat.
Hence latent heat depends on the mass of the substance more is the mass (m) of the substance more is the quantity of heat (Q) required, i.e., Q a m or Q = mL where L is the latent heat of substance.

Unit of Latent heat

We know Q = mL \( \Rightarrow \ L = \frac{Q}{m} = \frac{L}{kg} = J \ kg^{-1} \)
The S.I. unit of latent heat is \( J \ kg^{-1} \)
The CGS unit of latent heat is \( cal \ g^{-1} \)

Types of Latent heat

Depending upon the nature of the process, there are two kinds of specific latent heat.

  1. The specific latent heat of fusion. \( (L_f) \)

  2. The specific latent heat of vapourisation . \( (L_v) \)

Specific Latent heat of Fusion \( (L_f) \)

Specific latent heat of fusion of a substance is the quantity of heat required to convert, unit mass of the substance from solid to liquid state without change of temperature.
Example
The specific latent heat of fusion of ice is \( 80 \ cal \ g^{-1} \) . This statement means that for 1 gram of pure ice at \( 0 ^{\circ} C \) to convert to water at \( 0 ^{\circ} C \) amount of heat required is 80 cal.

Specific Latent heat of Vaporisation \( (L_v) \)

Specific latent heat of vapourisation of a substance is the quantity of heat required to convert unit mass of the substance from liquid to vapour state without change of temperature.
Example
The specific latent heat of vaporisation of water is \( 540 \ cal g^{-1} \) . This statement means that for 1 gram of pure water at \( 100 ^{\circ} C \) to convert to steam at \( 100 ^{\circ} C \) amount of heat required is 540 cal.,

Example 9:

The densities of two substances are in the ratio 1 : 2 and their specific heat in the ratio 2 : 3. compare their thermal capacities. If their volumes are in the ratio 3 : 4.

Solution:
Let \( C_1 \) , and \( C_2 \) be the thermal capacities of the two substances.
\( \begin{align} d_1 : d_2 = 1 : 2 \ \ \ \ s_1 : s_2 = 2 : 3 \\\\[3pt] V_1 : V_2 = 3 : 4 \ \ \ C_1 : C_2 = ? \end{align} \)
We know that, C = ms
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \ C = V \ ds \ \ \ \ ( \because \ m = Vd) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ \frac{C_1}{C_1} =\frac{V_1}{V_2} \times \frac{d_1}{d_2} \times \frac{s_1}{s_2} \\\\[3pt] \frac{C_1}{C_2} = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} \\\\[3pt] \frac{C_1}{C_2} = \frac{1}{4} \\\\[3pt] = C_1 : C_2 = 1 : 4 \end{align} \)

Example 12:

Boiling water extinguishes fire more quickly than ice or cold water. Can you explain why?

Solution:
Boiling water, when put on fire, starts absorbing the latent heat of vapourization immediately (540 cal) for each gram evaporated whereas, in the case of ice or cold water, first the temperature of water has to be increased from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \) . The heat absorption from fire starts instantaneously on a larger scale in the case of boiling water. Hence, the boiling water can extinguish fire more quickly than ice or cold water.

\( \begin{align} m_s L_s [m_{condensed \ water} \times s_w (100 - t)] = m_i s_i (0 - (-20)) + m_i L_i + m_{resultant \ water} s_w(t - 0) \\\\[3pt] [100 \times 540] + [100 \times 1 \times (100 - t)] =[500 \times 0.5 \times 20] + [500 \times 80] + [500 \times 1 \times t] \\\\[3pt] 54000 + 10000 - 100t = 5000 + 40000 + 500 t \\\\[3pt] 19000 = 600 t \Rightarrow \ t = \frac{19000}{600} = 31.67 ^{\circ} C \end{align} \)
Therefore, the resultant temperature is \( 31.67^{\circ} C \) .

Example 24:

A liquid X of specific heat capacity \( 1200 \ J \ kg^{-1} K^{-1} \) and at \( 80^{\circ} C \) is mixed with the liquid Y of specific heat capacity \( 900 \ J \ kg^{-1} K^{-1} \) at \( 30^{\circ} C \) , when the final temperature recorded is \( 40^{\circ} C \) . Find the ratio of the weights of the liquids mixed.

Solution:
Let mass of the liquids X and Y be \( m_x \) and \( m_y \) respectively According to calorimetry,
Heat lost by liquid X = Heat gained by liquid Y.
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ m_X s_X (0 - 40) = m_Y s_Y(40 - 30) \\\\[3pt] m_X (1200)(40) = m_Y (900)(10) \\\\[3pt] \frac{m_X}{m_Y} = \frac{9000}{48000} \ \Rightarrow \ \frac{m_X}{m_Y} = \frac{3}{16} \end{align} \)
