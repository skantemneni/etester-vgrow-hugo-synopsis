Introduction

In our daily life, we make guesses like the distance of your school from home, the time taken by you to take your lunch and the measure of your weight. The guesses made are as such may not be accurate. But in science, measurement need to be accurate.
Therefore, proper measurement techniques need to be learnt.
Measurement is the comparison of an unknown quantity with a known standard quantity of same kind. Another important thing is to identify a quantity that can be measured (known as physical quantity). Generally most of the measurements are based on physical quantities like length, mass, time, temperature etc.,
In measuring a physical quantity one needs to have an idea of its various units, their inter-relationships and also the various devices used for its measurements.

Physical quantity

  1. A physical quantity is a quantity that can be measured.

  2. Physical quantity is expressed by stating

    1. magnitude of the physical quantity.

    2. the unit employed. Therefore, physical quantity = magnitude \( \times \) unit.

Standard unit

In measuring a physical quantity one needs to have an idea of its various units, their inter-relationships and also the various devices used for its measurement.
The unit plays an important role in the description of a physical quantity. But the unit used for a particular physical quantity is person dependant.
Hence, they need to be specified and standardized to maintain uniformity. Under this treaty, an International Bureau of weights and measures was established.
This Bureau situated at Serves in Paris has specified standard units, measured more accurately with advanced techniques of science.

Characteristics of Standard unit

  1. It should be of convenient size.

  2. It should not change with respect to space and time.

  3. It should be possible to define, without any doubt or ambiguity.

  4. It should not be perishable.

  5. It should be easily reproduced.

Types of physical quantities

(a) Fundamental quantities

These quantities do not depend on other physical quantities. Length, mass and time are fundamental quantities.

(b) Dervied quantities

These quantities are defined in terms of fundamental quantities. Area, density, volume are derived quantities.

Types of Units

(1) Fundamental unit

The units of fundamental quantities are called fundamental units.

(2) Derived unit

The units of derived quantities are called derived units.

Obtaining units of derived quantities

The unit of a physical quantity can be obtained with the help of its formula or expression.

How to obtain

Step 1: Write the measuring formula of the physical quantity.
Step 2: Check the terms present in the measuring formula.
Step 3: Substitute the units of the terms identified.

Rules of writing units

  1. Full names of all units, even when they are named after a scientist are not written with a capital letter.
    Examples
    newton, joule, watt, ampere etc.,
    The SI unit of force should be expressed as newton but not Newton

  2. Symbol for a unit named after a scientist has capital letter.
    Examples

    1. The SI unit of force is newton, named after the scientist Issac Newton. Therefore the symbol for newton is N.

    2. The SI unit of energy is joule, named after the scientist Joule. Therefore the symbol for joule is J.

  3. The first letter of the unit is used as symbol for some units.
    Examples
    'm' for metre, 's' for second, etc.

  4. The units may be written in full or using agreed symbols, but not in abbreviated form.
    Examples
    Metre or 'm', second or 's', kilogram or 'kg'

  5. Symbols for units don't take plural form.
    Examples

    1. If the mass of a stone is 50 kilogram, it should be expressed as 50 kg but should not be expressed as 50 kgs.

    2. If the length of a stick is 10 metre, then it should be expressed as 10 m but should not be expressed as 10 ms or 10 mts.

  6. No full stop or other punctuation marks should be used within or at the end of symbols for units.
    Examples
    (a) Mass of a body 50 kg but should not be expressed as 50 k.g or 50.k.g.

Measurement of Length

Length a fundamental quantity, is used to measure the distance between two points.
Length can be measured using devices like metre scale, vernier callipers measuring tape etc.

Vernier Calipers

  1. An instrument used to measure lengths accurately upto 0.1 mm (i.e. 0.01 cm).

  2. A vernier calipers consists of two scale viz main scale, vernier scale.

  3. Principle of vernier calipers
    In a vernier calipers, 'N' vernier scale divisions are equal to (N - 1) main scale divisions.

  4. Least count of a vernier calipers (L.C.) = \( \frac{ \text{smallest value of 1 MSD}}{{No.of \ vernier \ scale \ division}} \) = 1 MSD - 1 VSD
    \( \therefore \) Least count = 1 mm - 0.9 mm = 0.1 mm

Errors in vernier calipers

Zero error

When the fixed and the movable jaws of a vernier callipers are made in contact and if the zero of the main scale does not coincide with the zero of the vernier scale, then the instrument is said to have a zero error.
The zero error can be of two types
(i) positive zero error and
(ii) negative zero error.
(i) positive zero error
If the zeroth division of the vernier scale is to the right of the zeroth division of the main scale when the two jaws are brought in contact with each other then the instrument is said to have a positive zero error.
If the division of vernier scale coinciding with some division on the main scale is n, then the zero error is (+ n \( \times \) LC) and the correction is (- n \( \times \) LC) cm.
(ii) negative zero error
If the zeroth of vernier scale is on the left hand side of zero of the main scale, the error is said to be negative and the correction is positive.
If the division of vernier scale coinciding with some division on the main scales is n then the zero error is (- (N - n) \( \times \) LC) and correction is (+ (N - n) \( \times \) LC) cm where N is number of divisions on the vernier scale.

Measurement of Area

Area is a derived quantity, that is obtained by multiplying the two measurements of a regular body. For example the area of a rectangle is calculated as the product of its length and breadth.
The SI unit of area is \( m^2 \) or square metre. The other units of area \( cm^2, \ mm^2, \ km^2 \) etc

The area of an irregular object can be calculated by drawing the outline of its shape on a graph paper.
The number of complete squares are calculated, next the squares greater than half are also counted as a square, leaving the squares less than half.
The sum of all the squares gives the area of leaf to be that many \( cm^2. \)

Measurement of Volume

Volume is the space occupied by an object. The SI unit of volume is \( m^3 \) or cubic metre.
The other units of volume are \( cm^3, \ ml, \ l \) etc.
\( \begin{align} 1 \ ml = 1 \ cm^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ ml = 10^{-6} \ m^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^3 \ cm^3 = 10^3 \ ml \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^{-3} \ m^3 \end{align} \)
Volume of regular bodies can be calculated by measuring the dimensions necessary for the calculations. The volume of a cube is given by \( (side)^3. \)

Measurement of Mass

Mass is the measure of matter contained in a body. Common balance, physical balance are the mostly used devices used for measuring it. SI unit of mass is kilogram.
Common Balance is used to find unknown mass of an object using known standard masses.

Measurement of Time

Time is the interval between two events. In our daily life, many events are observed like heart beat, blinking of eyes, appearance of comets etc. These events may be either periodic or may not be periodic.
In air lines and railways, a 24 hour clock system is used, for example 4 : 15 P. M. in 12 hour clock system is written as 16 hour 15 minutes in 24 hour clock system.

Simple pendulum

Take a brass bob and connect it to a thread of length 100 cm and suspend it from the nail fixed to a wall and the arrangement is as shown.

The bob is pulled gently towards the left and on releasing the bob swings towards 'B' and 'C' and then returns back to a position 'A'.
The motion of the bob from the point 'A' to 'C' and back to the point 'A' is called one oscillation and time taken to complete one oscillation is called time period.
If time period were to be 2 seconds then such a pendulum is called 'seconds pendulum'.

Density

It is defined as mass per unit volume of the substance. Density is a derived unit. It is scalar quantity.

Measuring formula

\( Density = \frac{mass}{volume} \ i.e., \ d = \frac{M}{V} \)

Units

S.I. unit of density is \( kg \ m^{-3} \)
C.G.S unit of density is \( g \ m^{-3} \)
\( 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = 10^{-3} \ g \ cm^{-3} \)

Relation between the SI and CGS units

\( \begin{align} 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1 \ kg}{1 \ m^3} = \frac{1 \ kg}{1 m \times 1 m \times 1 m} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1000 \ g}{100 \ cm \times 100 \ cm \times 100 \ cm} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1 g}{100 \ cm^3} \\\\[3pt] \therefore 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1}{1000} \ g \ cm^{-3} \ or \ 1 \ g \ cm^{-3} = 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3} \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) Density of a substances in SI system = 1000 \( \times \) density of the substance in CGS system

Relative density

It is the ratio of the density of the substance to the density of water at \( 4^{ \circ} C \) . Thus,
\( \begin{align} \text{Relative density (R.D.)} = \frac{ \text{density the substance}}{ \text{density of water at} 4^{ \circ} C} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{ \text{mass of the substance}}{ \text{volume of the substance}} \div \frac{ \text{mass of water at } 4^{ \circ} C}{ \text{ volume of water at} 4^{ \circ} C} \\\\[3pt] = \left[ RD = \frac{M}{V} \right] \end{align} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Find the CGS unit of force.

Solution:
Step 1: We have, Force = mass \( \times \) acceleration.
Step 2: We have, mass as a fundamental quantity, and acceleration as a derived quantity which can be written as
\( acceleration = \frac{ \text{change in velocity}}{time} \)
for unit purpose, acceleration = \( \frac{length}{(time)^2} \)
Step 3: Substituting the units of the quantities we have CGS unit of force
\( \begin{align} \frac{ \text{CGS unit of mass} \times \text{ CGS unit of length}}{ \text{(cgs unit of time)}^2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{g \ cm}{s^2} \\\\[3pt] = g \ cm \ s^{-2} \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) CGS unit of force is \( g \ cm \ s^{-2} \) which is also named as dyne.

Example 2:

Question:
Find the SI unit of density:

Solution:
The formula for density is
\( Density = \frac{mass}{volume} \)
SI unit of density = \( \frac{ \text{SI unit of mass}}{ \text{SI unit of volume}} = \frac{kilogram \ (kg)}{cubic \ metre \ (m^3)} \)
\( \therefore \) SI unit density = \( kg \ m^{-3} \)

Example 3:

Question:
If a new physical quantity by name 'AMBITION' is introduced, whose measuring formula is
\( AMBITION = \frac{Density \times Pressure}{Speed} \) find the S.I unit of AMBITION.

Solution:
Step 1: Formula
\( AMBITION = \frac{Density \times Pressure}{Speed} \)
In order to get SI unit of AMBITION, substitute the SI unit of density, SI unit of pressure and SI unit of speed.
SI unit of density : \( Density = \frac{Mass}{Volume} = \frac{kg}{m^3} = kg \ m^{-3} \)
SI unit of pressure : \( Pressure = \frac{force}{area} = \frac{ma}{area} \)
\( = \frac{kg \ ms^{-2}}{m^2} = kg \ m^{-1} s^{-2} \)
SI unit of speed : \( Speed = \frac{distance}{time} = \frac{m}{s} = ms^{-1} \)
SI unit of AMBITION = \( \frac{kg \ m^{-3} \times kg \ m^{-1} s^{-2}}{ms^{-1}} = kg^2 \ m^{-5} s^{-1} \)

Example 4:

Question:
Calculate the number of kilometres in one Astronomical unit.

Solution:
To find 1 Astronomical unit (A.U.)
= ? kilometre (m)
We know that,
1 A.U = \( 1.5 \times 10^{11} m........(1) \)
Also, 1 km \( = 10^3 m...........(2) \)

The 'femtometre' (the smallest unit of length) is often called 'fermi'. How many fermi's make up 1 millimetre. [1 femto = \( 10^{- 15} \) m].

To find 1 millimetere = ? fermi

Example 7:

Question:
Density of mercury is \( 13.6 \ g cm^{-3} \) . Express this value in MKS system.

Solution:
We have Density = \( \frac{Mass}{Volume} \)

Question:
\( 1 \ km \ h^{-1} = 'p' \ ms^{-1} \) , find 'p'

Solution:
To get 'p', we should convert km into m and hour into second

Question:
The main scale of a vernier callipers has least count of 0.5 mm divisions. If 20 divisions of this scale are divided into 25 equal parts of a vernier, what is the least count?

1 M.S.D = 0.5 mm
20 divisions on Main scale = 25 division on vernier scale
\( \Rightarrow \) 1 division on vernier scale = \( \frac{20}{25} \) divisions on main scale
\( \Rightarrow 1 \ V.S.D = 0.8 \ M.S.D \Rightarrow 1 \ V.S.D = 0.8 \times 0.5 \ mm = 0.4 \ mm \)

Example 12:

Question:
Calculate the radius of a sphere from the given data of readings using a vernier callipers.
In the main scale 1 cm is divided into 20 parts.
Number of divisions on the vernier = 25; zero error = + 0.016 cm
The position of the zero of vernier when the sphere is held between the jaws = between 2.75 and 2.8 cm.
Vernier coincidence = \( 9^{th} \) division

Solution:
\( 1 \ MSD = \frac{1}{20} \ cm \)
No. of VSD (n) = 25
\( \therefore LC = \frac{1}{n} \) of a MSD = \( \frac{1}{25} \times \frac{1}{20} cm \) = 0.002 cm
Zero error = + 0.016 cm
\( \therefore \) Zero correction = - 0.016 cm
Diameter of the sphere = \( [MSR + (VSR \times LC) + ZC] \)
\( = 2.75 \ cm + (9 \times 0.002) cm - 0.016 cm = 2.784 \ cm \)
\( Radius = \frac{1}{2} diameter = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.784 = 1.392 \ cm \)
\( \therefore \) Radius of the given sphere = 1.392 cm

= length \( \times \) area of cross section = \( 0.8 \times 0.75 \ m^2 = 0.6 \ m^3 \)
Density of cylinder (d) = \( 7500 \ kg \ m^{-3} \)
Mass of cylinder (m) = ?
We know,
\( d = \frac{m}{v} \Rightarrow m = v \times d = 0.6 \times 7500 = 4500 kg \)

Example 17:

Question:
The relative density of copper is 8.9 what is its density in C.G.S and SI units.

Solution:
We know that
\( R.D = \frac{ \text{density \ of \ substance}}{ \text{density \ of \ water}} \)
\( \therefore \) density of substance in C.G.S. = R.D of the substance \( \times \) density of water
\( = 8.9 \times 1 \ g \ cm^{-3} = 8.9 \ g \ cm^{-3} \)
Density of the substance in S.I. unit = R.D of the substance \( \times \) density of water in S.I. unit
\( 8.9 \times 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3} = 8.9 \times 10^3 \ kg \ m^{-3} \)
