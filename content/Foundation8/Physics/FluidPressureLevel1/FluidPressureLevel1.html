Introduction

Matter mainly exists in three physical states viz., solid, liquid and gases. Solids have a definite shape and size while liquids and gases do not have a definite shape. But liquids and gases have a common property of flowing. Hence they are termed as fluids. The interaction of fluids involves in exertion of pressure, transmission of pressure etc., which is studied under fluid pressure.

Thrust

Thrust is defined as the force acting on a body normal (perpendicular) to its surface. The unit of thrust is same as the unit of force; i.e, dyne in C.G.S system and newton (N) in S.I.system. Thrust is also expressed in gravitational units called kgwt (kilogram weight) or kgf (kilogram force). 1 kgwt or 1 kgf = 9.8 N.
Similarly 1 gwt or 1 gf = 980 dyne.
Every body has a certain weight and certain base area. When, a body rests on a surface it exerts a force equal to its weight.

Pressure

Thrust acting per unit surface area is defined as pressure.
\( Pressure (P) = \frac{Thrust (F)}{area (A)} \)
\( P = \frac{F}{A} \)

Pressure is a scalar quantity, because pressure on a body can be exerted in any direction.
Units of pressure
C.G.S unit: dyne \( cm^{-2} \ or \ gwt \ cm^{-2} \)
S.I units: \( Nm^{-2} \ or \ kgwt \ m^{-2} \)
Note: The S.I unit \( N m^{-2} \) is also known as pascal. It is denoted by Pa.
The other unit of pressure is bar.
\( 1 \ Pa = 1 \ Nm^{-2} \)
\( 1 \ bar = 10^5 \ Pa \)

Pressure in fluids

The term fluid refers to any substance which has no definite shape and has an ability to flow. Liquids and gases are fluids, since they have no definite shape and can flow. The pressure exerted by a fluid is called fluid pressure.

Types of fluid pressure

A fluid exerts three types of pressure

  1. downward pressure

  2. upward pressure

  3. lateral pressure

Fluid Pressure-Downward Pressure

Take a glass tube open at both the ends. Tie a thin rubber sheet on one of its ends. Hold the tube vertically and pour some water into it. The balloon bulges out showing that water has gone down and that it exerts a pressure on the rubber sheet. Add more water to the tube. The bulge increases.
From this we can conclude that water exerts a pressure on the bottom of the vessel in which it is kept.

Fluid Pressure-Upward Pressure

Take a foot ball and immerse it in water in a vessel (V) and leave it. The ball immediately comes up and floats on water. This shows that water (or fluid) exerts pressure in the upward direction.

Mathematical expression for fluid pressure

A beaker is filled with fluid having density 'd'.
Imagine a cylindrical column of liquid with a horizontal area 'A' at a level of 'B' and at a depth 'h' below the surface of the fluid.

Volume of the imaginary column of fluid.
V = Area of cross section \( \times \) height
\( V = A \times h \) .......(1)
Thrust on the cylindrical column = Weight of the fluid
\( = m \times g \)
\( = (V \times d) \times g \ \ \ ( \therefore m = V \times d) \)
\( \therefore Thrust = (A \times h) \times d \times g \) [from equation (1)]
But, pressure = \( \frac{Thrust}{Area} \)
\( \therefore Pressure (P) = \frac{Ahdg}{A} = hdg \)
\( \therefore \) Pressure due to a fluid column = P = hdg

Total pressure at a point in a liquid

Total pressure at a point (B) = Atmospheric pressure on liquid surface \( 'P_A' \) + in a liquid at a depth 'X' pressure due to liquid column (fluid pressure) above the point'B'.
Total pressure = Atmospheric pressure + fluid pressure
\( = P_A + hdg = P_A + xdg \)

Atmospheric Pressure

The blanket of air above the earth is called the atmosphere. We know that the air in the atmosphere is a mixture of gases. Thus, it is considered as a fluid.
As it has both mass and weight, it exerts pressure on all bodies located on the earth's surface. This exerted pressure is called atmospheric pressure.

Measurement of atmospheric pressure

Atmospheric pressure is measured by using a barometer. It was constructed by Torricelli in 1643.

Principle of Barometer

The basic principle of the barometer is to use atmospheric pressure to balance vertical column of mercury sealed at the top. The height of the mercury indicates the atmospheric pressure.
The space above the mercury column is called Torricellian vacuum.

Uses of Barometer

It is used in laboratory to find atmospheric pressure.
It is used in forecasting of weather.
Aneroid barometer can be used as an altimeter to measure the altitude.

Standard atmospheric pressure

The normal or the standard pressure is the average pressure of the atmosphere at sea level at 273 K and is expressed as a pressure of one atmosphere (1 atm).
It is found that 1 atm pressure can balance a column of mercury 0.76 m high. Thus, the normal or standard pressure is 0.76 m of Hg.
We can express it in pascal as :
\( \therefore 1 \ atm = 76 \ cm \times 13.6 \ g cm^{-3} \times 9.8 \ m s^{-2} \) (using \( h \rho g \) for pressure exerted by a liquid)
\( \begin{align} = 0.76 \times 13.6 \times 10^3 \ kg \ m^{-3} \times 9.8 \ ms^{-2} \\\\[3pt] = 101292.8 \ Nm^{-2} \ or \ Pa \approx \ 1.013 \times 10^5 \ Pa \end{align} \)
Note : If the atmospheric pressure \( (P_0) \) acting on the free surface of liquid is also taken into account then total pressure in a liquid at a depth h = Atmospheric pressure + pressure due to liquid column = \( (P_0) \) + hdg.

Factors influencing the atmospheric pressure

Height:
Atmospheric pressure decreases with increasing height.
Temperature
As the temperature of air increases, density of air decreases and hence atmospheric pressure decreases.

Uses of Atmospheric pressure

There are many applications of atmospheric pressure.

  1. Strawpipe

  2. Syringe

  3. Bicycle pump

Transmission of pressure

  1. Pressure is transmitted with equal magnitude in all directions through an incompressible fluid.

  2. This principle is illustrated using a flask with holes around it.

  3. When the piston is pushed into the flask, water shoots out with equal force in all directions.

  4. An application of this principle is in the hydraulic system such as the hydraulic press.

Applications of Pascals Law

Hydraulic Machines work under the principle of Pascal's law.
Hydraulic brakes
They are used in vehicles for easy and efficient functioning. The force applied to the brake pedal is transmitted to the wheels by the brake fluid.

Hydraulic press or Brahmah press

  1. It is used to compress bales of cotton and straw.

  2. It used to compress oil seeds for getting oil.

It consists of a cylinder that contains two pistons, one smaller than the other. The cylinder is filled with a fluid. A force applied to the smaller piston is transferred through the fluid to the larger piston.

Let pressure acting on smaller piston be \( P_1 = \frac{W_1}{a} \) and on larger piston be \( P_2 = \frac{W_2}{A} \)
Since pressure is transmitted equally in all the directions
\( P_1 = P_2 \Rightarrow \frac{W_1}{a} = \frac{W_2}{A} \Rightarrow W_2 = \frac{A}{a} W_1 \)
\( \therefore \) "In a hydraulic press we can multiply a small thrust applied into a big thrust, by making the ratio of area of cross sections very large".

Solved examples

Example 1:

Derive the relationship between the S.I. and C.G.S. units of pressure.

Solution:
The SI. unit of pressure = \( N m^{-2} \)
The C.G.S. unit of pressure = \( dyne \ cm^{-2} \)
1 S.I.units = ? C.G.S. units
\( 1 \frac{N}{m^2} = ? \frac{dyne}{cm^2} ........(1) \)
We know that
\( 1 N = 10^5 \ dyne \)
\( 1 m^2 = 10^4 \ cm^2 \)
Substituting the above values in equation (1), we get
\( \frac{1 N}{m^2} = \frac{10^5 \ dyne}{10^4 \ cm^2} = \left( \frac{10^5}{10^4} \right) dyne \ cm^{-2} \)
\( \therefore 1 \ Nm^{-2} = 10 \ dyne \ cm^{-2} \)

Example 2:

Express a pressure of \( 3 \times 10^5 \) pascal in units of

  1. bar

  2. dyne \( cm^{-2} \) and

  3. millibar

Solution:

  1. Pressure = \( 3 \times 10^5 \ pascal \)
    = 3 bar ( \( \therefore 1 \ bar = 10^5 \ Pa \) )

  2. Pressure = \( 3 \times 10^5 \ pascal \)
    \( 3 \times 10^5 \times 10 \ dynes \ cm^{-2} \)
    = (since pascal = \( 10 \ dynes \ cm^{-2} \) )
    \( = 3 \times 10^6 \ dynes \ cm^{-2} \)

  3. Pressure = \( 3 \times 10^5 \ pascal \)
    \( 3 \times 10^3 \times 10^2 \ pascal \)
    \( 3 \times 10^3 \ millibar \) ( \( \therefore 1 \ millibar = 10^2 \ Pa \) )

Example 3:

A force of 15 N acts on an area of \( 50 \ cm^2 \) . What is the pressure in pascal.

Solution:
Force, F = 15 N
Area, \( A = 50 \ cm^2 = \frac{50}{10, 000} m^2 \)
\( \begin{align} P = \frac{F}{A} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow P = \frac{15}{ \frac{50}{10000}} \\\\[3pt] P = \frac{15 \times 10000}{50} Pa = 3000 Pa \end{align} \)

Example 4:

What is the magnitude of force required to newton's produce a pressure of 37500 Pa on an area of \( 300 cm^2 \) ?

Solution:
Area, A = \( 300 \ cm^2 = \frac{300}{10000} m^2 \)
Pressure, P = 37500 Pa
Force, F = ?
\( P = \frac{F}{A} \)
\( F = P \times A = 37500 \times \frac{300}{1000} N = 1125 N \)
Therefore, magnitude of the force is 1125 N

Example 5:

A cubical block of wood of density \( 5g \ cm^{-3} \) stands on table with a sides of 10 cm. Find the thrust by the block of wood on the table if \( 10 \ ms^{-2} \) .

Solution:
Density of block, d = \( 5 \ g \ cm^{-3} \)
\( = 5 \times 1000 \ kg \ cm^{-3} ( \therefore 1 \ g \ cm^{-3} = 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3}) \)
\( = 5000 \ kg \ m^{-3} \)
Side of cube (l) = 10 cm = \( \frac{10}{100} m = \frac{1}{10} m \)
Volume of cube, (v) = \( l^3 = \left( \frac{1}{10} \right)^3 = \frac{1}{1000} m^3 \)
Thrust, T = ?
\( \begin{align} T = mg \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow T = v \times d \times g \ \ \ \left( \therefore d = \frac{m}{v} \Rightarrow m = v \times d \right) \\\\[3pt] T = l^3 \times d \times g \ \ \ \ ( \therefore v_{cube} = l^3) \\\\[3pt] \therefore T = \frac{1}{1000} \times 5000 \times 10 = 50 N \end{align} \)
Therefore thrust acting on the table is 50 N

Example 17:

In a Brarmah press a force of 10 kgwt is applied on a piston of area of cross section \( 5 \ cm^{2} \) , then the upward thrust exerted by the piston of area of cross section \( 50 \ cm^{2} \) is

Solution:
\( F_1 = 10 \ kgwt, \ \ \ \ A_1 = 5 \ cm^2 \)
\( F_2 = ? \ \ \ \ A_2 = 50 \ cm^2 \)
By the principle of hydraulic machine
\( \Rightarrow \frac{F_2}{F_1} = \frac{A_2}{A_1} \Rightarrow \frac{F_2}{10} = \frac{50}{5} \Rightarrow 5F_2 = 500 \)

Example 18:

In a hydraulic machine, the two pistons are of area of cross section in the ratio 1 : 10. What force is needed on the narrow piston to overcome a force of 100 N on the wider piston?

Solution:
Given
\( \begin{align} \frac{A_1}{A_2} = 1 : 10, \\\\[3pt] F_2 = 100 \ N \\\\[3pt] F_1 = ? \end{align} \)
By the principle of hydraulic machine
\( \frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2} \)
\( \Rightarrow F_1 = F_2 \times \frac{A_1}{A_2} = 100 \times \frac{1}{100} = 10 \ N \)
