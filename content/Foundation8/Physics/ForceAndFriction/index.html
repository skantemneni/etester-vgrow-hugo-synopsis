Common misconception Fact
1. Friction is not a type of force as it is neither push nor pull. 1. Friction is a force. It can change speed of a moving object.
2.Frictional force is not useful. 2. Friction is useful while braking a moving object. It also helps in walking.
3. Friction depends on the area of two surfaces on contact. 3. Friction is independent of the area of the two surfaces in contact.
It depends on the roughness of the surfaces and materials of the body.
4. Bodies must be in physical contact for application of force. 4. Magnetic, electrostatic and gravitational forces are non-contact forces.

Force

Definition

A force may be defined as an external cause which changes or tends to change the state of rest or of uniform motion of a body in a straight line.
In our everyday language force is associated with a push or pull.
Examples

  1. A foot ball lying at the centre of a football court at the beginning of a game remains at rest till a player kick it.

  2. When we want to open a door, we have to push the door handle. And when we want to close the door, we have to pull door handle with our hand.

Push

A body at rest can be set in motion and moving body can be brought to rest by pushing the body.
Examples

  1. A baby walks by pushing a baby walker.

  2. Pushing the door to open or to shut it.

Pull

The direction of motion of a body can be changed by means of pulling the body.
Examples

  1. A load lifted by using pulley

  2. The pull by engine when the train moves.

Effects of Force

  1. Change the shape of an object.

  2. Move an object from the position of rest.

  3. Change the speed of motion.

  4. Change the direction of motion.

Direction of force applied

Direction of the force applied determines the resultant motion.

1. Push or pull in one direction

In this case the object moves in the direction of force.

2. Push and pull in direction to one another

In this case two forces interact and the resultant force is zero and there will be no motion.

Types of forces

Force is classified into two types
1. Contact force
2. Non-contact force

1. Contact forces

Force is being applied by actually touching the body. This is called contact force.
I. Muscular forces:
The force applied by the muscles of our body is called muscular force or biological force.
II. Frictional forces:
The frictional force is the opposing force exerted by one surface over other due to relative motion between two surfaces in contact.
It is denoted by \( F_{friction} \)
\( F_{friction} \leq \mu \times F_{norm} \)
where \( \mu \) = coefficient of friction , \( F_{norm} \) = normal force

2. Non-contact force

Force is being applied without touching the body. This is called action at a distance forces.
It is also called non distance force.
It includes.

  1. Gravitational force

  2. Magnetic force

  3. Electrostatic force

1 Gravitational force
The gravitational force is the force with which the earth, the moon, or other massive large object attracts another object towards itself.
All the objects attracted by earth.
It is denoted by \( F_{grav} \)
\( F_{grav} = m \times g \)
2 Magnetic force
The force with which a magnet attracts or repels the objects (like iron or other magnet) is called magnetic force.
It is denoted by \( F_{magnetic} \)
3 Electrostatic force
The force with which the electric field attracts or repels the other objects is called electric force. It is denoted by \( F_{electric} \)

Mass

  1. Mass is the amount of substance contained in a certain object. Substance that makes up an object is known as matter. Hence mass is the quantity of matter.

  2. The mass of an object can be measured by using a beam balance or a level balance.

  3. The unit of mass is kilogram (kg), the smaller units are gram (g) and milligram (mg).
    1 kg = 1000 g
    1g = 1000 mg

  4. Different objects are made of different matter. Hence different objects, even though of the same size, may have different quantities of mass.

Weight

  1. All objects have mass. Every object on Earth's surface is pulled towards its centre by gravitational forces.
    The gravitational force pulling on an object towards itself is said to be its weight.

  2. Weight (w) = mass (m) \( \times \) acceleration due to gravity (g) where \( g_{earth} = 9.8 \ m s^{-2} \)

  3. On Earth, value of \( g_{earth} \) varies from place to place. The gravitational force at the poles is higher than at the equator.
    Hence an object appears to be heavier at the poles than at the equator.
    The gravitational force on Earth is six times stronger than on the Moon.
    Therefore if your weight is 60 N on Earth, you will only be 10 N on the moon.

  4. In outer space where there is no gravitational force, an astronaut will experience weightlessness, where his body has no weight although his body's mass remains the same.
    Therefore, although the mass of a body remains constant at all times, its weight may change from place to place.

  5. The weight of an object can be measured by a spring balance or compressed balance.

  6. Unit of weight is newton (N).

Introduction to Friction

Friction is nothing but a force which appears between two surfaces in contact and resists the motion of one body over another body.

Definition

A force arising when one body slides over the surface of another body such that it is directed opposite to the motion of the body, is called friction.

Causes of friction

When two surfaces are in contact with each other. Their surfaces appear to be smooth to naked eye. But when seen under a powerful microscope, surface appears with a number of depressions and elevations.
The force of friction arises because of interlocking of the depressions of one surface with elevations of the other. The direction of frictional force is always opposite to the direction of motion of an object.

Types of friction

There are two types of friction.

1. Static friction

The friction that exists between the surfaces in contact when there is no relative motion is called friction
Limiting friction
The maximum frictional force present when a body just begins to slip over the surface of another body is called the maximum static friction or force of limiting friction.

2. Dynamic friction

It is the friction experienced by a body, when it is in motion. The dynamic friction is also called kinetic friction and is less than the static friction.
It includes

  1. Sliding friction
    The friction that exists between two surfaces in contact when the body slides on the surface is known as the sliding friction.
    Example
    Movement of skies on the snow.

  2. Rolling friction
    The friction that exists between two surfaces when the body rolls on the surfaces when the body rolls on the surface is known as rolling friction. It is denoted by \( f_R . \)
    Magnitude of rolling friction is much less than that of sliding friction.
    Example
    Magnitude of rolling friction is much less than that of sliding friction.
    Rolling friction < Static friction or Kinetic friction < Limiting friction

Friction by liquids and Gases

Gases and liquids also exerted a friction on the bodies moving through them.
Examples
When an object or body falls from some height through air, air exerts a force of friction to oppose this motion.
The bodies of aeroplanes, automobile etc., are designed to reduce the effect of air friction.
We cannot walk on river as freely as we walk on land. Because water exerts much larger friction.
The shape of boats and submarines are designed to reduced the friction exerted by water.

Self adjusting nature of friction

  1. Case I: If the body is at rest, R = W and F = T

  2. Case II: If the value of T is increased to M, and if the body is still at rest, then F = M

  3. Case III: If T is increased to a certain value such that the body will be just on the point of moving, the limiting friction has been reached.

Normal Reaction

When the body is resting on a surface. The force exerted by the surface against the body, along the direction perpendicular to the surface is known as normal reaction.
It is denoted by R.

Weight is balanced by the normal reaction force.
\therefore R = W
R = mg
where 'm' is the mass of the body and g is acceleration due to gravity.

Units

As normal reaction is also a force.
So it has same units as that of force.
C.G.S: dyne
SI: newton (N)

Measurement of friction

The relation between frictional force (f) and normal reaction (R) is given by:
\( f \propto R \)
\( F = \mu R ...........(1) \)
Where \( \mu \) is called proportionality constant called "coefficient of friction".
Coefficient of friction has no units because it is the ratio of the magnitude of two forces (f and R).

Factors affecting \( \mu \)

The coefficient of friction depends on

  1. the type of materials the surfaces are made of.

  2. the condition of the surface (polished, rough, lubricated).

  3. other variables, such as temperature.

Calculation of different types of friction

(1) Static frictional force

The magnitude of limiting friction \( f_s \) is directly proportional to the normal reaction 'R' between surfaces.
\( \begin{align} f_s \propto R \\\\[3pt] f_s = \mu_s R \end{align} \)
where \( \mu \) is the coefficient of static friction.

(2) Dynamic or kinetic frictional force

The magnitude of the kinetic friction \( f_k \) is directly proportional to the normal reaction 'R' between the surfaces.
\( \begin{align} f_k \propto R \\\\[3pt] f_k = \mu_k R \end{align} \)
where \( \mu_k \) is the coefficient of kinetic friction.

(3) Rolling frictional force

The magnitude of the rolling friction \( f_R \) is directly proportional to the normal reaction 'N' between the surface.
\( \begin{align} f_R \propto N \\\\[3pt] f_R = \mu_R N \end{align} \)
\( \mu_s, \ \mu_k \) and \( \mu_R \) are the coefficients of static, kinetic and rolling friction respectively.

  1. Coefficient of static friction \( ( \mu_s) = \frac{ \text{Static frictional force}}{ \text{normal reaction}} \)

  2. Coefficient of kinetic friction \( ( \mu_k) = \frac{ \text{Kinetic frictional force}}{ \text{normal reaction}} \)

  3. Coefficient of rolling friction \( ( \mu_R) = \frac{ \text{Rolling frictional force}}{ \text{normal reaction}} \)

Comparision of \( \mu_s, \ \mu_k \) and \( \mu_R \)

The force of kinetic friction \( (f_k) \) is always less than the force of limiting or static friction \( (f_s) \) that is \( f_k > f_s. \)
\( \begin{align} \mu_k R < \mu_s R \\\\[3pt] \therefore \mu_k < \mu_s ........(1) \end{align} \)
A gain we know that it is much easier to roll an object on a surface, than to slide it. So rolling friction is much less than the sliding friction \( f_R < f_k . \)
\( \begin{align} \mu_R R < \mu_k R \\\\[3pt] \therefore \mu_R < \mu_k ........(2) \end{align} \)
from (1) and (2) we can make a comparision between the three kinds of coefficients of friction.
As.
\( \mu_R < \mu_k < \mu_s \)

Effects of Friction

Effect Illustration
Friction between two surfaces will make the surfaces warm If we rub our palms together, they will be warm because friction produces heat.
Friction tends to prevent one surface from moving across another surface easily. We find it hard to pull or push a heavy object because friction opposes the motion.
Friction causes wear and tear If we rub a eraser against a surface, the eraser becomes smaller.
Friction makes a moving object slow down and stop. If we roll a marble on the floor, it moves and gradually stops.
This is because there is friction between the marble and the floor.
Friction enables an object to stay in a stationary position.

Factors that affect friction

Affect Illustration
Friction depends on the condition of the surfaces that are rubbing against each other. Smooth surfaces cause smaller friction,while rough surfaces cause greater friction.
This is the reason why we find it hard to push or pull a heavy box along a rough surface than along a smooth surface.
The type of surface affects the distance moved by the object. If we roll a marble on different surfaces, it moves the furthest on the smoothest surface and the slowest on the roughest surface.
Friction also depends on the weight of an object. A heavier object exerts a greater frictional force.

Friction is a necessary Evil

If there is no friction between any two surfaces in contact, then the present day civilization will be no more as we cannot even walk properly on the earth.
There will be no fabrics and garments, no construction of buildings and no furniture, even no cooking and no vehicles.
So friction is necessary. But too much friction causes a heavy loss of energy, wear and tear of machinery.
Thus, the efficiency of machines will decrease tremendously. So it is right said that friction is a necessary evil.

Advantages of Friction

Advantages of friction Disadvantages of friction
Friction allows us to walk or run without slipping. Friction causes surfaces which are touching each other to wear out.
If there is no friction, we will not be able to walk or run on the ground. For example, the sole of shoes and tyres of cars or bicycles are worn out as a result of friction.
Friction also allows vehicles to travel on the roads safely. Worn - out tyres are dangerous because they can slide and skid easily causing accidents to occur.
The brake system in vehicles makes use of friction to slow down or stop the vehicles. Friction produces heat in machines. This heat can damage some parts of the machines.
Friction enables us to hold things because it prevents the object from moving. Friction also causes wasting of energy. Greater energy is needed to overcome the force of friction to make things move.
Friction enables us to sharpen a knife and other instruments.

Ways to Reduce Friction

The several ways to reduce friction are

  1. By using rollers or ball bearings because they reduce the surfaces that are touching each other.

  2. By using lubricants such as oil, wax or grease.

  3. By using talcum powder or air cushion.

  4. By smoothening the surfaces in contact.

  5. By using an aerodynamic shape.

Example 7:

Question:
Why horse has to pull the cart harder during first few steps of motion?

Solution:
The limiting friction is more than the dynamic friction. During the first few steps, the cart is moving slowly and limiting friction operates.
So, the horse has to pull harder during the first few steps.
Later, the dynamic friction comes to play and it becomes easy to pull the cart.

Question:

Solution:
The friction between rubber and road is less, than between iron and road.
Therefore, it is easy to ply a cart with rubber tyres, than with iron tyres.
