1 In the metallurgical processes it is easier to reduce sulphide and carbonate ore. No, it is easier to reduce oxide ore as compared to its sulphide and carbonate ore.
2 Roasting is used for dehydrating an ore. Calcination is a process of heating in the absence of air. Usually carbonate ores or ores containing water are calcinated to drive out carbonate and moisture impurities. Hence dehydrates an ore.

Introduction

About 97% of the Universe (by mass) is made up of hydrogen and helium. The next most common elements are oxygen, iron, nitrogen and carbon. The most abundant metal in the Earth's crust is aluminium, followed by iron, calcium, sodium and magnesium. Less common metals like copper, silver, gold and uranium are concentrated in areas which allow them to be profitably mined. Most metals occur in combination with other elements as ores. Unreactive metals like silver, gold and platinum exist in the free or native form. Sometimes, iron and copper also occur naturally in their elemental state. Metallurgy is the chemistry that deals with the extracting metals from their ores.

Physical Processes

  1. Crushing and Grinding
    The ore is crushed into a fine powder in big jaw crushers.

  2. Concentration of the Ore :
    The process of removal of gangue from the ore is called concentration or ore dressing.

  3. Gravity Separation or Hydraulic washing
    The differences in densities of the metallic ore and the gangue particles are used. The crushed ore is washed against a stream of water on a vibrating, grooved, sloping table. The lighter impurities are washed away, while the heavier ore particles remain behind in the grooves

Froth Flotation Process

The different wetting characteristics of the ore particles and gangue with oil and water are used. The ore slurry is mixed with a froth (usually pine oil) and a collector (usually ethyl xanthate) in a large tank. The collector makes the ore particles water repeE. The whole mixture is agitated with air. The oil coated ore, being lighter than the water coated impurities, forms a froth and rises to the surface of the tank. It is then skimmed off. The impurities cling to the water and settle down.

Electromagnetic Separation

Magnetic properties of the ores are used in this process. The powdered ore is placed on a leather conveyor belt which passes over two rollers, one of which is magnetic. As the magnetic ore particles come over the roller, they are attracted by it and fall down. The nonmagnetic impurities form a separate pile at a distance due to the high speed of the moving belt.

Chemical Processes

Roasting: Ore converted into oxide

The concentrated ore is strongly heated in excess of air.

Calcination : Ore converted into oxide.

The concentrated ore is heated in absence of air at a temperature, insufficient to melt the ore.

Reduction of the Metallic Oxide to the free Metal

The metallic oxide obtained either from the roasted ore or by calcination is reduced to the metal. Depending upon the position of the metal in the activity series, the method of reduction may be electrolytic or by the use of an agent like coke or carbon monoxide.

Reduction by Ore

  1. By Electrolysis:
    Oxides of metals from potassium to aluminium in the activity series.
    \( 2Al_2O_3 \rightarrow 4Al + 3O_2 \)

  2. Coke as the reducing agent:
    zinc oxide
    \( ZnO + C \rightarrow Zn + CO \)

  3. Carbon monoxide as the reducing agent:
    Iron oxide
    \( Fe_2O_3 + 3CO \rightarrow 2Fe + 3CO_2 \)

Refining of the metal

Metals obtained by the above processes contain impurities such as :

  1. Presence of other metals, nonmetals like silicon or phosphorus.

  2. Unreduced oxides and sulphides of the metal.

The method of purification used for a particular metal depends upon the following factors :

  1. Nature of the metal.

  2. Nature of the impurities present in it.

  3. Purpose for which the metal is to be used.

Accordingly, the following processes are used :

Liquation

This method is used to refine metals having a low melting point, e.g., lead and tin.

Distillation

Used to refine volatile metals like mercury and zinc which contain nonvolatile impurities.

Oxidation

Used to refine metals containing volatile impurities, which are easily oxidised, e.g., pig iron.

Electro-refining

This is an economical and effective method for purifying metals, e.g., copper, aluminium, lead

Aluminium

Aluminium is a highly reactive metal, belonging to the III A group of the periodic table. In nature, aluminium is found in the form of its oxide in its ores.

Ores

Bauxite \( Al_2O_3.2H_2O \)
Corundum \( Al_2O_3 \)
Cryolite \( Na_3AlF_6 \)

Metallurgy of Aluminium:

Aluminium is mostly extracted from its bauxite ore.
Dressing of ore: The ore is crushed and pulverised.

Concentration of Ore:

The bauxite ore contains ferric oxide and silica as impurities. It is first concentrated by gravity separating where silica is removed followed by the removal of ferric oxide impurities by the process of magnetic separation. The ore is then concentrated by chemical process. There are two chemical processes that are generally followed.

  1. Bayer's process

  2. Hall's process

\( \begin{align} Al_2O_3.2H_2O + Na_2CO_3 \rightarrow 2NaAlO_2 + 2H_2O + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] 2NaAlO_2 + 3H_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 + 2Al(OH)_3 \end{align} \)

Conversion to Oxide:

Aluminium hydroxide obtained by calcination gives alumina.
\( 2Al(OH)_3 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Al_2O_3 + 3H_2O \)
Alumina so formed is highly stable and has very high thermal resistance. It is therefore subjected to electrolytic reduction.

Electrolytic Reduction

Electrolysis is carried out in fused state. Since alumina has very high thermal resistance. it doesn't melt easily. Hence, cryolite and fluorspar are added to alumina to decrease the melting point and increase the electrical conductivity.
The fused electrolyte is taken in an iron taken lined with gas carbon which acts cathode. A number or parallel carbon rods dipped in the electrolyte act as anode. In order to prevent the loss of heat and burning of carbon electrodes, the electrolyte is covered with coke.
The fused electrolyte contains alumina, cryolite and fluospar in their ionised states. On the passage of current, the ions move towards their respective electrodes.
\( \begin{align} Al_2O_3 \rightarrow 2Al^{+3} + 3O^{-2} \\\\[3pt] Na_3AlF_6 \rightarrow 3Na^+ + Al^{+3} + 6F^- \\\\[3pt] CaF_2 \rightarrow C^{+2} + 2F^- \end{align} \)
The anions, \( O^{2-} \) and \( F^- \) move towards the anode. Oxide ion is oxidized to nascent oxygen which combines with carbon forming carbon dioxide.
At anode: \( \begin{align} O^{-2} â€” 2e^- \rightarrow [O] \\\\[3pt] C + 2[O] \rightarrow CO_2 \end{align} \)
The cations, \( Al^{+3}, \ Ca^{+2} \) and \( Na^+ \) move towards the cathode. Aluminium ion being the least electropositive metal, gets reduced forming aluminium metal.
At cathode: \( Al^{+3} + 3e^- \rightarrow Al \)
The temperature in the tank being higher than the melting point of aluminium the aluminium formed is in the molten state, and settles at the bottom of the tank from where it is collected from time to time. Aluminium extracted by the above process is 99.5% pure and is further refined by electrolytic refining (Hoope's electrolysis).

Electrolysis Refining: (Hoope's Electrolysis)

The electrolyte is composed of aluminium fluoride (36%), cryolite (18%) and calcium fluoride (16%) which forms the middle layer. The lower layer is the impure metal alloyed with copper and serves as the anode. On passing current, the aluminium ions from the middle layer go towards the cathode, get discharged there and form the upper layer of pure aluminium while an equivalent amount of aluminium passes into the middle layer from the bottom layer. Pure aluminium is collected from the top layer.

Uses

  1. Aluminium is used in making house hold appliances.

  2. It is used in electric transmission.

  3. It is used in packing food stuffs.

  4. As aluminium is a self protective metal, when iron is coated with a layer of aluminium it can be prevented from rusting.

  5. Aluminium powder along with ammonium nitrate can be used as an explosive.

  6. Aluminium being highly electropositive, is used in the extraction of the metals.

  7. Aluminium is used in reducing many organic compounds.

  8. Aluminium can displace certain metals from the metal oxides. The reaction being highly exothermic forms the basic principle of thermite welding.

Zinc

Zinc is a transition metal belonging to the II B group of the periodic table. It is highly reactive and found in a combined state in nature.

Ores

Zinc blend ZnS ;
Calamine \( ZnCO_3 \) ;
Zincite ZnO .

Metallurgy of Zinc

Zinc is generally extracted from zinc blend (ZnS) or calamine \( ZnCO_3 \) .

Crushing

The ore is crushed using ball mills and then pulverised. The pulverised ore is concentrated. The concentration process depends upon the type of ore.

Concentration

Gravity separation process is followed for oxide ore (zincite) and carbonate ore (calamine) and froth flotation process is employed for sulphide ore (zinc blend). If iron oxide is present as an impurity, it is separated by magnetic separation process.

Conversion to oxide:

In the case of sulphide ore, it is converted to oxide by roasting.
\( 2 ZnS + 3O_2 \xrightarrow{heat} 2ZnO + 2SO_2 \)
The carbonate ore is subjected to calcination where it undergoes decomposition to oxide giving out carbon dioxide gas.
\( ZnCO_3 \xrightarrow{\Delta} ZnO + CO_2 \) ,

Reduction of metal oxide to metal using coke

The powdered zinc oxide is mixed with charcoal and made into small briquettes by pressing. This is taken in the vertical retort where, due to the high temperature, carbon reduces zinc oxide to zinc and itself gets oxidised to carbon monoxide.
\( ZnO + C \xrightarrow{1400^{\circ}C} Zn + CO \)
The zinc formed in the vapour state escapes out along with carbon monoxide. These vapours are collected in the condenser where zinc condenses and is collected from time to time while carbon monoxide remains as gas and is used for further heating of the retort. The zinc obtained by this process is 97% pure and is called zinc spelter.

Refining or Purification

The spelter zinc obtained contains both volatile impurities (Cd and As) and nonvolatile impurities (Fe and C) and can be refined by two methods.

Distillation under low pressure:

The spelter is heated at a controlled temperature and the volatile impurities get vapourised. The metal is then strongly heated, when zinc changes to vapours. These vapours are condensed to obtain molten zinc which is poured into earthen moulds where it is cooled and solidified.

Electrolytic Method:

A block of impure metal (spelter) is taken as anode and a thin strip of pure metal is taken as cathode. Acidified saturated zinc sulphate solution is taken as the electrolyte. On passing current, zinc from the anode dissolved in the electrolyte in the form of \( Zn^{+2} \) ions, goes towards the cathode, gets discharged there and is deposited on the cathode.
At anode: \( Zn \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + 2e^- \)
At cathode: \( Zn^{+2} + 2e^- \rightarrow Zn \)
As electrolysis proceeds, the anode gets thinner and cathode (pure metal), thicker.

Uses of Zinc

  1. Galvanization

  2. In dry cells

  3. Reducing agent

  4. Alloys

Iron

Iron is the metal found in great abundance in nature. It is highly reactive and is hardly available in free state. Iron exists in the combined form in nature in various minerals among which oxide and sulphide ores are the most significant ones.

Ores

Haematite \( Fe_2O_3 \)
Limonite \( Fe_2O_3.H_2O \)
Magnetite \( Fe_2O_4 \)
Siderite \( FeCO_3 \)
Iron pyrites \( FeS_2 \)
Copper pyrites \( CuFeS_2 \)

Metallurgy or Iron

Iron is generally extracted from haematite, limonite, siderite. Among these haematite and limonite are the most preferred ores.

Dressing and Concentration of Ore

The ore is crushed to make small pieces from larger chunks. The crushed ore is concentrated by the process of magnetic separation and further concentration is done by gravity separation process.

Conversion to Oxide

The concentrated ore is converted to oxide form by the process of roasting which involves heating the ore in the presence of excess air.
On roasting, the carbonate ore decomposes to oxide giving out carbon dioxide. The process is also associated with other advantages like

  1. The removal of moisture and other volatile impurities.

  2. The removal of impurities like sulphur, phosphorous, arsenic, antimony in the form of gaseous oxides.

  3. The conversion of ferrous oxide to ferric oxide.

  4. Inducing porosity in the mass, thus rendering easy reduction.

Reduction of Oxide to Metal

The reduction of oxide to metal is carried out with carbon in a blast furnace by the process called smelting.

Smelting in Blast Furnace

Blast furnace is a huge chimney like structure about 30 mts high and 8 mts is diameter. It is made of iron plates and lined internally with refractory bricks. On top, it has a double cup and cone arrangement. Below this, there is an outlet for waste gases. A hearth is present at the bottom to collect cast iron and slag, and has separate outlets for each. The compressed air is blown into the furnace through pipes called tyres which are placed above the hearth. The ore is mixed with coke, and limestone approximately in the ratio of 8 : 4 : 1. This mixture is called charge. The charge is lowered into the furnace from the top through the cup and cone arrangement and various reactions take place in the furnace at different levels.

Lower region: \( (1500^{\circ}C - 2000^{\circ}C) \) :

The reaction, being highly exothermic increases the temperature in this zone upto \( 2000^{\circ}C \) .
\( C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 97 kcal \) . This region is also called the combustion zone

Middle region or fusion zone \( (1500^{\circ}C - 1000^{\circ}C) \) :

The carbon dioxide formed in the lower layer moves to the middle layer where it is reduced to carbon monoxide.
\( 2 C + O_2 \rightarrow 2CO + 39 k cal \)
At such a high temperature, the limestone added decomposes to calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.
\( CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2 - 43 k cal \)
The calcium oxide (quick lime) formed reacts with impurities like sand in the reaction and converts them to slag.
\( CaO + SiO_2 \rightarrow CaSiO_3 \)
The slag, calcium silicate goes towards the hearth present at the bottom of the furnace. As the reactions in the middle region are endothermic, the temperature of this Zone decreases.

Upper region or reduction zone \( (1000^{\circ}C - 500^{\circ}C) \) :

The carbon monoxide formed in the middle region moves to the upper region where it reduces the iron oxide to iron.
\( \begin{align} FeO + CO \xrightarrow{750 - 600^{\circ}C} Fe + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] Fe_2O_3 + 3CO \xrightarrow{600 - 400^{\circ}C} 2Fe + 3CO_2 \end{align} \)
The iron formed here melts due to the high temperature and then due to its high density settles down. Both the slag (calcium silicate) and iron settle down in hearth. Iron being heavier forms the bottom layer with slag on top. From here iron and slag are tapped through separate outlets. Iron thus obtained in called pig iron. Solid pig iron is remelted in a vertical furnace known as cupola and thereby gets refined. Then it is poured into suitable moulds of required shape. This remelted pig iron is called cast iron.

Conversion of Cast Iron to Wrought Iron

Wrought iron is the purest form of iron. Apart from iron, it has very small quantities of carbon (0.2%), silicon (0.15%), phosphorous (0.02%), sulphur (0.04%) and manganese (0.05%). It is the refined form of cast iron. It is prepared by heating cast iron in a reverberatory furnace. The furnace is lined internally with iron oxide. This oxidises carbon in the cast iron to gaseous oxides of carbon. The other impurities are slagged of using lime as flux. Heavy rollers are made to roll over the metal obtained, where the slag is squeezed out.

Conversion of Cast Iron to Steel

Cast iron, the most impure form of iron is converted to steel by Bessemer process. Steel is an alloy of iron. Small amounts of carbon and other metals like chromium, manganese, vanadium, nickel are added to iron to impart special properties required for a variety of uses. The carbon content of steel varies from 0.1% to 1.5% depending on its use. Cast iron is converted to steel by Bessemer process. This process is carried out in a Bessemer convertor.

Process of Bessemerisation

At first, the converter is tilted to a horizontal position and the charge of molten cast iron is introduced into it. Then it is brought back to the upright position and a blast of air is passed through tyres present at the bottom of the furnace. Carbon and other impurities present in cast iron combine with oxygen to form their respective oxides.
\( \begin{align} C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2; \ \ \ \ CO_2 + C \rightarrow 2CO; \ \ \ \ S + O_2 \rightarrow SO_2 \\\\[3pt] P_4 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 2P_2O_5 \text{(in basic process)} ; \ \ \ \ Si + O_2 \rightarrow SiO_2 \end{align} \)
\( \begin{align} MnO + SiO_2 \rightarrow MnSiO_3 \\\\[3pt] 6CaO + P_4O_{14} \rightarrow 2Ca_3(PO_4)_2 \text{(slag in basic process)} \end{align} \)

Open hearth process for making special type of steel

The shape of the furnace is like a hearth, the top of which is open. The charge is pig iron and steel scrap. The process can be carried out in two ways.

Acid process:

In this process, the furnace is lined with acid refractories, i.e., silica

Basic process:

In this process, the furnace is lined with magnesite or dolomite. The temperature on the top of the hearth is generated by hot fuel gas which burns on the hearth and produces a temperature of \( 1600^{\circ}C \) to \( 1650^{\circ}C \) . The final composition of the steel is adjusted by adding ferro silicon of ferro manganese alloy. It is a very lengthy process and takes about 5 to 15 hours.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Why gangue is removed from an ore ?

Solution:
A gangue

  1. interferes with the process of extraction,

  2. makes the metal impure, and

  3. increases the cost of production of the pure metal.

Hence, all efforts are made to remove the gangue from an ore.

Example 2:

Question:
What is leaching ?

Solution:
Leaching: This is a chemical method of enriching an ore. The desired metal compound present in the ore is leached, i.e., dissolved out, by a suitable reagent and the gangue is left behind. The desired compound is then precipitated or obtained from the solution by a suitable method.

Question:

The above apparatus is used to study the reactions between metals X, Y and Z with oxygen. The metals were first heated until red-hot before heating the potassium manganate (VII). The results of the experiment are shown in the table below.

Metal Observation
X Glowed weakly; black residue
Y Burned brightly; Brown residue
Z Glowed brightly; yellow residue

  1. How was oxygen produced in this experiment?

  2. Based on the observations in the table above, arrange the metals X, Y and Z in order of increasing reactivity.

  3. If each atom of metal Y lost three electrons in its reaction with oxygen, write an equation for the reaction of metal Y with oxygen.

  4. The residue obtained were dissolved in dilute nitric acid and the colours of the resulting solutions are shown below:

Black residue Blue solution
Brown residue Brown solution
Yellow residue Colourless solution

Give the formula of the ion responsible for each of the colours observed.

Solution:

  1. On heating potassium manganate (VII), it decomposed to liberate oxygen gas.

  2. X, Z, Y
    The more reactive the metal, the more vigorous is the reaction.

  3. \( 4Y(s) + 3O_2(g) \rightarrow 2Y_2O_3(s) \)

    The brown solution is due to the presence of \( Fe^{3+} (aq) \) ions.
    The colourless solution is due to the presence of \( Pb^{2+} (aq) \) ions.

Example 4:

Question:
A piece of zinc metal was dipped into a beaker containing aqueous lead (II) nitrate. After a few minutes, a grey solid was formed.

    1. Name the grey solid.

    2. Write an ionic equation to show the formation of the grey solid.

    3. If 0.02 moles of a grey solid was formed, what mass of zinc was dissolved?

  2. Name another metal which can produce the same grey solid when added to aqueous lead (II) nitrate.

  3. Describe what is observed when aqueous ammonia is added slowly to the resulting solution in the beaker.

Solution:

    1. Lead metal

    2. \( Zn(s) + Pb^{2+} (aq) \rightarrow Zn^{2+} (aq) + Pb(s). \)

    3. Mass of zinc dissolved = \( (0.02 \times 65) g = 1.30 g. \)

  2. Any metal higher than lead in the reactivity series, e.g., magnesium.

  3. A little ammonia \( \Rightarrow \) white precipitate.

The white precipitate dissolves in excess ammonia to form a colourless solution.

  1. How can metal X be extracted form its oxide ores?

  2. Write an equation for the reaction between metal X and steam.

  3. Metal Y is extracted from its oxide ore by heating with carbon in a blast furnace.

    1. Why is carbon used for the extraction of metal Y from its ore?

    2. Write an equation for the reaction that occurs in (i).

  4. Metal X is dropped into dilute hydrochloric acid.
    Write an equation to represent the reaction that occurs.

  5. Hydrogen is passed over the heated oxide of metal Y. Will a reaction take place? Explain your answer.

Solution:

  1. By electrolysis of its molten oxides ores.

Example 6:

Question:
Aluminium is extracted from its ore by electrolytic reduction. Iron is extracted from its ore by chemical reduction.

Solution:
The position of aluminium is above that of carbon in the reactivity series. Carbon is not able to reduce aluminium oxide to aluminium. Therefore, aluminium is extracted by electrolysis of its molten oxide. Aluminium ion is reduced at the cathode:
\( Al^{3+} (l) + 3e^- \rightarrow Al(l) \)
Iron is below carbon in the reactivity series. Therefore, carbon can reduce iron oxide to iron.
\( 2Fe_2O_3(s) + 3C(s) \rightarrow 4Fe(s) + 3CO_2(g) \)

Example 7:

Question:
Pure iron is soft, making it malleable and ductile. Steel is hard and tough, making it difficult to mould. Explain.

Solution:
The atoms in pure iron are closely packed and arranged in an orderly manner. This enables the layers of atoms to slide easily over one another. Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon together with some other metals. The presence of foreign atoms disrupts the orderly arrangement. This prevents the layers of atoms from sliding over one another.

Example 8:

Question:
Iron is not found naturally in the form of the free metal. Gold can be found naturally as the free metal. Explain.

Solution:
Iron is much higher than gold in the reactivity series. Hence, iron is much more reactive than gold. Iron will react readily with other elements like oxygen to form compounds. Being at the bottom of the reactivity series indicates that gold is unreactive. Gold does not react readily with other elements to form compounds.
