Introduction

Chemical reactions are the inevitable part of natural phenomenon. Basically they are either one directional reactions or two opposing directional reactions. Whether one directional or two directional all reactions take place with characteristic rates under the given experimental conditions. Therefore, to fully understand a chemical reaction, one shall have to know about certain parameters of the reaction such as: directional nature rate and the nature of the substances. Hence to learn some of these aspects in detail namely the rate of reaction, the factors affecting the rate of reaction. In the case of reactions of "opposing directions" nature, the concept of equilibrium and the factors that affect the extent of equilibrium. Chemical kinetics deals with measurement of reaction rates.
Classification of reactions:

  1. slow reactions

  2. moderate reactions

  3. instantaneous reactions

Rate of reaction

Rate of reaction is the change in molar concentrations of reactants or products in a definite interval of time. The change in the concentration of a given reactant with respect to time can vary as follows:

  1. Concentrations of reactants decrease with time exponentially. Rate of reaction is given negative sign.

  2. Concentrations of products increase with time exponentially - Rate of reaction is given positive sign.

The change in rate of reaction is not uniform throughout. The rate of reaction at a particular instant of time is instantaneous reaction. Instantaneops rate is determined by graphical method. Slope of tangent drawn at a point on the cube gives rate of reaction.

For the reaction \( XA + YB \rightarrow mC + nD \) .
The relation of rates measured wrt A, B, C, D is
\( - \frac{1}{x} \frac{[A]}{t} = - \frac{1}{y} \frac{[B]}{t} = + \frac{1}{m} \frac{ \Delta[C]}{ \Delta t} = + \frac{1}{n} \frac{ \Delta C}{ \Delta t} \)
Average rate of reaction = \( - \frac{ \Delta C}{ \Delta t} \ or \ + \frac{ \Delta C}{ \Delta t} \) . Units of rate of reaction \( mole \ l^{-1} \ s^{-1} \)

Collision Theory

There are number of reactions taking place at room temperature at measurable and moderate rates and these reactions are studied in chemical kinetics. Most of these reactions are molecular in nature. Chemical reaction can be speeded up or slowed down by changing the condition. For e.g., Decay of food materials can be slowed down by preserving them in refrigerators. There are two principle reasons for studying chemical kinetics.

  1. To predict the reaction rate of a particular reaction under specified conditions. This can be useful to alter the conditions according to the requirement and achieve maximum yields of the desired industrial products.

  2. To study the mechanism of the reaction which can help to check with the experimental data.

Chemical reactions take place due to collision among reactant molecules. Every collision doesn't bring a chemical change. Only those collisions causing reaction to take place are called effective collisions. For a collision to be effective, the following two barriers are to be cleared.

  1. Energy barrier
    The minimum amount of energy which the colliding molecules must possess for occurrence of a chemical reaction is called as threshold energy. The minimum amount of energy required for a molecule to participate in a chemical reaction is called activation energy.
    Activation energy = threshold energy - average kinetic energy of reacting molecules.
    Threshold energy = Initial potential energy of reactant molecules + activation energy.

    A collision between high energy molecules results in formation of cluster of molecules which are unstable and have short life. This is called activated complex. This breaks to form new products or reactants.

  2. Orientation barrier
    Apart from energy the effectiveness of collisions of reactant molecules is the proper manner the reaction to occur.

Factors affecting rate of reaction

  1. Nature of reactants
    Physical slate of reactants gaseous state is more effective than liquid and solid states. Similarly homogeneous state of reactants is more effective than heterogeneous state of reactants.

  2. Physical size of the reactants
    This plays a major role. Powdered reactants are faster than lumps as the surface area increases.

  3. Chemical nature of the reactants
    Chemical nature of the reactants like strong bonds and weaker bonds, number of bonds to be broken etc., can determine the rate of reaction.

  4. Concentration of reactants
    Higher the concentration, rate of reaction increases considerably.

  5. Effect of temperature
    Increase in temperature increases the rate of reaction normally as it increases number of collisions and activation energy

  6. Presence of a positive catalyst
    Rate of reaction and decreases the activation energy. Decreases the activation energy of reactants and increases reaction rate.

Rate law

Quantitative estimation of change in rate of reaction with concentration is given by rate law or rate expression.

  1. For single step reactions, the rate law follows stoichiometry of the chemical equation.

  2. For multi step reactions, the rate law follows stoichiometry of the rate determining step of the reaction.
    The slowest step of the reaction is called rate determining step.
    \( 2A + B \rightarrow P \) single step reaction \( r = K[A]^2[B] \)
    order of reactant A = 2, order of reactant B = 1
    Overall order = 2 + 1 = 3

Order of reaction

Order of reaction is the sum of the powers to which the concentrations are raised to the powers of their coefficients.
\( r = K[A]^m[B]^n \) ; order = m + n
Where K is rate constant
If concentrations of reactions is taken as unity, r = K. Rate of reaction is called specific reaction rate. Units of rate constant depends on the order of the reaction. Rate law is experimentally determined and useful for production of mechanism of reaction.

Chemical Equilibrium

In a chemical reaction, the chemical substances react chemically and give the final chemical substances. The initial starting chemical substances are called the reactants and the final chemical substances formed are called the products or resultants. Depending on the chemical nature of the products and the reaction conditions, the products formed may react and give back the initial substances or may not react at all. Reversible reactions: are the reactions which can take place, under same conditions, in both the forward and the backward directions, are called reversible reactions. Even in a reversible reaction, initially there will be reactants only. These react and give the products. At the beginning of the reaction, therefore, there will be no products. But with increase in reaction time

The state or stage at which the rate of the forward reaction is equal to the rate of the backward reaction in a reversible reaction is known as the equilibrium state or stage.
The physical properties such as colour, density etc., also do not change. The forward and the backward reactions take place with equal rates. This equilibrium is, therefore, known as dynamic equilibrium.

Characteristics of chemical equilibrium:

  1. Both the forward and the reverse reactions continue to take place.

  2. The rate of forward reaction is equal to the rate of the backward reaction.

  3. The properties such as pressure, concentration, density, colour etc., remain unchanged with time.

  4. Addition of a catalyst to the reaction does not alter the position of the equilibrium. It only speeds up the attainment of the equilibrium.

  5. The same chemical equilibrium can be reached by carrying out the reaction starting with the reactants or by carrying out the reaction starting with the products.

  6. Change in pressure or concentrations of the reactants or of the products may change the position of the equilibrium.

Types of chemical equilibria

Chemical equilibria are classified as homogeneous and heterogeneous equilibria depending on the physical states of the reactants and the products.

Homogeneous equilibrium

The equilibrium is considered homogeneous if the physical states of the participating substances are the same.
\( \begin{align} H_{2(g)} + I_{2(g)} \rightleftarrows 2HI_{(g)} \\\\[3pt] N_{2(g)} + 3H_{2(g)} \rightleftarrows 2NH_{3(g)} \end{align} \)

Heterogeneous equilibrium

If the physical states of all or some of the participating substances are different the equilibrium is considered as heterogeneous equilibrium.
Some examples are
\( \begin{align} CaCO_{3(s)} \overset{ \Delta}{ \rightleftarrows} CaO_{(s)} + CO_{2(g)} \\\\[3pt] NH_4HS_{(s)} \overset{ \Delta}{ \rightleftarrows} NH_{3(g)} + H_2S_{(g)} \end{align} \)

Law of mass action

At any instant the rate of a reaction at a temperature is proportional directly to the product of the molar concentrations of the reactants at the instant.
Equilibrium constant is the ratio of product of concentrations of products to the product of concentrations of reactants all of them raised to the powers of their coefficients.
\( \begin{align} XA + YB \rightleftarrows mC + nD \\\\[3pt] K_C = \frac{[C]^m[D]^n}{[A]^X[B]^Y} \end{align} \)
Equilibrium position is actual concentration of reactants and products at equilibrium. It depends on the initial concentrations of reactants and products. \( K_C \) changes with temperature, direction of reaction and stoichiometry. Concentrations of solids and pure liquids are neglected while counting \( K_C \) . Units of \( K_C - (mole \ l^{-1}) \ Dn_P - Dn_R \) . \( Dn_P \) and \( Dn_R \) are difference in number of moles of products and reactants. \( K_C \) values help for prediction of extent of completion of reaction and there by yield of the product.

Lechatlier's principle

If a chemical reaction at equilibrium is subjected to a change in temperature, pressure or concentration (of the reactants or products) that govern the chemical equilibrium the equilibrium position shifts in the direction in which this change is reduced or nullified.

Factors effecting the chemical equilibrium

  1. Increase in concentration of reactants or decrease in concentration of products shifts the equilibrium in first direction.

  2. Increase in pressure favours endothermic reaction and decrease in temperature favours exothermic reaction.

  3. Increase in pressure favours the reaction in a direction in which there is decrease in number of moles.

  4. For the equilibrium reactions where there is no change in number of moles there is no effect of pressure.

  5. For the equilibrium reactions where there is no change in number of moles there is no effect of pressure.

  6. Application of lechatlier's principle to Haber's process(synthesis of ammonia)
    \( N_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftarrows 2NH_3 + 92KJ \)

Favourable conditions:

  1. LOw temperature

  2. High pressure( \( \Delta n \) = -ve exothermic)

  3. Addition of \( N_2 \) or \( O_2 \) or removal of \( NH_3 \) .
    Optimum conditions : 200 atm pressure.
    725 K - 775 K temp.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
A reaction is represented by following stoichiometric representation:
\( A + 3B \longrightarrow 2C + D \)
With reference to the equation given explain the terms given below:

  1. \( \frac{d[A]}{dt} \)

  2. \( \frac{d[C]}{dt} \)

  3. Rate of reaction w.r.t conc. of A, B, C and D.

  4. Units of rate

Solution:

  1. \( \frac{d[A]}{dt} \) = rate of consumption of A

  2. \( \frac{d[C]}{dt} \) = rate of formation of C

  3. Rate of reaction = \( \frac{d[A]}{dt} = - \frac{1}{3}. \frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}. \frac{d[C]}{dt} \)
    - sign signifies the decrease in concentration with time.

  4. Unit of rate is always conc./time i.e, \( mol \ l^{-1} \ s^{-1} \) .

Example 2:

Question:
Calculate the average rate of reaction at different time intervals.

Time(in min) 0 5 10 15 20
Conc. of reactant \( mol \ L^{-1} \) 80 40 20 10 5

Solution:
Average rate = \( \frac{ \Delta [conc.]}{ \Delta t} \)

  1. after 5 mins, Rate = \( \frac{80 - 40}{5} = 8 \ mol \ l^{-1}min^{-1} \)

  2. after 10 mins, Rate = \( \frac{40 - 20}{5} = 4 \ mol \ l^{-1}min^{-1} \)

  3. after 15 mins, Rate = \( \frac{20 - 10}{5} = 2 \ mol \ l^{-1}min^{-1} \)

  4. after 20 mins, Rate = \( \frac{10 - 5}{5} = 1 \ mol \ l^{-1}min^{-1} \)

Example 3:

Question:
A reaction takes place in a single step. The rates of reactions of various reactants and products can be related as
\( \frac{1}{3} \frac{ \Delta [C]}{ \Delta t} = \frac{ \Delta [D]}{ \Delta t} = - \frac{1 \Delta [A]}{2 \Delta t} = - \frac{ \Delta [B]}{ \Delta t} \)

  1. What is the stoichiometric equation?

  2. Write rate law for the reaction.

  3. Give units of rate constant

  4. Find out order w.r.t each reactant and also over all order of the reaction.

  5. Concentration of each reactant is tripled by keeping the other one constant. What is the change in rate of reaction?

Solution:

  1. Stoichiometric equation \( 2A + B \rightleftarrows 3C + D \)

  2. Rate law \( r = k[A]^2[B] \)

  3. \( k = \frac{r}{[A]^2[B]} = \frac{mole \ l^{-1}s^{-1}}{mole^3l^{-3}} = mole^{-2}l^2s^{-1} \)

  4. Order w.r.t A = 2, order w.r.t B = 1, overall order = 3

  5. When concentration of A is tripled, the rate of reaction is increased by 9 times. When concentration of B is tripled, the rate of reaction is increased by 3 times.

  6. When volume is increased by 3 times, the concentration A and B are reduced by 3 times. Rate of reaction is reduced by 1/27 times.

Example 4:

Question:
For the reaction, \( 2A + B \rightarrow 2C + 3D \) , the rate of change in concentration of C is \( 1.0 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \) . Calculate the rate of reaction and rates of change in concentration of A, B and D.

Solution:
Rate of reaction
\( \begin{align} = - \frac{1}{2}. \frac{d[A]}{dt} = - \frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}. \frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{1}{3}.\frac{d[D]}{dt} \\\\[3pt] Given, \ \frac{d[C]}{dt} = 1.0 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \therefore Rate \ of \ reaction = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.0 = 0.5 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \therefore - \frac{d[A]}{dt} = \frac{d[C]}{dt} = 1.0 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] - \frac{d[B]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}. \frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.0 = 0.5 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \frac{d[D]}{dt} = \frac{3}{2}. \frac{d[C]}{dt} = \frac{3}{2} \times 1.0 = 1.5 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
For the reaction, \( N_{2(g)} + 3H_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2NH_{3(g)} \) , under certain conditions of pressure and temperature of the reactants, the rate of formation of \( NH_3 \) is \( 0.001 \ kg \ h^{-1} \) . Calculate the rate of reaction for \( N_2 \) and \( H_2 \) .

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Rate = \frac{d[N_2]}{dt} = - \frac{1}{3}. \frac{d[H_2]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{d[NH_3]}{dt} \\\\[3pt] \frac{d[NH_3]}{dt} = 0.001 \ kg \ h^{-1} = \frac{1 \times 10^{-3}}{17}mol \ h^{-1} \\\\[3pt] - \frac{d[N_2]}{dt} = \frac{1 \times 10^{-3}}{17 \times 2} = 0.029 \times 10^{-3} \times 28 = 8.12 \times 10^{-4}kg \ h^{-1} \\\\[3pt] - \frac{d[H_2]}{dt} = \frac{3}{2} \frac{d[NH_3]}{dt} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-3}}{2 \times 17} mol \ h^{-1} = 0.088 \times 10^{-3}mol \ h^{-1} \\\\[3pt] = 0.088 \times 10^{-3} \times 2 = 1.76 \times 10^{-4} kg \ h^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
A 22.4 litre flask contains 0.76 mm of ozone at \( 25^{\circ}C \) . calculate:

  1. The concentration of oxygen atoms needed so that the reaction \( O + O_3 \rightarrow 2O_2 \) having rate constant equal to \( 1.5 \times 10^7 L \ mol^{-1}s^{-1} \) can proceed with a rate of \( 0.15 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1}. \)

  2. The rate of information of oxygen under this condition.

Solution:

  1. On the basis of unit of rate constant reaction is of II order.
    \( \begin{align} Rate \ of \ reaction = K[O_3][O] \ or \ 0.15 = 1.5 \times 10^7 \\\\[3pt] \left[ \frac{0.76}{0.821 \times 298 \times 760} \right][O] \left( \therefore \frac{n}{V} \ for \ O_3 = \frac{P}{RT} = \frac{0.76}{0.0821 \times 298 \times 760} \right) \\\\[3pt] \therefore \frac{n}{V} = [O] = 2.45 \times 10^{-4} \end{align} \)

  2. \( \begin{align} \therefore \frac{P}{RT} \ for \ O = 2.45 \times 10^{-4} \left( \therefore \frac{n}{V} = \frac{P}{RT} \right) \\\\[3pt] or \ P_O = 2.45 \times 10^{-4} \times 0.0821 \times 298 \\\\[3pt] \ \ \ \ = 5.99 \times 10^{-3}atm \\\\[3pt] \ \ \ \ = 5.99 \times 10^{-3} \times 760 \ mm = 4.5 \ mm \end{align} \)
    Also rate of reaction
    \( \begin{align} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{d[O_2]}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \ rate \ of \ formation \ of \ O_2 \\\\[3pt] \therefore Rate \ of \ formation \ of \ O_2 = 2 \times 0.15 = 0.30 \ mol \ l^{-1}s^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Question:
Decomposition of \( H_2O_2 \) is a first order reaction. A solution of \( H_2O_2 \) labelled as 20 volumes was left open. Due to this some \( H_2O_2 \) decomposed. To determine the new volume strength after 6 hours, 10 ml of this solution was diluted to 100 mL. 10 mL of this diluted solution was titrated against 25 mL of 0.025 M \( KMnO_4 \) acidified solution. Calculate the rate constant for decomposition of \( H_2O_2 \) .
\( \left(Take \ K = \frac{2.303}{t}. log_{10} \frac{a}{(a - x)} \right) \)
Where a = initial concentration of reactant (a - x)= concentration left at time t.

Solution:
At t = 0
\( \begin{align} \therefore 22400 \ ml \ O_2 = 68 \ g \ H_2O_2 \\\\[3pt] \therefore 20 \ ml \ O_2 = \frac{68 \times 20}{22400}g \ H_2O_2 \ in \ 1 \ ml \\\\[3pt] = \frac{68 \ times 20 \times 10 \times 100}{22400} = 35.71 \end{align} \)
At = 6 hrs
molar equivalent of \( H_2O_2 \) in 10 ml diluted sol \( = 25 \times 0.025 \times 5 = 3.125 \)
molar equivalent of \( H_2O_2 \) in 10 ml conc. sol \( = 3.125 \times 10 = 31.25 \)
\( K = \frac{2.303}{6} \ log_{10} \frac{35.71}{31.25} = 0.022 \ h^{-1} \)

Example 8:

Question:
The equilibrium constant for the reaction,
\( \begin{align} N_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)} \rightleftarrows 2NO_{(g)} \\\\[3pt] NO_{(g)} + \frac{1}{2} O_2 \rightleftarrows NO_{2(g)} \end{align} \)
are \( K_1 \) and \( K_2 \) respectively. Find the equilibrium constant \( K_3 \) for the equilibrium \( N_{2(g)} + 2O_{2(g)} \rightleftarrows 2NO_{2(g)}. \)

Solution:
Given,
\( N_2 + O_2 \rightleftarrows 2NO \)
\( \begin{align} The \ equilibrium \ constant
K_1 = \frac{[NO]^2}{[O_2][N_2]} .....(1) \\\\[3pt] Given \ NO + \frac{1}{2} \rightleftarrows NO_2 \\\\[3pt] The \ equilibrium \ constant \\\\[3pt] K_2 = \frac{[NO_2]}{[NO][O_2]^{ \frac{1}{2}}} .....(2) \\\\[3pt] Given \ N_2 + 2O_2 \rightleftarrows 2NO_2 \\\\[3pt] The \ equilibrium \ constant \\\\[3pt] K_3 = \frac{[NO_2]^2}{[O_2]^2[N_2]} \\\\[3pt] Multiply \ and \ divide \ by \ [NO]^2 \\\\[3pt] K_3 = \frac{[NO_2][NO_2]}{\left[[O_2]^{ \frac{1}{2}} \right]^4[N_2]} \frac{[NO]^2}{[NO]^2} \\\\[3pt] From \ (1) \ and \ (2) \\\\[3pt] K_3 = K_1 K_2^2 \end{align} \)

Example 9:

Question:
The equilibrium concentration of X, Y and \( YX_2 \) are 4, 2 and 2 moles respectively for the equilibrium \( 2X + Y \rightleftarrows YX_2 \) . Find the value of \( K_c. \)

Solution:
Equilibrium concentration
\( \begin{align} \underset{4}{2X} \ \ \ \ \ \underset{2}Y \longrightarrow \underset{2}{YX_2} \\\\[3pt] K_c = \frac{[YX_2]}{[Y][X]^2} = \frac{2}{2 \times 4^2} = \frac{1}{16} = 0.0625 \end{align} \)
