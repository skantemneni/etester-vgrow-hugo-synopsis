Introduction

Chlorine, a VII A group element, is the element with highest electron affinity. Chlorine does not occur in free state due to its high reactive nature, high electronegativity and salt forming tendency. Chlorine combines with metals as well as non metals forming the corresponding chlorides.
The study of various compounds of chlorine involves in the understanding of compounds like HCl, NaCl and \( CaOCl_2 \)

Sodium Chloride

In the combined state, it is widely distributed in nature as chlorides. Sea water contains 2.6% sodium chloride and is the commercial source of chlorine. Hence it is one of compounds of of chlorine. The extraction of chlorine from NaCl can be done using process of electrolysis which is carried out in specially designed electrolytic cell either by using a porous diaphragm or using a mercury cathode.

Nelson cell (or Diaphragm cell) :

The cell shown in figure below consists of a cathode of perforated steel tube lined inside with asbestos. A graphite rod dipped in NaCl serves as anode. The salt solution is percolated through the porous diaphragm. On electrolysis following reactions occur
\( \begin{align} NaCl_(aq) \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^- \\\\[3pt] H_2O \rightarrow H^+ + OH^- \end{align} \)
At cathode (perforated steel) : \( 2H^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2 \)
At anode (carbon rod) : \( 2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2 + 2e^- \)

The solution left contains \( Na^+ \) and \( OH^- \) ions and about 10 - 15 % NaOH is obtained. It is concentrated under vacuum to 50 % when NaCl (being less soluble) is almost separated out. The filtrate is evaporated to get flakes of NaOH.

Castner - Kellner cell:

In this cell, cathode of Hg is used, which reacts with Na, formed after reduction, to form Na - Hg amalgam.
Components of cell:

Rectangular trough:

Divided into 3 chambers by slate partition. The lower part of these partitions do not touch the bottom but are dipped in Hg. Hg can flow from one compartment to another but NaCl solution can't.

Anode

Two graphite anodes fitted in outer compartments.

Cathode :

Iron cathodes fitted in inner compartments and dipped in dilute NaOH solution

Hg acts as an intermediate electrode :

As cathode in outer compartment while as anode in inner compartments by induction and at Hg cathode. \( D.P._{Na^+} < D.P._H \) . The cell is kept motion to remain Hg in circulation. It is done by eccentric wheel.

On electrolysis of aqueous NaCl solution in the outer compartment, \( Cl_2 \) , is evolved in the graphite anode while \( Na^+ \) ions are discharged at the Hg cathode. The liberated sodium forms amalgam with mercury.
\( NaCl_{(aq)} \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^- \)
At cathode (mercury):
\( \begin{align} 2Na^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow 2Na \\\\[3pt] Na + Hg \rightarrow \underset{sod. amalgam}{Na - Hg} \end{align} \)
At anode(graphite):
\( 2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2 + 2e^- \)

Hydrogen Chloride

Hydrogen chloride was first prepared by Glauber. He called it 'spirit of salt'. It is part of volcanic gases. In solution form, as hydrochloric acid, it is a common laboratory reagent. It colours in the gastric juices of human beings and animals. Hydrochloric acid was an one time known as 'muriatic acid'.
In HCl, Cl is monovalent and shares one of its electrons with H. Due to the appreciable difference in the electronegativities of \( H({\chi}_H = 2.1) \) and \( Cl ({\chi}_{Cl} = 3.0) \) , the bond in HCl is polar covalent.

Preparation

By Synthesis

\( H_2 \) and \( Cl_2 \) combine explosively on being kindled or exposed to light to form HCl.
\( H_2 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{light} 2HCl \)

As a by - product in the chlorination of hydrocarbons

HCl gas is obtained on the chlorination of hydrocarbons, e.g, methane. It is noticed, that one hydrogen atom of methane is replaced at every step in the following reactions.
\( \begin{align} CH_4 + Cl_2 \rightarrow CH_3Cl + HCl \\\\[3pt] CH_3Cl + Cl_2 \rightarrow CH_2Cl_2 + HCl \\\\[3pt] CH_2Cl_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow CHCl_3 + HCl \\\\[3pt] CHCl_3 + Cl_2 \rightarrow CCl_4 + HCl \end{align} \)
The reactions occur in diffused sunlight at room temperature.

By the action of concentrated \( H_2SO_4 \) on metal chlorides : Laboratory preparation

HCl can be prepared by the action of concentrated \( H_2SO_4 \) on metal chlorides or \( NH_4Cl \) . In the laboratory, NaCl is used to prepare HCl. The reaction occurs in two steps. On mild heating, \( NaHSO_4 \) and HCl are obtained. And on strong heating \( NaHSO_4 \) reacts with NaCl to release HCl and form the normal salt \( Na_2SO_4 \) .
\( \begin{align} NaCl + H_2SO_4 \xrightarrow{mild \ heat} NaHSO_4 + HCl \uparrow \\\\[3pt] NaHSO_4 + NaCl \xrightarrow{strong \ heat} Na_2SO_4 + HCl \uparrow \end{align} \)

Note:

Though \( HgCl_2 \) , is a metal chloride, it does not react with \( H_2S0_4 \)

Physical Properties

  1. HC1 is a colourless gas with a pungent odour.

  2. It is neither combustible nor a supporter of combustion.

  3. Its density at STP is \( 1.6392 g l^{-1} \) and it is, therefore, heavier than air.

  4. It is not easily liquefied. The liquid boils at \( - 85^{\circ}C \) .

  5. It is highly soluble in water, 1 volume of water dissolving about 460 volumes of HC1 gas at \( 15^{\circ}C \) . An aqueous solution turns blue litmus red.

  6. It is soluble in organic solvents such as benzene and toluene. These solutions have no effect on litmus.

Chemical Properties

The following reactions of hydrogen chloride are important.

Reaction with metals:

HC1 gas reacts with metals only at very high temperatures, liberating hydrogen. Metals such as Cu and Ag also react under these conditions.
Burning sodium burns with a golden yellow flame in dry HC1 gas.
\( \begin{align} 2Na + 2HCl(g) \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Fe + 2HCl(g) \rightarrow FeCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Cu + 2HCl(g) \rightarrow CuCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] 2Ag + 2HCl(g) \rightarrow 2AgCl + H_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

Catalytic Oxidation by air( \( O_2 \) )

HCl gas reacts with \( O_2 \) at high temperatures (> \( 600^{\circ}C \) ) in the presence of a catalyst \( (CuCl_2) \) .
\( 4HCl + O_2 \xrightarrow[CuCl_2]{>600^{\circ}C} 2H_2O \uparrow + 2Cl_2 \uparrow \)

Oxidation by \( MnO_2 \) and \( PbO_2 \)

On being passed over warm \( MnO_2 \) and at room temperature with \( PbO_2 \) , HCl gas gives \( Cl_2 \) .
\( \begin{align} MnO_2 + 4HCl(g) \rightarrow MnCl_2 + Cl_2 \uparrow + 2H_2O \\\\[3pt] PbO_2 + 4HCl(g) \rightarrow PbCl_2 + Cl_2 \uparrow + 2H_2O \end{align} \)

Reaction with \( NH_3 \)

HCl gas combines with \( NH_3 \) to form dense white fumes of \( NH_4Cl \) , which settle in solid form.
\( NH_3(g) + HCl(g) \rightarrow NH_4Cl(s) \)

HydroChloric acid

An aqueous solution of HCl gas is called hydrochloric acid. It can be obtained by dissolving the HCl gas prepared in the laboratory in water. However, the delivery tube carry the HCl gas should not be directly dipped into the water. This is because the dissolution of the gas would create a partial vacuum and suck water into the reaction vessel, leading to an explosion. To avoid this, the following measures are taken.

  1. An antisuction device is used. This is an empty flask between the reaction and absorption vessels. Any liquid sucked in will go to this flask but not to the reaction vessel, and so no explosion will occur.

  2. The delivery tube is connected to a big funnel which is kept inverted, just touching the surface of the water in the absorption vessel. Even a large volume of water rise only a few millimeters into the funnel, and will fall back into the vessel.

At \( 25^{\circ}C \) , the saturated solution contains about 43% HCl by mass. The commercial acid generally contains about 39% HCl.

Physical Properties

  1. The pure acid solution is colourless but the commercial acid is often yellow due te presence of \( FeCl_3 \) as impurity.

  2. The concentrated solution fumes strongly in air.

  3. The boiling point of the acid varies with its concentration. When the acid content is 20.24% by mass, the boiling point is constant at \( 110^{\circ}C \) . This is the maximum boiling point for an HCl solution. This solution boils like a pure liquid - the vapour and the liquid having the same composition. At other concentrations the vapour contains more water than in the constant - boiling liquid, and over a concentrated solution, more HCl.

Chemical Properties

Hydrochloric acid has the following important properties

Dissociation and electrolytic property :

It is a strong acid. Although HCl is a covalent compound, it completely dissociates into H_3O^+ and \( Cl^- \) ions in aqueous solutions.
\( HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^- + Cl^- \)
Owing to the presence of ions, the solution is a good conductor of electricity. On electrolysis, \( H_2 \) is liberated at the cathode. The product at the anode depends on the concentration of the acid, concentrated solutions produce \( Cl_2 \) and dilute solutions give \( O_2 \) .
Concentrated solution: \( 2Cl^- \xrightarrow{anode} Cl_2 + 2e^- \)
Dilute solution : \( 4H_20 \rightarrow 4H^+ + 4OH^- \)
\( \begin{align} \downarrow \ anode \\\\[3pt] 2H_2O + O_2 \uparrow + 4e^- \end{align} \)

Displacement of \( H_2 \) by metals

It liberates \( H_2 \) , upon reacting with metals (except Pb) above hydrogen in the activity series. Pb does not react with HCl as it forms a protective layer of \( PbCl_2 \) .
\( \begin{align} Mg + 2HCl \rightarrow MgCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Fe + 2HCl \rightarrow FeCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Sn + 2HCl \rightarrow SnCl_2 + H_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

Neutralisation of bases

Since most metallic oxides and hydroxides are bases, HCl neutralises them to form the respective metal chloride and water.
\( \begin{align} CaO + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] Zn(OH)_2 + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + 2H_2O \\\\[3pt] CuO + 2HCl \rightarrow CuCl_2 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] Fe(OH)_3 + 3HCl \rightarrow FeCl_3 + 3H_2O \end{align} \)

Reactions with Carbonates, sulphites, and Sulphides

Carbonates and hydrogen carbonates: It reacts with carbonates and hydrogen carbonates to give \( CO_2 \) with effervesence
\( \begin{align} CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] NaHCO_3 + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O + CO_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)
Sulphides and hydrogensulphides : It reacts with sulphides and hydrogen sulphides to give \( H_2S \) with effervesence.
\( \begin{align} Na_2S + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2S \uparrow \\\\[3pt] NaHS + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2S \uparrow \end{align} \)
Sulphites and Hydrogen sulphites: It reacts with sulphites and hydrogen sulphites to give \( SO_2 \) with effervesence.
\( \begin{align} Na_2SO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2O + SO_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] NaHSO_3 + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O + SO_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

Liberation of \( Cl_2 \) by strong oxidising agents

while reacting with strong oxidising agents, it functions as a reducing agent, forming \( Cl_2 \)
\( \begin{align} MnO_2 + 4HCl \rightarrow MnCl_2 + 2H_2O + Cl_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] K_2Cr_2O_7 + 14 HCl \rightarrow 2KCl + 2CrCl_3 + 7H_2O + 3Cl_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] 2KMnO_4 + 16HCl \rightarrow 2KCl + 2MnCl_2 + 8H_2O + 5Cl_2 \uparrow \end{align} \) (in cold conditions)

Precipitation of some metal Chlorides

AgCl, \( PbCl_2 \) and \( Hg_2Cl_2 \) , being soluble, are precipitated when hydrochloric acid is added to solutions containing \( Ag^+, \ Pb^{2+} \ or \ Hg^{2+} \) ions. \( PbCl_2 \) dissolves in hot water and reappears when the solution is cooled.
\( \begin{align} AgNO_3 + HCl \rightarrow AgCl \downarrow + HNO_3 \\\\[3pt] Pb(NO_3)_2 + 2HCl \rightarrow PbCl_2 \downarrow + 2HNO_3 \end{align} \)

Uses

  1. As a laboratory reagent

  2. For pickling i.e., for removing the oxide scales (rust) on steel before galvanisation.

  3. For decomposing bones to make gelatine.

  4. For refining oils, fats and waxes.

  5. In the tanning of leather.

  6. In the manufacture of dyes and textiles.

Bleaching Powder

Bleaching powder is a mixed salt of calcium hypo - chloride \( [Ca(OCl)_2.3H_2O] \) and basic calcium chloride \( [CaCl_2.Ca(OH)_2.H_2O] \) . Actually it is not a true compound but simply it is represented by formula \( CaOCl_2 \) .

Methods of Preparation

It is prepared by action of \( Cl_2 \) on dry slaked lime.
\( Ca(OH)_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow CaOCl_2 + H_2O \) According to Clifford, the formation of bleaching powder is explained as
\( \begin{align} 2Ca(OH)_2 + 2Cl_2 \rightarrow Ca(OCl)_2 + CaCl_2 + 2H_2O \\\\[3pt] CaCl_2 + Ca(OH)_2 + H_2O \rightarrow CaCl_2.Ca(OH)_2.H_2O \\\\[3pt] 3Ca(OH)_2 + 2Cl_2 \rightarrow Ca(OCl)_2 + Ca(OH)_2.H_2O + H_2O \end{align} \)

Physical properties

It is pale yellow powder having smell of \( Cl_2 \) . It is sparingly soluble in water and gives a milky suspension when dissolved in water. Its aqueous solution gives positive test for \( Ca^{2+}, \ Cl^- \ and \ OCl^- \) .

Chemical properties

Decomposition:

On standing, it decomposes (auto oxidation) to calcium chloride and calcium chlorate.
\( 6CaOCl_2 \rightarrow 5CaCl_2 + Ca(ClO_3)_2 \)
On standing chlorine percentage decreases regularly. Due to this germicidal nature is lost. Decomposition of bleaching powder is catalysed by cobalt chloride.
\( 2CaOCl_2 \xrightarrow{CoCl_2} 2CaCl_2 + O_2 \)

Reaction with dilute acid:
With limited quantity of acid

With limited quantity of dilute acids \( (HCl, \ H_2SO_4 etc., ) \) bleaching powder liberates oxygen.
\( \begin{align} 2CaOCl_2 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow CaSO_4 + H_2O + Cl_2 \\\\[3pt] HOCl \rightarrow HCl + [O] \end{align} \)
Evolution of oxygen is responsible for its bleaching action.

With excess of acid :

With excess of acid, bleaching powder liberates chlorine which is called available chlorine. Due to available chlorine, bleaching powder acts as disinfectant.
\( \begin{align} CaOCl_2 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow CaSO_4 + H_2O + Cl_2 \\\\[3pt] CaOCl_2 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + Cl_2 \end{align} \) \( Cl_2 \) is liberated with \( CO_2 \) also.
\( CaOCl_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + Cl_2 \)

Oxidising Nature:

Bleaching powder is good oxidising agent because it gives nascent oxygen. e.g.,
\( \begin{align} CaOCl_2 + Na_3AsO_3 \rightarrow Na_3AsO_4 + CaCl_2 \\\\[3pt] CaOCl_2 + 2FeSO_4 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow Fe_2(SO_4)_3 + CaCl_2 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] CaOCl_2 + KNO_2 \rightarrow CaCl_2 + KNO_3 \\\\[3pt] CaOCl_2 + 2KI + 2CH_3COOH \rightarrow (CH_3COO)_2Ca + H_2O + I_2 + 2KCl \\\\[3pt] 3CaOCl_2 + 2NH_3 \rightarrow 3CaCl_2 + 3H_2O + N_2 \end{align} \)

Formation of Chloroform:

Ethyl alcohol, acetone etc., give chloroform when treated with bleaching powder.

Bleaching Action:

As bleaching powder gives nascent oxygen, it shows bleaching property.
Coloured matter + [O] \( \rightarrow \) Colourless matter.

Uses:

  1. As bleaching agent in textile, paper and jute industry.

  2. As disinfectant.

  3. As oxidant.

  4. In the preparation of several compounds.

  5. For making wool unshrinkable.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Calculate the oxidation state of the halogen in the following :

  1. \( Cl_2O \)

  2. \( ClO_2 \)

  3. \( NaClO_4 \)

Solution:

  1. Let oxidation state of Chlorine in \( Cl_2O \) be x
    \( \therefore \ In \ Cl_2O \)
    2x - 2 = 0
    2x = 2
    x = +1

  2. Let oxidation state of Chlorine in \( Cl_2 \) be x
    \( \therefore \ In \ Cl_2O \)
    x - 4 = 0
    x = +4

  3. Let oxidation state of Chlorine in \( NaClO_4 \) be x
    \( \therefore \ In \ NaClO_4 \)
    1 + x - 8 = 0
    x = +7

Example 2:

Question:
Write the balanced chemical equation for the reaction of \( Cl_2 \) with hot and concentrated NaOH. Is this reaction a disproportionation reaction ? Justify.

Solution:
\( 3Cl_2 + 6NaOH \rightarrow 5NaCl + NaClO_3 + 3H_2O \)
This is a disproportionation reaction because the oxidation state of Cl is changed from zero (in \( Cl_2 \) ) to - 1 (in NaCl) and 5 (in \( NaClO_3 \) )

Example 3:

Question:
HCl when reacts with finely divided iron forms ferrous chloride and not ferric chloride. Why ?

Solution:
HCl reacts with finely divided iron and produces \( H_2 \) gas
\( 2Fe + 62HCl \rightarrow 2FeCl_3 + 3H_2 \) ,
Liberation of hydrogen prevents the formation of ferric chloride.

Question:
Complete the following reactions

  1. \( \underset{hot \ and \ conc}{NaOH + Cl_2} \rightarrow \)

  2. \( NH_3 + Cl_2(excess) \rightarrow \)

  3. \( NaNO_2 + HCl \rightarrow \)

  4. \( K_2Cr_2O_7 + HCl \rightarrow \)

  5. \( K_2CO_3 + HCl \rightarrow \)

Solution:

  1. \( \underset{hot \ and \ conc}NaOH + Cl_2 \rightarrow 5NaCl + NaClO_3 + 3H_2O \)

  2. \( NH_3 + Cl_2(excess) \rightarrow NCl_3 + 3HCl \)

  3. \( NaNO_2 + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2O + NO_2 + NO \)

  4. \( K_2Cr_2O_7 + 14HCl \rightarrow 2CrCl_3 + 7H_2O + 3Cl_2 \)

  5. \( K_2CO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow K_2CO_3 + 2HCl \)

Example 5:

Question:
Bleaching powder loses its bleaching property when it is kept in an open bottle for a long time. Explain.

Solution:
Bleaching action of bleaching powder is because of chlorine present in it. When kept exposed to air in the open bottles for long time it decomposes and chlorine is lost in atmosphere.
\( CaOCl_2 + H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + Cl_2 \uparrow \)

Example 6:

Question:
Anhydrous HCl is a bad conductor of electricity whereas aqueous HCl conducts electricity.

Solution:
Anhydrous HCl is a bad conductor of electricity whereas aqueous HCl conducts electricity. Because anhydrous HCl is a covalent molecule and a bad conductor of electricity. But in aqueous solution, HCl ionises to give \( H^+ \) and \( Cl^- \) ions because of which it conducts electricity.

Example 7:

Question:
Write the reaction between ammonia and sodium hypochlorite.

Solution:
\( 2NH_3 + NaOCl \rightarrow \underset{hydrogen}{H_2N.NH_2} + NaCl + H_2O \)

Example 9:

Question:
On strong heating \( NaHSO_4 \) reacts with NaCl to release HCl and \( Na_2SO_4 \) but salt cake formed is difficult to remove. Why ? How can it be avoided ?

Solution:
The reaction mixture is not heated strongly as the salt cake \( Na_2SO_4 \) formed is difficult to remove completely from the flask. This difficulty does not arise when \( NH_4Cl \) is used, because the \( (NH_4)_2SO_4 \) formed can be completely removed.
\( 2NH_4Cl + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow (NH_4)_2SO_4 + 2HCl \uparrow \)
However, NaCl is generally used because it is more commonly available.

Example 10:

Question:
A certain compound (X) gives brick red flame on performing the flame test and shows the following reactions :

  1. When KI is added to an aqueous suspension of (X) containing acetic acid, iodine is liberated.

  2. When \( CO_2 \) is passed through an aqueous suspension of (X) the turbidity transforms to a precipitate.

  3. When a paste of (X) in water is heated with ethyl alcohol, a product of anaesthetic use is obtained. Identify (X) and write down chemical equations for reactions at step (i) and (ii).

Solution:
(X) gives brick red flame so, it contains \( Ca^{2+} \) . Reactions (i), (ii) and (iii) suggest that the probable compound is bleaching powder \( (CaOCl_2) \)
Reactions :

  1. \( CaOCl_2 + H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + \underset{smell}{Cl_2} \uparrow \)

  2. \( \underset{X}{CaOCl_2} + 2CH_3COOH \rightarrow Ca(CH_3COO)_2 + Cl_2 + H_2O \)

  3. \( 2KI + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2KCl + I_2 \)
