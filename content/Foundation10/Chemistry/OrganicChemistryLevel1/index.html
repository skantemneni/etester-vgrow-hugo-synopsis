Introduction

Till 1828 the term organic chemistry was used, only for the study of such substances which were obtained from living things i.e., animals or plants. Chemists found it impossible to synthesis organic compounds in laboratory. They thought that some vital force is necessary to synthesise the organic compounds, which is present only in the living animals and plants. This in turn gave rise to vital force theory, according to which, organic compounds cannot be prepared in the laboratory from the elements. In 1828, a German chemist Friedrich Wohler succeeded in synthesizing urea, an organic compound from elements. This achievement of Wohler, gave death blow to vital force theory. Now a days chemists are preparing more synthetic compounds in their laboratories more than the known organic compounds found in the plants and animals. Organic chemistry is branch of chemistry dealing with hydro carbon and their derivatives.

Unique Features of Carbon

Catenation Property

The ability of an atom to join with the other atoms of the same element and form long chains is called catenation. Carbon is an element which shows maximum catenation.
The ability of catenation can be explained on the basis of the electronic configuration of carbon.

There are four unpaired electrons in the excited state of carbon atom and this gives tetravalency for carbon.

Multiple Bond Formation

Due to the presence of four unpaired electrons in the valence shell of a carbon atom in its excited state, there is a possibility of formation of double bonds or triple bonds between carbon atoms. This ability to form multiple bonds also gives rise to various types of carbon chains.

Isomerism

Isomerism is the phenomenon in whith two or more organic molecules have the same molecular formula but differ in the structural arrangement of atoms. Those compounds are called isomers.

Isomerism in Hydrocarbons

The organic compounds having same molecular formula, but different structural formulae are called isomers and the phenomenon is called isomerism.
The two different types of isomerism are structural and stereo isomerism. Structural isomerism is the type of variation in the structures of the molecules. Under this the following are the types :

Chain Isomerism

The phenomenon in which two or more organic compounds have the same molecular formula but differ in the arrangement of carbon atoms in the longest chain. All types of hydrocarbons (alkanes, alkenes and alkynes) with more than 3 carbon atoms exhibit type of isomerism.

Position Isomerism

The phenomenon in which the hydrocarbon has the same chain but differs in the postion of multiple bonds or constituents on the parent chain is called position isomerism.
This is exhibited by unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes and alkynes) with more than 3 carbon atoms (or) saturated hydrocarbons with substituents as side chain.

Functional Isomerism

Compounds with the same molecular formula but different functional groups are called functional isomers and this phenomenon is called functional isomerism.

Metamerism

The isomers in which there is unequal distribution of carbon chain or atoms on either side of the functional group are called metamers and this phenomenon is termed 'metamerism'. Thus metamers differ in the size of an alkyl group or chain on either side of the functional group.

Homologous Series

  1. Successive members differ by 14 in molecular weight.

  2. Difference in molecular formula corresponds to \( -CH_2 \) , unit.

  3. Preparation by similar methods.

  4. Similar chemical properties.

  5. Regular gradation in physical properties.

  6. Same general formula.

IUPAC Nomenclature

Following rules are applied in naming isomers or branched chain hydrocarbons:

  1. First of all the longest chain of carbon atoms is located in the structural formula or condensed formula of the saturated hydrocarbon. The compound is, therefore, named as the derivative of alkane of hydrocarbon of longest chain, which is commonly called parent chain.
    Ex: \( CH_3 - CH_2 - \underset{\underset{CH_3}|}{CH_2} - CH_2 - CH_3 \)
    In the above condensed formula, the longest chain of carbon atoms has six carbon atoms. Thus, the above compound is named as a derivative of alkane hexane

  2. The alkyl groups present as branches or side chains are called substituents. They are named separately as methyl \( – (–CH_3) \) or ethyl \( (–C_2H_5) \) groups, etc.,

  3. The carbon atoms in the longest chain are then numbered in such a way what the carbon atom attached to substituent (alkyl group) gets the lowest possible number. This can be achieved either by numbering the carbon atoms from left to right or right to left.
    Ex: \( 6CH_3 - 2CH_2 - 3CH_2 - \underset{\underset{CH_3}|}{4CH} - 5CH_2 - 6CH_3 \)
    In the above condensed formula, the numbering is incorrect, as the carbon atom attached to methyl group does not have the smallest number.

  4. The alkyl groups present as branches or side chains are called substituents. They as named separately as methyl \( (-CH_3) \) or ethyl \( (-C_2H_5) \) groups, etc.,
    Ex : In the condensed formula given in 1 above is methyl group which is substitutent.

  5. The carbon atoms in the longest chain are then numbered in such a way that the carbon atom attached to substituent (alkyl group) gets the lowest possible number. This can be achieved either by numbering the carbon atoms from left to right or from right to left.
    Ex: \( 6CH_3 - \underset{\underset{3 CH_3}|}{5CH_2} - 4CH_2 - 3CH - 2CH_2 - 1CH_3 \)
    In the above condensed formula, the numbering is correct as the carbon atom attached to methyl group has the smallest number 3.
    \( 1CH_3 - \underset{\underset{CH_3}|}{2CH_2} - 3CH_2 - 4CH - 5CH_2 - 6CH_3 \)
    In the above condensed formula, the numbering is incorrect, as the carbon atom attached to methyl group does not have the smallest number.

  6. \( 1CH_3 - \underset{\underset{CH_3}|}{2CH} - 3CH_2 - 4CH_2 - \overset{ \overset{C_2H_5}|}{5 CH} - 6CH_2 - 7CH_2 \)
    The name of compound is 2 - methyl ; 5 - ethyl heptane.

Hydrocarbons

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons having the general formula \( C_nH_{2n + 2} \) . Successive members of the homologous series of alkane differ in the molecular formula by \( -CH_2 \) , Alkanes are also called paraffins (meaning 'little affinity' in Latin). These compounds show very little activity towards most reagents. All alkanes end in -ane. The first part of the name of the compound indicates the number of carbon atoms in the molecule. If one hydrogen atom is removed from the general formula of alkane, the resulting group \( (C_nH_{2n + 1}) \) is called an alkyl group. The alkyl group is symbolised as R.

General Methods of Preparation of Alkanes

Decarboxylation(removal of -COONa group):

Action of heat on a dry mixture of sodium salt of fatty acid and soda lime.
\( \begin{align} CH_3COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[CaO]{\Delta} \underset{Methane}{CH_4} + Na_2CO_3 \\\\[12pt] CH_3CH_2COONa + NaOH \xrightarrow[CaO]{\Delta} \underset{Ethane}{C_2H_6} + Na_2CO_3 \end{align} \)

Reduction of an alkyl halide:

This can be done in two ways.

  1. Reduction in presence of HI.
    \( \underset{Methyl \ Iodide}{CH_3I} + HI \rightarrow \underset{Methane}{CH_4} + I_2 \)

  2. Reduce with Zn—Cu couple and ethyl alcohol of Zn/HCl
    \( \underset{Ethyl \ Iodide}{C_2H_5I} + 2H \xrightarrow[+ethyl \ alcohol]{Zn - Cu} \underset{Ethane}{C_2 H_6} + HI \)

Reduction of aldehyde or ketone:

By heating it with zinc amalgam and hydrochloric acid.
\( \begin{align} \underset{Formaldehyde}{HCHO} + 4H \xrightarrow[+HCl]{Zn - Cu} \underset{Methane}{CH_4} + H_2O \\\\[12pt] \underset{Acetaldehyde}{CH_3CHO} + 4H \xrightarrow[+HCl]{Zn - Hg} \underset{Ethane}{C_2H_6} + H_2O \end{align} \)

Physical Properties

These are colourless, odourless, tasteless gases which are insoluble in water, but soluble in nonpolar solvents like alcohol, carbon tetrachloride etc.

Chemical Properties

Substitution reaction

Substitution reaction is a characteristic reaction of alkanes. Here one or more hydrogen atoms of methane or ethane are substituted by some other functional group. If the substitution is carried out by halogen, the reaction is called halogenation. The products depend upon the amount of chlorine.
\( \underset{Ethane}{C_2H_6} \xrightarrow {+Cl_2} \underset{Ethyl \ Chloride}{C_2H_5Cl} \xrightarrow{+Cl_2} \underset{Di - chloroethane}{C_2H_4Cl_2} \xrightarrow{+Cl_2} \underset{Tri - chloroethane}{C_2H_3Cl_3} \xrightarrow{+Cl_2} \underset{penta - chloroethane}{C_2H_2Cl_4} \xrightarrow{+Cl_2} \underset{HexaChloroethane}{C_2Cl_6} \)

Combustion

In excess of \( O_2 \) these burn with a pale blue flame to form \( CO_2 \) and \( H_2O \) .
\( CH_4 + 2O_2 \xrightarrow{Excess air} CO_2 + 2H_2O + heat \) \( 2C_2H_6 + 7O_2 \xrightarrow{Excess air} 4CO_2 + 6H_2O + heat \) In inadequate supply of air, CO gas and water vapour are formed.
\( 2CH_4 + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2CO + 4H_2O + heat \) \( 2C_2H_6 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 4CO + 6H_2O + heat \)

Pyrolysis (or) Cracking

Heating to a very high temperature in the absence of air is called cracking or Pyrolysis.
Methane decomposes to carbon and hydrogen when heated to \( 1000^{\circ}C \) in the absence of air. \( CH_4 \xrightarrow{1000^{\circ}C} C + 2H_2 \) Ethane when heated to \( 450^{\circ}C \) in absence of air, decomposes to give ethylene, methane and hydrogen. \( 3C_2H_6 \xrightarrow{450^{\circ}C} 2C_2H_4 + 2CH_4 + H_2 \)

Uses

  1. Methane and liquefied ethane are used as nonpolluting fuels in automobile industries and other industries also.

  2. These are used for the manufacture of important organic compounds like alcohol, aldehyde, carboxylic acid which are widely used in the paint industry and other different industries.

  3. Hydrogen obtained by the pyrolysis of methane is used in the manufacture of ammonia which is further used for the manufacture of nitrogenous fertilizers.

Alkenes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons having the general formula \( C_nH_{2n} \) , where n is the number of carbon atoms. The valencies of carbon atoms are not satisfied with the help of single covalent bonds. These valencies are then completed with the help of carbon carbon double bonds.
The smallest and simplest alkene is ethene \( (C_2H_4) \) . Other members are propene \( (C_3H_6) \) butane \( (C_4H_8) \) and so on.

Some Common Methods of Preparation of Alkenes

Dehydration of Alcohols:

When alcohols are heated with conc. Sulphuric acid at \( 160^{\circ}C — 170^{\circ}C \) , they lose a molecule of water and form the corresponding alkene.
\( C_2 H_5 OH \xrightarrow[170^{\circ}]{95 \% H_2 SO_4} H_2C = CH_2 + H_2O \)

By Dehydrohalogenation of Alkyl Halides:

When an alkyl halide is heated in an alcoholic solution of sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide, a molecule of halogen halide is removed and an alkene is formed.
\( H - \underset{ \underset{H}|} {\overset{ \overset{H} |} C} - \underset{ \underset{Br3} |} {\overset{ \overset{H} |} C} - H \xrightarrow [ \Delta] {KOH /ethanol} H - { \overset{ \overset{H} |} C} = { \overset{ \overset{H} |} C} - H + KBr + H_2O \)

Cracking of Alkanes:

Catalytic cracking of alkanes at high temperature and pressure yields alkenes. The catalyst generally used is silica-alumina \( (SiO_2 — Al_2O_3) \) . Ethylene and propylene are prepared by the cracking of petroleum.

Chemical Properties

Addition Reactions:

  1. Hydrogenation:
    In the presence of catalysts like finely divided platinum, nickel or palladium, alkenes add on hydrogen and form alkanes (saturated). This is the principle of hydrogenation of oils to form 'vanaspati'.

  2. Halogenation: Alkenes readily react with chlorine and bromine to form dihalides. There is no reaction with iodine.
    \( \underset{ethylene}{C_2H_4} + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{catalyst} \underset{Ethylene \ dichloride}{C_2H_4Cl_2} \)

  3. Addition of halogen acids: Ethene on treatment with halogen acids (in solution) forms ethyl halides.

Polymerisation:

In this process a large number of simple molecules of an unsaturated compound combine to form a large molecule at high temperature and pressure in the presence of a catalyst. The large molecule formed is called polymer.

Combustion:

Ethene burns in oxygen with a luminous flame to produce carbon dioxide and water.

Oxidation with Ozone (Ozonolysis):

When ozone is passed through ethene in an inert solvent like ether, it adds across the double bond to form an ozonide.

Oxidation with cold dilute alkaline \( KMnO_4 \)

Ethene reacts with cold dilute potassium permanganate to form ethene glycol. Glycols are compounds whose molecules contain two - OH groups on adjacent carbon atoms.

Uses:

  1. Ethylene have a wide application in organic chemical industries.

  2. It is used in the manufacture of polythene.

  3. Ethylene is used for the manufacture of ethylene glycol which is used as an antifreeze.

  4. It is also used for artificial ripening of fruits like bananas and mangoes.

Alkynes

Alkynes are hydrocarbons having the general formula \( C_n H_{2n - 2} \) . Alkynes are unsaturated compounds containing a triple bond. Alkynes have the tendency to convert the triple bond to single bond by adding up simple molecules. The first member of the alkyne series is ethyne (acetylene).

Preparation of Acetylene (Ethyne):

Action of water on Calcium carbide:

Cold water is added dropwise on calcium at room temperature.
\( \underset{Calicium \ carbide}{CaC_2} + 2H_2O \rightarrow \underset{Acetylene}{H - C \equiv C - H} + Ca(OH)_2 \)

By Synthesis:

It is prepared by striking an electric arc between carbon electric arc between the presence of hydrogen gas.
\( 2C + H_2 \xrightarrow{Electric arc} \underset{Acetylene}{H - C \equiv C - H} \)

From ethylene dibromide (by dehydrohalogenation):

Acetylene is formed ethylene dibromide is boiled with alcoholic KOH solution.
\( \underset{CH_2 Br} { \overset{CH_2 Br} |} + 2 KOH \xrightarrow [boil] {Alcohol} H - C \equiv C - H + C - 2 KBr + 2 H_2O \)

From Methane or Natural gas:

When methane is heated to about When methane is heated to about \( 1500^{\circ}C \) in an arc and then suddenly cooled, the products are ethyne and hydrogen.
\( \underset{Methane}{2CH_4} \xrightarrow[1500^{\circ}C]{Electric arc} \underset{Acetylene}{H - C \equiv C - H} + 3H_2 \)

Chemical Properties of Ethyne (Acetylene):

Combustion

Ethyne burns in air or oxygen with a luminous flame, producing carbon dioxide, water and heat. Ethyne contains a high percentage of carbon, the Whole which does not burn. This makes the flame sooty.

Addition Reactions:

  1. Addition of hydrogen (Hydrogenation): When vapours of acetylene are mixed with hydrogen and passed over a catalyst like nickel, palladium or platinum at high temperatures, addition products are, formed in two stages (alkene and alkane).
    \( \underset{Actylene}{C_2H_2} + H_2 \xrightarrow[100^{\circ}C]{Ni} \underset{Ethylene}{C_2H_4} \xrightarrow[Ni/100^{\circ}C]{H_2} \underset{Ethane}{C_2H_6} \)

  2. Addition of Halogens:

    1. Chlorine
      Gaseous chlorine adds in the dark to give acetylene dichloride, addition of two chlorine atoms) and acetylene tetrachloride (by further addition of two chlorine atoms).
      \( \underset{Acetylene} {H - C \equiv C - H} \xrightarrow{+ Cl_2} \underset{ \underset{(Acetylene \ Dichloride)} {1, 2 dichloroethene}}{H - \overset{ \overset {Cl} |}{C} = \overset{ \overset {Cl} |}{C} - H} \xrightarrow{+ Cl_2} \underset{1, 1, 2, 2 tetra \ chloroethane} {H - \underset{ \underset{Cl}|} {\overset{ \overset{Cl} |} C} - \underset{ \underset{Cl}|} {\overset{ \overset{Cl} |} C} - H} \)

    2. Bromine
      When reacted with dilute bromine water, acetylene forms acetylene dibromide. When reacted with liquid bromine, acetylene forms acetylene tetrabromide.

      Bromine:

  3. Polymerisation Reaction When acetylene gas is passed through a hot copper tube at \( 600^{\circ}C \) , the molecules of acetylene join together to form benzene.
    \( \underset{Acetylene}{3C_2H_2} \xrightarrow {Cu /600^{\circ}C} \underset{Benzene}{C_6H_6} \)

  4. Combustibility Acetylene forms an explosive mixture with air and burns with a luminous white flame. An oxy - acetylene flame produces temperatures as high as \( 3500^{\circ}C \) . At this temperature almost all metals melt. Hence, acetylene is used in cutting and welding of metals.
    \( \underset{Ethylene}2C_2H_2 + 5O_2 \rightarrow \underset{Carbon dioxide}{4 CO_2} + 2H_2O + Heat \)

Uses

  1. Oxy acetylene flame is used for cutting and welding metals.

  2. Acetylene lamps are used for illustration purpose.

  3. Ripening of fruits like apples, mangoes takes place in the atmosphere of acetylene.

  4. Acetylene on hydrogenation to Ethene which can be polymerised to form polyethylene.

  1. 5 - Ethyle - 3 - Methyl heptane

  2. 4 - Isoproppyl-5-sec-butyl decane

  3. 2 - Ethyl pentane

Why are the given names incorrect?

  1. \( ^7CH_3 - ^6CH_2 - \underset{\underset{CH_3}|}{^5CH} - ^4CH_2 - \underset{\underset{C_2H_5}|}{^3CH} - ^2CH_2 - ^1CH_3 \)

  3. \( CH_3 - \underset{ \underset{ ^2CH_2 - ^1CH_3}|}{^3CH} - ^4CH_2 - ^5CH_2 - ^6CH_3 \)
    Longest chain is of 6 carbon atoms and not that of 5.Hence the correct name is of : 3 - Methyl hexane.

  2. \( H_3CCH_2 - CH = CH_2 \rightarrow H_3C - CH_2 - CH(OH)CH_2OH \)

  3. \( H_3CC \equiv CH \rightarrow H_3CCOCH_3 \)

  4. \( Br_2CHCHBr_2 \rightarrow HC \equiv CH \)

Solution:

  1. \( H_3CCH_2CH = CH_2 \xrightarrow{B_2H_6, \ H_2O_2, \ OH^{-}} CH_3CH_2CH_2OH \)

  2. \( H_3CCH_2CH = CH_2 \xrightarrow{Alk, \ KMnO_4} H_3C - CH_2 - \underset{ \underset{OH}|}{CH} - \underset{ \underset{OH}|}{CH_2} \)

  3. \( H_3CC \equiv CH \xrightarrow[Hg^{2+}]{H_2SO_4} CH_3\underset{ \underset{OH}|}CH = CH_2 \xrightarrow{Rearrangement} \underset{ \underset{O}{||}}CH_3CCH_3 \)

  4. \( Br_2CHCHBr_2 \xrightarrow{Zn} HC \equiv CH \)
