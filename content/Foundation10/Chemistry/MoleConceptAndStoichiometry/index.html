S.No Common Misconception Fact
1 Mole is short form of molecule. Mole is short form of gram mole or gram molecule.
2 Gram atomic mass is same as mass of one atom. Gram atomic mass is the number of times an atom is heavier than \( {\frac{1}{12}}^{th} \) of carbon atom.
3 One mole of any gas always corresponds to 22.4 litre volume. 22.4 litre volume Corresponds to volume of one mole gas only at STP.

Introduction

The study of chemistry was initially qualitative. But, later chemists felt necessary to study chemistry quantitatively in addition to qualitative study. The further developments lead to the concept of mole and chemical stoichiometry. Chemical stoichiometry describes the quantitative substances under going chemical changes.

Atomic mass

Atoms are extremely small and their absolute mass was measured by taking the reference of mass of other atoms as assigned relative to it.

Hydrogen Scale

The atomic weight of an element is a number which represents the number of times an atom of an element is heavier than one atom of hydrogen \( \left ( H^{\prime} \right ) \) .
\( Atomic \ weight = \frac{\text{weight of one atom of an element}}{\text{weight of one atom of hydrogen(H)}} \)
E.g. Atomic weight of Al is 27. Al atom is 27 times heavier than one hydrogen atom.

Oxygen Scale

The atomic weight of an element is a number which represents the number of times an atom of an element is heavier than \( { \frac{1}{16}}^{th} \) part of the weight of an oxygen atom \( \left ( O^{16} \right ) \)
. \( Atomic \ weight = \frac{\text{weight of one atom of an element}}{weight \ of \ { \frac{1}{16}}^{th} \ of \ an \ atom \ of \ Oxygen \left ( O^{16} \right )} \)

Carbon Scale

The atomic weight of an element is a number which represents the number of times an atom of an element is heavier than \( {\frac{1}{12}}^{th} \) part of the weight of carbon atom \( \left( C^{12} \right ) \) .

Molecular weight (Relative molecular mass)

Like atoms, the molecules, are also very minute and their weights are also measured in the same way as the weight of atoms.
Following are the different scales, used to define molecular weight.

Hydrogen Scale:

The molecular weight of an element or a compound is a number which represents the number of times a molecule of the element or compound is heavier than one atom of hydrogen \( (H^1) \) .
\( Molecular \ weight = \frac{\text{weight of one molecule of the substance}}{\text{weight of one atom of hydrogen}(H^1)} \) .

Oxygen Scale:

The molecular weight of an element or a compound is a number which represents, the number times a molecule of the elements or compounds is a heavier than \( { \frac{1}{16}}^{th} \) part of the weight of an oxygen atom \( \left ( O^{16} \right ) \)
. \( Molecular \ weight = \frac{\text{weight of one molecule of the substance}}{weight \ of \ { \frac{1}{16}}^{th} \ of \ an \ atom \ of \ Oxygen \left ( O^{16} \right )} \) .

Carbon Scale

The molecular weight of an element or a compound is a number which represents the number of times a molecule of the element or compound is heavier than \( {\frac{1}{12}}^{th} \) part of the weight of carbon atom \( \left( C^{12} \right ) \)
\( Molecular \ weight = \frac{\text{weight of one molecule of the substance}}{weight \ of \ { \frac{1}{12}}^{th} \ of \ one \ atom \ of \ \left ( C^{12} \right ) isotope} \) .

Gram Atomic weight

Gram atomic weight of an element is its atomic weight expressed in grams.

Gram atom

It is the amount of substance that is equal to its atomic weight expressed in grams. For example, one gram atom of helium is that amount of helium which is equal to the atomic weight of helium (4) expressed in grams i.e. one gram atom of helium is 4 grams. Similarly, 1 gram atom of nitrogen and oxygen weigh 14 grams and 16 grams respectively.

Gram molecular weight

Gram molecular weight of a substance is its molecular weight expressed in grams. For example, molecular weights of \( N_2 \ and \ O_2 \) and 28 and 32 respectively. Hence, their gram molecular weights are 28 g and 32 g respectively.

Gram Molecule

It is the amount of substance that is equal to its molecular weight expressed in grams.
For example, one gram molecule of water is that amount of water \( (H_2O) \) which is equal to the molecular weight of water (18) expressed in grams i.e. 18 gram. Similarly, the weight of 1 gram molecule each of methane and ammonia is equal to 16 g and 17 g respectively. Thus one gram molecule of a substance represents the definite weight of that substance which is equal to its molecular weight expressed in grams.

Formula weight and gram formula weight

For ionic compounds like salts, we do not use the term molecular weight. Ionic compounds are not molecules, they are giant network of opposite ions. Thus, NaCl crystal is the network of many \( Na^+ \) . and \( Cl^- \) ions, which is represented as \( (Na^+ Cl^-) \) n; as 'n' is not fixed, we cannot talk about molecular formula and molecular weight. For ionic compounds, we use formula weight in place of molecular weight. While considering formula weight, we consider a formula unit i.e., NaCl. For example, the formula unit of sodium chloride is NaCl and its formula weight is equal to the sum of atomic weights of Na and Cl.
\( \Rightarrow formula \ weight \ of \ NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 \)

Atomic mass unit - Unit of mass at microscopic level

Today measurement of weight, the units used are kilograms and grams. Measuring the weight of atoms, molecules and elementary particles, the unit used is a.m.u. One atomic mass unit (a.m.u) is equal to \( { \frac{1}{12}}^{th} \) of the mass of one carbon - 12 atom.
1 a.m.u = \( { \frac{1}{12}}^{th} \) (mass of C - 12 atom) = \( 1.66 \times 10^{-27} g \) .
Mass of electron, proton and neutron in terms of a.m.u.

S.No Atomic particles Mass(in grams) Mass(in a.m.u)

2 Neutron \( 1.674 \times 10^{-24} g \) 1.0084

Molecular weights of Gases

Gram Molecular volume method

It has been possible to determine the weight - volume relationship between gases with the help of Avogadro's hypothesis and this relation may be stated thus:
One gram molecular weight of any substance in the gaseous state will occupy a volume equal to 22.4 litres at N.T.P. This volume is known as the Gram Molecular Volume - (G.M.V.).
To calculate the molecular weight:

  1. Determine the weights of a certain volume of the gas under known conditions of temperature and pressure.

  2. Convert the volume to N.T.P. by making use of the gas equation.

  3. Calculate the weight of 22.4 litres of the gas N.T.P.

Mole

Mole is the mass of the substance containing particles equal to Avagradro's number: \( ( 6.023 \times 10^{23} ) \)
1 mole of any substance = \( ( 6.023 \times 10^{23} ) \) atoms/ molecules/ ions/ electrons, etc.

Relation between Mole and Weight

The weight of 1 mole (Avogadro number) atoms of an element is equal to its atomic weight expressed in grams (GAW).
\( 1 mole \xrightarrow{Contains} 6.023 \times 10^{23} \ atoms \xrightarrow {Weigh} \ atomic \ weight \ expressed \ in \ grams \) .
Similarly, the weight of 1 mole or Avogadro number of molecules of different elements or different compounds.

The weight of 1 mole (Avogadro number) of molecules is equal to molecular weight expressed to grams (GMW)
\( 1 mole \xrightarrow{Contains} 6.023 \times 10^{23} \ molecules \xrightarrow {Weigh} \ molecular \ weight \ expressed \ in \ grams \)
. \( \text{1 mole of any substance} \xrightarrow {Contains} 6.023 \times 10^{23} atoms/molecules \xrightarrow{Weigh}\text{Gram atomic weight/gram molecular weight respectively} \) .

Other relations related to mole

In summary equation, there are four terms. Other relations related to mole (n). By selecting two terms at a time, we can get six relations. We have such three of them. Mole - particles, mole - weight and mole - volume. i.e. weight - particles, weight - volume and volume - particles.

Relation between weight and particles

GAW or GMW of any substance contains \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) (Avogadro number (N)) particles (or) 'N' particles of any substance weigh GAW or GMW.
Examples:
16 g of oxygen contains \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) atoms (or) \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) atoms of oxygen weigh 16 g.
32 g of oxygen \( (O_2) \) gas contains \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules (or) \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of oxygen gas weigh 32 g.

Relation between weight and volume (Only for gases)

GAW or GMW of any gas occupies 22.4 litres at S.T.P. or 22.4 litres of any gas weigh GAW or GMW at S.T.P.
Examples:
2 g of hydrogen \( (H_2) \) gas occupies 22.4 litres at S.T.P. or 22.4 litres of hydrogen gas at S.T.P. weigh 2 g.
4 g of helium (He) gas occupies 22.4 litres at S.T.P. (or) 22.4 litres of helium gas at S.T.P. weigh 4 g.

Relation between volume and particular (only for gases)

22.4 litres of any gas at S.T.P. contains \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules (or) \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of any gas at S.T.P. occupy 22.4 litres of gas.
Examples:
22.4 litres of any gas at S.T.P. contains \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules at S.T.P. (or) \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of methane gas occupies 22.4 litres of volume at S.T.P.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
An atom of an element weighs \( 6.644 \times 10^{-23} g \) . Calculate the gram atomic mass of the element. Find out the number of gram atoms in 40 kg of the element.

Solution:
weight of an atom = \( 6.644 \times 10^{-23} g \)
Weight of an Avagadro number of atoms = \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \times 6.644 \times 10^{-23} = 40 \)
Gram atomic mass = 40
Number of gram atoms in 40 kg = \( \frac{40000}{40} = 1000 \)

Example 2:

Question:
Calculate the mass of

  1. single atom of sulphur

  2. single molecule of chlorine

  3. single molecule of \( CO_2 \)

  4. number of electrons in 1 mole of \( CO_2 \)

Solution:

  1. Weight of \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) atoms of sulphur = 32 g
    Weight of 1 atm of sulphur \( = \frac{32}{6.023 \times 10^{23}} = 5.31 \times 10^{-23} g \)

  2. Weight of \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of chlorine = 71 g
    Weight of single molecule \( = \frac{71}{6.023 \times 10^{23}} = 11.79 \times 10^{-23} g \)

  3. Weight of \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of \( CO_2 \) = 44 g
    Weight of 1 molecule of \( CO_2 = \frac{44}{6.023 \times 10^{23}} = 7.3 \times 10^{-23} g \)

  4. Number of electrons in one molecule of \( CO_2 = 22 \)
    Number of electrons in one molecule of \( CO_2 = 22 \times 6.023 \times 10^{23} = 132.5 \times 10^{23} \)

Example 3:

Question:
A polymer containing iron has 0.165% by weight of metal in it. Its molecular weight 67200. Find out the number of iron atoms present in each molecule of it.

Solution:
100 g of polymer contains 0.165 gm iron
67200 g polymer contains \( \frac{0.165 \times 67200}{100} = 110.8 g \)
Atomic weight of iron = 56
Number of iron atoms = \( \frac{110.8}{56} = 1.98 \approx 2 \)

Example 4:

Question:
A metal oxide has the formula \( M_2 O_3 \) It can be reduced by \( H_2 \) to metal. 0.1596 g of oxide require 6 mg of \( H_2 \) . Calculate the atomic weight of the metal.

Solution:
\( M_2 O_3 + 3 H_2 \xrightarrow 2M + 3H_2O \)
6 g hydrogen can reduce 1 mole \( M_2 O_3 \)
\( 6 \times 10^{-3} \) g hydrogen can reduce \( \frac{6 \times 10^{-3}}{6} = 10^{-3} \) moles \( M_2 O_3 \)
\( 10^{-3} \) moles \( M_2 O_3 \rightarrow \) 0.1596 g metal
1 mole \( M_2 O_3 \rightarrow \frac{0.1596}{10^{-3}} = 159.6 g \)
Amount of oxygen = 48 g
Amount of metal = 111.6 g
Atomic weight of metal = \( \frac{111.6}{2} = 55.8 \approx 56 \)

Example 5:

Question:
0.3 g of an organic compound gave (containing O, H and O) 0.66 g of \( CO_2 \) on combustion. It also contain 13.3% hydrogen. 1 litre of that compound weighs 2.67 g at STP. calculate the molecular formula.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} 44 \ g \ CO_2 \rightarrow 12 \ g \ carbon \\\\[3pt] 0.66 \ g \ CO_2 \rightarrow \frac{12 \times 0.66}{44} = 0.18 \ g \ carbon \\\\[3pt] 0.3 \ g \ organic \ compound \rightarrow \frac{0.18 \times 100}{0.3} \end{align} \)
= 60%
Element % age / Atomic wt = (Simple ratio) Element / At wt = Simple ratio

Element % Ratio Relative ratio
Carbon 60 \( \frac{60}{12} = 5 \) \( \frac{5}{1.66} = 3 \)
Hydrogen 13.3 \( \frac{13.3}{1} = 13.3 \) \( \frac{13.3}{1.66} = 8 \)
Oxygen 26.7 \( \frac{26.7}{16} = 1.66 \) \( \frac{1.66}{1.66} = 1 \)

Emp. formula = \( C_3 H_8 O \) ; EFW = 60
1 l of the compound at STP weighs 2.67 g
22.4 l of the compound \( 2.67 \times 22.4 = 60 g \) = molecular weight
Emp. formula weight = molecular formula weight = 60
\( n = \frac{EFW}{MFW} = 1 \)
\therefore Mol. formula = \( C_3 H_8 O \)

Example 6:

Question:
How many moles of the substances is present in :

  1. 49 g of glucose

  2. 25 g of \( CaCO_3 \)

  3. 196 g of \( H_2 SO_4 \)

Solution:
number of moles (n) = \( \frac{weight (m)}{Gram \ molecular \ weight (GMW)} \) .....(1)
Applying (1) to each of the given cases,

  1. No.of moles of glucose \( (n) = \frac{m}{GMW} = \frac{49}{180} = 0.27 \)

  2. No.of moles of \( CaCO_3 = \frac{m}{GMW} = \frac{25}{100} = 0.25 \)

  3. No.of moles of \( H_2SO_4 = \frac{m}{GMW} = \frac{196}{98} = 2 \)

Example 7:

Question:
Calculate the mass of 0.5 moles of calcium carbonate.

Solution:
Number of moles = n = 0.5
Gram molecular weight of \( CaCO_3 \) = GMW = 100 g
Mass = m = ?
We know, \( n = \frac{m}{GMW} \Rightarrow m = n \times GMW = 0.5 \times 100 = 50 g \)
Therefore, the mass of 0.5 moles of calcium carbonate is 50 g

Example 8:

Question:
Ratio of weights of \( O_2 \) and \( O_3 \) is 1:1. What is the ratio of their number of moles?

Solution:
Ratio of weights of \( O_2 \) and \( O_3 = m_{O_2} : m_{O_3} = 1 : 1 \) (given)
Ratio of number of moles of \( O_2 \) and \( O_3 = n_{O_2} : n_{O_3} = ? \)
Terms in the problems are weight (m) and number of moles (n) and the formula connecting these terms is, \( n = \frac{m}{GMW} \) .......(1)
Let us note the gram molecular weights of the given molecules.
GMW of \( O_2 \) = 32 g GMW of \( O_3 \) = 48 g
From equation (1), we get \( n \propto \frac{1}{GMW} \) (mass of oxygen = mass of ozone = constant)
\( \Rightarrow \frac{n_{o_2}}{n_{o_3}} = \frac{GMW_2}{GMW_1} \) ......(2)
Applying equation (2) to the given problem, we get,
\( \frac{n_{O_2}}{n_{O_3}} = \frac{GMW_{O_3}}{GMW_{O_2}} = \frac{48}{32} = \frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow n_{O_2} : n_{O_3} = 3 : 2 \)
Therefore, the ration of number of moles of equal weights of oxygen to ozone is 3:2

Example 9:

Question:
How many molecules and atoms are present in 0.25 moles of hydrogen gas ?

Solution:
Terms in the problems are molecules and moles.
The standard relation connecting the above two term is : 1 mole of any substance \( \xrightarrow{contains} 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) molecules
0.25 moles of hydrogen gas \( \xrightarrow{contains} \) x molecules
\( \Rightarrow x = \frac{6.023 \times 10^{23} \times 0.25}{1} = 1.5075 \times 10^{23} \) molecules.
Therefore, 0.25 moles of hydrogen gas conatin \( 1.5075 \times 10^{23} \) molecules.
1 mole of \( H_2 \) contains \( 2 \times 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) atoms
Therefore, 0.25 moles of \( H_2 \) has \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \times 2 \times 0.25 = 3.115 \times 10^{23} \) atoms.

Example 10:

Question:
0.125 moles of any gas at S.T.P occupies Y litres, Find Y.

Solution:
Terms in the problems are moles and volume at STP.
The standard relation connecting the above two terms is :
1 mole of any gas at S.T.P. \( \xrightarrow{occupies} \) 22.4 litres
\therefore 0.125 moes of any gas at S.T.P \( \xrightarrow{occupies} \) Y litres.
\( Y = \frac{0.125 \times 22.4}{1} = 2.8 \ litres. \)
Therefore, 0.125 moles of any gas at S.T.P occupies 2.8 litres.
