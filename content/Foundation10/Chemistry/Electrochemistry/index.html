S.No Common Misconception Fact
1 In the electrolysis of aqueous sodium chloride, cations discharged at cathode is sodium and anion discharged at anode is chloride. On Electrolysis on aqueous sodium chloride two cations \( \left ( Na^+, \ H^+ \right ) \) and two anions \( \left ( Cl^-, \ OH^- \right ) \) are produced. These ions migrate to their respective electrodes. However, Hydrogen is discharged at cathode in presence to sodium and hydroxyl ion is discharged at the anode in preference to chloride as sodium and chloride are placed below their respective ions in the electrochemical series.
2 Electro chemical corrosion is a cathodic dissolution of the metal. The electrochemical corrosion is considered as the anodic dissolution of metal undergoing corrosion. The anodic dissolution is an oxidation process of the metal and the metal getting corroded undergoes oxidation to the metal ion under the conditions of corrosion.
\( M \rightarrow M^{+n} + ne^- \)
Therefore corrosion can be defined in electrochemical terms as anodic dissolution of the metal.

Introduction

Metals conduct electric current without any changes occurring in the metal. The conduction in them is due to flow of electrons. Unlike metals solution of salts, acids and alkalies too conduct electricity. The conduction in such cases involves chemical changes. The current conduction is due to movement of ions which are produced by the dissociation of these compounds. The conduction of electric current and their effects are studied under topic electrochemistry.

Electrochemistry deals with three aspects.

  1. Study of electrolytic cells

  2. Study of galvanic cells

  3. Applications of electrochemical processes in various fields such as metallurgy.

Electric Conductors

The substance which allows the electric current to flow through it is called electric conductor. These conductors may be solid metals, fused metals or fused salts or aqueous solutions of salts, acids and alkalies. Based on this fact electric conductors are classified into two categories mainly. These are :

S.No Electronic Conductors Electrolytic Conductors
1 Flow of electrons without transfer of matter. Flow of electrons with transfer of matter.
2 Conduction in solid state. No conduction in solid state.
3 Conductivity decreases with temperature. Conductivity increases with temperature.
4 No chemical change takes place. Chemical change (Redox reaction) takes place.

Electrochemical Process

Electrolysis

The decomposition of a chemical compound in the molten state or in the solution state into its constituent elements (generally) under the influence of an applied emf (electro motive force) is called electrolysis.

Mechanism of Electrolysis

An electrolte on dissolution in water or on fusion furnishes cations and anions. These ions are free to move and when electricity is passed through the solution, ions move towards the oppositely charged electrodes. This movement of ions in solution is responsible for the conduction of current. Thus, during the passage of current through electrolytic solution :

  1. The cations migrate towards cathode, where they gain electrons (Reduction).
    \( M^{n+} + ne^- \rightarrow M \)

  2. The anions migrate towards anode, where they lose electrons (Oxidation).
    \( A^{n-} \rightarrow A + ne^- (oxidation) \)
    The product of electrolysis depend upon:

    1. Nature of electrodes (attacked or non - attacked electrodes)

    2. Nature of electrolytic solution (molten or aqueous solution)

    3. Charge density flown during the process.

    4. Concentration of solution used.

Examples
During the electrolysis of aqueous NaCl solution, the \( Na^+ \ and \ Cl^- \) becomes mobile ions followed by following reactions at cathode and anode.
\( \begin{align} NaCl_{l} \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^- \\\\[3pt] Na^+ + e^- \rightarrow Na (cathode : reduction) \\\\[3pt] 2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2 + 2e^- ( anode : Oxidation) \end{align} \)

Arrhenius theory of electrolytic dissociation

  1. Electrolyte dissociates into positive and negative ions.

  2. The fraction of molecules which dissociate into ions is called degree of dissociation.

  3. At anode, negative ions lose electrons and oxidation occurs.

  4. At cathode, positive ions gain electrons and reduction occurs.

  5. Passage of electric current brings about a chemical reaction. That means, there occurs a non spontaneous redox reaction in electrolytic cell.

Faraday's laws of Electrolysis

Faraday's laws of electrolysis gives a quantitative relationship between the amount of electricity passed and the substance deposited or liberated at the electrodes.

Faraday's 1st law of electrolysis

The amount of substance deposited at the electrode is proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.
\( m \propto ct \)
\( m = Zct \)
Where 'm' is mass of substance deposited, 'c' is current strength, 't' is time of flow and 'Z' is electrochemical equivalent.

Electrochemical equivalent

Electrochemical equivalent is the amount of substance deposited or liberated at an electrode by the passage of 1 coulomb of electricity.
(1 coulomb = 1 ampere of current for 1 s time).

Units of electrochemical equivalent - \( kilogram \ coulomb^{-1} \) .
Equivalent weight

Equivalent weight is the amount of substance deposited when 1 faraday of electricity is passed through the electrolyte.
1 faraday = 96500 coulombs
since, 96500 C discharges E ions.
\( w = \frac{Ect}{96500} = \frac{Ect}{F} \)
where E = Equivalent weight
\( \begin{align} Equivalent \ weight = \frac{Molecular \ weight}{\text{No. of electrons involve in electrode reaction}} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ 1 C \ discharges \frac{E}{96500} g \ of \ ion .i.e. z = \frac{E}{96500} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ W = \frac{E.i.t}{96500} \\\\[3pt] or \ \frac{W}{E} = \frac{Q}{96500} = \frac{Q}{F} \end{align} \)
Note

  1. If E is in g then W is obtained in g. And if E is in kg then W is obtained in kg.

  2. No. of equivalents oxidized or reduced i.e., \( \frac{W}{E} = \frac{i.t}{96500} \)

Faraday's 2nd law of electrolysis

When same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the amount of substances deposited at the respective electrodes is directly proportional to the equivalent weights of substances.
\( \frac{m_1}{m_2} = \frac{E_1}{E_2} \)

Electrochemical cells or Galvanic cells

When plates of two dissimilar metals are placed in a conducting liquid such as an aqueous solution of a salt or an acid, the resulting system becomes a source of electricity. This source of electricity is called as voltaic cell or galvanic cell in honor of Volta and Galvanic respectively. Electrochemical cell is a device which makes use of a spontaneous redox reaction for the generation of electrical energy. In this device chemical energy is transformed into electrical energy. Daniel cell is one example for such a device. These cells are also referred to as galvanic cells. The spontaneous oxidation - reduction reaction made use in the working of Daniel cell,
\( \underset{(s)}{Zn} + \underset{(aq)}{CuSO_4} \rightarrow \underset{(aq)}{ZnSO_4} + \underset{(s)}Cu \)
An electrochemical cell consists of two half - cells and these are electrically connected to each other by a salt bridge. Oxidation reaction occurs in one half - cell and reduction reaction occurs in the other half cell.
Example
In the Daniel cell, the two half cells are \( Zn/ZnSO_{4(aq)} \) (zinc rod dipped in zinc sulphate solution) and \( Cu/CuSO_{4(aq)} \) (Cu rod dipped in \( CuSO_4 \) solution).
In \( Zn/ZnSO_{4(aq)} \) half cell, oxidation reaction occurs
\( Zn \leftrightharpoons Zn^{++} + 2e^{-} \)
In \( Cu/CuSO_{4(aq)} \) half cell, reduction reaction occurs
\( Cu^{++} + 2e \leftrightharpoons Cu \)
The total oxidation - reduction reaction is written as
\( Zn + Cu_{(aq)}^{++} \leftrightharpoons Zn_{(aq)}^{++} + Cu \)
( \( Cu^{++} \ and \ Zn^{++} \) are from \( CuSO_4 \ and \ ZnSO_4 \) respectively)
or \( Zn + CuSO_{4(aq)} \leftrightharpoons ZnSO_{4(aq)} + Cu \)
The two half cells are also referred to as single electrodes.

Representation and notation of electrochemical cells with salt bridge

A daniel cell is constructed by combining a Zn electrode and a Cu electrode. As per IUPAC convention, electrode where oxidation occurs is written at the left hand side (LHS) and where reduction occurs is at the right hand side (RHS). A set of parallel lines is written in between these two single electrodes to indicate the salt bridge. Therefore daniel cell is represented as :
\( Zn | Zn_{(aq)}^{++} || Cu_{(aq)}^{++} | Cu \)

Salt Bridge

Salt bridge is an U shaped glass tube filled with gelatinous substances like \( KCl, \ KNO_3 \ or \ NH_4N0_3 \) . Salt bridge prevents intermixing of solutions of the half cells.

Single Electrode Potential

  1. Potential developed at the interface of the element and the solution of its own ions.

  2. Factors

    1. Chemical nature of elements

    2. No. of electrons transferred

    3. Concentration of ions

    4. Temperature

  3. Based on degree of dissociation Electrolytes are of 2 types.

    1. strong electrolytes

    2. weak electrolytes

  4. For weak electrolytes The degree of dissociation increases with dilution.

Preferential discharge of ions

  1. When there is more than one cation or anion, the discharge of ions at the electrodes takes place based on their discharge potentials.

  2. Discharge potential gives a measure of energy required for its discharge at the electrode.

  3. Order of discharge potentials of cations - \( K^+ > Na^+ > Ca^{+2} > Mg^{+2} > Al^{+3} > Zn^{+2} > H^+ > Cu^{+2} > Hg^{+2} > Ag. \)

Order of discharge potentials of anions - \( SO_4^{-2} > NO_3^- > OH^- > Cl^- > Br^- > I^- . \)

Reversible and Irreversible Cells

On the basis of principle of thermodynamic reversibility, cells may be of two types, reversible and irreversible.

A cell is said to be reversible if the current drawn from it is infinitesimally small and cell reaction always remains virtually in a state of equilibrium.

The nature of cell, whether reversible or irreversible, is checked by connecting it to the external source of EMF, acting in opposite direction. In this situation, there may be three cases.

  1. \( E_{applied} = E_{cell} \) , in this case no current is given out by the cell and no chemical reaction takes place in the cell.

  2. \( E_{applied} \) is infinitesimally less than \( E_{cell} \) , in such case, extremely small current is given out by the cell and corresponding small amount of a chemical reaction takes place in the cell.

  3. \( E_{applied} \) is infinitesimally greater \( E_{cell} \) , in such case, an extermely small current flows through the cell in opposite direction and a small amount of chemical reaction also takes place in reverse direction. e.g.,

Daniel cell, lead storage battery, cell composed of \( Zn \ and \ Ag/AgCl \) electrode dipped in aqueous \( ZnCl_2 \) solution etc.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Two metals 'A' and 'B' undergo oxidation and reduction when coupled with 'C' respectively. 'A' can liberate hydrogen gas when it reacts with \( H_2SO_4 \) . 'C' and 'B' cannot liberate hydrogen gas on reaction with \( H_2SO_4 \) . Predict the positions of A, B, C in the electrochemical series. Justify.
Solution:
A is an active metal present above hydrogen in electrochemical series. B and C are present below hydrogen. Since 'B' undergoes reduction when coupled with 'C', 'C' is present above 'B' in electrochemical series. Reduction potential of 'B' is maximum followed by 'C' followed by 'A'.

Example 2:

Question:
Two brass spoons are taken and electroplated with silver. In one case, \( AgNO_3 \) , is Used as electrolyte and in the other, a mixture of NaCN + AgCN is used as electrolyte. When observation do you find ? Justify.

Solution:
In the first case, \( AgNO_3 \) is a strong electrolyte and dissociates completely to give Ag ions. The concentration of \( Ag^+ \) ions is more and when electroplating is done, a thin uniform coating cannot be formed. When ,a mixture of NaCN + AgCN is used being Weak electrolyte, it ionises partially to give less concentration of \( Ag^+ \) ions. Electroplating process will be slow and thin uniform coating can be formed.

Example 3:

Question:
Mercuric oxide and lead oxide are less stable and decompose on heating whereas magnesium oxide and aluminium oxide are thermally stable. Explain with respect to the electrochemical series.

Solution:
Mercury and lead are present below hydrogen in the electrochemical series. The oxidation potentials are low and their tendency to get oxidized is less. Hence their oxides are less stable. Magnesium and aluminium are present above hydrogen in the electro chemical series. They have higher values of oxidation potentials and hence their tendency to undergo oxidation is more. That is why their oxides are highly stable.

Example 4:

Question:
A current of 5.8 amperes is passed for 4 hours between nickel electrodes in 500 ml of 3 mole solution of nickel nitrate. What will be the molarity of the solution at the end of electrolysis?

Solution:
\( \begin{align} C = 5.8 A, \ t = 4 \times 60 \times 60 = 14400s \\\\[3pt] \text{Wt. of nickel in the solution} = \frac{3 \times 58 \times 500}{1000} = 87 \ g \\\\[3pt] Q = 5.8 \times 4 \times 60 \times 60 = 83520 \ Ca \\\\[3pt] \text{Wt. of nickel deposited} = 23.4 \ g \\\\[3pt] \text{Remaining amount of nickel} = 87 - 23.4 = 63.6 \ g \\\\[3pt] \text{Molarity of solution} = \frac{63.6}{58} \times \frac{1000}{500} = \frac{63.6}{29} = 2.19 \ M \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
0.25 moles of electrons were passed through three electrolytic solutions connected in series. The solutions correspond to stable metal salt solutions belonging to IB group. Calculate the amount of each metal deposited at respective cathodes of each cell. Also calculate volumes of gases liberated at anode at STP provided chloride salts are taken and the solutions are saturated.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} \text{IB gropu elements} - CU, \ Ag, \ Au \\\\[3pt] \text{Stable ions} - Cu^{+2}, \ Ag^{+}, \ Au^{+3} \\\\[3pt] \text{1 mole of electrons} - 31.75 \ g \ Cu \\\\[3pt] \text{0.25 electrons} - 31.75 \times 0.25 = 7.94 \ g \\\\[3pt] \text{1 mole of electrons} - 108 \ g \ Ag \\\\[3pt] \text{0.25 electrons} - 108 \times 0.25 = 27 \ g \\\\[3pt] \text{1 mole of electrons} - 65.67 \ g \ Au \\\\[3pt] \text{0.25 electrons} - 65.67 \times 0.25 = 16.42 \ g \\\\[3pt] 1 \ faraday - 11.2 \ I \ Cl_2 \\\\[3pt] 0.25 \ faraday - 11.2 \times 0.25 = 2.8 \ I \ Cl_2 \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
What changes occur at the electrodes and in the solution, when a solution of \( CuSO_4 \) is electrolysed between copper electrode using 10 A current for 1 hour

Solution:
By using copper electrodes, electrode reactions are
At anode \( Cu \rightarrow Cu^{2+} + 2e^{-} \)
At cathode \( Cu^{2+} + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Cu \)
and number of equivalents of Cu oxidised at anode are equal to the number of equivalent of \( Cu^{2+} \) ions reduced at cathode. Thus, the concentration of \( CuSO_4 \) in solution remains unchanged.
\( Charge \ passed = i \times t = 10 \times 60 \times 60 = 36000 \ C \)
or Number of Faraday passed = \( \frac{36000}{96500} = 0.373 \ F \)
Since 2F causes oxidation of one mole copper at anode and reduction of one mole \( Cu^{2+} \) at cathode, number of mole of \( Cu \) and \( Cu^{2+} \) oxidised and reduced at respective electrode by
\( o.373 \ F = \frac{0.373}{2} = 0.186 \ mol \)
Thus, 0.186 mol of Cu is dissolved from anode and \( 0.186 \ of \ Cu^{2+} \) is deposited at cathode.

Example 7:

Question:
The same quantity of electricity that liberated 2.158 g Ag was passed through a gold salt , and 1.314 g gold was deposited. The equivalent weight of Ag is 107.9. Calculate oxidation of Au in the salt?(At. wt. of Au = 197)

Solution:
Number of equivalents of gold deposited = number of equivalents of silver deposited
\( \begin{align} i.e, \ \frac{W_{gold}}{E_{gold}} = \frac{W_{silver}}{E_{silver}} \\\\[3pt] E_{gold} = \frac{E_{silver} \times W_{gold}}{w_{silver}} = \frac{107.9 \times 1.314}{2.158} = 65.7 \\\\[3pt] Equivalent \ wt. = \frac{Atomic \ wt.}{Oxidation \ no. \ of \ Au \ in \ salt} \\\\[3pt] Thus, \ Ox.no, \ of \ Au = \frac{Atomic \ wt.}{E_{gold}} = \frac{197}{65.7} = +3 \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Question:
How long will it take for a uniform current of 6.00 A to deposit 78 g of gold from a solution of \( AuCl_4 \) ? What mass of chlorine gas will be formed simultaneously at anode of the cell?(Atomic weight of Au = 197)

Solution:
Reaction takes place at electrodes are:
At cathode \( AuCl_3^{-} + 3e^{-} \rightarrow Au + 3Cl^{-} \)
At anode \( Cl^{-} - e^{-} \rightarrow \frac{1}{2}Cl_2 \)
For the deposition of 197 g(1 mol) Au = 3F charge is required thus for the deposition of 78 g Au, charge required
\( \begin{align} = \frac{3}{197} \times 78 = 1.19 \ F \\\\[3pt] or \ \ \ 1.19 \times 96500 \ coulomb. \\\\[3pt] From \ \ \ q = i \times t \\\\[3pt] t = \frac{q}{i} = \frac{1.19 \times 96500}{6} = 19100 \ sec, \end{align} \)
By 1 F charge, 35.5 g \( Cl_2 \) gas is formed, thus from 1.19F mass of chlorine gas formed = \( 35.5 \times 1.19 \times = 42.24 \ g \)
