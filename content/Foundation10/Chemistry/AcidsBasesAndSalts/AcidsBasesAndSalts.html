Introduction

In our surrounding we observe a wide variety of different characteristic substances. In these substances we mainly contain two kinds viz elements and compounds.

Chemical compounds we come across can be broadly classified into three categories: acids, bases and salts. The preparation, physical properties, chemical properties differ from one compound to another. Apart from this how chemical compounds reacts with one another to form products are given in this chapter.

Acids

Acids belong to an important class of compounds. The word acid is derived from the Latin word acidus meaning sour. All acids have one property in common - they are sour to taste. Numerous acids occur in nature. Oranges and lemons contain citric acid and apples contain malic acids. Luscious tomatoes are rich in oxalic acid. Tamarind and grapes contain tartaric acid. Lactic acid is found in sour milk.

Classification of Acids

  1. Depending upon the elements present, acids may be classified as follows.

    1. Oxyacid : Acids that contain both hydrogen and oxygen are called oxyacids. For example, nitric acid \( (HNO_3) \) , sulphuric acid \( (H_2SO_4) \) and phosphoric acid \( (H_3PO_4) \) are oxyacids.

    2. Hydracid : Acids that contain hydrogen and other monometallic element (s), except oxygen, are called hydracids. For example, hydrochloric acid (HCl) and hydrocyanide acid (HCN) are hydracids.

Organic and inorganic acids :

All sour things like tamarind, tomato, lemon, grapes, Oranges and apples that we use in our daily food contain acids. These acids are organic acids. Some of the common acids that are generally used in the laboratory are hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid \( (H_2SO_4) \) and nitric acid \( (HNO_3) \) . These are inorganic acids, also called mineral acids.

Concentrated and dilute acids:

An acid solution may be concentrated or diluted depending upon the amount of the acid present in the solution. Concentrated and dilute solutions of acids are generally used in laboratories.

Strong and weak acids:

The strength of an acid is determined by the amount of hydrogen ions \( (H^+) \) that the acid provides when dissolved in water.
Some of the acids, when dissolved in water, get almost completely dissociated to provide hydrogen ions. These acids are called strong acids. There are some acids which when dissolved in water, are only incompletely dissociated to give hydrogen ions. These are called weak acids.

Basicity of an Acid

The basicity of an acid is the number of replaceable hydrogen atoms present in a molecule, of the acid.

Acid Basicity
\( HCl \) 1
\( HNO_3 \) 1
\( H_2SO_4 \) 2
\( H_3PO_4 \) 3

Preparation of Acids

There are several methods for preparing acids.

Synthetic method :

In the synthetic method, acids are prepared by direct combination of elements. For example, hydrogen and chlorine react together under the action of an electric spark to produce hydrogen chloride gas which is absorbed by water to give hydrochloric acid.
\( H_2 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{electric \ spark} 2HCl \)

From metallic oxides :

Acids are formed when nonmetallic oxides are dissolved in water.
\( \begin{align} \underset{sulphur \ dioxide}{SO_2} + H_2O \rightarrow \underset{sulphurous \ acid}{H_2SO_3} \\\\[3pt] \underset{sulphur \ trioxide}{SO_3} + H_2O \rightarrow \underset{sulphuric \ acid}{H_2SO_4} \\\\[3pt] \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} + H_2O \rightarrow \underset{carbonic \ acid}{H_2CO_3} \end{align} \)

By the action of an acid on the salt of another acid :

An acid having higher boiling point can react with the salt of an acid of lower boiling point to produce an acid. For example, NaCl is a salt of HCl. The boiling point of HCl is lower than that of \( H_2SO_4 \) . When NaCl (salt of HCl) reacts with \( H_2SO_4, \ HCl \) is formed.
\( H_2SO_4 + NaCl \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + HCl \)

Bases

Bases are metallic oxides or hydroxides, which react with acids to give salt and water. Bases that are soluble in water are called alkalis. For example, sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide, calcium hydroxide are soluble in water. Therefore, they are alkalis, But bases like copper hydroxide \( (Cu(OH)_2) \) , ferric hydroxide \( (Fe(OH)_3) \) , aluminium hydroxide \( (Al(OH)_3) \) do not dissolve in water. They are therefore, not alkalis.

Hence, all alkalis are bases, but all bases are not alkalis.

Acidity of a Base

The acidity of a base is defined as the number of hydroxyl (OH) groups preset in the molecule of the base.

In each molecule of NaOH, KOH and \( NH_4OH \) only one hydroxyl group is present. Therefore, the acidity of all these bases is 1. In \( Ca(OH)_2 \ and \ Ba(OH)_2 \) there are two hydroxyl groups present in each molecule. Hence, their acidity is 2.

Preparation

By the direct union of a metal with oxygen :

Some metals when heated in air or oxygen form the oxides of the metals.
\( \begin{align} 4Na + O_2 \rightarrow \underset{sodium \ oxide}{2Na_2O} \\\\[3pt] 4K + O_2 \rightarrow \underset{Potassium \ oxide}{2K_2O} \end{align} \)
These oxides when dissolved in water make the hydroxides of metals.
\( \begin{align} Na_2O + H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH \\\\[3pt] K_2O + H_2O \rightarrow 2KOH \end{align} \)

By the action of water or steam on some active metals :

Some active metals like sodium and potassium react with cold water to form hydroxides with the evolution of hydrogen gas.
\( 2Na + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + H_2 \uparrow \)

By heating carbonates of some metals :

When calcium carbonate is heated, calcium oxide and carbon dioxide are formed.
\( CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2 \uparrow \)

By the action of an alkali on a salt solution

For example, when an aqueous solution of sodium hydroxide is added to an aqueous solution of magnesium sulphate, magnesium hydroxide gets precipitated and sodium sulphate remains in the solution.
\( MgSO_4 + 2NaOH \rightarrow \underset{Magnesium \ hydroxide}{Mg(OH)_2} \downarrow + \underset{sodium \ sulphate}{Na_2SO_4} \)

Neutralization :

Reaction of an acid with a base to produce salt and water.

\( Acid + Base \rightarrow Salt + Water + Energy \)

  1. Strong acids and strong bases undergo complete ionisation and produce large concentration of \( H^+ \ and \ OH^- \) ions respectively.
    Ex: \( HNO_3 \rightarrow H^+ + NO_3^- \)

  2. Weak acids and weak bases undergo incomplete ionisation and produce less concentration of \( H^+ \ and \ OH- \) ions.
    \( \begin{align} CH_3COOH \leftrightarrows CH_3COO^- + H^+ \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH \leftrightarrows NH_4^+ + OH^- \end{align} \)

Arrhenious theory of acids and bases :

  1. Acid - Produces \( H^+ \) ions in aqueous solution
    Ex: \( \begin{align} HCl \rightarrow H^+ + Cl^- \\\\[3pt] CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow 2H^+ CO_3^{-2} \end{align} \)

  2. Base - Produces \( OH^- \) ions in aqueous solution
    Ex: \( \begin{align} NH_3 + H_2O \rightarrow NH_4^+ + OH^- \\\\[3pt] NaOH \rightarrow Na^+ + OH^- \end{align} \)

Ionic product of water

Partial ionisation of water gives \( H^+ \ and \ OH^- \) ions in equal concentrations.
\( \begin{align} H_2O \leftrightarrows H^+ + OH^- \\\\[3pt] k_w = \frac{[H^+][OH^-]}{H_2O} \\\\[3pt] k[H_2O] = k_w = [H^+][OH^-] \\\\[3pt] At \ 25^{\circ}C, \ [H^+] = [OH^-] = 10^{-7} \ moles \ I^{-1} \\\\[3pt] k_w = 10^{-7} \times 10^{-7} = 10^{-14} \ mole^2 \ I^{-2} \\\\[3pt] At \ 25^{\circ}C, \ Neutral \ solution \rightarrow [H^+] = [OH^-] = 10^{-7} \ moles \ I^{-1} \\\\[3pt] Acidic \ Solution \rightarrow [H^+] > [OH^-] \\\\[3pt] [H^+] < 10^{-7} \\\\[3pt] Basic \ Solution \rightarrow [H^+] < [OH^-] \\\\[3pt] [OH^-] > 10^{-7} \end{align} \)

\( p^H \)

It was introduced by Sorensen to avoid use of negative exponents in concentrations \( p^H \) is negative logarithm of \( H^+ \) ion concentration to base 10.

\( p^H = - log_{10} [H^+] \)
Ex: \( \begin{align} [H^+] = 10^{-4} \\\\[3pt] P^H = - log 10^{-4} = 4 \\\\[3pt] At \ 25^{\circ}C, \ nuetral \ solution \rightarrow [H^+] = 10^{-7}; \ P^H = 7 \\\\[3pt] Acidic \ solution \rightarrow [H^+] > 10^{-7}; \ P^H < 7 \\\\[3pt] Basic \ solution [H^+] < 10^{-7}; \ p^H > 7 \end{align} \)

When a strong acid and a strong base are mixed, to calculate the pH of a mixed solution

  1. The normality of the mixture of the strong acid and a strong base is given by
    \( (N) = \left [ \frac{V_A N_A - V_B N_B}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] \)
    If the \( V_A N_A > V_B N_B \) and where \( V_A, \ N_A \) are volume and normality of strong acid, \( V_B, \ N_B \) are volume and normality of strong base.
    Then \( [H^+] \) = normality of the mixed solution = N
    \( \therefore pH = - log[H^+] = - log(N) = - log \left [ \frac{(V_A N_A - V_B N_B)}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] \)

  2. The normality of the mixture of the strong acid and a strong base mixture is given by
    \( (N) = \left [ \frac{ (V_B N_B - V_A N_A)}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] \)
    If the \( V_B N_B > V_A N_A \) and \( V_A, \ N_A, \ V_B \ and \ N_B \) have the same significance.
    Then \( [OH^+] \) = normality of the mixed solution = N
    \( \therefore pOH = - log[OH^-] = - log(N) = - log \left [ \frac{ (V_B N_B - V_A N_A)}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] \) pH + pOH = 14.0
    \( \therefore \ pH = (14.0 - pOH) \)

Salts

salt is a substance formed by the neutralization of an acid with a base. It is formed by the replacement of the hydrogen atoms of the acid by metal atoms.
\( \underset{Alkali \ or \ base}{NaOH} + \underset{Acid}{HCl} \xrightarrow{Neutralisation \ reaction} \underset{Salt}{NaCl} + \underset{water}{H_2O} \)

Classification

Simple salts

Simple salts are classified into the following types :

Normal salts

No replaceable hydrogen or \( OH^- \) ions.
Ex: \( 2NH_4OH + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow (NH_4)_2SO_4 + 2H_2O \)

Acidic salts

Contain replaceable hydrogen. They react with bases.
Ex: \( NaOH + H_SO_4 \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + H_O \)

Basic salts

Contain replaceable \( OH^- \) ions. They react with acids.
Ex: \( Cu(OH)_2 + HNO_3 \rightarrow Cu(OH)NO_3 + H_2O \)

Double salts:

Mixture of two or more simple salts.
Ex: \( K_2SO_4, \ Al_2(SO_4)_3. 24H_2O \)

Mixed salts:

Salts containing more than one acidic or basic radicals.
Ex : Bleaching powder

Complex salts:

One complex ion and one or more simple ions.
Ex: \( K_4[Fe(CN)_6] \)

Preparation of Salts

By the reaction between metal and acid

Certain metals (for example, Zn and Mg )react with \( HCl \ or \ H_2SO_4 \) to form salt and water.
\( \begin{align} Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2+ H_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Zn + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow ZnSO_4 + H_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

By the reaction between an acid and base :

All acid - base reactions (neutralization reactions) produce salts.
\( \begin{align} NaOH + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O \\\\[3pt] CuO + 2HCl \rightarrow CuCl_2 + H_2O \end{align} \)

By the direct union of a metal and a nonmetal:

Sodium and chlorine combine directly to form sodium chloride.
\( 2Na + Cl_2 \rightarrow 2NaCl \)
Similarly, when sulphur is heated with iron filings, ferrous sulphide (FeS) is formed
\( Fe + S \rightarrow FeS \)

By the union between an acidic oxide and a basic oxide :

A salt is produced by reaction of an acidic oxide with a basic oxide.
\( \begin{align} \underset{carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} + \underset{Calcium \ oxide}{CaO} \rightarrow \underset{Calcium \ carbonate}{CaCO_3} \\\\[3pt] \underset{sulphur \ trioxide}{SO_3} + \underset{sodium \ oxide}{Na_2O} \rightarrow \underset{sodium \ sulphate}{Na_2SO_4} \end{align} \)

By the reaction between a metal and a base

When zinc is heated with an aqueous solution of NaOH, sodium zincate (salt) is formed with the evolution of hydrogen gas.
\( Zn + 2NaOH \rightarrow \underset{sodium \ sulphate}{Na_2ZnO_2} + H_2 \uparrow \)

Properties of Salts

Reaction with an acid :

When a salt reacts with an acid, another salt and acid are formed.
\( \begin{align} NaCl + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + HCl \text{(at low temperature)} \\\\[3pt] 2NaCl + H_2SO4 \rightarrow Na_2S0_4 + 2HCl \text{(at high temperature )} \end{align} \)

Reaction with a base :

A salt reacts with a base to produce another salt and base.
\( (NH_4)_2SO_4 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SO_4 + 2NH_4OH \)

Reaction with a metal :

Sometimes, a salt solution may react with a metal. For example, when an iron nail is dipped into an aqueous solution of copper sulphate, copper gets deposited on the surface of the nail and the ferrous sulphate formed remains in the solution.
\( CuSO_4 +Fe \rightarrow FeSO_4 + Cu \downarrow \)
A normal salt can give either an acidic or a basic or a neutral solution.
A normal salt derived from a strong acid and a strong base gives a neutral solution. For example, the aqueous solutions of \( NaCl \ and \ K_2SO_4 \) are neutral to litmus.
A normal salt derived from a weak acid and a strong base gives an alkaline solution. example, the aqueous solutions of both sodium carbonate \( (Na_2CO_3) \) and sodium acetate \( (CH_3COONa) \) are alkaline.
\( \begin{align} Na_2CO_3 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + CO_2 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] CH_3COONa + H_2O \rightarrow CH_3COOH + NaOH \end{align} \)
A normal salt derived from a strong acid and a weak base gives an acidic solution. For example, both aluminium chloride \( (AlCl_3) \) and ammonium chloride \( (NH_4Cl) \) make acidic aqueous solutions.
\( \begin{align} AgCl_3 + 3H_2O \rightarrow Al(OH)_3 + 3HCl \\\\[3pt] NH_4Cl + H_2O \rightarrow NH_4OH + HCl \end{align} \)
Solutions of acidic salts are acidic to litmus, i.e., these solutions turn blue litmus paper red. For example, a solution of sodium hydrogensulphate \( (NaHSO_4) \) turns blue litmus paper red.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Calculate the pH value of \( 10^{-8} M HCl \)

Solution:

\( \begin{align} HCl \rightarrow H^+ + Cl^- \\\\[3pt] [H^+] = 10^{-8} \\\\[3pt] H_2O \rightarrow H^+ + OH^- \\\\[3pt] [H^+] = 10^{-7} \\\\[3pt] [H^+] = 10^{-8} + 10^{-7} = 10^{-7}(1 + 0.1) = 1.1 \times 10^{-7} \\\\[3pt] pH = 7 - log 1.1 \ \ \ \ log 1.1 = 0.0414 \\\\[3pt] pH = 7.0 - 0.0414 = 6.9586 \ or \ 6.96 \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Question:
Calculate the pH values of the following solutions.

  1. 0.049g of \( H_2SO_4 \) in 200 ml of solution at \( 25^{\circ} C \) .

  2. pH of barium hydroxide solution if 10 ml of this solution nuetralises 20 ml of 0.01M HCl solution at \( 25^{\circ}C \)

Solution:

  1. Normality of \( \begin{align} H_2SO_4 = \frac{0.049}{49} \times \frac{1000}{200} = 0.005N \\\\[3pt] pH = - log 5 \times 10^{-3} \\\\[3pt] = 3 - log 5 = 3 - 0.6990 = 2.301 \end{align} \)

  2. According to law of neutralisation:
    \( \begin{align} V_a N_a = V_b N_b \\\\[3pt] 20 \times 0.01 = 10 \times N_b \\\\[3pt] N_b = \frac{20 \times 0.01}{10} = 0.02 \\\\[3pt] pOH = - log 2 \times 10^{-2} = 2 - 0.3010 = 1.7 \\\\[3pt] pH = 14 - 1.7 = 12.3 \end{align} \)

Example 3:

Question:
A solution of sodium carbonate is prepared by dissolving 0.53 g in 100 ml of the solution. To this 200 ml of 0.001 M NaOH is added. What is the \( p^H \) of resulting mixture ?

Solution:

\( \begin{align} N_{Na_2CO_3} = \frac{0.53}{53} \times \frac{1000}{100} = 0.1 \\\\[3pt] V_1 N_1 = 0.1 \times 100 = 10 V_2 N_2(NaOH) = 200 \times 0.001 = 0.2 \\\\[3pt] [OH^-] = \frac{10 + 0.2}{300} = \frac{10.2}{300} = 3.4 \times 10^{-2} \\\\[3pt] pOH = 2 - 0.5315 = 1.4685 \ say \ 1.47 \\\\[3pt] pH = 14 - 1.47 = 12.53 \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Question:
100 ml of solution of pH = 2 and 400 ml of solution of pH = 9 are mixed at \( 25^{\circ}C \) . The resultant solution is diluted to one litre. Calculate the pH of solution.

Solution:

\( \begin{align} N_1 = 10^{-2}, \ V_1 N_1 = 100 \times 10^{-4} = 10^{-2} = [OH^-] = N_2 = 10^{-3} \\\\[3pt] V_2 N_2 = 400 \times 10^{-3} = 0.4 \\\\[3pt] N_{mix} = \frac{1 - 0.4}{500} = \frac{0.6}{500} = 0.0012 \\\\[3pt] 0.0012 \times 500 = N \times 1000 \\\\[3pt] N = \frac{500 \times 0.0012}{1000} = 0.0006 \\\\[3pt] pH = -log(6 \times 10^{-4}) = 4 - log 6 = 3.4 \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
What is the pH of a solution having a \( [H^+] = 2.512 \times 10^{-6} M \) ?

Solution:

\( \begin{align} log [H^+] = log 10^{-6} + log 2.512 \\\\[3pt] log [H^+] = -5.60 \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ - log [H^+] = 5.60 = pH \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
Find the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution of pH = 3.75?

Solution:

\( \begin{align} pH = - log [H^+] = 3.75 \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ log[H^+] = -3.75 \ or \ \bar{4}.2500 \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ [H^+] = antilog[ \bar{4}.2500] = 1.8 \times 10^{-4}M \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Question:
What is the pH of a solution containing 0.63 g of \( HNO_3 \) in 100 ml of solution ?

Solution:

Concentration of
\( \begin{align} HNO_3 (N) = \left [ \frac{0.63}{63} \times \frac{1000}{100} \right ] = \frac{1}{10} = 0.1 N \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ [H^+] = 0.1 N; \ log[H^+] = \bar{1}.000 \\\\[3pt] - log[H^+] = 1.0000 = pH \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Question:
Calculate the pH of an aqueous solution having 2 g of NaOH per 500 ml of it.

Solution:
Molarity (M) of the solution \( = \left [ \frac{2}{40} \times \frac{1000}{500} \right ] = \frac{1}{10} M = 10^{-1} M \) NaOH is a strong base
Hence, \( [OH^-] = [Base] \) = Normality of the base = \( 10^{-1} N \)
\( log [OH^-] = -1.0 \) (hence molarity = Normality)
\( - log(OH^-) \Rightarrow pH = 1.0 \)
But pH + pOH = 14.0; pH + 1 = 14.0; pH = 13.0
When a mixture of strong acids is present in the aqueous solution: Then the normality (N) of the mixture of acids = \( \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] \) where \( V_1 \) and \( N_1 \) are the volume and normality of the first acid, and \( V_2 \) and \( N_2 \) are of the second acid.
\( [H^+] \) in the mixed solution = Normality of the mixed solution = N.
\( \therefore \ pH = -log(N) = - log \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] \)

Example 9:

Question:
150 ml of 0.5 N HCl and 100 ml of 0.2 N HCl are mixed. Find the pH of the resulting solution.

Solution:
Normality of the mixed solution is given by
\( \begin{align} \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] = \left [ \frac{(150 \times 0.5) + (100 \times 0.2)}{(150 + 100)} \right ] = \frac{75 + 20}{250} \\\\[3pt] N = \frac{95}{250} = 0.38 N \end{align} \)
\( [H^+] \) in the mixed solutions = Normality of the mixed solution = 0.38
\( \therefore \ pH = -log (0.38) = (0.42) = 0.42 \)
When strong alkalies are mixed to calculate the pH or pOH of the mixed solution :
The normality of the mixture of the strong bases is given by
\( N = \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] \) where \( V_1, \ N_1 \) , are volume and normality of the first solution and \( V_2, \ N_2 \) are the values for the second solution.
Then \( [OH^-] \) in the mixture = Normality of the mixed solution = N.
\( \therefore \ pOH = - log (N) = - log \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] \) and pH + pOH = 14.0 at room temperature
\( \therefore \ pH = (14.0 - pOH). \)

Example 10:

Question:
Equal volumes of 0.5 N NaOH and 0.3 N KOH are mixed in an experiment. Find the pOH and pH of the resulting solution.

Solution:
Normality of the mixed solution,
\( N = \left [ \frac{(V_1 N_1 + V_2 N_2)}{(V_1 + V_2)} \right ] = \frac{V_1 (0.5) + V_1 (0.3)}{2V_1} \) ( \( \therefore \) Equal volumes are mixed)
\( \begin{align} = \frac{0.5 + 0.3}{2} = 0.4N = 0.4 M (also) \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ pOH = -log (N) = - log(0.4) = -(-0.398) = 0.398 \\\\[3pt] Then \ pH + 0.398 = 14.0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow pH = 14.0 - 0.398 = 13.602 = 13.6 \end{align} \)

Example 11:

Question:
50 ml of 0.2 M HCl is added to 30 ml of 0.1 M KOH solution. Find the pH of the solution

Solution:
In the mixed solution \( V_A N_A > V_B N_B \)
\( \begin{align} N = \left [ \frac{(V_A N_A - V_B N_B)}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] = \left [ \frac{(50 \times 0.2) - (30 \times 0.1)}{(50 + 30)} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = \frac{10 - 3.0}{80} = \frac{7}{80} = 0.0875 N \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ [H^+] = 0.0875 N = 0.0875 M \ also \\\\[3pt] pH = - log[H^+] - log(0.0875 M) = - (-1.06) = 1.06 \end{align} \)

Example 12:

Question:
40 ml of 0.2 N \( HNO_3 \) , when reacted with 60 ml of 0.3 M NaOH, gave a mixed solution. What is the pH of the solution?

Solution:
In the mixed solution \( V_B N_B > V_A N_A \)
\( \therefore \ N = \text{ Normality of the mixed solution} = \left [ \frac{(V_B N_B - V_A N_A)}{(V_A + V_B)} \right ] \)
\( \therefore \) Normality of the mixed solution (N)
\( \begin{align} = \left [ \frac{(60 \times 0.3) - (40 \times 0.2)}{(60 + 40)} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = \frac{(18 - 8)}{100} = \frac{10}{100} = \frac{1}{10} = 0.1 N \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ [OH^-] = 0.1N \text{since alkali is more} \\\\[3pt] pOH = - log(0.1) = -[-1.0] = 1.0 \\\\[3pt] and \ pH = [14.0 - pOH] = (14.0 - 1.0) = 13.0 \end{align} \)

Example 14:

Question:
Calculate the pH of a \( 10^{-5} M \) HCl solution if 1 ml of it is diluted to 1000 ml at \( K_w = 1 \times 10^{-14} \) .

Solution:
When 1 ml of \( 10^{-5} M \) HCl is diluted to 1000 ml, the concentration of HC1 will be \( 10^{-8} M \) and accordingly the pH of the diluted solution of HCl should be 8 which comes in the range of pH. But it is not true. It should be noted that in this case while calculating pH we have to consider the \( H^+ \) ions produced by the dissociation of water in addition to \( H^+ \) ions given by HCl, i.e, \( 10^{-8} \ mole \ l^{-1} \) , because \( H^{+} \) concentration from acid and water are comparable.
To calculate the \( H^{+} \) concentration from water, let us consider the following equilibium in the presence of HCl; the dissociation of \( H_2O \) is suppressed by HCl by the common ion effect
\( H_2O \leftrightharpoons H_x^+ + H_x^_ \)
where x is \( [H^+] \ or \ [OH^-]. \)
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ Total \ H^+ \ concentration = (x + 10^-8) \ \ \ \ ([H^+] = [HCl] = 10^{-8}) \\\\[3pt] Now, \ K_w = [H^+] [OH^-] \\\\[3pt] 1 \times 10^{-14} = (x + 10^{-8}) x; \ x = 9.5 \times 10^{-8} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ total [H^+] = (9.5 \times 10^{-8} + 10^{-8}). \\\\[3pt] Thus, \\\\[3pt] pH = - log [H^+] = - log(9.5 \times 10^{-8} + 10^{-8}) = - log (1.05 \times 10^{-7}) = 6.98. \end{align} \)
