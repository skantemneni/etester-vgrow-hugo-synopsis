Introduction

Discovery of fundamental particles lead to the study of pattern of arrangement of these particles inside the atom. Various atomic models visualizing various patterns of arrangement have been proposed by different scientists such as Thomson, Rutherford, Niels Bohr etc.,

Atomic Models

Many attempts were made to propose various atomic models to describe the position of the fundamental particles in the atom.

J.J. Thomson's atomic model

J.J. Thomson proposed the atomic model (the discoverer of electrons) in 1898. According to him an atom is considered as a sphere of positive charge in which negatively charged particles (electrons) are embedded uniformly to neutralise that positive charge.

Rutherford's atomic model

Rutherford performed the \( \alpha \) - ray scattering to know about the position of fundamental particles (to test J.J. Thomson's atomic model).
In his experiment he observed that most of the \( \alpha \) particles passed through the gold foil without any deflection from the path, few \( \alpha \) particles were deflected by small angles. One in 10,000 \( \alpha \) particles bounded back in the same direction. On the basis of the above observations, Rutherford drew the following conclusions.

  1. Atom consists of predominately empty space.

  2. Nucleus is located inside the atom, containing positively charged particles.

  3. Electrons revolve at high speed in the extra nuclear space around the nucleus in circular orbits like the planets revolve around the sun. The model of an atom is popularly known as planetary model.

  4. As per the law of electrodynamics of classical mechanics, the charged particles like electron moving around the opposite charge in circular motion should continuously lose energy by emission and spiral down into the nucleus. If this happens atom should collapse, which is not happening. If the electron in an atom continuously radiates energy, the spectrum of an element should be a continuous spectrum, But the atoms give rise to discontinuous line spectra.

Note
The arrangement of different electromagnetic radiation in the order of increasing wavelength or frequency is known as electromagnetic spectrum.

Bohr's atomic theory

Among the various models proposed, Bohr's model of atom has been proved with some experimental evidences. Bohr's theory was mainly based on Planck's quantum theory. According to this, emission or absorption of energy is always in the form of packets called quanta. Each quantum carries an energy equal to \( E = h \nu \) where h is called Planck's constant ( \( h = 6.625 \times 10^{-34} \) joule - s and \( \nu \) is frequency of radiation). Niels Bohr's atomic model was based on Planck's quantum theory retaining Rutherford's nuclear model.
The main postulates of this model are:

  1. Electrons revolve around the nucleus in specified circular paths with definite amount of energy called stationary orbits or stationary shells.

  2. These are also called energy levels and are designated as K, L, M, N corresponding to 1,2,3,4 respectively.

  3. The energy associated with a certain energy level increases with the increase of its distance from the nucleus. Hence, if the energy associated with the K, L, M, N shells are \( E_1, \ E_2, \ E_3 \) respectively, then \( E_1 < E_2 < E_3 \) , etc.,

  4. An electron jumps from a lower energy level to a higher energy level by absorbing energy. But when it jumps from a higher to lower energy level, energy is emitted in the form of electromagnetic radiations. The energy emitted or absorbed \( ( \Delta E) \) is an integral multiple of \( h \nu \)

  5. The electron can revolve only in the orbit in which the angular momentum of the electron (mvr) is quantised, i.e, mvr is a whole number multiple of \( \frac{h}{2 \pi} \) This is known as principle of quantization of angular momentum. \( \left ( mvr = \frac{nh}{2 \pi} \right ) \) .

By applying the concept of quantization of energy Bohr calcuated the radii and energy of the \( n^{th} \) orbit of single electron species.
\( r_n = \frac{n^2h^2}{4 {\pi}^2 me^2 Z} = \frac{0.529 \times n^2}{Z} ^{\circ}A, \)
for hydrogen atom Z = 1
\( \begin{align} E_n = \frac{-2 {\pi}^2 me^4}{n^2 h^2}. Z^2 \\\\[3pt] E_n = - \frac{13.6}{n^2} . Z^2 eV/atom \end{align} \)
for hydrogen atom Z = 1
\( \begin{align} r_n = 0.529 \times {n^2} A^{\circ} \\\\[3pt] E_n = - \frac{13.6}{n^2} eV/atom = \frac{21.72 \times 10^{-19}J}{n^2} \end{align} \)
where h = Planck's constant.
m = mass of the electron
e = charge of an electron
Z = atomic number or nuclear charge
The energy of electron in the ground state in the hydrogen atom is equal to - 13.6 eV or \( - 21.72 \times 10^{-19}J \)
Velocity of electron in an orbit \( v_n = \frac{Ze^2}{2nh} \) with the help of these expressions, Bohr gave a satisfactory explanation for the spectra of hydrogen and hydrogen like species (ions having one electron e.g. \( He^+, \ Li^{+2}, \ Be^{+3} \) ) etc.,

merits

  1. Explanation of spectra of hydrogen and hydrogen like species

  2. Derivation of equations for radius and energy of orbits

Demerits

This model could not explain :

  1. Spectra of multi electron atoms.

  2. Proper explanation for quantization of angular momentum of orbits.

  3. Fine structure of hydrogen spectrum.

  4. Zeeman effect and Stark effect.

Sommerfeld's extension of Bohr's theory

Sommerfeld's elliptical model could explain :

  1. Concept of elliptical orbits with a major axis and a minor axis(n and k).

  2. \( \frac{n}{k} = \frac{\text{length of the major axis}}{\text{length of the minor axis}}. \)

  3. Presence of subshells.

  4. Variation of angular momentum of electron in elliptical orbit.

  5. Azimuthal quantum number(l = k - 1)

Quantum Mechanical Model

De Broglie's theory

Louis de Broglie pointed out that the wave behaviour of an electron is similar to that of a standing wave. The circumference of each Bohr's orbit must be equal to an integral multiple of the wave length of the wave associated with the moving electron. Orbits whose circumferences are not equal to the integral multiple of the wave length are impossible for an electron in Bohr's atom.
The expression is \( \lambda = \frac{h}{momentum \ of \ electron} \) .
or \( \lambda = \frac{h}{mv} \)
where m = mass of electron.
v = velocity of electrons.
h = planck's constant.
This equation is called de Broglie's equation, this hypothesis was experimentally confirmed by Davison and Germer who proved wave like properties of electron in 1927.
During this period on the basis of the wave properties of electrons, Austrian Physicist Erwing Schrondinger derived an equation called Schrondinger wave equation to describe the behaviour and energies of electrons in an atom.
Based on this wave equation, the three dimensional region in space around the nucleus, where the probability of finding an electron with a particular energy is determined to be maximum, is called the atomic orbital.
On the basis of Schrodinger wave equation a quantum mechanical model of atom has been proposed. In this model, the state of an electron in an atom can be explained with respect to four quantum numbers.

Heisenberg's uncertainity principle

Accroding to this principle, both position and velocity of microparticle such as moving electron cannot be determined simultaneously and accurately.
\( \Delta x. \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4 \pi} \) .

Demerits

Failed when applied to larger bodies since the wave nature is insignificant.
The principle lead to the concept of probability of finding an electron and eventually to the concept of orbital.

Quantum Numbers

Principal quantum number

  1. Denoted by n and proposed by Bohr.

  2. Values are designated as 1, 2, 3, 4 or K, L, M, N.

  3. It determines the size and energy of orbit.

Azimuthal quantum number

  1. Denoted by 1 and proposed by Sommerfeld.

  2. Values are 0 to n - 1.

  3. Values represent subshells s, p, d, f and their shapes are spherical, dumbbell, double dumbbell for s, p, d subshells respectively.

  4. It determines the shape of the subshell and angular momentum of subshell.

Magnetic quantum number

  1. Denoted by m and proposed by Lande

  2. Values are -1 to + 1 including zero

  3. Total number of values are (2l + 1) which give number of orientations in space.

  4. Each value of m represents an orbital.

Spin quantum number

Apart from the above three quantum numbers there is another quantum number which is not derived from quantum mechanical treatment of atom. This is based on the direction of spin of electron.

Electronic Configuration of Atoms

Pauli's exclusion principle

No two electrons in an atom can have all the four quantum numbers identical.An Orbital contains a maximum of two electrons with opposite spins.The maximum number of electrons in a shell are given by \( 2n^2 \) .

Electronic Configurations of Elements

The distribution of electrons in orbitals and shells. The distribution follows certain rules.

Aufbau Principle

Aufbau means building up in German. This rule explains about the order of filling up of orbitals. The orbitals are filled by electrons in an increasing order of energies. The order of energies is determined based on n + 1 values. The orbital with greater n + 1 value has greater energy and lower value has lower energy. Among the orbitals with equal n + 1 values, the orbital with lower n value possesses lower energy. The various orbitals depicting the order of energies are arranged in the form of a pictorial representation which is called Moeller diagram.

Moller Diagram

Hund's rule of maximum multiplicity

Accroding to Hund's rule of maximum multiplicity, the orbitals in the same subshell possess equal energies and called degenerate orbitals. These degenerate orbitals are singly occupied by electrons till all the orbitals are half filled. Pairing starts only when the orbitals in a subshell are exactly half filled. The orbitals lose their degeneracy in the presence of external electric or magnetic fields.
The electronic configurations of all the elements are written based on the above three principles. The method of writing the configuration can be shell method \( nl_x \) method. In shell method, the total number of electrons in K, L, M, N shells are written in the same order. In \( nl_x \) method, n represents the main energy level or principal quantum number, l represents the subshell for the corresponding azimuthal quantum number and x represents number of electrons in the subshell.

Anamolous electronic configurations

In case of few elements, the actual rules are slightly violated for writing the electronic configurations. this is because of the greater stabilities of half filled and fully filled subshells in comparison to the neighboring configurations.
Examples: Chromium-half filled configuration
Copper-fully filled configuration.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
An electron in 'H' atom and the one in \( He^{+} \) ion are present in the \( 2^{nd} \) orbits of the respective species. Both the electrons jump to the ground states. Calculate the ratio of wave lengths of the emmitted radiation.

Solution:
Energy of an electron is \( 2^{nd} \) orbit of 'H' atom
\( E_2 = \frac{-21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{2^2} \times 1^2 = -5.43 \times 10^{-19} joules \)
Energy of an electron in \( 1^{st} \) orbit of 'H' atom
\( \begin{align} E_1 = \frac{-21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{1^2} \times 1^2 = -21.72 \times 10^{-19} joules \\\\[3pt] ( \Delta E)_H = -16.29 \times 10^{-19} J \end{align} \)
Energy of an electron is \( 2^{nd} \) orbit of \( He^{+} \) ion
\( E_2 = \frac{-21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{2^2} \times 2^2 = -21.72 \times 10^{-19} joules \)
Energy of an electron in \( 1^{st} \) orbit of \( He^{+} \) ion
\( \begin{align} E_1 = \frac{-21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{1^2} \times 2^2 = -86.88 \times 10^{-19} joules \\\\[3pt] (\Delta E)_{He^+} = -65.16 \times 10^{-19} J \\\\[3pt] \Delta E_H : \Delta E_{He^+} = -16.29 \times 10^{-19} : -65.16 \times 10^{-19} \\\\[3pt] = 1 : 4 \\\\[3pt] {\lambda}_H : {\lambda}_{He^+} = 4 : 1 \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Question:
The ratio of radii of certain orbits of Hydrogen \( He^{+} \) and \( Li^{+2} \) is 12 : 6 : 4. Calculate the energies of the electrons in those orbits.

Solution:
Radius of orbit
\( \begin{align} = \frac{0.529 \times n^2}{z} ^{\circ}A \\\\[3pt] r_H = \frac{0.529 \times {n_H}^2}{1} ; \ r_{He^+} = \frac{0.529 \times n^2}{2}; \\\\[3pt] r_{Li^{+2}} = \frac{0.529 \times n^2}{3} \\\\[3pt] r_H : r_{He^+} : r_{Li + 2} = {n_H}^2 : {n_{He^+}}^2 : {n_{Li^{+2}}}^2 = 12 : 6 : 4 \\\\[3pt] n_H = n_{He^+} = n_{Li^{+2}} = 2 \end{align} \)
Energy of orbit =
\( \begin{align} - \frac{13.6}{n^2} \times 2^2 \\\\[3pt] E_H = - \frac{13.6}{n^2} \times 1^2 = - 3.4 eV \\\\[3pt] E_{He^+} = - \frac{13.6}{n^2} \times 2^2 = -13.6 eV \\\\[3pt] E_{Li^{+2}} = - \frac{13.6}{n^2} \times 3^2 = -30.6 eV \end{align} \)

Example 3:

Question:
The angular momentum of an electron in an orbit is \( 3.16 \times 10^{-34} kg \ m^2s^{-1} \) . The energy of an orbit in which the electron is present is \( 21.72 \times 10^{-19} joules \) . Calculate radius and energy of ground state in that species.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} mvr = \frac{nh}{2 \pi} = \frac{n \times 6.625 \times 10^{-34}}{2 \times \frac{22}{7}} = 3.16 \times 10^{-34} \Rightarrow n = 3 \\\\[3pt] n = 3; \ \ \ \ \ E = - \frac{21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{n^2} \times z^2 \\\\[3pt] = - \frac{21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{3^2} \times z^2 = -21.72 \times 10^{-19} \\\\[3pt] Z = 3 \ The \ species \ is \ Li^{+2}, \\\\[3pt] E_1 = - \frac{21.72 \times 10^{-19}}{1^2} \times 3^2 = - 195.48 \times 10^{-19}eV \\\\[3pt] r_1 = - \frac{0.529 \times 3^2}{3} = 1.587 \ A^{\circ} \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Question:
Assuming that an element with 'g' subshell exists, answer the following questions.

  1. What are the values of principal and azimuthal quantum numbers associated with that electrons?

  2. Give the number of orbitals and maximum number of electrons in that subshell.

  3. Give two preceding and succeeding subshells to it w.r.t electron filling.

Solution:

  1. n = 5; l = 4

  2. There would be 9 orbitals and a maximum of 18 electrons can be accommodated in it.

  3. Preceding orbitals could be 6d, 7p and 8s. This is because they have n + l values equal to 8 and are filled in increasing order of 'n' value.
    The succeeding orbitals could be 6f, 7d, 8p. This is because they have n + l values equal to 9 equal to that of 5g. When they are filled according to the increasing order of 'n' value they succeed 5g in the same order.

Example 5:

Question:
Calculate the wave numbers corresponding to the following wavelengths.

  1. \( 3000 \ nm \)

  2. \( 600 \ A^{\circ} \)

Solution:

  1. \( \begin{align} \lambda = 3000 \ nm = 3000 \times 10^{-9}m \ or \ 3 \times 10^{-6}m \\\\[3pt] \overline{ \nu} = \frac{1}{ \lambda} = \frac{1}{3 \times 10^{-6}} = 3.33 \times 10^{-5} m \end{align} \)

  2. \( \begin{align} \overline{ \nu} = 600 \ A^{\circ} = 600 \times 10^{-8}cm = 6 \times 10^{-6}m \\\\[3pt] \overline{ \nu} = \frac{1}{ \lambda} = \frac{1}{6 \times 10^{-6}} = 1.66 \times 10^{-5}m^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
What is the frequency of radiation of wave length \( 6000 \ A^{\circ} \)

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Frequency( \nu) = \frac{velocity \ of \ light(c)}{wavelength( \lambda)}, \\\\[3pt] c = 3 \times 10^{10} cm \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \lambda = 6000 \ A^{\circ} = 6000 \times 10^{-8}cm = 6 \times 10^{-5}m \\\\[3pt] \nu = \frac{3 \times 10^{10}}{6 \times 10^{-5}} = 0.5 \times 10^{15}s^{-1}. \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Question:
Calculate the frequency and energy of photon whose wavelength is \( 3000 \ A^{\circ} \)

Solution:
\( \begin{align} c = 3 \times 10^{10} cm \ s^{-1} \ and \ \lambda = 3000 \times 10^{-8}cm = 3 \times 10^{-5}cm \\\\[3pt] \nu = \frac{c}{ \lambda} \\\\[3pt] \nu = \frac{c}{ \lambda} = \frac{3 \times 10^{10}}{3 \times 10^{-5}} = 1 \times 10^{15} s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] E = h \nu, \ h = 6.625 \times 10^{-27}erg; \\\\[3pt] \nu = 1 \times 10^{-15}s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] E = 6.625 \times 10^{-27} \times 1 \times 10^{15} = 6.625 \times 10^{-12}erg. \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Question:
When an electromagnetic radiation of wavelength \( 3000 \ A^{\circ} \) strikes the surface of a metal electrons are emitted with energy \( 1.68 \times 10^5 J \ mole^{-1} \) . Find the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the metal.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} E = h \nu = \frac{hc}{ \lambda} \left( \nu = \frac{c}{ \lambda} \right) \\\\[3pt] \lambda = 3000 \ A^{\circ} = 3 \times 10^{-5}cm \\\\[3pt] h = 6.625 \times 10^{-27}ergs \\\\[3pt] c = 3 \times 10^{10}cm \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] E = 6.625 \times 10^{-27} \times 3 \times 10^{-5} = 6.625 \times 10^{-12} \ erg \ or \ 6.625 \times 10^{-19}joules \\\\[3pt] \text{Kinetic energy of the electrons emitted} = 1.68 \times 10^5 \ J \ mole^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \text{For one electron, kinetic energy} = \frac{1.68 \times 10^5}{6.023 \times 10^{23}} = 2.78 \times 10^{-19}J \\\\[3pt] \text{Energy required to remove one electron from the metal} \\\\[3pt] = 6.625 \times 10^{-19} - 2.78 \times 10^{-19} = 3.845 \times 10^{-19}joules \end{align} \)

Example 9:

Question:
An electron in hydrogen atom jumps from \( 5^{th} \) shell to \( 1^{st} \) shell. How many spectral lines are produced?

Solution:
number of spectral lines \( \begin{align} \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} \\\\[3pt] For \ n = 5; \ \frac{5(5 - 1)}{2} = 10 \ lines \end{align} \)

Example 10:

Question:
An electron jumps from \( 5^{th} \) shell to \( 2^{nd} \) shell. How many states of transitions are possible?

Solution:
Possible transitions \( \sum \Delta n = \sum (5 - 2) = 1 + 2 + 3 = 6 \)
