1 Alcohols are alkaline due to the presence of OH groups. - OH group of alcohols behaves as acidic as reactions with active metals like K, Na. Mg etc., liberates hydrogen gas.
2 Phenols do not show any visible reaction with carboxylic acids because both are acidic and acids do not react with each other. Phenols are weakly acidic but still they react with acid chlorides and acid anhydrides to form esters.
3 Acid chlorides are more reactive than carboxylic acids towards esterification because they are more acidic. In esterification, - C - OH cleavage occurs and in acid chlorides - C - Cl cleavage occurs readily than in the acids.

Introduction

Carbon forms millions of compounds though it combines only with few elements like hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, halogens and sulphur. The compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen are called hydrocarbons. The compounds which possess other elements in addition to carbon and hydrogen are called organic compound with heteroatoms. The atom or group of atoms present in a molecule of an organic compound which is responsible for the characteristic properties of the compound is called functional group. The organic compounds containing each type of heteroatoms can be further classified on the basis of the nature of the functional group.

Nomenclature of organic compounds

According to IUPAC system, which follows the same pattern for all families of organic compounds, the name of an organic compound can be divided into three major parts namely:

  1. Root word

  2. Primary suffix

  3. Secondary suffix

Thus, for the organic compounds with functional groups corresponding secondary suffix is added along with root word and primary suffix.

Secondary suffix

This is the part of the name which follows the primary suffix. It represents the furctions group attached to the carbon chain. 'e' of the primary suffix is replaced by the corresponding seccondary suffix.

Rules for IUPAC nomenclature of organic compounds

  1. At first, the longest continuous carbon chain which includes the 'functional group' is selected. Then the carbon chain is numbered from the end nearer to the functional group. The carbon bearing the functional group should get the least number.
    Ex:
    \( \underset{1 - Propanol}{ \overset{3}{H_3C} - \overset{2}{CH_2} - \overset{1}{CH_2} - OH} \)

  2. If both the functional group and the alkyl substituent are present in an organic compound, the numbering of the parent carbon chain is given from the end nearer to functional group (the carbon atom bearing functional group should get the least number in preference to the substituent).
    Ex:
    \( \underset{2 - Methyl \ propanol}{ \overset{3}{H_3C} - \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{\overset{2}{CH}} - \overset{1}{CH_2} - OH} \)

  3. If both the alkyl substituent and the halogen group are present in an organic compound, then the carbon chain is numbered from the end nearer to halogen group (the carbon bearing the halogen group should get the least number).
    The prefixes (of both alkyl and halogen group) are to be arranged in alphabetical order while writing the name of the compound.
    Ex:
    \( \underset{2 - Chloro - 4 - methyl \ pentane}{ \overset{5}{H_3C} - \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{\overset{4}{CH}} - \overset{3}{CH_2} - \underset{ \underset{Cl}|}{ \overset{2}{CH}} - \overset{1}{CH_3}} \)
    When the substitent like alkyl group is present in an organic compound, the corresponding prefix 'alkyl' is used and when the hetero atom like halogen is present the corresponding prefix 'halo' (like chloro, bromo, iodo etc.,) is used and the corresponding IUPAC name is 'haloalkane'.

  4. When more than one alkyl substituent is present on the parent carbon chain, then the numbering is given in such a way that the 'sum of the locants' is least.
    Ex:
    \( \underset{2, \ 3 - dimethyl \ pentane}{ \overset{1}{H_3C} - \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{\overset{2}{CH}} - \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{\overset{3}{CH}} - \overset{4}{CH_2} - \overset{5}{CH_3}} \)

  5. If 'sum of the locants' is equal, then the numbering of carbon chain starts from the end nearer to the smaller alkyl group.
    Ex:
    \( \underset{4 - Ethyl - 3 - methyl \ hexane}{ \overset{6}{H_3C} - \overset{5}{H_2C} - \underset{ \underset{ \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{CH_2}}|}{ \overset{4}{HC}} - \underset{ \underset{CH_3}|}{ \overset{3}{CH}} - \overset{2}{CH_2} - \overset{1}{CH_3}} \)

IUPAC nomenclature for esters and ethers

The IUPAC names of majority of the organic compounds with different functional groups` are given by following the above rules. But IUPAC nomenclature of some functional groups such as ethers and esters follows a slightly different trend.

Ethers

Ethers are a class of compounds which contain an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl groups.
General molecular formula : \( C_nH_{2n + 2}O \)
Functional group : \( - O - \)
General formula : \( R^1 - O - R \) ( \( R, \ R^1 \) - same or different alkyl groups).

Nomenclature

The first name is given 'alkoxy' for the smaller alkyl group ('R') attached to '0' and is followed by a second name 'alkane'; for the larger alkyl group ( \( R^1 \) ) attached to '0'.
Ex: \( H_3C - O - CH_3 \) Methoxy methane.
\( H_3C - O - CH_2 - CH_3 \) Methoxy ethane.

Esters

These are derivitives of carboxylic acids where carboxylic acids where carboxylate group is attached to two alkyl groups.
Functional group:
\( - \overset{ \overset{O}{||}}C- O - \)
Esters are functional derivatives of carboxylic acids since they contain the common `acyloxy' group.

Nomenclature

The 'R' group attached to 'O' \( \left(R' - \overset{ \overset{O}{||}}C - O - R \right) \) is given the first name as corresponding 'alkyl' and the remaining structure is given the second name 'alkanoate'.
Thus, the IUPAC name for esters is 'Alkyl alkanoate'.
Ex:
\( H - \overset{ \overset{O}{||}}C - O - CH_3 \) Methyl methanoate
\( H_3C - \overset{ \overset{O}{||}}C - O - CH_3 \) Methyl ethanoate

Aldehydes and Ketones

Carbonyl compounds are the class of compounds in which there is a \( {\underset{}{\diagdown}\atop\diagup}{C} = O \) group. The general formula of the carbonyl compounds is \( C_nH_{2n}O. \) If the carbonyl compounds contains \( {\underset{}{\diagdown}\atop\diagup}{C} = O \) group linked to hydrogen on one side and alkyl group on the other side, that class of compounds are called aldehydes. If the carbonyl group is linked to two same or different alkyl groups on either side, the class of componds are called ketones.
Aldehydes are represented by a general formula, \( {\underset{}{ ^H \diagdown}\atop_R\diagup}{C} = O \)
Ketones are represented by a general formula, \( {\underset{}{ ^R \diagdown}\atop_R\diagup}{C} = O \)
IUPAC names of aldehydes are 'alkanals' and those of ketones are 'alkanones'.
Ex: \( CH_3CHO \ \ \ \ \ \ \ H_3CCOCH_3 \)
Common name: Acetaldehyde - Acetone
IUPAC name: ethanal - Propanone

Formaldehyde(Methanal):

Preparation:

Methyl alcohol on controlled oxidation in the presence of a catalyst gives formaldehyde.
\( 2CH_3OH \xrightarrow[573 \ K]{Cu} HCHO + 2H_2O \)

Physical properties

  1. It is a colourless gas.

  2. It is a strong smelling gas.

  3. Its boiling point is 252 K.

  4. It is highly soluble in water.

Chemical properties

  1. Oxidation:
    Methanal on oxidation in presence of oxidising agents such as \( HNO_3 \) or acidified \( K_2Cr_2O_7 \) or acidified \( KMnO_4 \) gives formic acid(methanoic acid).
    \( HCHO + [O] \rightarrow HCOOH \)

  2. Oxidation in presence of Tollen's reagent(Ammonical silver nitrate solution):
    Formaldehyde on heating with tollen's reagent gets oxidized to formic acid. \( Ag^{+} \) ions get reduced to metallic silver. As a result of this, a shining silver mirror is formed along the inner walls of the test tube.
    \( HCHO + AgNO_3 \xrightarrow[{[O]}]{NH_4OH} HCOOH + NH_4NO_3 + NH_3 + Ag + H_2O \)

  3. Oxidation in presence of Fehling's reagent:
    Fehling's reagent is an alkaline cupric salt solution dissolved in sodium potassium tartarate (Rochelle salt). Formaldehyde on heating with Fehling's reagent in hot water gives a red precipitate of cuprous oxide. Methanal gets oxidized to methanoic acid.
    \( HCHO + 2Cu(OH)_2 + NaOH \xrightarrow{ \Delta} HCOONa + Cu_2O + 3H_2O \)

  4. Reduction:
    Methanal on reduction in the presence of palladium gives methyl alcohol.
    \( HCHO + H_2 \xrightarrow[{[H]}]{Pd} CH_3OH \)

Carboxylic acids

The organic compounds which contain \( - \overset{ \overset{O}|}C - OH \) (carboxyl group) as functional group are known as carboxylic acids, since this group is a combination of carbonyl group ( > C = O) and hydroxyl group (- OH). The general molecular formula of carboxylic acids is \( C_nH_{2n}O_2 \) . Since they show significant acidity, they are called acids.

Nomenclature

The common names of these acids are mostly derived from the source of their origin.
Ex: Formic acid (Formica - ants)
Acetic acid (Acetum - vinegar)
According to IUPAC system, these compounds are named by adding a secondary suffix `oic acid' to the corresponding `alkane'. These are named as alkanoic acids.

Carbohydrates and Proteins

Carbohydrate can be defined as "the polyhydroxy aldehydes/ ketones or the compounds which can give these compounds on hydrolysis". Glucose, fructose, sucrose, lactose, cellulose and starch are a few examples of carbohydrates.

Classification of carbohydrates

The widely accepted classification of carbohydrates into monosaccharides, oligosaccharides and polysaccharides is based on their behaviour towards hydrolysis.

Monosaccharides

These are simple carbohydrates which cannot be hydrolyzed to still simpler carbohydrates. Glucose, fructose and mannose are some examples of monosaccharides.

Oligosaccharides

These are the carbohydrates which on hydrolysis give two to nine units of monosaccharides. Accordingly, they may be further divided into di, tri, tetra saccharides etc., depending upon the actual number of monosaccharide units formed by the hydrolysis of a particular oligosaccharide. Sucrose and maltose are examples of oligosaccharides.

Polysaccharides

These are polymeric carbohydrates which give a large number of monosaccharide units upon hydrolysis. They have the general formula \( (C_6H_{10}O_5)_n \) . where n is a large number. Starch and cellulose are examples of polysaccharides.

Identification tests for some carbohydrates

Carbohydrates that reduce Tollen's, Fehling's and Benedict's reagent are called reducing sugars.
Both glucose and fructose (monosaccharides) give positive result for Tollen's, Benedict's and Fehling's test.

Tollen's test:

Ammonical silver nitrate solution is called Tollen's reagent. Addition of this reagent to glucose or fructose solution followed by heating in water bath lead to the formation of shining silver mirror on the walls of the test tube due to the reduction of \( Ag^{+} \) ions to Ag.

Benedict's test:

Measured proportions of sodium citrate and sodium carbonate are dissolved in water. In this solution, copper sulphate is dissolved. The resulting solution is called Benedict's reagent. Addition of Benedict's reagent to glucose or fructose solution followed by heating in water bath results in the formation of a red precipitate. This is due to the reduction of \( Cu^{+2} \) to \( Cu^{+1} \) .

Fehling's test:

Fehling's reagent is an alkaline cupric ion complexed with sodium or potassium tartarate ions. When this reagent is added to glucose or fructose solution followed by heating in water bath results in the formation of red precipitate, due to the formation of cuprous oxide. (Reduction of \( Cu^{+2} \) to \( Cu^{+1} \) ).

Amino - Acids and Proteins

Amino - Acids

The name amino - acids indicates that these are compounds having an amino group ( \( - NH_2 \) ) and carboxylic acid group (-COOH) in a molecule. The general formula of amino - acids is:
\( R -\underset{ \underset{NH_2}|}{ \overset{ \overset{H}|}C} - COOH \)
where R is a group that differs from one amino - acid to the other. The properties of amino - acids depend upon the nature of R. Although amino - acids are covalent compounds, they are water soluble crystalline substances and have surprisingly high melting points like salts. These salt like properties of amino - acids can be well explained by considering the salt like structure of amino - acids called "zwitterions" or "dipolar ions" produced by the transfer of a proton from COOH to \( NH_2 \) .
\( R - \underset{ \underset{ \underset{Natural \ form}{NH_2}}|}{ \overset{ \overset{H}|}C} - COOH \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ R - \underset{ \underset{ \underset{Zwitterionic \ form}{NH_3}}|}{ \overset{ \overset{H}|}C} - COO \)
So far 26 amino acids are known to occur in living bodies. These amino acids are either synthesized in the body or taken through diet. Out of 26 amino acids, human body cannot synthesize 9 amino acids. These are called essential amino acids. The essential amino acids must be supplied through diet only.

Oils and Fats

Oils and fats are triesters of glycerol with fatty acids. Oils and fats are useful for cooking, for lighting in industries.

Manufacture of soap

Chemically soap is a sodium or potassium salt of fatty acids of long carbon chains. Soaps can be directly obtained from oils or fats by hydrolysis in the presence of a base. This process is called saponification of oils.
\( (C_{17}H_{33}COO)_3C_3H_5 + 3NaOH \rightarrow \underset{Soap}{3 \ C_{17}H_{33}COONa} + \underset{Glycerol}{CH_2OH - CH(OH) - CH_2OH} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Sodium metal can be used for drying diethyl ether and benzene but not ethanol.

Solution:
Ethanol has a replaceable hydrogen atom and therefore, reacts with sodium:
\( C_2H_5OH + Na \rightarrow C_2H_5ONa + \frac{1}{2}H_2 \)
While benzene and diethyl ether do not have replaceable hydrogen atom and hence do not react with sodium. Therefore, these can be dried by sodium metal.

Example 2:

Question:
Arrange the following in the decreasing order of their boiling points:
\( CH_3CH_2OH, \ HOCH_2CH_2OH, \ CH_3CH_2Cl \)

Solution:
\( HOCH_2CH_2OH > CH_3CH_2OH > CH_3CH_2Cl \)

Example 3:

Question:
Explain why alcohols are comparatively more soluble in water than the corresponding hydrocarbon.

Solution:
The comparatively higher solubility of alcohols in water in comparison to hydrocarbon is due to the formation of hydrogen bonds between alcohol and water molecules.

Example 4:

Question:
\( H_2SO_4 \) Explain why dehydration of alcohols to form alkenes is always carried out with conc. and not with conc. \( HCl \) or \( HNO_3 \) ?

Solution:
Dehydration of alcohols to alkenes occurs through the formation of carbocation intermediate. If HCl is used then chloride \( (Cl^{-}) \) ion being a good nucleophile will result into substitution reaction forming alkyl chloride.
\( \underset{(Elimination \ product)}{ \overset{R - CH = CH_2}{Alkene}} \xleftarrow[-H_2SO_4^{-}]{HSO_4^{-}} \overset{R - CH_2 - CH_2^{+}}{Carbocation} \xrightarrow{Cl^{-}} \underset{(Substitution \ product)}{ \overset{R - CH_2 - CH_2Cl}{Alkene \ halide}} \)
However, if \( H_2SO_4 \) is used, then the bisulphate \( (HSO_4^{-}) \) ion being very weak nucleophile cannot result into substitution reaction. Rather the carbocation loses a proton to form elimination product alkene. On the other hand, conc. \( HNO_3 \) also cannot be used because it is a strong oxidising agent and will preferentially result into the oxidation of alcohols to aldehydes, ketones or carboxylic acids. Thus, the dehydration of alcohols to form alkenes is always carried out with such strong acids \( (H_2SO_4, \ H_3PO_4, \ etc) \) which have non nucleophilic anions.

  3. 3 - Methylbutan - 2 - ol

Solution:
\( Pentan - 1 - ol > 3 - Methylbutan - 2 - ol > 2 - Methylbutan - 2 - ol \) . This is because with branching the shape becomes spherical and therefore, vander Waals' forces decrease. As a result, boiling point decreases.

Example 6:

Question:
Why do aldehydes and ketones have high dipole moments?

Solution:
The carbonyl group in aldehydes and ketones contains a double bond between carbon and oxygen atoms. Since oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, the carbonyl group is a polarised group:

Oxygen has got a considerable negative charge while carbon has got a considerable positive charge. Therefore, aldehydes and ketones have high dipole moments.

Example 7:

Question:
In the preparation of acetaldehyde from ethyl alcohol, it is distilled out as soon as it is formed. Explain.

Solution:
Aldehyde is easily oxidisable to acetic acid. Therefore, to prevent its oxidation, it is distilled out as soon as it is formed.

Example 8:

Question:
Aldehydes and ketones have lower boiling points than corresponding alcohols and acids. Explain.

Solution:
Aldehydes and ketones are polar compounds having sufficient intermolecular dipole - dipole interactions between opposite ends of > C = 0 dipoles. However, these dipole - dipole interactions are weaker than the intermolecular hydrogen bonds present in alcohols and carboxylic acids. Therefore, boiling points of aldehydes and ketones are lower than alcohols and carboxylic acids.

Example 11:

Question:
Why do alcohols have higher boiling points than haloalkanes of the same molecular mass?

Solution:
The boiling points of alcohol are higher than the corresponding haloalkanes of the same molecular mass. This is mainly due to strong intermolecular hydrogen bonding between alcohol molecules. Because of hydrogen bonding in alcohols, energy has to be supplied to overcome the force of attraction and consequently boiling points are high. No such hydrogen bonding exists in haloalkanes. For example, b.pt. of \( CH_3OH \) is 337.5 K while that of \( CH_3Cl \) is only 249 K.
