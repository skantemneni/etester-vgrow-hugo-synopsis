1 Liquor ammonia and liquid ammonia are same. Liquor ammonia is a saturated solution of ammonia in water. Liquid ammonia is the ammonia, converted to liquid by applying pressure.
2 The oxidant nature of \( HNO_3 \) , increases with dilution. The oxidant nature of \( HNO_3 \) decreases with dilution i.e., More is concentration of \( H^+ \) and \( NO_3 \) , more is oxidizing power.

Introduction

Nitrogen is a major constituent of air and is widely present in earth's crust in the form of nitrates. Compounds of nitrogen have wide ranging applications in laboratories, agriculture, medicine, space sciences etc., Some of the important compounds of nitrogen are ammonia, nitric acid and nitrates.

Ammonia

  1. Synthesis of nitrogen and hydrogen by Haber's Process
    \( N_2 + 3H_2 \xrightarrow{\text{finely divided iron}} 2NH_3 + heat \)
    Temp \( 450^{\circ} C \) ; 200 - 1000 atmospheres pressure.

  2. It is obtained by the action of alkalies on ammonium salts on heating.
    \( (NH_4)_2SO_4 + Ca(OH)_2 \rightarrow CaSO_4 + 2NH_3 + 2H_2O \)

  3. Metal nitrides decompose to give ammonia gas when treated with water
    \( Mg_3N_2 + 6H_2O \rightarrow 3Mg(OH)_2 + 2NH_3 \)

Laboratory Preparation

By heating slaked lime with ammonium chloride:

It is collected by downward displacement of air as it is lighter than air. The gas cannot be collected over water because of its high solubility in water. It is dried by passing through quicklime (CaO) but not over conventions drying agents like \( H_2SO_4 \ and \ P_2O_5 \) (acidic) as it reacts with them.
\( Ca(OH)_2 + 2NH_4Cl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + 2H_2O + 2NH_3 \uparrow \)
In large scale, it is manufactured by Haber's process from nitrogen (from air) and hydrogen (from water) in the presence of finely divided iron as catalyst along with molybdenum as promoter.
\( N_2 + 3H_2 \underset{\overset{\rightleftarrows}{400^{\circ}C - 500^{\circ}C Fe/ Mo}}{200 - 300 \ atm} 2NH_3 + Heat \)

Physical properties :

  1. It is a colourless gas with characteristic pungent odour.

  2. It is lighter than air and basic in nature.

  3. It is highly soluble in water.

Chemical properties :

  1. It reduces hot metallic oxides like copper(II) oxides to their metals. It reacts with chlorine to form nitrogen and hydrogen chloride.
    \( \begin{align} 2NH_3 + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow N_2 + 6HCl \text{(in excess of chlorine)} \\\\[3pt] NH_3 + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow NCl_3 + HCl \text{(in excess of ammonia)} \\\\[3pt] 8NH_3 + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow 6NH_4Cl + N_2 \end{align} \)

  2. It reacts with various metallic compounds to give different coloured hydroxides which are useful in detection of metal ions.
    \( \begin{align} 2NH_4OH + Cu^{2+} \rightarrow \underset{blue \ ppt}{Cu(OH)_2} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] \underset{excess}{4NH_3} + Cu^{2+} \rightarrow \underset{\text{deep blue solution}}{[Cu(NH_3)_4]} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH + Fe^{2+} \rightarrow \underset{dirty \ green}{Fe(OH)_2} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH + Fe^{3+} \rightarrow \underset{reddish \ brown}{Fe(OH)_2} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH + Al^{3+} \rightarrow \underset{white}{Al(OH)_3} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH + Zn^{2+} \rightarrow \underset{white}{Zn(OH)_2} \downarrow \\\\[3pt] NH_4OH + Pb^{2+} \rightarrow \underset{white}{Pb(OH)_2} \end{align} \)

Reducing Properties

  1. It reduces bleaching powder to calcium chloride.
    \( 3CaOCl_2 + 2NH_3 \rightarrow CaCl_2 + N_2 + 3H_2O \)

  2. Chlorine to hydrogen chloride.
    \( 2NH_3 + 3Cl_2 \rightarrow N_2 + 6HCl \)

  3. Certain hot metallic oxides to metals.
    \( \begin{align} 2NH_3 + \underset{Black}{3CuO} \rightarrow \underset{Reddish}{3Cu} + 3H_2O + N_2 \\\\[3pt] 2NH_3 + 3PbO \rightarrow \underset{silvery \ white}{3 Pb} + 3H_2O + N_2 \end{align} \)

Uses :

  1. It is used in manufacture of fertilizers, large scale preparation of nitric acid, nitrogen and ammonium compounds like nylon, rayon, plastics and explosives etc.,

  2. In the manufacture of sodium carbonate, refrigerant, as a source of hydrogen and cleaning agent as it emulsifies fats and grease.

Oxides of Nitrogen

Nitrous oxide ( \( N_2O \) ) :

It is prepared by heating ammonium nitrate
\( NH_4NO_3 \rightarrow N_2O + 2H_2O \)
It is a colourless neutral gas with pleasant odour. It supports combustion.
\( \begin{align} S + 2N_2O \rightarrow SO_2 + 2N_2 \\\\[3pt] Mg + N_2O \rightarrow MgO + N_2 \end{align} \)

Nitric oxide(NO) :

It is obtained by the action of dilute nitric acid on copper.
\( 3Cu + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO \)
It is a colourless neutral gas and sparingly soluble in water. It combines with oxygen to form brown fumes of nitrogen dioxide.
\( 2NO + O_2 \rightarrow 2NO_2 \)

Nitrogen dioxide ( \( NO_2 \) ) :

It is liberated when lead nitrate is heated.
\( 2Pb(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2PbO + 4NO_2 + O_2 \)
It is a brown coloured pungent smelling acidic gas. It is absorbed by alkalies to give nitrites and nitrates.
\( 2NaOH + 2NO_2 \rightarrow NaNO_2 + NaNO_3 + H_2O \)

Nitrogen trioxide ( \( N_2O_3 \) ) :

It is obtained by cooling a mixture of "NO" and " \( NO_2 \) " taken in equimolar ratio 253 K
\( NO + NO_2 \rightarrow N_2O_3 \)
It is an acidic oxide and can be condensed to form a blue coloured liquid and is an acidic oxide. It combines with alkalies to form nitrites.
\( 2NaOH + N_2O_3 \rightarrow 2NaNO_2 + H_2O \)

Nitrogen pentoxide ( \( N_2O_5 \) ) :

It is prepared by dehydrating nitric acid with \( P_4O_{10} \)
\( 4HNO_3 + P_4O_{10} \rightarrow 4HPO_3 + 2N_2O_5 \)
It is a white crystalline solid with melting point \( 30^{\circ}C \) to give yellow liquid which decomposes to give \( NO_2 \) at \( 40^{\circ}C \) .
\( 2N_2O_5 \rightarrow 4NO_2 + O_2 \)
It oxidizes iodine to \( I_2O_5 \) .
\( I_2 + 5N_2O_5 \rightarrow I_2O_5 + 10NO_2 \)
Ionic reactions take place in the presence of aq. NaCl proving the existence of ionic nitronium and nitrate \( \left ( NO_2^+, \ NO_3^- \right ) \) .
\( N_2O_5 + NaCl \rightarrow NaNO_3 + NO_2Cl \)

Nitric acid

It is prepared by heating \( KNO_3 \) or \( NaNO_3 \) with equimolar quantity of concentrated sulphuric acid in a glass retort. Nitric acid being more volatile, it is displaced and collected Fuming nitric acid is prepared by distilling \( KNO_3 \) or \( NaNO_3 \) with concentrated sulphuric acid in the presence of starch. Starch reduces nitric acid to \( N_2O_3 \) and \( N_2O_4 \) which dissolve in the acid and produce yellow colour. This is called fuming nitric acid and is used in oxidation reactions. On large scale, nitric acid is manufactured by catalytic oxidation of ammonia(Ostwald's process).
\( \begin{align} 4NH_3 + 5O_2 \xrightarrow[900^{\circ}C]{Pt(catalyst)} 4NO + 6H_2O \\\\[3pt] 2NO + O_2 \rightarrow 2NO_2 \\\\[3pt] 4NO_2 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 4HNO_3 \end{align} \)

Physical Properties:

  1. It is a volatile acid.

  2. It is a colourless hygroscopic liquid with an irritating chocking smell and acid taste.

  3. Pure nitric acid (98%) boils at \( 86^{\circ}C \) .

  4. Aqueous nitric acid (68%) forms a constant boiling mixture at \( 121^{\circ}C \) .

  5. It acquires yellow colour due to dissolved \( NO_2 \) , formed by decomposition of nitric acid.

  6. Dilute nitric acid turns skin yellow. Concentrated nitric acid causes blisters on the skin and is highly corrosive.

Chemical Properties:

  1. It reacts with bases to form salt and water only
    \( 2HNO_3 + MgO \rightarrow Mg(NO_3)_2 + H_2O \)

  2. With non metals
    \( \begin{align} C + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow CO_2 + 4NO_2 + 2H_2O \\\\[3pt] P + 5HNO_3 \rightarrow H_3PO_4 + 5NO_2 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] S + 6HNO_3 \rightarrow H_2SO_4 + 6NO_2 + 2H_2O \end{align} \)

  3. With inorganic compounds
    \( 2KI + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow 2KNO_3 + 2H_2O + 2NO_2 \)

  4. Oxidation of Organic Compounds :
    Sawdust, turpentine and alcohols burst into flames on addition of conc. nitric acid or fuming nitric acid. Sawdust contains cellulose. Cellulose is an organic compound which contains carbon, hydrogen and oxygen.

  5. Oxidation of Metals (Exceptions : Magnesium and manganese):
    Very dilute nitric acid (1%) reacts with magnesium and manganese at low temperatures producing hydrogen.
    \( \begin{align} Mg + 2HNO_3 \rightarrow Mg(NO_3)_2 + H_2 \\\\[3pt] Mn + 2HNO_3 \rightarrow Mn(NO_3)_2 + H_2 \end{align} \)
    Reaction of dil. nitric acid at ordinary temperatures with
    \( \begin{align} 3Cu + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO \\\\[3pt] 3Zn + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Zn(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO \\\\[3pt] 3Fe + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Fe(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO \end{align} \)
    Metal + Conc.nitric acid \( \rightarrow \) Metal nitrate + water + nitrogen dioxide
    \( \begin{align} Cu + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2 + 2H_2O + 2NO_2 \\\\[3pt] Zn + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow Zn(NO_3)_2 + 2H_2O + 2NO_2 \\\\[3pt] Fe + 6HNO_3 \rightarrow Fe(NO_3)_2 + 3H_2O + 3NO_2 \end{align} \)

  6. When hot conc. nitric acid is added to iron, a thin film of iron oxide is formed on the metal. As a result the metal does not react further with the acid. The iron is said to be rendered passive.

Nitrates

They are all soluble in water and thermally unstable.

Action of heat on nitrates :

  1. Nitrates of active metals
    \( \begin{align} 2KNO_3 \xrightarrow{heat} 2KNO_2 + O_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] 2NaNO_3 \xrightarrow{heat} 2NaNO_2 + O_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

  2. Moderately active metals
    \( \begin{align} 2Zn(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2ZnO + 4NO_2 \uparrow + O_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] 2Cu(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2CuO + 4NO_2 \uparrow + O_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

  3. Nitrates of metals like silver and mercury
    \( \begin{align} 2AgNO_3 \rightarrow 2Ag + 2NO_2 \uparrow + O_2 \uparrow \\\\[3pt] Hg(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow Hg + 2NO_2 \uparrow + O_2 \uparrow \end{align} \)

Brown - ring test for nitrates :

Slow addition of concentrated sulphuric acid from the sides to a solution of nitrate and freshly prepared aqueous solution of ferrous sulphate results in the formation of brown ring at the junction of layers. This is commonly known as brown-ring test.
\( \begin{align} 2NO_3^- + SO_4^{2-} + Fe^{2+} + 8H^+ \rightarrow 6Fe^{3+} + 2NO + 4H_2O \\\\[3pt] FeSO_4 + NO \rightarrow \underset{Brown}{[Fe(NO)]SO_4} \end{align} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Concentrated \( HNO_3 \) turns yellow in sun-light.Explain.

Solution:
\( HNO_3 \) is partially decomposed in sunlight to produce \( NO_2 \) . The \( NO_2 \) formed dissolves in \( HNO_3 \) to produce yellow colour.
\( 4HNO_3 \xrightarrow{Sunlight} 4NO_2 + 2H_2O + O_2 \)

Example 2:

Question:
A bottle of liquor ammonia should be cooled before opening. Explain.

Solution:
To lower the pressure of \( NH_3 \) inside the bottle otherwise \( NH_3 \) will bump out.

Example 3:

Question:
Conc. \( H_2SO_4 \) , anhydrous \( CaCl_2 \ or \ P_4O_{10} \) cannot be used as drying agent for ammonia, why?

Solution:
Because these drying agents are acidic and so they will react with \( NH_3 \) .

Example 4:

Question:
Nitrous oxide supports combustion more vigorously than air. Explain.

Solution:
\( N_2O \) is better source of oxygen than air because air has oxygen about \( \frac{1}{5} \) of its volume, while \( N_2O \) on decomposition produces oxygen about \( \frac{1}{3} \) of the volume.

Example 5:

Question:
Nitric oxide becomes brown when released into air. Explain.

Solution:
Nitric oxide reacts with air to get oxidized to \( NO_2 \) which has brown yellow vapours.
\( 2NO + O_2 \rightarrow 2NO_2 \)

Example 6:

Question:
Ammonia is used as refrigerating agent. Why ?

Solution:
\( NH_3 \) , has high heat of vaporisation and so high value of Joule - Thomson coefficient.

Example 7:

Question:
\( Mg_3N_2 \) hydrolysis to give \( NH_3 \) but \( MgCl_2 \) does not liberate HCl by hydrolysis at room temperature. Why ?

Solution:
\( MgCl_2 \) (a salt of strong acid and strong base) is not hydrolysed. On the other hand \( Mg_3N_2 \) a salt of strong base \( Mg(OH)_2 \) . and weak acid \( NH_3 \) is hydrolysed to give \( NH_3 \) .

Example 8:

Question:
Aluminium containers can be used for storing conc. \( HNO_3 \) . Why ?

Solution:
Al becomes passive on coming in contact with \( HNO_3 \) due to a fine coating of its oxide on its surface and thus Al containers can be used to store conc. \( HNO_3 \) .

Example 9:

Question:
Copper dissolves in \( HNO_3 \) , but not in HCl. Why ?

Solution:
Copper is placed below H in electrochemical series and does not liberate \( H_2 \) , from However, \( HNO_3 \) oxidises Cu due to its strong oxidant nature. \( \begin{align} 3Cu + 8HNO_{3(dil)} \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO \\\\[3pt] Cu + 4HNO_{3(conc)} \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2 + 2H_2O + 2NO_2 \end{align} \)

Example 10:

Question:
Calcium cyanamide is used as fertilizer. Why ?

Solution:
\( CaCN_2 + 3H_2O \rightarrow CaCO_3 + 2NH_3 \)
Calcium cyanamide is used as a fertiliser as it on contact with water decomposes, to liberate ammonia.

Example 11:

Question:
Commercial nitrogen is passed through copper chips kept in ammonia solution for removing oxygen. Why ?

Solution:
Oxygen present in commercial nitrogen is removed by copper chips in ammonia in the form of cupra ammonium hydroxide complex.
\( 2Cu + 8HNO_3 + O_2 + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2Cu[(NH_3)_4](OH)_2 \)

Example 12:

Question:
Magnesium continues to burn in nitric oxide, while burning of sulphur is extinguished. Why?

Solution:
Heat evolved during burning of Mg is appreciably high which decomposes \( NO \) to \( N_2 \and \ O_2 \) . \( O_2 \) formed supports combustion of Mg. However, heat evolved during burning of S is not enough to decompose NO.

Example 13:

Question:
\( N_2, \ CO, \ CN^- \ and \ NO^+ \) are isoelectronic but the former is chemically inert and later these are very reactive, why ?

Solution:
Although each of the above has same electronic distribution in bonding orbitals but \( N_2 \) is inert because of (i) non - polar nature of bond, (ii) high dissociation energy of \( N \equiv N \) . Rest all ( \( CO, \ CN^-, \ NO^+ \) ) are polar.

Example 14:

Question:
\( NF_3 \) is stable but \( NCl_3 \ and \ NI_3 \) are readily hydrolysed and are explosives. Explain.

Solution:
\( NF_3 \) is not hydrolysed because neither N nor F can expand their octet by using d-orbitals. On the other hand \( NCl_3 \ and \ NI_3 \) hydrolyse violently because Cl and I can expand their octet by using d - orbitals.
\( NCl_3 + 3H_2O \rightarrow NH_3 + 3HOCl \)

Example 15:

Question:
The Haber's process is carried out at high temperature even through the equilibrium constant for the reaction decreases with increasing temperature. Explain, why a high temperature is necessary ?

Solution:
For this reaction, the temperature dependence of the equilibrium constant and the reaction rate are opposing effects. At ambient temperatures, the rate of the reaction is extremely slow, and equilibrium is not established in an economically viable length of time. To establish a reasonable rate, the reaction must be carried out at high temperature.
