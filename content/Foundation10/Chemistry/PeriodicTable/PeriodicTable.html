Introduction

Only a few elements were known before eighteenth century and it was easy to remember them and their properties. As more and more elements were discovered, it became impossible to study as well as remember the properties of the elements individually. Hence it was necessary to classify these elements on the basis of some simple and logical pattern.
An arrangement in which similar elements are grouped together and are separated from the dissimilar elements is known as classification of elements.

Doberenier's law of traids

John Doberenier suggested that properties of various elements can be related to their atomic masses. He arranged elements with similar properties in groups of three called triads. In a triad, the atomic mass of the middle element was approximately the mean of the atomic masses of the other two elements.

Alkali metal group

The elements lithium, sodium and potassium have similar chemical properties and form a traid.

Newton's law of Octaves

John Newlands arranged the elements in the increasing order of atomic masses and observed that every eighth element lead the properties similar to that of first element like the octaves of musical notes.
The main drawback of this concept is it could not be applied to elements having atomic mass greater than 40. The sequence of elements was disturbed with the discovery of noble gases.

Anomalies of Mendeleef's periodic table:

  1. Elements with similar properties were placed in different groups and those with dissimilar properties were placed in the same group. Subdivision of groups I to VII.

  2. Elements with higher atomic weights were placed before elements with lower atomic weight so as to maintain similar chemical nature. Example : Co, Ni ; Ar, K : Te, I

  3. Isotopes did not find any place in the periodic table

Modern Periodic Table:

The physical and chemical properties of elements are the periodic functions of their atomic numbers or electronic configurations.

Description of Periods

There are 7 periods (horizontal rows) in the modern periodic table. Each period begins with the outermost electron entering into a new principal quantum number and complete after the outermost shell's p - subshell is complete. The number of the period is given by the number of the outermost shell of that element. The first element of each period is an alkali metal (except I period) and the last element is an inert gas. The 14 elements of VI and VII periods in which f orbital is successively filled are placed in two separate rows at the bottom of the periodic table called as Lanthanides [atomic number 58 (Ce) to atomic number 71 (Lu)] and Actinides [(from atomic number 90 (Th) to atomic number 103 (Lr)].

Description of Groups

There are 18 vertical columns called groups. All the members of a particular group have same outer shell electronic configuration. The elements of the groups 1, 2, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17 and 18 are known as normal or representative elements. Except for the group 18 elements, all the elements of other groups have their outermost energy level incomplete.

Classification of elements into Blocks:

Based on the electronic configuration elements are classified into s, p, d and f blocks. This is based on the type of atomic orbital to which the differentiating electron enter.

The electron by which the electronic configuration of the given element differs from that the preceeding element is called the differentiating electron and that electron is the last entering electron of its atom.

s - block elements

Elements in which differentiating electron enters into outermost s - subshell are called s - block elements. The elements have either \( ns^1 \) (group 1) or \( ns^2 \) (group 2) outer electronic configuration. Group 1 elements are called alkali metals (except H) while group 2 elements are called alkaline earth metals.

p - block elements

Elements in which the differentiating electron enters into p-orbital of outermost shell. Groups 13, 14, 15, 16, 17 and 18 belong to this block. The outermost electronic configuration varies from \( ns^2 np^1 \) to \( ns^2 np^6 \) . The elements of this block include metals, nonmetals and few metalloids.

d - block elements

Elements in which the differentiating electron enters the penultimate shell of d - orbital (or) (n -1) d orbital. Groups 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11 and 12 belong to this block. Elements having two incomplete outer shells are known as transition elements. There are three complete and one incomplete d series. They are
\( \begin{align} 3d series \rightarrow \ _{21}Sc \ to \ _{30}Zn \\\\[12pt] 4d series \rightarrow \ _{39}Y \ to \ _{48}Cd \\\\[12pt] 5d series \rightarrow \ _{57}La, \ _{72}Hf \ to \ _{80}Hg \\\\[12pt] 6d series \rightarrow \ d(incomplete) _{89}Ac, \ _{104}Rf \ to \ _{111}Rg \end{align} \)
The outermost electronic configuration of these elements is \( (n - 1) d^{1 - 10} ns^{1 - 2} \) .

f - block elements

Elements in which the differentiating electron enters the antipenultimate shell of orbitals ((n - 2) f - orbital). There are two series of f - block elements 4f series and 5f series.
Elements of 4f series are called lanthanides , \( _{58}Ce \ to \ _{71}Lu \) .
Elements of 5f series are called actinides \( _{90}Th \ to \ _{103}Lr \) .
The actinides are radio active elements and elements after atomic number 93 are called transuranic elements or man made elements. The outermost electronic configuration of these elements is \( (n - 2)f^{1 - 14} (n - 1)d^{0, \ 1} ns^2 \) .

Classification of elements based on properties

Based on the properties and electronic configuration elements are classified into 4 types.

  1. noble gases

  2. representative elements

  3. transition elements

  4. inner transition elements

Noble gas elements

Eements of group 18 ('O' Group) are called noble gas elements. The outermost electronic configuration of these elements is \( ns^2 np^6 \) except He which has \( 1s^2 \) . They are He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe and Rn. They are highly inactive due to completely filled valence shell. They exist as monoatomic gases.

Representative elements

Elements of s and p blocks except the 18th group are called representative elements. The outermost electronic configuration is \( ns^{1 - 2}np^{1 - 5} \) . The outermost shell is incompletely filled with electrons. These elements attain the nearest inert gas configuration by losing, gaining or sharing of electrons.

Transition elements

Elements of d - block in which the d - orbital is incompletely filled are called transition elements (except, Zn, Cd and Hg). They are metals with high melting and boiling points. They exhibit variable valency. They are good conductors of heat and electricity. Metals, and their oxides act as catalyst. They form alloys. d block are called transition elements as they are present between s and p blocks and their properties lie between them.

Inner transition elements

Elements present between La and Hf of 6th period and Ac and Lr of 7th period of group 3 are called inner transition elements.
They have the general electronic configuration of \( (n - 2) f^{1 - 14} (n - 1)d^{0, \ 1} ns^2 \) .
They show a common oxidation state of +3 in most of their compounds (due to less difference in the energy between (n - 1)d and ns orbitals, the electrons are involved the bond formation.
The properties of elements change gradually with a change in electronic configuration of the elements, which repeat after regular intervals called periodicity.
The basis for the periodicity lies in the periodic repetition of the elements, with atomic number. In the periodic table the properties of elements repeat at the intervals of 2, 8, 18, 18 and 32.

Some of the Periodic Properties of element are

  1. Atomic radius

  2. Ionisation potential (Ionisation energy)

  3. Electron affinity

  4. Electronegativity

  5. Electropositivity

  6. Metallic and Non - metallic nature

  7. Oxidation state.

Periods Groups
1 Atomic size : In a period the increased nuclear charge attracts the electrons more strongly and the size of the atom decreases. On moving down a group, the atomic radii of the elements increase with an increase in the atomic number.
2 Ionisation potential: Ionization energy generally increases from left to right along a period because the atomic size of the elements gradually decreases due to the increase in the atomic number. Ionisation energy decreases from top to bottom in a group because of the increase in the atomic radius. which is due to the increase in the number of shells.
3 Electron affinity : Electron affinity values generally increase on moving from left to right along a period due to the decrease in the atomic size and the increase in its effective nuclear charge. Electron affinity gradually decreases from top to bottom of a group. This is due to the steady increase in the atomic radius and the decrease in the effective nuclear charge of the elements.
4 Electron Negativity : Electronegativity increases on moving along a period from left to right. This is due to the increase in nuclear charge and decrease in atomic size, as a result of which shared electron pair can be attracted more towards itself. Electronegativity decreases from top to bottom in a group due to an increase in the atomic size, as a result of which the shared electron pair is attracted less towards itself.
5 Electropositivity: Electropositive character decreases from left to right along a period to the increase in ionization potential. Electropositive character increases from top to bottom along a group due to the decrease in ionization potential.
6 Oxidising / reducing capacity : Due to the increase in electron affinity from left to right of a period, the oxidizing capacity of the elements increases and the reducing capacity decreases from left to right along a period. Due to the decrease in ionization potential as well as electron affinity from top to bottom of a group, oxidizing capacity of the element decreases whereas its reducing capacity increases on moving down a group.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Arrange the following ions in the order of increasing size :
\( Be^{2+}, \ Cl^-, \ S^{2-}, \ Na^+, \ Mg^{2+}, \ Br^- \)

Solution:
Anion is greater in size than its neutral atom. As charge on anion increases size decreases. Secondly, in case of cation, its size is less than the size of its neutral atom.
\( Be^{2+} < Mg^{2+} < Na^{+} < Cl^{-} < S^{2-} < Br^{-} \)
In the above, the ions also belong to different period and group. Along group from top to bottom size increases and along a period from left to right size decreases.

Example 6:

Question:
Arrange the following elements in the increasing order of metallic character : Si, Be, Mg, Na, P.

Solution:
We know that metallic character increases down a group and decreases along a period as we move from left to right.
Hence the order of increasing metallic character is \( P < Si < Be < Mg < Na \) .

Example 7:

Predict the position of the element in the periodic table satisfying the electronic configuration \( (n - 1)d^1 ns^2 \) , for n = 4.

Solution:
For n = 4, the electronic configuration is \( 3d^14s^2 \) . The element belongs to d-block elements. Group number = No. of electrons in (n - 1)d subshell + No. of electrons in \( n^{th} \) shell
= 1 + 2 = 3

Example 8:

On the basis of quantum numbers, justify that the sixth period of the table should have 32 elements.

Solution:
The sixth period of the periodic table begins with n = 6. It involves the filling of 6s, 4f, 5d and 6p subshell in the increasing order of energy. The total number of orbitals available are 16 and therefore, the maximum number of electrons that can be accommodated is 32. Thus, the sixth period of the periodic table should have 32 elements.

Example 9:

Which of the following species will have the largest and the smallest size ? \( Mg, Mg^{2+}, Al, Al^{3+} \)

Solution:
Atomic radii decrease across a period. So, Mg will have larger size than Al. Cations are smaller than their parent atoms. So size is : \( Mg^{2+} < Mg \) and \( Al^{3+} < Al \) . Now, among isoelectronic ions, the ion with the larger positive nuclear charge will have a smaller radius i.e., size is : \( Al^{3+} < Al \) .
Hence, the largest species is Mg, the smallest species is \( Al^{3+} \) .
