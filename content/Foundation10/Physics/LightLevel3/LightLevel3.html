Introduction

Light is one of the principal means by which we gain the knowledge of the world. Consequently, the understanding of nature of light becomes prime importance. In the study, whether the light has a particle nature or a wave nature has been revealed. Apart from this, the phenomenon are studied based on these theories. The present topic also deals with study of these theories along with understanding optical instruments, dispersion, scattering of light etc.

The Eye

Construction and Working

The eye is one of our most important sense organs. For us to see properly, the eye primarily performs three functions: it makes adjustments to admit appropriate amount of light, it bends the rays of light to form a sharp image, and it collects and sends information about the image to the brain for further 'processing'. Different parts of the eye perform these functions.
The different parts of the eye are contained with in the spherical eyeball, which is about 2.5 cm in diameter.

Parts of human eye Function
Sclerotic The outer most coating of the eye is called the sclerotic. It is opaque, tough and white in colour. It protects and holds the eye ball (the main and delicate part of the eye)
Cornea The sclerotic layer continues in the front of the eye as the cornea. This is the transparent white portion of the eye that visibly bulges out of the eyes socket. It allow the light to enter the eye ball.
Choroid It is a grey membrane attached to the sclerotic on its inner side. It is dark pigmented and is richly supplied with blood vessels

  1. It darkens the interior part of the eye and hence prevents internal reflection.

  2. It nourishes the inner parts of the eye.
Optic nerve The signals of the image are sent to the brain through optic nerves
Retina The Optic nerve on entering the eye ball, spreads like a canopy and each nerve endings form a kind of hemispherical screen called retina. The inner most coating of the eye is called the retina. It is a semitransparent delicate membrane which is sensitive to light. Thus, any image formed on the retina is converted to optical impulses which are sent to brain. The retina has two very important regions, the yellow spot and the blind spot. It serves as a sensitive screen and receives the images formed by the optical pulses and sends them to the brain through the optic nerve.
Yellow spot It is situated at the centre of the retina and is slightly raised. It has a little depression called fovea centrals which is extremely sensitive to light. Yellow spot helps in forming a extremely clear image. When an object is very small, then it is brought to focus at this yellow spot.
Blind spot The region on the retina, where the optic nerve enters the eye ball is called the blind spot. It has no nerve endings and hence, is insensitive to light. In actual sense, it has no function. Any image formed at this spot is not visible.
Crystalline lens It is situated just behind the iris and is held in position with the help of ciliary muscles. The lens is a transparent crystalline double convex lens. Its inner side is more convex than the outer one, i.e, the inner side of the lens has a smaller radius of curvature than that of the outer side. The ciliary muscles, together with the suspensory ligaments, can increase or decrease the curvature of this lens. This crystalline lens divides the eye ball into two unequal parts. The front part of the eye is called the anterior portion and the back part is called the posterior portion. To focus the images of the distant and near object clearly on the retina.
Ciliary muscles It is a ring of muscles, along with suspensory ligament, which holds the crystalline lens in position. Its function is to alter the focal length of the crystalline lens. When these muscles contract, the focal length of the crystalline lens decreases.
Iris Behind the cornea is an opaque diaphragm called the iris. It lends colour to the eye. It has a central circular aperture called the pupil. The diameter of the pupil is regulated by the iris which is of muscular fibres. It thus, regulates the amount of light that enters the eye. It controls the amount of light entering the eye.
Vitreous humour It is a dense, jelly like fluid and is slightly grey in colour. It fills the posterior (back) part of the eye ball. It prevents the eye ball from collapsing due to change in atmospheric pressure.
Aqueous humour It is a watery, saline fluid. It fills the anterior (front) portion of the eye. It prevents the anterior (front) position of the eye from collapsing, due to the changes in the atmospheric pressure.

Accomodation

Accommodation is the process by which the eye changes the focal length of the eye-lens such that a sharp image is always formed on the retina.

Far point and near point

The farthest point up to which the eye can see properly is called the far point of the eye. The closest point at which an object can be placed and seen clearly is called the near point of the eye. The distance of the closest point at which an object can be placed and seen clearly is called the least distance of distinct vision or least distance of clear vision. The standard value of the least distance of distinct vision for the normal human eye is taken as 25 cm.

Power of accomodation

The maximum variation in the power \( \left ( \frac{1}{f} \right ) \) of the eye-lens that can be achieved by the eye of a person is called its power of accommodation.

Persistence of Vision

The phenomenon of the continuance of visual sensation of an image in the brain for a short time after the object, whose image is being formed, changes or is removed, is called persistence of vision.

Near - sightedness(Myopia)

Near - sightedness is a defect of vision due to which a person is not able to see distant object clearly. In near-sightedness, a parallel beam of light falls to converge on the retina even when the focal length of the eye-lens is maximum, but a divergent beam converges on it. So, to correct this defect, a parallel beam is made divergent before it enters the eye by putting a concave lens in front of the eye. The focal length of the concave lens is so chosen that divergent rays entering the eye appear to come from the far point of the unaided myopic eye.

Far-sightedness (Hypermetropia)

Far - sightedness is a defect of vision due to which a person is not able to clearly see objects placed at the normal near point.In far-sightedness, a parallel beam of light falls to diverge on the retina even when the focal length of the eye-lens is maximum, but a convergent beam diverges on it. So, to correct this defect, a parallel beam is made convergent before it enters the eye by putting a convex lens in front of the eye. The focal length of the convex lens is so chosen that convergent rays entering the eye appear to come from the near point of the unaided Hypermetropic eye.

Presbyopia

Presbyopia is that defect of the eye due to which an old person cannot read comfortably and clearly without spectacles. Presbyopia occurs in old age due to the gradual weaken-ing of the ciliary muscles and diminishing flexibility of the eye-lens. The near - point of the old person having presbyopia gradually recedes and becomes much more than 25 centimetres away. Presbyopia defect is corrected in the same way as hypermetropia by using spectacles having convex lenses.

Astigmatism

Astigmatism is that defect of the eye due to which a person cannot focus on both horizontal and vertical lines simultaneously. Astigmatism can be corrected by superimposing cylindrical lenses upon the spherical shape of spectacle lenses.
Cataract develops when the eye-lens of a person becomes hazy (or even opaque) due to formation total loss of vision of the eye.

Refraction of light through a prism

A is the angle of the prism or refracting angle, D is the angle of deviation. \( i_1, \ i_2 \) are the angle of incidence and the angle of emergence and \( r_1, \ r_2 \) are the angles of refraction.

  1. angle of prism \( A = r_1 + r_2 \)

  2. angle of deviation \( D = i_1 + i_2 - A \)

  3. refractive index of the prism \( \mu = \frac{sin \ i_1}{sin \ r_1} = \frac{sin \ i_2}{sin \ r_2} \)

Minimum deviation

The angle through which a light ray is deviated after passing through a given prism, depends upon the angle of incidence of the light ray falling on the prism. If a light ray is allowed to fall on the prism at different angles of incidence than for each angle of incidence the angle of deviation will be different. The angle of deviation whose value is minimum is called minimum deviation.

When the prism is in the minimum deviation position

  1. The light ray is symmetrical about the prism.

  2. \( i_1 = i_2 = i \) and \( r_1 = r_2 = r \)

  3. The angle of minimum deviation \( D_m = 2i - A \)

  4. Angle of incidence, \( i = \frac{A + D_m}{2} \)

  5. Angle of refraction, \( r = \frac{A}{2} \)

  6. Refractive index of prism, \( \mu = \frac{sin \left( \frac{A + D_m}{2} \right)}{sin \frac{A}{2}} \)

  7. In minimum deviation position, the refracted ray inside the prism is parallel to the base.

Mirage

Mirage is a phenomenon in which atmospheric refraction of light causes more than one image of an object, of which at least one is inverted.

Atmospheric refraction and optical phenomena

The earth is surrounded by a layer of air called the atmosphere, which extends several hundred kilometres above its surface. Density of air in the atmosphere is not the same everywhere. In general, it is greatest at the earth's surface and goes on decreasing as we move higher. The refractive index of air depends on its density - higher the density of air, greater its refractive index. Because of atmospheric refraction, a heavenly body always appears higher in the sky than its actual position.
All the air in the atmosphere is not at the same temperature. Some of the air layers of the atmosphere are cold whereas other air layers of the atmosphere are comparatively warm(or hotter). Now the cooler air layers of the atmosphere behave as optically denser medium for light rays whereas the warmer the air layers of the atmosphere behave as optically rarer medium for the light rays. So, in the same atmosphere we have air layers having different optical densities. And when light rays pass through the atmosphere having air layers of different optical densities, then refraction of light takes place . The refraction of light caused by the earth's atmosphere having air layers of varying optical densities is called atmospheric refraction.
Some of the optical phenomena in nature which occur due to the atmospheric refraction of light.

  1. Twinkling of stars:

    • Twinkling of a star is due to the atmospheric refraction of star's light.

  2. The stars seems higher than they actually are:

    • Due to atmospheric refraction, the stars seem to be higher in the sky than they actually are.

  3. Advanced sunrise and delayed sunset:

    • We can see the sun about 2 minutes before the actual sunrise and 2 minutes after the actual sunset because of atmospheric refraction.

Scattering of light

Scattering of light means to throw light in various random directions. Light is scattered when it falls in various types of suspended particles in its path. Depending on the size of particles, the scattering can be of white sunlight as such or of the coloured lights which make up the white sunlight.

Tyndall Effect

The scattering of light of particles in its path is called Tyndall effect.

The colour of scattered light depends on the size of scattering particles

  1. Dust particles and water droplets suspended in the atmosphere are much larger than the wavelength range of visible light.

  2. The air molecules (nitrogen and oxygen gas molecules) present in the atmosphere are smaller than the wavelength range of visible light.

The sky is Blue

The scattering of blue component of the white sunlight by air molecules present in the atmosphere causes the blue colour of the sky.

The sun appears red at sunrise and sunset

The sun and the surrounding sky appear red at sunrise and at sunset because at that time most of the blue colour present in sunlight has been scattered out and away from our line of sight, leaving behind mainly red colour in the direct sunlight beam that reaches our eyes.

Dispersion of light

In the year 1665, Newton discovered by his experiment with glass prisms that white light (like sunlight) consists of a mixture of seven colours. The band of seven colours formed on a white screen, when a beam of white light is passed through a glass prism, is called spectrum of white light. The seven colours of the spectrum are: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo and Violet. The splitting up of white light into seven colours on passing through a transparent medium like a glass prism is called dispersion of light. The dispersion of white light occurs because of the angle of refraction (or angle of bending) of lights of different colours is different when passing through the glass prism. The red colour is deviated least, violet colour is deviated the maximum.

Colour

Colour is a sensation produced in the brain due to the excitation of retina, by an electromagnetic wave of some particular wavelength.

Primary colours

The primary colours are the colours of light which, when mixed in equal proportions give white light.

Secondary colours

The colours of light obtained by mixing two primary colours are called secondary colours.

Simple microscope

A convex lens of short focal length can be used to see magnified image of a small object and is called a magnifying glass or a simple microscope.

Principle:

When a small object is placed between the optical centre and the focus of a convex lens, its virtual, erect and magnified image is formed on the same side of the lens. The lens is held close to the eye and the distance of the object is adjusted, till the image is formed at the least distance of distinct vision from the eye. For a normal, eye the least distance of distinct vision is 25 cm.

Compound microscope

A compound microscope is used to see the extremely small objects. It consists of two lenses. A lens of short aperture and short aperture and short focal length facing the object is called the object lens and another lens of short focal length but large aperture is called the eye lens. The two lenses are placed coaxially at the two ends of a tube. To focus over an object, the distance of the object lens from the object is adjusted with the help of a rack and pinion arrangement.

Principle:

When a small object is placed just outside the focus ( \( F_0 \) ) of the object lens, a real, inverted and magnified image is produced on the other side of the lens beyond \( 2F_0 \) . The image produced by the object lens acts as object for the eye lens. The distance of the object from the object lens so adjusted that the final image is formed at the least distance of distinct vision from the eye.

Telescope

A telescope is an instrument for seeing the distant objects clearly and magnified (though apparently). It is of two types.

Astronomical Telescope

A telescope used to see the heavenly bodies is called an astronomical telescope. It produces a virtual and inverted image. As the heavenly bodies are round, the inverted image does not affect the observation.
An astronomical telescope consists of two lens system. The lens system facing the object is called objective. It has large aperture and is of large focal length ( \( f_0 \) ). The other lens system is called eye - piece. It has large aperture and is of short focal length ( \( f_e \) ). The objective and the eye - piece are mounted coaxially in two metallic tubes. The tube holding the eye - piece can be made to slide inside the tube holding the objective with the help of rack and pinion arrangement.

Principle:

The objective forms the real and inverted image of the distant object at its focal place. The distance of the eye - piece from the objective is adjusted, till the final image is formed at the least distance of distinct vision.(In case, the telescope is said to be in normal adjustment)

Terrestrial Telescope

For observing object on earth, it would be necessary to have an erect image. A telescope used for observing objects on the earth is called a terrestrial telescope.

Theories of light

The earlier understanding of nature of light was given by Newtons in the form of corpuscular theory. This theory could not explain certain experimental observations. So Huygen propounded wave theory of light.

Newton's Corpuscular Theory of Light

The first scientific attempt to explain the nature of light was made by Sir Isaac Newton. About the middle of 17th Century. Newton proposed 'Corpuscular theory of light'.
According to this theory,

  1. light consists of tiny and perfectly elastic particles called 'corpuscles'.

  2. These corpuscles are emitted by a luminous source like sun, candle, electric lamp etc.

  3. The corpuscles travel in all directions in straight lines.

  4. The velocity of corpuscles is different for different dispersive media.

  5. When these corpuscles fall on the retina of the eye, they cause the sensation of vision.

  6. The corpuscles can be of different sizes and the various colours of light are supposed to be due to difference in their sizes.

Wavefront

According to wave theory of light, a source of light sends out disturbance in all directions. In a homogeneous medium, the disturbance reaches all those particles of the medium in phase, which are located at the same distance from the source of light and hence at any instant, all such particles must be vibrating in phase with each other.
The locus of all the particles of the medium, which at any instant are vibrating in the same phase, is called the wavefront. Depending upon the shape of the source of light, wavefront can be of the following types:

  1. Spherical wavefront:A spherical wavefront is produced by a point source of light. It is because, the locus of all such points, which are equidistant from the point source, is a sphere.

  2. Cylindrical wavefront:When the source of light is linear in shape (such as a slit), a cylindrical wavefront is produced. It is because, all the points, which are equidistant from the linear source, lie on the source of a cylinder.

  3. Plane wavefront:A small part of a spherical or a cylindrical wavefront originating from a distant source will appear plane and hence it is called a plane wavefront.

Huygens' principle

Huygens' principle is a geometrical construction, which is used to determine the new position of a wavefront at a later time from its given position at any instant. In other words, the principle gives a method to know as to how light spreads out in the medium.
Huygen's principle is based on the following assumptions:

  1. Each point on the given or primary wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets sending out disturbance in directions in a similar manner as the original source of light does.

  2. The new position of the wavefront at any instant (called secondary wavefront) is the envelope of the secondary wavelets at that instant.

Interference

When two light waves superimpose, the resultant amplitude or intensity in the region of superposition is different than the amplitude or intensity of the individual waves. This modification in the distribution of intensity of the individual waves. This modification in the distribution of intensity in the region of superposition is called interference.
When the resultant amplitude is the sum of amplitudes due to the two waves, the interference is known as constructive interference. When the resultant amplitude is the difference of amplitudes due to the two waves, the interference is known as destructive interference.

Diffraction

The bending of light rays around the edges of an obstacle or aperture is called diffraction. Diffraction can be noticed when the size of the obstacle is comparable to that of wave length of light and for extended sources. The amount of bending of light depends upon the size of obstacle and the wavelength of the wave.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

The waves travelling with velocity of light and consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and also perpendicular to the direction of their propagation are called the electromagnetic waves. Such waves with different ranges of frequency constitute an electromagnetic spectrum.

Types of electromagnetic radiations

Visible spectrum:
The visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum is most familiar to us, because our eye is sensitive to this region. The wavelength of the visible spectrum extends about \( 0.4 \ \mu m \) to \( 0.7 \ \mu m \) , that is, from violet colour to red colour. Visible spectrum is emitted when the excited valence electrons in atoms jump back to their normal states.
Infrared (IR) spectrum:
Infrared radiations have wavelengths larger than those of visible light. That is from \( 0.7 \ \mu m \) to about \( 100 \ \mu m \) . They are emitted by molecules when they change their states of rotational or vibrational motion.
Microwaves:
Micro waves are electromagnetic waves with wavelengths in the range \( 10 \ \mu m \) to 10 m.
Radio waves:
Radio waves have wavelenghts from 1 m to about 100 km
Ultraviolet(UV) spectrum:
The radiations of wavelengths shorter than the visible light ranging from about \( 0.4 \ \mu m \) to 1 nm are called ultraviolet(UV) radiations. These radiations are produced by the transitions of the electrons in atoms.
X - rays:
X - rays are produced in discrete wavelenghts in individual transitions among the inner electrons of an atom. Wavelength of X - rays range from 0.001 nm( \( 0.01^{\circ} A \) ) to 10 nm ( \( 100^{\circ} A \) ).
Gamma \( \gamma \) rays:
Gamma rays are electromagnetic radiations with shortest wavelengths ranging from 0.0001 nm to 0.1 nm or from \( 0.001^{\circ} A \) to \( 1^{\circ} A \) . They are the most penetrating electromagnetic radiations.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
In what respects is Huygens' wave theory of light similar to Maxwell's electromagnetic theory? In what respect are the two theories different?

Solution:
The two theories are similar in the respect that according to both the theories, light is a wave motion. On the other hand, the two theories are different in the respect that according to electromagnetic theory, light does not require any material medium for its propagation; a material medium is necessary for the propagation of light wave according to the wave theory. For this, Huygens assumed the existence of the all pervading either medium.

Example 2:

Question:
Why ether was called a hypothetical medium?

Solution:
The two properties of ether i.e. very low value of density and very large value of volume elasticity are contradictory in nature. A medium having such properties cannot be conceived. For this reason, ether is called a hypothetical medium.

Example 3:

Question:
What is shape of wavefront orginating from

  1. a point source and

  2. a line source

Solution:

  1. The wave front originating from a point source is spherical in shape. It is because, the locus of all such points, which are equidistant from the point source, is a sphere.

  2. The wave front originating from a line source is cylindrical in shape. It is because, all the points, which are equidistant from the linear source, lie on the surface of a cylinder.

Example 4:

Question:
Why the angle of incidence must not be less then \( 30^{\circ} \) while calculating angle of minimum deviation ?

Solution:
The angle of incidence must not be less than \( 30^{\circ} \) because in that case the light ray, after refraction, goes through the first surface and enters the prism but due to total internal reflection by the second surface, it will not come out of the prism.

Example 5:

Question:
A person having a myopic eye uses a concave lens of focal length 50 cm. What is its nature ?

Solution:
Here we have a concave lens. Now, the focal length of a concave lens is considered negative, so it is to be written with a minus sign.
Thus, Focal length, f = -50 cm
\( \begin{align} = - \frac{50}{100} m = -0.5 m \\\\[3pt] Now, \ power, \ p = \frac{1}{f(in \ meters)} \\\\[3pt] p = \frac{1}{-0.5} \\\\[3pt] p = - \frac{1 \times 10}{5} \\\\[3pt] p = -2 \ dioptres \ (or \ -2D) \end{align} \)
Thus, the power of this concave lens is, -2 dioptres, which can also written as, -2D. The minus with the power indicates that it is a diverging lens or concave lens.

Example 6:

Question:
The power of a lens is +2.5 D. What kind of lens it is and what is its focal length ?

Solution:
The power of this lens has positive sign, so it is a convex lens. Now,
\( \begin{align} Power, \ p = \frac{1}{f(in \ meters)} \\\\[3pt] So, \ +2.5 = \frac{1}{f} \\\\[3pt] and \ f = \frac{1}{2.5}m \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2.5} \times 100 \ cm \end{align} \)
So, focal length, f = 40 cm (or + 40 cm)

Example 7:

Question:
Is the focal length of a lens the same for lights of all colours.

Solution:
No, The focal length of a lens depends upon its refractive index. Since the refractive index is different for lights of different colours, the focal length of a lens will be different for lights of different colours.

Example 8:

Question:
Eye is more sensitive to yellow colour. Why are then danger signals red ?

Solution:
The scattering (rayleigh scattering) of light is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the wavelength of light. So, the scattering of red light is much less than the yellow light and accordingly, the red light signals can be seen upto a longer distance without much loss in their intensity. For this reason, the danger signals are red in colour.

Example 9:

Question:
Explain, why white light is dispersed when passing through a prism.

Solution:
The velocity of light in a material medium depends upon its colour (wavelength) i.e the refractive index of a material is different for different colours. If we incident a ray of white light on a prism ; on emerging, the different colours are deviated through different angles. Due to this, white light splits into its constituent colours and the phenomenon is called dispersion.

Example 10:

Question:
What does a welder protect against, when he wears a mask ?

Solution:
The ultra - violet light coming out of the welding arc is harmful to eyes. The mask has a filter that absorbs the ultra - violet light.

Example 11:

Question:
A ray of white light shows no dispersion on emerging from a glass plate (slab). Is there no dispersion inside the glass plate as well ?

Solution:
The light disperses into different colours on entering the glass slab i.e. dispersion takes place inside a glass slab also. But when it emerges from its other parallel face, the different colours again combine to form white light.

Example 12:

Question:
Calculate the power of the eye - lens of the normal eye when it is focused at its

  1. far point, infinity and

  2. near point, 25 cm from the eye.

Assume the distance of the retina from the eye - lens to be 2.5 cm.

Solution:

  1. When the object is at infinity, the image forms at the focus of the lens (v = f). Hence, the focal length in this case is 2.5 cm. Thus the power is
    \( p = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{2.5 \times 10^{-2}m} = 40D \)

  2. In this case, the object is at 25 cm from the eye - lens, and the image is formed at 2.5 cm from the eye - lens. So, u = -25 cm, v = 2.5 cm. Then
    \( \begin{align} \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{2.5 cm} + \frac{1}{25 cm} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{100}{2.5m} + \frac{100}{25m} \\\\[3pt] = 44m^{-1} = 44 D. \end{align} \)
    The maximum variation in power is thus 4D.
