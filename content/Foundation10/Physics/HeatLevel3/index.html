S.No Common Misconception Fact
1 It is possible to just melt a given mass of ice completely by using an equal mass of water at \( 100^{\circ} C \) No. Since the latent heat of fusion of ice is \( 80 \ cal \ g^{-1} \) , the required temperature is or \( 80^{\circ} C \) as is given by the formula.
\( m \times L = m \times 1 \times (t - 0) \)
2 The water equivalent of a body is numerically equal to its thermal capacity irrespective of the units used No, the statement is true only for the CGS system in which the specific heat capacity of water is unity. It is not true for the SI system of units.

Introduction

Heat is a form of energy, and this heat energy is possessed by a body, is due to the vibratory motion of its molecules. Thus, two ways to increase the temperature of a body are:

  1. Whenever a body is subjected to friction, compression or any other form of mechanical stress, the vibratory motion of the molecules becomes more violent and the temperature of the body rises.

  2. The supply of heat to a body generally results in an increase in the kinetic energy of its molecules. This energy manifests itself as the body's temperature.

Heat Energy

Heat energy is the sum total of kinetic and potential energy of all the molecules in a given substance.

Units of Heat Energy

The quantity of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 g of pure water through \( 1^{\circ} C \) is called one calorie.

  1. 1 calorie = 4.186 J = 4.2 j(approx)

  2. 1 kilocalorie = 4186 j = 4200 j(approx)

Heat Capacity or Thermal Capacity(C)

The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of a given mass of a substance through \( 1^{\circ} C \) is called heat capacity or thermal capacity of that substance.

Units of Heat Capacity or Thermal Capacity

In SI system, the unit of heat capacity is \( \frac{J}{K} \) or \( J \ K^{-1} \) . However, if heat absorbed is expressed in calories or kilocalories, the units of heat capacity are:

  1. calorie per degree celsius or calorie \( ^{\circ}C^{-1} \) .

  2. kilocalorie per degree celsius or kilocalorie \( ^{\circ}C^{-1} \) .

Note
In case of thermal capacity, mass is unspecified. Thus, same substance can have different heat capacities, depending upon its mass. For example, heat capacity of 10 g of water is \( 42 \ J^{\circ}C^{-1} \) whereas heat capacity of 100 g of water is \( 420 \ J^{\circ}C^{-1} \) .

Factors on which Heat Absorbed/given out by a body depends

Heat absorbed by a body

  1. Heat energy absorbed by a cold body is directly proportional to the mass of the body. If H is the heat energy absorbed and m is the mass of the body, then
    \( H \varpropto m \longrightarrow(1) \)

  2. Heat energy absorbed by a cold body is directly proportional to the rise in temperature of the body. If H is the heat energy absorbed and \( \theta_R \) is the rise in temperature of the body, then
    \( H \varpropto \theta_R \longrightarrow(2) \)
    Combining (1) and (2)
    \( H \varpropto m \theta_R \) or \( H = mc \theta_R \)
    [where c is the constant of proportionality and is commonly called specific heat capacity]

Heat given out by a body

  1. Heat energy given out by a hot body is directly proportional to the mass of the body, if H is the heat energy given out by a body and m is the mass of the body, then
    \( H \varpropto m \)

  2. Heat energy given out by a hot body is directly proportional to the fall in temperature of the body. If H is the heat energy given out by a body and \( \theta_F \) , is the fall in temperature of the body, then
    \( H \varpropto \theta_F \)
    Combining (1) and (2), we get
    \( H \varpropto m \theta_F \)
    \( H = mc \theta_R \)
    [where c is the constant of proportionality and is commonly called specific heat capacity]

Specific Heat Capacity

The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of unit mass of a substance through \( 1^{\circ} C \) or 1 K is called specific heat capacity.

Units of specific heat capacity

  1. In CGS system, unit of specific heat capacity is
    \( \frac{J}{g ^{\circ}C} \) or \( J \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C \)

  2. In SI system unit of specific heat capacity is
    \( \frac{J}{kg K} \) or \( J \ kg^{-1} K^{-1} \)

Note:

  1. \( 1 \ cal \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1} = 4.2 \ J \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C \)

  2. \( 1 \ cal \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1} = 4200 \ J \ kg^{-1} ^{\circ}C \)

Calorimeter

Note
Whenever the calorimeter is weighed, it is always weighed with stirrer which is made from a copper wire

Principle of Calorimetry

It is based on the law of conservation of energy which states "The energy in a system can neither be created nor can it be destroyed and the sum total of energy is a constant quantity."
When a hot body is brought in contact with a cold body then the heat energy flows from the hot body to the cold body till both the bodies attain same temperature. If no heat energy is transferred to the surroundings, i.e., system is fully insulated, then
Heat energy given out by hot body = Heat energy absorbed by cold body.
If no heat is lost to surroundings, then
Heat given out by hot substance = Heat absorbed by cold substance
\( \Rightarrow m_1 c_1 \left ( \theta_1 - \theta \right ) = m_2 c_2 \left ( \theta - \theta_2 \right ) \)

Advantages of High Specific Heat Capacity of Water

  1. Formation of land and sea breeze

  2. Use of water in hot water bottles

  3. Use of water as coolant in car radiators

  4. Use of water in internal heating of buildings

Change of State

There are three states of matter

  1. Solid State

  2. Liquid State

  3. Gageous State

Depending on the conditions of temperature and pressure the same substance be in a solid, liquid or gaseous state. For example, ice, (solid) water (liquid) and water vapour(gaseous) are different states of the same substance.

The constant temperature at which a solid starts changing into liquid state is called melting point or fusion point. The constant temperature at which a liquid changes into solid state is called solidification point or freezing point.
Numerical value of boiling point and liquefaction point is same, e.g., if boiling point of water is \( 100^{\circ}C \) , then liquefaction point of steam is \( 100^{\circ}C \) .

Heating Curve

Part AB represents rise in temperature of solid with the gain of heat energy.

Latent Heat of Fusion

The heat energy supplied to the solid so as to change it into the liquid state, without any rise in temperature is called latent heat of fusion.

Specific latent heat (S.L.H.) of Fusion

Amount of heat energy required to melt one kilogram of solid (1 g in case of CGS system) at its melting point, without any rise in temperature is called specific latent heat of fusion.

Units of specific latent heat of fusion

In CGS system it is measured in \( J \ g^{-1} \) (calories \( g^{-1} \) in old system). In SI system, it is measured in \( J \ kg^{-1} \) (calories \( kg^{-1} \) in old system).

Disadvantages of high Latent Heat of Vaporisation of Water

Burns caused by steam \( \left ( 100^{\circ}C \right ) \) are more severe than those caused by boiling water \( \left ( 100^{\circ}C \right ) \) at the same temperature. The specific latent heat of vaporisation of steam is \( 2260 \ J \ g^{-1} \) .
Thus, 1 gram of steam at \( 100^{\circ}C \) contains 2260 J of heat energy more than water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) .

Effect of Pressure on Boiling Point of Liquids

The boiling point of a liquid is related to pressure.

  1. An increase of pressure raises the boiling point.

  2. A decrease in pressure lowers the boiling point.

Effect of impurities of Boiling Point

Impurities on dissolving in a liquid increases its boiling point. Impurities like common salt when added to pulses or meat tend to raise the boiling point of water and hence, makes cooking faster. For the same reason tea prepared by boiling the water with sugar and tea leaves in it is too hot to drink as the boiling point is raised above \( 100^{\circ}C \) .

Evaporation

A change from the liquid state to the gaseous state which occurs at the surface of the liquid is called evaporation.
Factors which influence the rate of evaporation of liquid.

  1. Nature of the liquid

  2. Area of exposed surface

  3. Temperature of the liquid

  4. The pressure on the liquid surface

  5. Dryness of the air

Regelation or Refreezing

The phenomenon due to which ice at \( 0^{\circ}C \) melts to water at a temperature below \( 0^{\circ}C \) on the application of external pressure and refreezing of water formed into ice on the released pressure is called regelation.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
A mass \( (m_1) \) of 0.8 kg of water at \( 25^{\circ}C \left ( { \theta}_1 \right ) \) is mixed with 0.2 kg \( (m_2) \) of boiling water \( \left ( { \theta}_2 \right ) \) . The temperature of the mixture obtained is \( 40^{\circ}C \left ( { \theta}_1 \right ) \) .

Solution:
Heat gained by the cold water
\( \begin{align} Q_1 = m_1 c_1 \left ( {\theta}_f - {\theta}_1 \right ) \\\\[12pt] = 0.8 \times 4200 \times \left ( 40^{\circ} C - 25^{\circ} C \right ) \\\\[12pt] = 50400 J.........(1) \end{align} \)
Heat lost by the boiling water to heat the cold water
\( \begin{align} Q_2 = m_2 c_2 \left ( {\theta}_2 - {\theta}_f \right ) \\\\[12pt] = 0.2 \times 4200 \times \left ( 100^{\circ} C - 40^{\circ} C \right ) \\\\[12pt] = 50400 J.........(2) \\\\[12pt] But \ Q_1 = Q_2 \\\\[12pt] \therefore \ m_1 c_1 \left ( {\theta}_f - {\theta}_1 \right ) = m_2 c_2 \left ( {\theta}_2 - {\theta}_f \right ) \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Question:
Why is ice at \( 0^{\circ}C \) more effective in cooling than water at \( 0^{\circ}C \) ?

Solution:
Ice has a high latent heat of fusion \( \left ( 336000 \ J \ kg^{-1} \right ) \) . Therefore, ice at \( 0^{\circ}C \) extracts 336000 \( J \ kg^{-1} \) of heat from its surrounding to melt. Since heat is removed from the system, the system cools down.

Example 3:

Question:
What is the amount of heat required to heat 1 kg of ice from - \( 10^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \) ?

Solution:
Total heat = Heat required to melt the ice + Heat required to raise its temperature from - \( 10^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \)
\( \begin{align} \therefore Q = (m \times L_f) + (m \times c \times \theta)_{ice} \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ C_{water} = 4200 \ J \ kg^{-1} \ ^{\circ}C^{-1} \\\\[12pt] Q = (1 \times 336000) + (1 \times 2100 \times 10) \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ L_{ice} = 33600 \ J \ kg^{-1} \\\\[12pt] Q = 336000 + 21000 = 357000 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ C_{ice} = 2100 \ J \ kg^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1} \end{align} \)
The total heat required is 357000 J.

Example 4:

Question:
To make tea, a tourist puts into a kettle 2 kg of ice at a temperature of \( 0^{\circ}C \) .

  1. What quantity of heat is required to transform the ice into boiling water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) temperature?

  2. How much heat would be needed if the tourist had taken 2 kg of water from an ice hole, instead of ice at \( 0^{\circ}C \) ?

Solution:

  1. The heat required to melt the ice is given by the expression Q = mL, where L is the latent heat of fusion of ice.
    Given: m = 2kg
    \( \begin{align} t_1 = 0^{\circ} C, \ t_2 = 100^{\circ} C, \\\\[12pt] L = 3.4 \times 10^5 \ J \ kg^{-1}, \ c_w = 4.2 \times 10^3 \ J \ kg^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1} \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = mL \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = 2 \times 3.4 \times 10^5 \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = 6.8 \times 10^5 \ J \longrightarrow (1) \end{align} \)
    The heat required to raise the temperature of the water(obtained by melting the ice) from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \) , is
    \( \begin{align} \therefore Q_2 = mc_w(t_2 - t_1) \\\\[12pt] Q_2 = 2 \times 4.2 \times 10^3 \times (100 - 0) \\\\[12pt] Q_2 = 8.4 \times 10^5 \ J \longrightarrow (2) \end{align} \)
    The total quantity of heat require
    \( \begin{align} Q = Q_1 + Q_2 ...... from \ eq. \ 1 \ and \ 2 \\\\[12pt] Q = 6.8 \times 10^5 + 8.4 \times 10^5 \\\\[12pt] Q = 15.2 \times 10^5 \ J \end{align} \)

  2. If instead of ice he had taken 2 kg of water at \( 0^{\circ}C \) , the quantity of heat required to heat it from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \)
    \( \begin{align} Q = mc_w(t_2 - t_1) \\\\[12pt] Q = 2 \times 4200 \times 100 \\\\[12pt] Q = 840000 \ J \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
what is the amount of heat required to heat 1 kg of ice from \( - 10^{\circ}C \) to \( 5^{\circ}C \) ?

Solution:
Total heat = Heat required to raise its temperature from \( - 10^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \) ..... \( Q_1 = (m_{(ice)}c_{(ice)} \theta_1) \)
+ Heat required to melt the ice ..... \( Q_2 = (m \times L_f) \)
+ Heat required to raise its temperature from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 5^{\circ}C \) .... \( Q_3 = (m_{(water)}c_{(water)} \theta_2) \)
\( \begin{align} Q = (m \times L_f) + (m \ c \ \theta_1) + (m \ c \ \theta_2) \\\\[12pt] Q = (1 \times 336000) + (1 \times 2100 \times 10) + (1 \times 4200 \times 5) \\\\[12pt] Q = 336000 + 21000 + 21000 = 378000 \ J \end{align} \)
The total heat required is 378000 J.

Example 6:

Question:
What quantity of energy is required to convert 2 kg of water at \( 20^{\circ}C \) into vapour at \( 100^{\circ}C \) ?( \( L = 2.3 \times 10^3 \ KJ \ kg^{-1} \) ).

Solution:

  1. Let \( Q_1 \) = energy spent on heating water from \( 20^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \)
    \( \begin{align} Q_1 = mc(t_2 - t_1) \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = 2 \times 4200 \times (100 - 20) J \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = 0.67 \times 10^6 J \longrightarrow (1) \end{align} \)

  2. Let \( Q_2 \) = energy spent on conversion of water into vapour without change in temperature.
    \( \begin{align} Q_2 = mL = 2.3 \times 10^6 \times 2 \ J.....(L = 2.3 \times 10^3 KJ \ kg^{-1} = 2.3 \times 10^6 KJ \ kg^{-1}) \\\\[12pt] \therefore Q_2 = 4.6 \times 10^6 J \longrightarrow (2) \end{align} \)

  3. Total quantity of energy spent:
    \( \begin{align} Q = Q_1 + Q_2 \\\\[12pt] Q = 0.67 \times 10^6 + 4.6 \times 10^6 \longrightarrow from \ (1) \ and \ (2) \\\\[12pt] Q = 5.27 \times 10^6 J \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Question:
1 g of ice at \( - 5 ^{\circ}C \) is heated.

  1. Show the temperature - time graph(a) for the change of solid state(ice) to liquid state(water) at \( 0^{\circ}C \) (melting point) (b) for the change of liquid state(water) at \( 100^{\circ}C \) (boiling point) to vapour state at \( 100^{\circ}C \) (steam).

  2. Calculate the total heat energy to bring about the conversion of ice at \( - 5^{\circ}C \) to vapour at \( 100^{\circ}C \) .

Solution:
Part AB shows rise in temperature of ice( \( - 5^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \) )
Let \( Q_1 \) = Energy needed to raise the temperature from \( - 5^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \)
\( \begin{align} Q_1 = m_{ice} \times c_{ice} \times (t_2 - t_1) \\\\[12pt] Q_1 = 1 \times 0.5 \times [0 - (-5)] = 2.5 \ calories....(c_{ice} = 0.5 \ cal \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1}) \end{align} \)
Part BC represents the melting of ice which starts at point B and completes at point C. During this melting process(BC part) the temperature remains constant at \( 0^{\circ}C(L = 80 \ cal \ g^{-1}). \)
Let \( Q_2 \) = heat energy required to melt ice at \( 0^{\circ}C \)
\( \begin{align} Q_2 = mL(L = specific \ latent \ heat \ of \ ice) \\\\[12pt] Q_2 = 1 \times 80 = 80 \ calories \end{align} \)
Part CD represents the rise in temperature of water from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \) .
Let \( Q_3 \) = heat energy required to raise the temperature of water from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \ (L = 540 \ cal \ g^{-1}) \)
\( Q_3 = m_w \times c_w(t_2 - t_1) = 1 \times 1 \times (100 - x) = 100 \ calories \) .... \( (c_w = 1 \ cal \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1}) \)
Part DE represents the conversation of water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) to vapour or steam at \( 100^{\circ}C \ (L = 540 \ cal \ g^{-1}) \)
Let \( Q_4 \) = heat energy required to vapourize water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) during this process temp. remains constant at \( 100^{\circ}C \)
\( \begin{align} Q_4 = m_w L.....(L = specific \ latent \ heat \ of \ steam) \\\\[12pt] Q_4 = 1 \times 540 = 540 \ calories \end{align} \)
Total energy(Q) required to change the ice at \( - 5^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \) steam(water vapour)
\( Q = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + Q_4 = 2.5 + 80 + 100 + 540 = 722.5 \ calories. \)

Example 9:

Question:
A piece of iron weighing 100 g was heated to \( 98.5^{\circ}C \) and dropped in a calorimeter weighing 46 g and containing 85.4 g water at \( 15^{\circ}C \) . The final temperature was found to be \( 22^{\circ}C \) . Calculate the sp. heat of iron. Sp. heat of material of calorimeter is 0.1.

Solution:
Mass of iron = 100 g
Temperature of iron = \( 98.5^{\circ}C \)
Water equivalent of calorimeter = \( 46 \times 0.1 = 4.6 \ g \)
Temperature of water = \( 15^{\circ}C \)
Final temperature = \( 22^{\circ}C \)
If S is the specific heat of iron, then Heat lost by iron = \( 100 \times S(98.5 - 22) = 7650 \ S \ cal \)
Heat gained by water and calorimeter = (85.4 + 4.6)(22 - 15) = 630 cals
As Heat lost = Heat gained
\( \therefore \) 7650 S = 630
or S = 0.0824

Example 10:

Question:
Equal volumes of copper and mercury have the same thermal capacity. Mercury has a specific heat of 0.046 and density \( 13.6 \ g \ cc^{-1} \) . Calculate the density of copper if its specific heat is 0.092.

Solution:
Thermal capacity = mass \( \times \) specific heat
Let v be the volume of mercury and copper and d the density of copper, then
Thermal capacity of mercury = \( V \times 13.6 \times 0.046 \ cal \)
Thermal capacity of copper = \( V \times d \times 0.092 \ cal. \)
\( V \times d \times 0.092 \ cal = V \times 13.6 \times 0.046 \ cal \)
Density of copper \( d = \frac{13.6 \times 0.046}{0.092} = 6.8 \ g \ cc^{-1} \)

Example 11:

Question:
What would be the final temperature of the mixture when 5 g of ice at \( - 10^{\circ}C \) are mixed with 20 g of water at \( 30^{\circ}C \) . Specific heat of ice is 0.5 and latent heat of water \( 80 \ cal \ g^{-1}. \)

Solution:
If \( \theta \) is the final temperature of the mixture, then
Heat lost by water \( = 20(30 - \theta) = (600 - 20 \theta)cal \)
Heat required to raise 5 g of ice at \( - 10^{\circ}C \) to \( 0^{\circ}C \) = \( 5 \times 10 \times 0.5 = 25 \ cal \)
Heat required to melt 5 g of ice water at \( 10^{\circ}C \) = \( 5 \times 80 = 400 \ cal \)
Total heat gained by ice \( = (25 + 400 + 5 \theta) cal \)
Now Heat lost = Heat gained
\( \begin{align} \therefore 600 - 20 \theta = 425 + 5 \theta \\\\[12pt] or \ \ \ \ \ \theta = \frac{600 - 425}{25} = 7^{\circ}C \end{align} \)

Example 12:

Question:
5 g of water at \( 30^{\circ}C \) and 5 g of ice at \( - 20^{\circ}C \) are mixed in a calorimeter. Find the final temperature, water equivalent of calorimeter is negligible. Specific heat of ice is \( 0.5 \ cal \ g^{-1} ^{\circ}C^{-1} \) and latent heat of ice is 80 cal per gram.

Solution:
Heat supplied by water at \( 30^{\circ}C \) in cooling down \( 0^{\circ}C = 5 \times 80 = 400 \ cal \)
Total heat required to convert ice into water at \( 0^{\circ}C = 50 + 400 = 450 \ cal \)
As heat supplied by water to come down to \( 0^{\circ}C \) is much less than 450 cal. Hence whole of ice will not melt.
Available heat at \( 0^{\circ}C \) = 150 - 50 = 100 cal
\( \therefore \) Mass of ice that will melt \( = \frac{100}{80} = 1.25 \ g \)
Thus (5 - 1.25) = 3.75 g of ice will not melt and the temperature of the mixture will be \( 0^{\circ}C. \)

Example 13:

Question:
A mixture of 250 g of water and 200 g of ice is kept in a calorimeter of water equivalent 50 g. If 200 g of steam at \( 100^{\circ}C \) is passed through this mixture, calculate the final temperature and weight of contents of the calorimeter. Latent heat of steam is \( 540 \ cal \ g^{-1}. \)

Solution:
Heat required to condense 200 g of steam at \( 100^{\circ}C \) into water of \( 100^{\circ}C = 200 \times 540 = 10800 \ cal. \)
Heat required to raise the temperature of calorimeter and its contents from \( 0^{\circ}C \) to \( 100^{\circ}C \)
\( \begin{align} = 250 \times 100 + 200 \times 80 + 200 \times 100 + 50 \times 100 \\\\[12pt] = 100(250 + 160 + 200 + 50) = 66000 \ cal \end{align} \)
Now as 66000 < 10800, whole of steam will not condense
Let m be the mass of steam that will condense into water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) then
Heat supplied by m g of steam at \( 100^{\circ}C \) to condense into water at \( 100^{\circ}C \) = 540 m cal
\( \therefore 540 \ m = 66000 \)
Mass of steam condensed \( m = \frac{66000}{540} = 122.2 \ g \)
Hence final temperature of the contents will be \( 100^{\circ}C \) and
Mass of contents in cal = 250 + 200 + 122.2 = 572.2 g

Example 14:

Question:
The temperature of equal masses of three different liquids A, B and C are \( 12^{\circ}C \) , \( 19^{\circ}C \) and \( 28^{\circ}C \) respectively. The temperature when A and B are mixed is \( 16^{\circ}C \) and when B and C are mixed is \( 23^{\circ}C \) . What will be the temperature when A and C are mixed?

Solution:
Given masses of liquids A, B, C are equal to m
On mixing(A, B)
Equilibrium temperature \( \theta = 16^{\circ}C \)
According to principle of calorimetry,
Heat lost by A = Heat gained by B
\( \begin{align} mS_A(16 - 12) = mS_B(19 - 16) \\\\[12pt] 4S_A = 3S_B \longrightarrow (1) \end{align} \)
On mixing(B, C)
Equilibrium temperature \( \theta = 23^{\circ}C \)
According to principle of calorimetry,
Heat lost by B = Heat gained by C
\( \begin{align} mS_B(23 - 19) = mS_C(28 - 23) \\\\[12pt] 4S_B = 5S_C \longrightarrow (2) \end{align} \)
Let equlibrium temperature be \( \theta \)
According to principle of calorimetry,
Heat lost by A = Heat gained by C
\( mS_A( \theta - 12) = mS_C(28 - \theta) \)
From (1) and (2)
\( \begin{align} \frac{3S_B}{4}( \theta - 12) = \frac{4}{5}S_B(28 - \theta) \\\\[12pt] 15 \theta - 15 \times 12 = 16 \times 28 - 16 \theta \\\\[12pt] 31 \theta = 448 + 180 \\\\[12pt] 31 \theta = 628 \\\\[12pt] \theta = 20.25^{\circ}C \end{align} \)

Example 15:

Question:
Latent heat of ether at \( 0^{\circ}C \) is 90 cal per g. How much ether should be evaporated to freeze 60 g of water at \( 0^{\circ}C \)

Solution:
Let mass of ether to be evaporated be m g
To freeze water at \( 0^{\circ}C \) by evaporating ether at \( 0^{\circ}C \) , both undergo process of phase change. During phase change no temperature changes.
Hence
Latent heat of ether to evaporate at \( 0^{\circ}C = m \times L = 90 \ m \)
Latent heat of water to freeze at \( 0^{\circ}C = 60 \times 80 = 4800 \ cal \)
\( \begin{align} m \times L = 60 \times 80 \\\\[12pt] m \times 90 = 60 \times 80 \\\\[12pt] m = \frac{4800}{90} = 53.3 \ g \end{align} \)
