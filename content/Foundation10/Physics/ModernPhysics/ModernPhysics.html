2 For similar reasons,the binding energy per nucleon decreases for nuclei with small as well as large A values. The reason for the decrease in the BE per nucleon for nuclei with large A is that with an increase in the number of protons, the coulombic repulsion increase. On the other hand, the decrease in the BE per nucleon for nuclei with small A is due to a surface effect the nucleons at the surface being less strongly bound than those in the interior.

4 The mass per nucleon in a hydrogen atom is the same as that in an oxygen atom. The energy released during the formation of nuclei of different atoms from their constituent nuclei is different. Hence the mass per nucleon is dependent upon the atom considered. The oxygen nucleus, being particularly stable, has a much larger value of the binding energy per nucleon. Hence the mass per nucleon in an oxygen atom is slightly less than the mass per nucleon in a hydrogen atom.

Conclisions from Rutherford's Experiment

  1. An atom may be regarded as a sphere of diameter 10-10 m but whole of the positive charge and almost the entire mass of the atom is concentrated in a small central core called nucleus, having a diameter of about \( 10^{14}m \) .

  2. The nucleus is surrounded by electrons. In other words, the electrons are spread over the remaining part of the atom, leaving plenty of empty space in the atom.

  3. As the atom is electrically neutral, the total positive charge on the nucleus is equal to the total negative charge of the electrons in the atom.

  4. Rutherford pointed out that the electrons inside the atom are not stationary for if they were at rest, they would be pulled into the positive nucleus due to the strong electrostatic force of attraction between the nucleus and the electrons. He assumed that the electrons are revolving around the nucleus in circular orbits. The necessary centripetal force is provided to them by the electrostatic force of attraction between the electrons and the nucleus.

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Mass defect

The difference between the sum of the masses of the nucleons constituting a nucleus and the rest mass of the nucleus is known as mass defect. It is denoted by \( \Delta m \)

Let us calculate the mass defect in case of the nucleus of an atom \( _ZX^A \) . The nucleus of such an atom contains Z protons and (A - Z) neutrons. Therefore, mass of the constituting nucleons = \( Z m_p + (A - Z) m_n \)

If \( m_N \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \) is mass of the nucleus of the atom \( _ZX^A \) , then the mass defect is given by
\( \Delta m = \left [ Z m_p + (A - Z)m_n \right ] - m_N \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \)

Binding energy

The binding energy of nucleus may be defined as the energy equivalent to the mass defect of the nucleus.
If \( \Delta m \) is mass defect of a nucleus, then according to Einstein's mass-energy relation, Binding energy of the nucleus = \( \Delta m c^2 (in joule) \)
Here, mass defect \( \Delta m \) has to be measured in kilogram. In case, mass defect is measured in a.m.u., then
Binding energy of the nucleus = \( \Delta m \times 931.5 \) (in MeV)
The binding energy of a nucleus may also be expressed in terms of masses of protons, neutrons and the nucleus as explained below:
From the equation, we have
Binding energy = \( \left [ \{ Z m_p + (A - Z) m_n \} - m_N \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \right ] \times 931.5 \)
Here, \( m_N \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \) is mass of the nucleus of the atom \( _ZX^A \) (or) Binding energy = \( \left [ \{ Z m \left (_1 H^1 \right ) + (A - Z) m_n \} - m \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \right ] \times 931.5 \)
Here, \( m \left ( _1H^1 \right ) \) and \( m \left ( _ZX^A \right ) \) represent the masses of hydrogen atom \( \left ( _1H^1 \right ) \) and the atom \( _ZX^A \) .

Binding energy per nucleon

The binding energy per nucleon is the average energy required to extract one nucleon from the nucleus. It is obtained by dividing the binding energy of the nucleus by the number of nucleons it contains..
Thus, binding energy per nucleon = \( \frac{binding \ energy}{A} \)

Packing fraction

The packing fraction of a nucleus is defined as the mass defect per nucleon of the nucleus
Thus, packing fraction = \( \frac{mass \ defect}{A} \)

Properties of alpha - rays:

  1. \( \alpha \) particles have been identified as helium nuclei (atoms) of helium that have lost two orbital electrons. Thus \( \alpha \) particles carry double the positive charge of proton and are four times as heavy.

  2. The velocity of \( \alpha \) particles ranges from \( 1.4 \times 10^7 m s^{-1} \) to \( 2.05 \times 10^7 m s^{-1} \) depending upon the radioactive substance emitting them.

  3. Although \( \alpha \) particles possess large amount of energy, they are least penetrating of the three types of rays.

  4. They have unusually great ionising power. Each \( \alpha \) particle produces thousands of ions before being absorbed.

  5. At normal pressure in air, the range of \( \alpha \) particles varies from 3 to 8 cm depending on the initial kinetic energy of the particles.

  6. \( \alpha \) particles produce fluorescence in substances like zinc sulphide and barium platinocyanide.

  7. \( \alpha \) particles are deflected by electric and magnetic fields.

  8. \( \alpha \) particles affect a photographic plate.

  9. \( \alpha \) particles while passing through metal fuels, get scattered.

  10. They can produce artificial radioactivity in certain elements and can produce nuclear reactions

Properties of beta - rays

  1. \( \beta \) particles have been found to be stream of electrons.

  2. Although the velocities of these particles are enormous, their average energy is onl:, 2 to 3 MeV. It is because, \( \alpha \) particles passes extremely small mass.

  3. \( \beta \) particles can easily pass through a few millimetre thick aluminium sheets.

  4. They can produce ionization in gases, but their ionizing power is quite small compared to that of a -particles.

  5. The range of \( \beta \) particles in air is several metres.

  6. They can also produce fluorescence in materials like zinc sulphide and bariui platinocyanide.

  7. \( \beta \) particles are deflected by electric and magnetic fields.

  8. They can affect a photographic plate.

  9. They can produce artificial radioactivity.

Properties of gamma - rays:

  1. \( \gamma \) rays are electromagnetic waves and have velocity equal to that of light.

  2. \( \gamma \) rays are highly penetrating. They can penetrate through several centimeters thick iron and lead blocks.

  3. They have got small ionising power.

  4. They can produce fluorescence in a substance like willemite.

  5. They can affect a photographic plate.

  6. \( \gamma \) rays are not deflected by electric and magnetic field.

  7. \( \gamma \) rays knock out electrons from the surface on which they fall.

  8. \( \gamma \) rays produce heating effect in the surface exposed to them.

  9. \( \gamma \) rays can produce nuclear reactions.

  10. In hospitals, hard y -rays are used in radio--therapy.

Half - Life

The half-life of a sample of a radioactive isotope is defined as the time taken for half the original unstable radioactive nuclei to decay.

The number of radioactive atoms in a substance decreases exponentially over time. Therefore every radioactive element has a characteristic time called half-life. After that time, the number of radioactive nuclei in the sample would have decreased to half the number originally present.
\( T = \frac{0.693}{\lambda} \)

Carbon-14 has been used for dating the cultural history or for estimating the age of some geological formation.

Nuclear Reactions

In nuclear reactions, the nucleus of an atom undergoes a change forming new atoms and releasing a tremendous amount of energy. New atoms (or new elements) can produced in a nuclear reaction, which is not possible in the case of a chemical reaction Thus, a nuclear reaction can convert one element into another element. Some of thE¬nuclear reactions take place in nature on their own. The natural radioactivity shown by certain elements involves such nuclear reactions. These days, nuclear reactions can also be carried out in the laboratory.

The first ever nuclear reaction in which one element was changed into another element was carried out by Rutherford in 1919.

Rutherford found that when a beam of alpha particles was put in a container of nitrogen gas, then nitrogen was converted into oxygen and protons were given out. This nuclear reaction can be written as :
\( \underset{Nitrogen} {_7^{14}N} + \underset{Alpha particle}{ _2^4He} \xrightarrow[reaction]{Nuclear} \ \underset{Oxygen (new element)}{ _8^{17}O} + \underset{Proton} {_1^1H} \)

The changing of a stable element into another element artificially by changing the number of protons in its nucleus is called transmutation of elements.

The particles which are used to bombard the nuclei of atoms for causing nuclear reactions or transmutation of elements are called projectiles.

Transuranic Elements

The elements having atomic numbers higher than that of uranium are called transuranic elements.

Nuclear Fission

The process in which an unstable nucleus of a heavy atom (like uranium-235) splits up into two medium weight nuclei with the liberation of an enormous amount of energy, is called nuclear fission. The nuclear fission is carried out by bombarding the heavy nuclei with neutrons. The energy of neutrons which cause the fission of uranium-235 is low. The low energy (slow moving) neutrons which can produce nuclear fission are called thermal neutrons. There is a small loss of mass in the fission process which appears as a great amount of energy.

Example:

When uranium-235 atoms are bombarded with slow moving neutrons, the heavy uranium nucleus breaks up to produce two medium-weight atoms, barium-139 and krypton-94, with the emission of 3 neutrons. A large amount of energy is produced during the fission of uranium. The fission reaction can be represented in the form of a nuclear equation as:
\( \underset{Heavy} { _{95}^{235}U } + \underset{nuetron} {_0^1n} \xrightarrow{fission} \ \underset{medium \ weight} {_{56}^{139} Ba} + \underset{atom} { _{36}^{94}Kr } + \underset{neutrons} {3 \ _0^1n} + Large \ amount \ of \ energy \)

Heavy Neutron Medium weight atom Neutrons of energy In the fissioning of uranium, some mass of uranium disappears (is lost), and an enormous amount of energy is produced. This energy is used for generating electricity. Thus, the importance of a nuclear fission process is that it is accompanied by the release of an enormous amount of energy (which can be used for generating electrical power).

Types of Fission Reactions

All the nuclear fission reactions can be divided into three categories

  1. Spontaneous fission

  2. Prompt fission, and

  3. Delayed fission

When a nucleus undergoes fission on its own (without being hit by a projectile like neutron), it is called spontaneous fission.

When a nucleus plits up into smaller nuclei instantaneously, as soon as it is bombarded with a projectile (like neutrons), it is called promt fission.

When a projectile like neutron enters a nucleus and causes instability which leads to the fission of the nucleus after a short while, it is called delayed fission.

Chain Reactions

A reaction in which the particle which initiates (starts) the reaction, is also produced during the reaction to carry on the reaction further and further, is called a chain reaction.

Nuclear Fusion

The process in which two nuclei of light atoms (like that of hydrogen) combine to form a heavy, more stable nucleus (like that of helium), with the liberation of a large amount of energy is called nuclear fusion.

Example:

When deuterium atoms (heavy hydrogen atoms of mass number 2) are accelerated by a particle accelerator or heated to an extremely high temperature, then two deuterium nuclei combine together to form a heavy nucleus of helium, and a neutron is emitted. An enormous amount of energy is liberated in the fusion reaction. This fusion reaction can be written as:
\( \underset{\text{Two deuterium atoms (light atoms)}} { _1^2H } + _1^2H \xrightarrow{fusion} \ \underset{\text{one helium atom (heavy atom)}} {_2^3 He} + \underset{neutron} { _0^1n } + \text{enormous amount of energy} \)

Example 1:

Obtain approximately the ratio of the nuclear radii of the gold isotope silver isotope \( _{79}Au^{197} \) and the silver isotope \( _{47}Ag^{107} \) .
Solution:
We know that \( R \propto A^{\frac{1}{3}} \)
therefore, \( \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \left ( \frac{A_1}{A_2} \right )^{\frac{1}{3}} \)
Here, \( A_1 = 197 \) , \( A_2 = 107 \)
\( \therefore \ \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \left ( \frac{197}{107} \right )^{\frac{1}{3}} = 1.226 \)

Example 2:

Find out the binding energy per nucleon of an \( \alpha \) - particle in MeV. It is given that masses of \( \alpha \) -particle, proton and neutron are respectively 4.00150 a.m.u., 1.00728 a.m.u and 1.00867 a.m.u.
Solution:
An \( \alpha \) -particle i.e., nucleus of helium, consists of two protons and two neutrons.
Here, m(He) = 4.00150 a.m.u.; \( m_p = 1.0728 \ a.m.u. \) ;
\( m_n = 1.00867 \ a.m.u. \)
Therefore, mass of the protons and neutrons constituting an \( \alpha \) - particle,
\( 2 m_p + 2 m_n = 2 \times 1.00728 + 2 \times 1.00867 = 4.0319 \ a.m.u \)
Mass defect, \( \Delta m = (2m_p + 2 m_n) - m (He) \)
\( = 4.0319 - 4.00150 = 0.0304 \ a.m.u. \)
Now, 1 a.m.u. = 931.5 MeV
Therefore, binding energy of \( \alpha \) - particle
\( = \Delta m \times 931.5 = 0.0304 \times 931.5 = 28.32 MeV. \)

Solution:

\( m_p + m_n = 1.007275 + 1.008665 = 2.015940 a.m.u. \)

= 2.015940 - 2.013553 = 0.0011935 a.m.u.
Packing fraction, \( \frac{\Delta m}{A} = \frac{0.002387}{ 2} = 0.0011935 a.m.u. \)
Now, binding energy of deutron
\( \begin{align} = \Delta m \times 931.5 = 0.002387 \times 931.5 \\\\[3pt] = 2.22 MeV \end{align} \)
