Introduction

An electric current can produce three important effects. These are

  1. Heating effects

  2. Chemical effects and

  3. Magnetic effects

When an electric current is passed through a high resistance wire, like nichrome wire, the resistance wire becomes very hot and produces heat. This is called the heating effect of current. The heating effect of current is obtained by the transformation of electrical energy into heat energy.

A current flowing through solids such as a copper wire, the filament of a bulb, the element of an electric heater produces heat. If a current is allowed to flow through certain liquids then a chemical change usually takes place. So this effect of electric current that brings out a chemical change is called as chemical effects of electric current.

Electric energy or electric work

Electric work, is said to be done when an electric charge flows through a conductor, at some potential difference.

If 'W' is the amount of work done in carrying charges 'Q' from one point to another point in an electric field, such that 'V' is the potential difference, the
\( V = \frac{W}{Q} \)

Units of electric work

Unit of electric work (or electric energy) is joule. It is defined as the amount of Work done when a charge of one coulomb flows through a conductor at a.p.d of 1 volt.
\( 1 joule = 1 coulomb \times 1 volt \)
Bigger units of electric work :

  1. kilo - joule (kJ) = \( 10^3 J \)

  2. Mega - joule (MJ) = \( 10^6 J \)

  3. Giga - joule (GJ) = \( 10^6 J \)

Electric Power

Rate of doing electric work or the amount of electric work done in one second, is electric power.
\( \begin{align} P = \frac{W}{t} \\\\[3pt] But, W = I^2 Rt \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ P = \frac{I^2 Rt}{t}, \\\\[3pt] or \ P = I^2 R........(1) \\\\[3pt] Also, \ W = IVt \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ P = \frac{IVt}{t} \ or \ P = IV.......(2) \\\\[3pt] Also, W = \frac{V^2 t}{R} \ P = \frac{V^2 t}{Rt} \\\\[3pt] or \ P = \frac{V^2}{R} \end{align} \)
Thus, electric power \( P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \)
The SI unit of electric power is watt. When a current of one ampere flows through a conductor at a.p.d. of 1 volt, then electric power is said to be one watt.
\( 1 W = 1 J s^{-1} \)
Bigger units of electric power:
Following are bigger units of power :

  1. 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W = \( 10^3 W \)

  2. 1 Mega - watt (MW) = 1000,000 W = \( 10^6 W \)

  3. 1 Giga - watt (GW) = \( 10^9 W \)

  4. 1 Horse power (HP) = 746 W = 750 W (approx).

Commercial units of electric energy:

The commercial unit of energy the time is kept in hours. It is defined as the amount of electric energy, which flows through a conductor in one hour, at a power of one watt.
\( \begin{align} 1kWh = 10^3 Wh = 10^3 J s^{-1} \times 3.6 \times 10^{3} s \\\\[3pt] = 3.6 \times 10^{6} J = 3.6 M J. \end{align} \)

Dangers of electricity:

Most dangerous subsequences of using electricity are electric shock and electric fire. They are usually caused by damaged insulation, overheating of cables or damped conditions.

Damaged insulation

This damaged insulation would result in :
Severe electric shock leading to serious injury or even death.
Short circuit in the current path by connecting exposed current-carrying conductors together.

Overheating of cables

This overheating of cables would result in :
Melting of the insulation and catching fire.

Damping condition

This damp condition, coupled with unsafe usage of electrical appliances, would result in:
Large currents flowing through our body causing electrical shock and leading to serious injury or even death.

Safe use of electricity in the home

Electricity is supplied to the house through the mains:
Live wire (L) : high-voltage (240 V) wire or main wire that carries current into the house circuit.
Neutral wire (N); zero-voltage wire or main wire that carries current out of the house circuit.

Current is carried into the house by the live wire (L) through a main fuse, an electricity meter, a main switch and a consumer distribution unit (fuse box) as shown and then returned from the house via the neutral wire (N) to the public power supply.

Connecting an electrical appliance between a pair of live and neutral wires, forms a complete circuit. A voltage difference (240 - 0 V) is set up between the terminals of electrical appliance resulting in a current flowing through the appliance. The electrical energy is then converted in the appliance into other form of useful energy (refer to figure).

Fuse and circuit breakers

In order to prevent excessive currents flowing into the home circuit, electrical appliances and its cables, fuses and circuit breakers are wired into the live wire and used as safety devices.

A fuse is usually made up of a tin-coated copper wire. When currents exceeds its design rating value. The wire will overheat and melt, thus opening the electrical circuit. It will prevent further damage to the appliance or user. It cannot be resused.

A circuit breaker is usally made up of a reusable spring-loaded type of switch. The function of the circuit breaker is similar to that of the fuse. If current exceeds its breaking setting it will spring open and break the circuit as in a fuse. The device can be reused by resetting the spring-loaded switch.

It is correct to fix the fuse or circuit breaker at the live wire before the appliance. When the circuit is loaded with excessive current, the fuse or circuit breaker will break and open the circuit. It will prevent overloading, burning or damaging of the appliance.

Connecting the fuse or circuit breaker to the neutral wire is incorrect, i.e., even when the circuit is opened due to excessive currents , the appliance may still be at live potential, creating possibility of an electric shock.

The current limit through the fuse (fuse rating) can be controlled by varying the thickness of the tin-coated copper wire. Thicker the wire, the larger the heating effect needed melt the connection, thus permitting larger current to flow.

Different fuse ratings and circuit breaker settings are used in different appliances according to their power requirements. The rating limit used is normally slightly higher than the normal current needed by the appliance.

Switch

Switch is used to open or close the electrical circuit, connecting the appliance.

It is correct to fix the switch at the live wire after the fuse, but before the appliance.

Connecting the switch to the neutral wire is incorrect, i.e., even when the circuit is opened, the appliance may still be at live potential, creating possibility of an electric shock.

Need for earthing and double insulation

Sometimes, even the fuse cannot capture the high currents coming from the live wire into the household appliance, earthing further protects the user of the appliance from electric shock by connecting the metal casing of the appliance to earth (a wired connection to the bare ground).

In the event that the metal casing of the appliance becomes live, earthing diverts large currents into the earth by providing an alternate path to the large current flow via the earth wire, rather than through the user's body.

Alternatively, instead of earthing, appliance can be made with double insulation. Double insulation protects the user of the appliance from an electrical shock by preventing any possibility of the external casing becoming live, thus eliminating the need for an earth connection.

First insulation : Insulating electrical cable from the internal component of the appliance.

Second insulation : Insulating internal metal part which could become live from the external casing.

Chemical effects of Current

Electric current can bring about chemical changes. So it is said to have a chemical effect. For example, when electric current is passed through a copper sulphate solution by using carbon electrodes, then a chemical reaction takes place to form copper metal sulphuric acid and oxygen gas:
\( \underset{Copper \ sulphate}{2CuSO_4} + \underset{water}{2 H_2O} \rightarrow \underset{Copper \ metal}{2Cu} + \underset{Sulphuric \ acid}{2H_2SO_4} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \)

This reaction is an example of the chemical effects of current. When we pass electric current through copper sulphate solution using carbon electrodes, then copper metal is deposited as a reddish layer on the negatively charged carbon electrode (cathode) as shown in figure. The longer the current flows, more copper is deposited on the negative carbon electrode. The oxygen gas is liberated at the positively charged carbon electrode (anode), whereas sulphuric acid solution is gradually converted into sulphuric acid solution. The process which is just described is called electrolysis of copper sulphate solution.

The decomposition of an electrolyte by the action of electric current is called electrolysis.

In electrolysis 1 faraday is defined as the quantity of electricity required to liberate 1 gram - equivalent of a substance at the electrode.

1 faraday is the quantity of electricity possessed by 1 mole of electrons \( \left ( 6.023 \times 10^{23} electrons \right ) \) , and it is equal to 96500 coulombs. Thus :

1 faraday = 96500 coulombs

Chemical Equivalent

The atomic mass of an element divided by its valency is called the chemical equivalent of the element, That is :
\( Chemical \ equivalent = \frac{atomic \ mass}{valency} \)
The chemical equivalent of an element expressed in grams is called its gram - equivalent. For example, the chemical equivalent of copper is 31.75, therefore, the gram - equivalent of copper will 31.75 grams (or 31.75 g). The gram - equivalent of an element is also known as its equivalent weight. The atomic masses, valencies and gram equivalents of some common elements are given below :

S.No Element Atomic mass valency (or charge on ion) Chemical Equivalent Gram - equivalent(Equivalent weight)
1 Sodium 23 1 23 23g
2 Silver 107.9 1 107.9 107.9g
3 Magnesium 24 2 12 12g
4 Copper 63.5 2 31.75 31.75g
5 Aluminium 27 3 9 9g

The quantity of electricity required to deposit (or liberate) 1 gram equivalent of a substance by electrolysis is 1 faraday (or 96500 coulombs).

  1. The gram - equivalent (or equivalent weight) of sodium is 23 g. Thus, 1 faraday (or 96500 coulombs) of electricity will be needed to deposit (or liberate) 23 grams of sodium metal by the electrolysis of molten sodium chloride.

  2. The gram - equivalent (or equivalent weight) of silver is 107.9 g. So, 1 faraday (or 96500 coulombs) of electricity will be needed to deposit (or liberate) 107.9 gram of silver metal by the electrolysis of silver nitrate solution, and so on.

Faraday's First Law of Electrolysis

Michael Faraday carried out a series of experiments in which he passed current through an electrolyte for different durations of time and weighed the mass of the substance which was deposited (or produced) at the electrode in each case. He found that longer the current is passed through an electrolyte, more is the mass of the substance deposited on the electrode. From the results of such experiments, he discovered the first law of electrolysis.

The mass of a substance produced (or deposited) on an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

If Q is the quantity of electricity passed and m is the mass of the substance produced then :
\( m \propto Q \)

We know that \( Q = I \times t \) , we can write the above relation as :
\( m \propto I \times t \ \ m = Z \times I \times t \)

Where Z is a constant known as 'electrochemical equivalent' of the substance (or element).Thus, the Faraday's first law of electrolysis gives us the formula :

Mass of substance deposited, \( m = Z \times I \times t \)
where Z = electrochemical equivalent of the substance
I = current passed (in amperes)
and t = time in seconds (for which the current is passed)

If 1 ampere current is passed for 1 second (so that the quantity of electricity passed is 1 coulomb), then the equation \( m = Z \times I \times t \) becomes \( m = Z \times 1 \times 1 \) or m = Z. Under these conditions, the mass of substance deposited becomes equal to its chemical equivalent. This gives us the following definition of electrochemical equivalent.

The electrochemical equivalent of a substance is defined as the mass of the substance (in grams) which is produced during electrolysis by passing 1 coulomb of electricity. The electrochemical equivalent is denoted by the letter Z. The unit of electrochemical equivalent is 'grams per coulomb' which is written short form as ' \( g C^{-1} \) '. The electrochemical equivalent of some common substances (or elements) are given below:

S.No Substance ( or Element) Electrochemical Equivalent
1 Sodium 0.00023 \( g C^{-1} \)
2 Silver 0.001118 \( g C^{-1} \)
3 Copper 0.00033 \( g C^{-1} \)

Note:
In the formula \( m = Z \times I \times t \) , if we put the value of Z in grams per coulomb, the value of current I in amperes and the value of time t in seconds, then the value of mass m will come in grams.

Faraday's Second Law of Electrolysis

The Faraday's second law of electrolysis gives the relationship between the mass of the various substances liberated (or deposited) during electrolysis and their chemical equivalents.

If the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of different substances produced (or deposited) on the electrodes are directly proportional to their chemical equivalents.

Suppose the chemical equivalent of one element is \( E_1 \) and that of another element is \( E_2 \) . Again suppose that when the same quantity of electricity is passed through the solution of the compounds of these two elements, the masses of the two elements produced on the electrodes are \( m_1 \) and \( m_2 \) respectively.

Now, according to Faraday's second law of electrolysis :
\( m_1 \propto E_1 \)
and
\( m_2 \propto E_2 \)
The above relations can also be written as by using a constant as :
and \( m_1 = a \ constant \times E_1; \ m_2 = a \ constant \times E_2 \)
on dividing
\( \frac{m_1}{m_2} = \frac{a \ constant \times E_1}{ a \ constant \times E_2} \)
cancelling 'a constant' we get:
\( \frac{m_1}{m_2} = \frac{E_1}{E_2} \)
where \( m_1 \) = mass of first element deposited during electrolysis
\( m_2 \) = mass of second element deposited during electrolysis
\( E_1 \) = Chemical equivalent of first element
and \( E_2 \) = Chemical equivalent of second element

Applications of Chemical Effect of Current

The chemical effect of current is used :

  1. in the electroplating

  2. in the extraction of metals

  3. in the purification of metals, and

  4. in the production of compounds

Electroplating

The process of depositing a thin layer of a desired metal over another metal object with the help of electric current is called electroplating. Electroplating is used for decorative purposes as well as for protection against corrosion (rusting).
The following points should be remembered while electroplating :

  1. The 'metal article on which' electroplating is to be done is made the negative electrode (cathode) : It is connected to the negative terminal of the battery.

  2. The metal to be deposited is made the positive electrode (anode) : It is connected to the positive terminal of the battery.

  3. A water soluble salt of the 'metal to be deposited is taken as the electrolyte.

  1. For silver - plating a metal article :

    1. The article to be silver - plated is made negative electrode (cathode) : It is connected to the negative terminal of the battery.

    2. A silver rod is made positive electrode (anode) : It is connected to the positive terminal of the battery.

    3. Silver nitrate solution \( (AgNO_3 \ solution) \) is taken as electrolyte.

  2. For nickel - plating a metal article :

    1. The article to be nickel - plated is made negative electrode (cathode).

    2. A nickel rod is made positive electrode (anode).

    3. Nickel ammonium sulphate solution \( \left ( NiSO_4.(NH_4)_2SO_4.6H_2O \ solution \right ) \) is taken as electrolyte.

  3. For chromium - plating a metal article :

    1. The article to be chromium - plated is made negative electrode (cathode).

    2. A rod of chromium is made positive electrode (anode).

    3. Chromic acid solution \( \left ( H_2CrO_4 \ solution \right ) \) is taken as electrolyte.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
why household distribution is done in parallel ?

Solution:

  1. If there is short - circuit or overloading in one particular circuit, then only the fuse of that circuit will melt, but the power supply to other circuits is not affected.

  2. Since all the circuits are connected in parallel. Therefore, as the resistance due to appliances goes on increasing, the overall resistance of circuit decreases. Thus, more current flows in the various circuits depending upon the resistance of appliances. Hence, each appliances receives optimum amount of current. Moreover p.d. at the ends of each appliance remains same.

Density of water = \( 1 g cc^{-1} = 10^3 kg m^{-3} \)
mass of water = \( \text{Density of water} \times \text{Volume of water} = 10^3 \times 10^{-2} kg = 10 kg \)
Rise in temperature = \( 20^{\circ}C \) = 20 K
Power of the heater = 1000 W
Time taken by the heater to raise the temperature of water through 20 K = t = ?
The sequence consisting electric and heat terms is
\( \begin{align} H = \frac{W}{J} = \frac{VQ}{J} = \frac{Vit}{J} = \frac{Pt}{J} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{i^2Rt}{J} = \frac{V^2t}{RJ} = ms \Delta t \\\\[3pt] \frac{Pt}{J} = ms \Delta t \Rightarrow t = \frac{ms \Delta t \times t}{P} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ t = \frac{10 \times 4200 \times 20 \times 1}{1000} = 840s = 14 minutes \end{align} \)
