1 The force between any two charges is affected by the presence of other charges. The force between any two charges is not affected by the presence of other charges. Only the net force acting on a given charge is affected.
2 A charge experiences a force due to electric field produced by it. A charge does not experience a force due to electric field produced by it. But another charge placed in its electric field will experience a force due to its electric field.
3 It is a normal practice to assume the potential of the earth to be zero. This means that the earth has no net charge By assuming that the potential of the earth is zero, it only mean that the net charge, if any, is negligible in comparison to the capacitance of the earth as a conductor \( \left ( V = \frac{Q}{C} \rightarrow 0 \right ) \)

Introduction

In 600 B.C., the Greek philosopher Thales observed that amber, when rubbed with wool, acquires the property of attracting objects like small bits of paper, dry leaves, dust particles, etc., There are many other substances like amber, which can also acquire the property of attracting such light objects on being rubbed with suitable substances. The amber, glass rod, ebonite rod or plastic comb are said to be electrified or charged with electricity. Wool (in case of amber), silk (in case of glass rod) and fur (in case of ebonite rod) are also found to be electrically charged. Static electricity is the study of charges under rest.

Electric Charge

The additional property of protons and electrons, which gives rise to electric force between them, is called electric charge. Electric charge is a scalar quantity. A proton possesses positive charge + e, while an electron possesses an equal negative charge - e, where
\( e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} coulomb \)

Additive Nature of Charge

The total electric charge on an object is equal to the algebraic sum of all the electric charges distributed on the different parts of the object. If \( q_1, \ q_2, \ q_3 \) are electric charges present on different parts of an object, then total electric charge on the object.
\( q = q_1 + q_2 + q_3 +......... \)

Quantization of Charge

The fact that all observable charges are always some integral multiple of elementary charge \( \left ( e = \pm 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C \right ) \) is known as quantization of charge.
\( q = \pm ne \)

Conservation of Charge

Just as in mechanics, the total linear momentum of an isolated system always remains constant, the electric charge also obeys a similar law. It is called law of conservation of charge. It states that for an isolated system, the net charge always remains constant. In any physical process, the charge may get transferred from one part of the system to another, but net charge will always remains the same. In other words, charge can neither be created not destroyed.

Properties of Electric Charge

The following are a few important properties of electric charge:

  1. Like charges repel each other and unlike charges attract each other.

  2. The magnitude of elementary negative or positive charge is same and is equal to \( 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C. \)

  3. The electric charge is additive in nature. It implies that total charge on an object is algebraic sum of the charges located at different points in the object.

  4. The charge is quantized i.e., charge carried by a charged object is equal to \( \pm n e \) , where n is an integer.

  5. Unlike mass, the electric charge on an object is not affected by the motion of the object.

Coulomb's law in Electrostatics

It states that two point charges attract or repel each other with a force which is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, the force acts along the line joining the two charges.
\( \begin{align} F \propto q_1 q_2 ........(1) \\\\[12pt] F \propto \frac{1}{r^2}..........(2) \end{align} \)
Combining equations (1) and (2), we have
\( \begin{align} F \propto \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \\\\[12pt] or \ F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \end{align} \) ,
where k is constant of proportionality. Its value depends upon the nature of the medium in which the two charges are located and also the system of units adopted to measure \( F, \ q_1, \ q_2, \ and \ r \) . In SI system, when the two charges are located in vacuum or air,
\( k = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0} \)
where \( {\epsilon}_0 \) is called absolute permittivity of free space.
\( F_{vac} = 9 \times 10^{9} \times \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \) (in newton)
Note:
In electromagnetic cgs system, the unit of charge is known as electromagnetic unit of charge (e.m.u. of charge).
\( 1 C = \frac{1}{10} \) e.m.u. of charge.

Principle of Superposition

The principle of superposition states that when a number of charges are interacting, the total force on a given charge is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted on the given charge by all the other charges.

Electric Field

The concept of electric field provides an explanation as to how a charge can exert force on another charge kept at a distance from it. Electric field due to a charge is the space around the charge, in which any other charge is acted upon by an electrostatic force.

Electric Field Intensity

The electric field at a point is a vector quantity. It follows that unit of electric field in SI system is newton coulomb \( \left ( N C^{-1} \right ). \)
Force on a charge inside electric field. If \( \overrightarrow{E} \) is electric field at a point, then by definition, a charge q placed at that point will experience a force \( \overrightarrow{F} \) given by
\( \overrightarrow{F} = q \overrightarrow{E} \)

Electric Lines of Force

An electric lines of force is the path along which a unit positive charge would move, if it is free to do so.

Properties of electric lines of force

  1. The lines of force start from the positive charge and end at the negative charge.

  2. The line of force originate (from a positive charge) or terminate (at a negative charge) always at right angles to the surface of the charge.

  3. The lines of force do not pass through a conductor.
    It indicates that the electric field inside a conductor is always zero.

  4. The lines of force never intersect each other.
    In case, two lines of force intersect at a point, then it will mean that at that point, electric field, has two directions. As it is not possible, the lines of force can never intersect each other.

  5. The lines of force contract longitudinally i.e., lengthwise.
    This property of lines of force leads to explain attraction between two unlike charges.

  6. The lines of force exert a lateral pressure on each other.

Electric Potential

The electrostatic potential difference between two points B and A in an electric field is defined as the amount of work done per unit positive test charge in moving it from point A to B against the electrostatic force due to the electric field.
\( V_B - V_A = \frac{W_{AB}}{q_0} \)

Capacitor

Capacitor is a device which can store certain amount of charge. When a conductor is given some electric charge, it is raised to some potential. As more and more charge is given, its potential increases accordingly. If charge q is given to the conductor so that its potential increases by an amount V, then it is found that
\( q \propto V \)
or \( q = CV \)
where the constant of proportionality C is called the capacitance of the conductor. Its value depends upon the shape and dimensions of the conductor.
\( C = \frac{q}{V} \)
The Capacitance of a conductor is numerically equal to the electric charge required to raise its electric potential through one unit.
\( 1 farad(F) = \frac{1 coulomb(C)}{1 volt (V)} = 1 coulomb \ volt^{-1} \left ( C V^{-1} \right ). \)
A Conductor is said to have a capacitance of one farad,if 1 coulomb of charge raises its electric potential through 1 volt.
In Practice, the following smaller units of capacitance are also used:
\( \begin{align} 1 microfarad ( \mu F) = 10^{-6} farad \\\\[12pt] 1 \ micromicrofarad ( \mu \mu F) = 10^{-12} farad \end{align} \)
\( 1 \mu \mu F \) is also known as 1 picofarad(pF).

Capacitors in series

The method of connecting three capacitors of capacitances \( C_1, \ C_2 \ and \ C_3 \) in series.

Let \( V_1, \ V_2 \ and \ V_3 \) be potential differences accross the plates of the three capacitors, If V is the potential difference across the series combination, then
\( V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 \)
from the definition of Capacitance,
\( \begin{align} V_1 = \frac{q}{C_1}; \ V_2 = \frac{q}{C_2} \ and \ V_3 = \frac{q}{C_3} \\\\[12pt] \therefore \ V = \frac{q}{C_1} + \frac{q}{C_2} + \frac{q}{C_3} \\\\[12pt] = q \left [ \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} \right ] \end{align} \)
Let C be the equivalent capacitance of the series combination. Therefore, when a potential difference V is applied across a capacitor of capacitance C, it will also store the same amount of charge.
Hence, \( V = \frac{q}{C} \)
from equations, we have
\( \begin{align} \frac{q}{C} = q \left [ \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} \right ] \\\\[12pt] or \ \frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} \end{align} \)
Thus, the reciprocal of the resultant (equal) capacitance of the series combination of a number of capacitors is equal to the sum of the reciprocals of the capacitances of individual capacitors.
Note: In series combination of capacitors,

  1. charge on each capacitor is same.

  2. potential difference across a capacitor is inversely proportional to its capacitance and

  3. the equivalent capacitance is less than the capacitance of the smallest capacitor.

Capacitors in Parallel

The method of connecting three capacitors of capacitances \( C_1, \ C_2 \ and \ C_3 \) in parallel.

If \( q_1, \ q_2 \ and \ q_3 \) potential differences across the parallel combination, then
\( q_1 = C_1 V ; \ q_2 = C_2 V \ and \ q_3 = C_3 V \)
Total Charge on the parallel Combination of the Capacitors,
\( \begin{align} q = q_1 + q_2 + q_3 = C_1 V + C_2 V + C_3 V \\\\[12pt] or \ q = \left ( C_1 + C_2 + C_3 \right )V \end{align} \)
If C is the equivalent capacitance of the parallel Combination, then
\( q = CV \)
from equations, we have
\( \begin{align} CV = \left ( C_1 + C_2 + C_3 \right )V \\\\[12pt] or \ C = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 \end{align} \)
Thus the resultant (equivalent) capacitance of the parallel combination of a number of capacitors is equal to the sum of the capacitance of the individual capacitors.
Note:
In parallel combination of capacitors,

  1. potential difference across each capacitor is same.

  2. charge on a capacitor is proportional to its capacitance and

  3. the equivalent capacitance is greater than the capacitance of the largest capacitor

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
A polythene piece rubbed with wool is found to have a negative charge of \( 3 \times 10^{-7}C \) . Estimate the number of electrons transferred from wool to polythene.

Solution:
Here, total charge transferred, \( q = -3 \times 10^{-7}C \)
Charge on an electron, \( e = -1.6 \times 10^{-19}C \)
From quantization of charge, \( q = ne \)
Therefore, number of the electrons transferred,
\( n = \frac{q}{e} = \frac{-3 \times 10^{-7}}{-1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 1.875 \times 10^{12} \)

Example 2:

Question:
What is the force between two small charged spheres having charges of \( 2 \times 10^{-7} C \) and \( 3 \times 10^{-7} C \) placed 30 cm in air?

Solution:
Here,
\( \begin{align} q_1 = 2 \times 10^{-7} C ; \ q_2 = 3 \times 10^{-7} C \\\\[12pt] r = 30 cm = 0.3m \\\\[12pt] in \ air \ F = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \\\\[12pt] = 9 \times 10^{9} \times \frac{(2 \times 10^{-7}) \times (3 \times 10^{-7})}{(0.3)^2} = 6 \times 10^{-3} N(repulsive) \end{align} \) .

Example 3:

Question:
The electrostatic force on a small sphere of charge \( 0.4 \mu C \) due to another small sphere of charge \( 0.8 \mu C \) in air is 0.2 N.

  1. What is the distance between the two spheres?

  2. What is the force on the second sphere due to the first?

Solution:
Here, \( \begin{align} q_1 = 0.4 \mu C = 0.4 \times 10^{-6} C; \\\\[12pt] q_2 = - 0.8 \mu C = - 0.8 \times 10^{-6} C \end{align} \)

  1. Force on charge \( q_1 \) due to \( q_2, \ F_{12} = 0.2 N \)
    In air, \( F_{12} = \frac{1}{4 \pi { \epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \)
    \( \therefore \ 0.2 = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{(0.4 \times 10^{-6}) \times (0.8 \times 10^{-6})}{r^2} \ or \ r = 0.12 m \)

  2. Force on charge \( q_2 \) due to \( q_1 \) ,
    \( F_{21} = F_{12} = 0.2N (attractive) \)

Example 4:

Question:
Two point charges of charge values Q and q are placed at a distance of x and \( \frac{x}{2} \) respectively from a third charge of charge value 4q, all charges being in the same straight line. Calculate the magnitude and nature of charge Q, such that the net force experienced by the charge q is zero. (Assume q is located between charges 4q and Q).

Solution:
Suppose that the charge 4q is located at point A. The charges Q and q are placed at the points B and C, such that AB = x and AC = \( \frac{x}{2} \) . Also, all the charges lie on the same straight line.
Given net force on q to be zero then 4q and q are of same nature i.e, either positive or negative
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow F_{AC} = F_{CB} \\\\[12pt] \frac{4(9 \times 10^9 \times q \times 4q)}{x^2} = \frac{9 \times 10^9 \times q \times Q}{x^2} \end{align} \)
Q = 16q. Here Q = 16q and can be either positive or negative.

Example 5:

Question:
Two charges each of + Q units are placed along a line. A third charge - q is placed between them. At what position and for what value of q, will the system be in equilibrium?

Solution:
Let the two charges + Q each be placed at points A and B at a distance r between them and charge - q be placed at point O, such that AO = x and OB = r - x.

The system will be in equilibrium, if the charge - q as well as either of the two charges + Q each placed at the points A and B experience zero net force.

  1. For charge - q to be in equilibrium : For this, force on the charge - q at point O due to the charge + Q at point A should be equal and opposite to that due to the charge + Q at the point B i.e.,
    \( \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{Qq}{x^2} = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{Qq}{(r - x)^2} \)
    or \( x^2 = (r - x)^2 \ or \ x = \pm(r - x) \ or \ x = \frac{r}{2} \)

  2. For charge +Q at A(or at B) to be in equilibrium :For this, force on the charge + Q at the point A due to the charge - q at O should be equal and opposite to that due to the charge + Q at B i.e.,
    \( \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{Qq}{\left ( \frac{r}{2} \right )^2} = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{QQ}{r^2} \ or \ q = \frac{Q}{4} \)
    Therefore, the system of three charges will be in equilibrium, if charge - q is equal to \( - \frac{Q}{4} \) and is placed at the midpoint of the distance between the two charges of + Q each.

Example 6:

Question:

  1. If you accumulate a static charge and then touch a wooden frame of a door, you often find no spark or shock, although there would be, you touched the metal handle Why?

  2. Sometimes, if you touch the wooden frame and then touch the metal handle, there is no spark or shock in either case, although there would have been, if you had touched the handle first. Suggest an explanation.

Solution:

  1. Metals are good conductors. When we are carrying a static charge and touch the metal handle of the door, the charge transfers to it immediately in the form of a spark and distributes itself quickly throughout the conductor.

  2. When the wooden frame (insulator) of the door is touched, the charge is unable to flow through. As such, only a little charge may get transferred and there is no spark.

Example 7:

Question:
What is the significance of electric field ?

Solution:
The concept of electric field is of a great significance. Two charges kept at a distance exert force on each other, which is termed as action at a distance. The concept of electric field helps us to understand the mechanism by which two charges kept at a distance exert force on each other. Further, as an alternative to Coulomb's law in electrostatics, it gives a method to find the force experienced by one charge due to the other in terms of the electric field produced by it. .

Example 8:

Question:
Two point charges of \( 2 \times 10^{-7}C \ and \ 1.0 \times 10^{-7} \) are 1.0 cm apart. What is the magnitude of the field produced by either charge at the site of the other? Use standard value of \( \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0} \)

Solution:
Here,
\( \begin{align} q_1 = 2.0 \times 10^{-7} C ; \ q_2 = 1.0 \times 10^{-7} C \\\\[12pt] and \ r = 1.0 cm = 10^{-2} m \end{align} \)
The electric field at the site of a charge will be electric field produced by the other charge.
Therefore, electric field due to the charge \( q_1 \) at the site of \( q_2 \) ,
\( E_1 = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_1}{r^2} = \frac{9 \times 10^9 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-7}}{(10^{-2})^2} = 1.8 \times 10^7 N C^{-1} \)
Also, electric field due to the charge \( q_2 \) at the site of \( q_1 \) ,
\( E_2 = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_2}{r^2} = \frac{9 \times 10^9 \times 1.0 \times 10^{-7}}{(10^{-2})^2} = 9 \times 10^6 N C^{-1} \)

Example 9:

Question:
If 100 joule of work must be done to move electric charge equal to 4 C from a place, where potential is - 10 volt to another place, where potential is V volt, find the value of V.

Solution:
Here,
\( \begin{align} q_0 = 4 C ; \ V_A = - 10 volt; V_B = V = volt \\\\[12pt] W_{AB} = 100 joule \end{align} \)
Now,
\( V_B - V_A = \frac{W_{AB}}{q_0} \ or \ V - (- 10) = \frac{100}{4} = 25 \)
or V + 10 = 25 or V = 15 volt

Example 10:

Question:
A charge of \( 20 \mu C \) produces an electric field. Two points are 10 cm and 5 cm from this charge. Find the values of potentials at these points and also find the amount of work done to take an electron from one point to the other.

Solution:
Here,
\( q = 20 \mu C = 20 \times 10^{-6} C \)
Let A and B be two points at distance 10 cm and 5 cm from the charge. Thus,
\( r_1 = 10 cm = 0.1 m ; \ r_2 = 5 cm = 0.05 m. \)
Now, \( \begin{align} V_A = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q}{r_1} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{20 \times 10^{-6}}{0.1} \\\\[12pt] = 1.8 \times 10^6 V \end{align} \)
Also, \( \begin{align} V_B = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q}{r_2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{20 \times 10^{-6}}{0.05} \\\\[12pt] = 3.6 \times 10^6 V \end{align} \)
\( V_B - V_A = 3.6 \times 10^6 V - 1.8 \times 10^6 V = 1.8 \times 10^6 V \)
Work done to take an electron from A to B,
\( \begin{align} W_{AB} = (V_B - V_A) \times \text{charge of an electron} \\\\[12pt] = 1.8 \times 10^6 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} = 2.88 \times 10^{-13} J. \end{align} \)

Question:
Two capacitors of capacitances \( C_1 \ and \ C_2 \) are joined in series and this combination is joined in parallel with a capacitor of capacitance \( C_3 \) . Find the resultant capacitance.

Solution:
The three capacitors of capacitances \( C_1, \ C_2 \ and \ C_3 \) are joined as shown in figure.

The capacitance of the series combination of \( C_1 \ and \ C_2 \) ,

Therefore, the resultant capacitance,
\( C = C^{\prime} + C_3 = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2} + C_3 \)

Example 14:

Question:
Calculate the total positive or negative charge on a 2.35 g copper penny. Given Avogadro number = \( 6.02 \times 10^{23} \) , atomic number of copper = 29 and atomic mass of copper = 63.5.

Solution:
Here, mass of copper penny, m = 2.35 g
Z = 29 ; A = 63.5 and N = \( 6.02 \times 10^{23} \)
Number of atoms in the copper penny, \( n = \frac{N}{A} \times m \)
\( = \frac{6.02 \times 10^{23} \times 2.35}{63.5} = 2.22 \times 10^{2} \)
Number of protons or electrons in the copper penny = \( n \times Z = 2.22 \times 10^{22} \times 29 \)
Therefore, total positive or negative charge on the copper penny,
\( q = 2.22 \times 10^{22} \times 29 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} = 1.03 \times 10^5 C \)

Example 15:

Question:
Two equal charges placed in air and separated by a distance of 2 m repel each other with a force of \( 10^{-4} kgf \) . Calculate the magnitude of either of the charges.

Solution:
Here,
\( \begin{align} F = 10^{-4} kgf = 10^{-4} \times 9.8N; r = 2m \\\\[12pt] Let \ q_1 = q_2 = q \\\\[12pt] Now, \ F = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q \times q}{r^2} \\\\[12pt] \therefore \ 10^{-4} \times 9.8 = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{q^2}{r^2} \\\\[12pt] or \ q = \sqrt{ \frac{4 \times 10^{-4} \times 9.8}{9 \times 10^9}} = 6.6 \times 10^{-7} C \end{align} \) .

Example 16:

Question:
The electrostatic force of repulsion between two equal positively charged ions is \( 3.7 \times 10^{-9} N \) , when they are separated by a distance of \( 5 \overset{\circ}{A} \) . How many electrons are missing from each ion?

Solution:
Here, \( F = 3.7 \times 10^{-9} N; \ r = 5 \overset{\circ}{A} = 5 \times 10^{-10} m \)
Suppose, that n electrons have been removed to form each positive ion. Then, charge on each positive ion,
\( \begin{align} q_1 = q_2 = n \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}C \\\\[12pt] Now, \ F = \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \\\\[12pt] \therefore \ 3.7 \times 10^{-9} = \frac{9 \times 10^9 \times( n \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} )^2}{( 5 \times 10^{-10} )^2} \end{align} \)
or n = 2

Question:
The electrostatic force on a small sphere of charge \( 0.4 \pi C \) due to another small sphere of charge - \( 0.8 \pi C \) in air is 0.2 N.

  1. What is the distance between the two spheres?

  2. What is the force on the second sphere due to the first?

Solution:
Here,
\( \begin{align} q_1 = 0.4 \pi C = 0.4 \times 10^{-6} C; \\\\[12pt] q_2 = -0.8 \pi C = - 0.8 \times 10^{-6} C \end{align} \)

  1. Force on charge \( q_1 \) due to \( q_2, \ F_{12} = 0.2 N \)

  2. Force on charge \( q_2 \) due to \( q_1 \) ,

Example 18:

Question:
A free pith-ball of 8 g carries a positive charge of \( 5 \times 10^{-8} C \) . What must be the nature and magnitude of charge that should be given to a second pith-ball fixed 5 cm vertically below the former pith-ball so that the upper pith-ball is stationary?

Solution:
Here, charge on the pith-ball A, \( q_1 = 5 \times 10^{-8} C \)
mass of the pith-ball A, \( m_1 = 8 g = 8 \times 10^{-3} kg \)
The weight \( m_1 g \) of the pith-ball A acts vertically downwards.
Let \( q_2 \) be charge on the pith-ball B held 5 cm below the pith-ball A, so that the pith-ball A remains stationary. It can be possible only, if the charges on two pith-balls are of same signs i.e., if charge on the pith-ball A is positive, charge on B should also be positive. As such, the force on the pith-ball A due to B i.e., \( F_{AB} \) will act vertically upwards.

for charge \( q_1 \) to remain stationary,
\( \begin{align} F_{AB} = m_1 g \\\\[12pt] or \ \frac{1}{4 \pi {\epsilon}_0}. \frac{q_1 q_2}{AB^2} = m_1 g \end{align} \)
Here, AB = 5 cm = 0.05 m
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{5 \times 10^{-8} \times q^2}{(0.05)^2} = 8 \times 10^{-3} \times 9.8 \\\\[12pt] or \ q_2 = 4.36 \times 10^{-7} C \end{align} \)
