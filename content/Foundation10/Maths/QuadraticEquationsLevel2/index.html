Introduction

An equation of the form
\( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)
Where \( a, \ b, \ c \epsilon R \) and \( a \neq 0 \) is called a quadratic equation. The numbers a, b, c are called the coefficient of this equation.
A root of the quadratic equation is a complex number \( \alpha \) such that \( a \alpha^2 + b \alpha + c = 0 \)
Discriminant \( D = b^2 - 4ac \)
The roots of the above quadratic equation are given by the formula
\( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{D}}{2a} \ \ or \ \ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)

Properties of Quadratic Equations

  1. A quadratic equation has only two roots.

  2. A quadratic equation cannot have more than two different roots.

  3. If \( \alpha \) be a root of the quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) , then \( (x - \alpha) \) is a factor of \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)

Note:

The possible values of x which satisfy the quadratic equation are called the roots of the quadratic equation.

Sum and Product of the roots of a quadratic equation

Let \( \alpha, \ \beta \) be the roots of a quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0; \ a \neq 0 \) , then
\( \alpha + \beta = \frac{-b}{a} = - \left( \frac{coefficient \ of \ x}{coefficient \ of \ x^2} \right) \ and \ \alpha.\beta = \frac{c}{a} = \left( \frac{constant \ term}{coefficient \ of \ x^2} \right) \)
Therefore,

  1. If the two roots \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) be reciprocal to each other, then a = c.

  2. If the two roots \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) be equal in magnitude and opposite in sign, then b = 0.

Sign of the roots

  1. The roots \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are both negative, if \( ( \alpha + \beta) \) and \( \alpha \beta \) are both positive
    \( \left(i.e., \ if \ \frac{-b}{a} \ and \ \frac{c}{a} \ are \ both \ positive \right) \)

  2. The roots \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are both positive, if \( \alpha + \beta \) is negative and \( \alpha \beta \) is positive
    \( \left(i.e., \ if \ \frac{b}{a} \ is \ negative \ and \ \frac{c}{a} \ is \ positive \right) \)

  3. The roots \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are of opposite sign, if \( \alpha \beta \) is negative
    \( \left(i.e., \ \frac{c}{a} \ is \ negative \right) \)

Sign of \( ( \alpha + \beta) \) Sign of \( ( \alpha \beta) \) Sign of the \( \alpha, \ \beta \)
+ ve + ve \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) are positive
- ve + ve \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) are negative
+ ve - ve \( \alpha \) is positive and \( \beta \) is negative if \( \alpha > \beta \) (numerically)
- ve - ve \( \alpha \) is negative and \( \beta \) is positive if \( \alpha > \beta \) (numerically)

Nature of the roots

For a quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) where \( a, \ b, \ c \epsilon R, \ a \neq 0 \) and \( D = b^2 - 4ac. \)

  1. If \( D > 0 \) , roots are imaginary

  2. If \( D \geq 0 \) roots are real.

  1. If \( a, \ b, \ c \epsilon R \) and p + iq is one root of quadratic equation ( where \( q \neq 0 \) ), then the other roots must be conjugate p - iq and vice - versa.( \( p, \ q \epsilon R, \ a \neq 0 \) and \( i = \sqrt{-1} \) )

  2. If \( a, \ b, \ c \epsilon Q, \ a \neq 0 \) and \( p + \sqrt{q} \) is one root of the quadratic equation, then the other roots must be the conjugate \( p - \sqrt{q} \) and vice - versa(where p is a rational and \( \sqrt{q} \) is a surd).

  3. If a = 1 and \( b, \ c \epsilon I \) and the roots of quadratic equation are rational numbers, then these roots must be integers.

  4. If the quadratic equation has more than 2 roots(complex numbers), then the equation becomes as identity i.e., a = b = c = 0.

Condition for Common Roots

Consider two quadratic equations
\( \begin{align} ax^2 + bx + c = 0..........(i) \ a \neq 0 \\\\[3pt] and \ \ a^{|}x^2 + b^{|}x + c^{|} ..........(ii) \ a^{|} \neq 0 \end{align} \)

  1. If one root is common then,
    \( (ab^{|} - a^{|}b)(bc^{|} - b^{|}c) = (ca^{|} - c^{|}a)^2 \)

  2. If two roots are common then,
    \( \frac{a}{a^{'}} = \frac{b}{b^{'}} = \frac{c}{c^{'}} \)

Condition that \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) is Factorizable into two Linear Factors

When \( D \geq 0 \) , then the equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) id factorizable into two linear factors i.e., \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \Rightarrow (x - \alpha)(x - \beta) = 0 \) , where \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) are the roots of quadratic equation.

Formation of a Quadratic Equation:

Let \( \alpha, \ \beta \) be the two roots, then we can form a quadratic equation as follows:
\( \begin{align} x^2 - (sum \ of \ roots)x + (product \ of \ roots) = 0 \\\\[3pt] i.e., \ \ \ x^2 - ( \alpha + \beta)x + ( \alpha \beta) = 0 \\\\[3pt] or \ \ \ \ (x - \alpha)(x - \beta) = 0 \end{align} \)

Graph of a Quadratic Equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0, \ a \neq 0 \)
Characteristics of the function \( b^2 - 4ac < 0 \) \( b^2 - 4ac = 0 \) \( b^2 - 4ac > 0 \)
When 'a' is positive
\( i.e., \ a > 0 \)
When 'a' is negative
\( i.e., \ a < 0 \)

  1. The graph of a quadratic function(expression) is called a parabola. The point at which its direction changes is called its turning point, commonly called the vertex of the parabola.

  2. The graph of the function is concave upwards when a > 0 and concave downwards when a < 0.

  3. If the graph has no points in common with the x - axis, the roots of the equation are imaginary and cannot be determined from the graph.

  4. If the graph is tangent to the x - axis, the roots are real and equal.

  5. If the graph cuts the x - axis, the roots of the equation will be real and unequal. Their values will be given by the abscissa of the points of intersection of the graph and the x - axis.

Roots of Cubic and Quartic Equations

If the equation is of a cubic form, \( ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0 \) and \( \alpha, \ \beta \) and \( \gamma \) are the 3 roots of the cubic equation, then
\( \begin{align} \alpha + \beta + \gamma = - \frac{b}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha \beta + \beta \gamma + \gamma \alpha = \frac{c}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha \beta \gamma = - \frac{d}{a} \end{align} \)
If the equation is of a quartic form, \( ax^4 + bx^3 + cx^2 + dx + e = 0 \) , and \( \alpha, \ \beta, \ \gamma, \ \delta \) are the four roots of the quartic equation,
\( \begin{align} \alpha + \beta + \gamma + \delta = - \frac{b}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha \beta + \alpha \gamma + \alpha \delta + \beta \gamma + \beta \delta + \gamma \delta = \frac{c}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha \beta \gamma + \alpha \beta \delta + \alpha \gamma \delta + \beta \gamma \delta = - \frac{d}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha \beta \gamma \delta = \frac{e}{a} \end{align} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
The product of two consecutive numbers is 56. Find them.

Solution:
Let the smaller number be x.
Then larger number will be (x + 1)
Given that x(x + 1) = 56
i.e., \( x^2 + x - 56 = 0 \)
Factorizing, (x - 7)(x + 8) = 0
\( \therefore x = 7 \ or \ - 8 \)
If we take the positive value of x, the number are 7 and 8.
Taking negative value of x, we have - 8 and - 7.

Example 2:

Question:
The length of a hall exceeds its breadth by 8m and its area is \( 180 \ m^2. \) Find the dimensions of the hall.

Solution:
Let the length of the room be x meters.
Its breadth will be (x - 8)m.
\( \therefore \) Its area \( = x(x - 8)m^2 \)
But by the hypothesis x(x - 8) = 180.
\( \begin{align} i.e., \ x^2 - 8x - 180 = 0 \Rightarrow (x - 18)(x + 10) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \therefore x = 18 \ m, \end{align} \)
(x = -10 is not permissible as the length of a room can't be negative)
So the length x = 18 m.
and the breadth x - 8 = 18 - 8 = 10 m.

Example 3:

Question:
A man walks a distance of 48 km in a given time. If he walks 2 km an hour faster, he will perform the journey 4 hours before. Find his normal rate of walking.

Solution:
Let the normal rate of walking of the man be \( x \ \frac{km}{hour}. \)
Time taken to walk 48 km \( = \frac{48}{x} \ hours \)
At 2 km an hour faster he will take \( = \frac{48}{x + 2} \ hours. \)
This time is 4 hour less than the usual time.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \frac{48}{x} = \frac{48}{x + 2} + 4 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 48(x + 2) = 48x + 4x(x + 2) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 + 2x - 24 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (x - 4)(x + 6) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \therefore x = 4 \ or \ -6 \\\\[3pt] \therefore Rate \ of \ walking \ is \ 4 \frac{km}{hour} \ as \ (x = -6 \ is \ not \ admissible) \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Question:
Find the value of \( \sqrt{a + \sqrt{a + \sqrt{a + .... \infty}}}. \)

Solution:
Let \( \begin{align} x = \sqrt{a + \sqrt{a + \sqrt{a + .... \infty}}} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = \sqrt{a + x} \end{align} \)
Squaring on both sides,
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow x^2 = ( \sqrt{a + x})^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 = a + x \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 - x - a = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4a}}{2} \\\\[3pt] \therefore x = \frac{1 + \sqrt{1 + 4a}}{2a} \ \ \ [since \ x > 0] \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
Find the value of \( \sqrt{a \sqrt{a \sqrt{a .... \infty}}}. \)

Solution:
Let \( \begin{align} x = \sqrt{a \sqrt{a \sqrt{a .... \infty}}} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = \sqrt{ax} \end{align} \)
Squaring on both sides,
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow x^2 = ax \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 - ax = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x(x - a) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = 0 \ or \ x = a \ \ \ \ [since x > 0] \\\\[3pt] \therefore x = a \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
Find the equation whose roots are the reciprocals of the roots of \( 3x^2 - 5x + 7 = 0. \)

Solution:
The equation whose roots are the reciprocals of the roots of
\( f(x) = 0 \) is
\( \begin{align} f \left( \frac{1}{x} \right) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow The \ required \ equation \ is \\\\[3pt] 3 \left( \frac{1}{x} \right)^2 - 5 \left( \frac{1}{x} \right) + 7 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{3}{x^2} - \frac{5}{x} + 7 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 7x^2 - 5x + 3 = 0 \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Question:
If the sum of the roots of \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) is equal to the sum of the squares of the roots then find the condition.

Solution:
If \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) then \( \alpha + \beta = \frac{-b}{a}, \ \alpha. \beta = \frac{c}{a} \)
Given that
\( \begin{align} \alpha + \beta = \alpha^2 + \beta^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \alpha + \beta = ( \alpha + \beta)^2 - 2 \alpha. \beta \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{-b}{a} = \left(- \frac{b}{a} \right)^2 - 2. \frac{c}{a} = \frac{b^2}{a^2} - \frac{2c}{a} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{-b}{a} = \frac{b^2 - 2ac}{a^2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{-b}{a} \times a^2 = b^2 - 2ac \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow - ab = b^2 - 2ac \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 2ac = b^2 + ab \\\\[3pt] \therefore 2ac = b^2 + ab \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Question:
If one root of \( x^2 - x - k = 0 \) is square that of other, then find the value of k.

Solution:
Let one root be \( \alpha \)
Other root \( = \alpha^2 \)
Sum of the roots = + 1
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \alpha. \alpha^2 = -k \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \alpha^3 = -k \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \alpha = (-k)^{ \frac{1}{3}} \\\\[3pt] \alpha^2 + \alpha = +1 \\\\[3pt] [(-k)^{ \frac{1}{3}}]^2 - k^{ \frac{1}{3}} = +1 \\\\[3pt] k^{ \frac{2}{3}} - k^{ \frac{1}{3}} = 1 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \left( k^{ \frac{2}{3}} - k^{ \frac{1}{3}} \right)^3 = (1)^3 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow k^2 - k - 3k = 1 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow k^2 - 4k = 1 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow k^2 - 4k - 1 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow k = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 4}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2 \sqrt{5}}{2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow k = 2 \pm \sqrt{5} \end{align} \)

Example 11:

Question:
If \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) then find the equation for which the roots are \( \frac{1}{ \alpha} \) and \( \frac{1}{ \beta}. \)

Solution:
\( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \)
\( \begin{align} \therefore \alpha + \beta = - \frac{b}{a} \\\\[3pt] \alpha. \beta = \frac{c}{a} \end{align} \)
If \( \frac{1}{ \alpha}, \ \frac{1}{ \beta} \) are the roots then the sum of the roots
\( = \frac{1}{ \alpha} + \frac{1}{ \beta} = \frac{ \beta + \alpha}{ \alpha \beta} \)
Product of the roots \( = \frac{1}{ \alpha}. \frac{1}{ \beta} = \frac{1}{ \alpha. \beta} \)
Required equation is
\( \begin{align} x^2 - x \left( \frac{ \alpha + \beta}{ \alpha. \beta} \right) + \frac{1}{ \alpha \beta} = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 - x \left( \frac{- \frac{b}{a}}{ \frac{c}{a}} \right) + \frac{1}{ \frac{c}{a}} = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 + \frac{bx}{c} + \frac{a}{c} = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow cx^2 + bx + a = 0 \\\\[3pt] \therefore cx^2 + bx + a = 0 \end{align} \)

Example 12:

Question:
If \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of the equation \( 2x^2 + 3x + 7 = 0 \) , then find

  1. \( \alpha^2 + \beta^2 \)

  2. \( \alpha^3 + \beta^3 \)

  3. \( \frac{1}{ \alpha} + \frac{1}{ \beta} \)

  4. \( \frac{1}{ \alpha^2} + \frac{1}{ \beta^2} \)

Solution:
Since, \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of the equation \( 2x^2 + 3x + 7 = 0, \) we have
\( \alpha + \beta = \frac{-3}{2} \ \ \ \ and \ \ \ \ \alpha \beta = \frac{7}{2} \)
Therefore,

  1. \( \begin{align} \alpha^2 + \beta^2 = ( \alpha + \beta)^2 - 2 \alpha \beta \\\\[3pt] = \left( \frac{-3}{2} \right)^2 - 2 \times \frac{7}{2} = \frac{-19}{4} \end{align} \)

  2. \( \begin{align} \alpha^3 + \beta^3 = ( \alpha + \beta)^3 - 3 \alpha \beta ( \alpha + \beta) \\\\[3pt] = \left( \frac{-3}{2} \right)^3 - 2 \times \frac{7}{2} = \frac{-19}{4} \end{align} \)

  3. \( \begin{align} \frac{1}{ \alpha} + \frac{1}{ \beta} = \frac{ \alpha + \beta}{ \alpha \beta} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{-3}{2} \times \frac{2}{7} = \frac{-3}{7} \end{align} \)

  4. \( \begin{align} \frac{1}{ \alpha^2} + \frac{1}{ \beta^2} = \frac{( \alpha + \beta)^2 - 2 \alpha \beta}{( \alpha \beta)^2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{ \left(- \frac{3}{2} \right)^2 - 2 \times \frac{7}{2}}{ \left( \frac{7}{2} \right)^2} = \frac{-19}{49} \end{align} \)

Example 13:

Question:
If \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of the quadratic equation \( px^2 + qx + r = 0, \ p \neq 0, \) then form the quadratic equation whose roots are

  1. \( 2 \alpha, \ 2 \beta \)

  2. \( \alpha^2, \ \beta^2 \)

  3. \( \frac{1}{ \alpha}, \ \frac{1}{ \beta} \)

  4. \( \alpha^2 \beta, \ \alpha \beta^2 \)

Solution:
Since \( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of the given equation, we have
\( \alpha + \beta = \frac{-q}{p} \ \ \ \ and \ \ \ \ \alpha \beta = \frac{r}{p} \)
Let \( \alpha^{'} \) and \( \beta^{'} \) be the roots of the required equation. Then

  1. In this case
    \( \begin{align} \alpha^{'} + \beta^{'} = 2 \alpha + 2 \beta = 2( \alpha + \beta) = \frac{-2q}{p} \\\\[3pt] \alpha^{'} \beta^{'} = (2 \alpha)(2 \beta) = 4 \alpha \beta = \frac{4r}{p}. \end{align} \)
    The equation whose roots are \( \alpha^{'}, \ \beta^{'} \) is given by \( x^2 - ( \alpha^{'} + \beta^{'})x + ( \alpha^{'} \beta^{'}) = 0. \)
    Hence, the required equation is
    \( \begin{align} x^2 - \frac{(-2q)}{p}x + \frac{4r}{p} = 0 \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] px^2 + 2qx + 4r = 0 \end{align} \)

  2. In this case
    \( \begin{align} \alpha^{'} + \beta^{'} = \alpha^2 + \beta^2 = ( \alpha + \beta)^2 - 2 \alpha \beta = \frac{q^2}{p^2} - \frac{2r}{p} = \frac{q^2 - 2pr}{p^2} \\\\[3pt] \alpha^{'} \beta^{'} = \alpha^2 \beta^2 = ( \alpha \beta)^2 = \frac{r^2}{p^2} \end{align} \)
    Hence the required equation is
    \( \begin{align} x^2 - \frac{(q^2 - 2px)}{p^2}x + \frac{r^2}{p^2} = 0 \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] p^2x^2 - (q^2 - 2pr)x + r^2 = 0 \end{align} \)

  3. In this case
    \( \begin{align} \alpha^{'} + \beta^{'} = \frac{1}{ \alpha} + \frac{1}{ \beta} = \frac{ \alpha + \beta}{ \alpha \beta} = \frac{-q}{r} \\\\[3pt] \alpha^{'} \beta^{'} = \frac{1}{ \alpha \beta} = \frac{p}{r} \end{align} \)
    Hence the required equation is
    \( \begin{align} x^2 - \left( \frac{-q}{r} \right)x + \frac{p}{r} = 0 \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] rx^2 + qx + p = 0 \end{align} \)

  4. In this case,
    \( \begin{align} \alpha^{'} + \beta^{'} = \alpha^2 \beta + \alpha \beta^2 = \alpha \beta ( \alpha + \beta) = \frac{-qr}{p^2} \\\\[3pt] \alpha^{'} \beta^{'} = ( \alpha^2 \beta)( \alpha \beta^2) = ( \alpha \beta)^3 = \frac{r^3}{p^3} \end{align} \)
    Hence the required equation is
    \( \begin{align} x^2 - \left( \frac{-qr}{p^2} \right)x + \frac{r^3}{p^3} = 0 \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] p^3x^2 + pqrx + r^3 = 0 \end{align} \)

Example 14:

Question:
Evaluate

  1. \( \sqrt{20 + \sqrt{20 + \sqrt{20 +....}}} \)

  2. \( 20 + \frac{1}{20 + \frac{1}{20....}} \)

Solution:

  1. Let,
    \( \begin{align} x = \sqrt{20 + \sqrt{20 + \sqrt{20 +....}}} \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] x = \sqrt{20 + x} \end{align} \)
    Squaring on both sides, we get
    \( \begin{align} x^2 = 20 + x \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] x^2 - x - 20 = 0 \\\\[3pt] Which \ gives \ \ \ x = \frac{1 + \sqrt{1 + 80}}{2} = 5 \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] x = \frac{1 - \sqrt{1 + 80}}{2} = -4 \end{align} \)
    Since the value of the given expression is positive neglect -4. Hence the value of the given expression is 5.

  2. Let,
    \( \begin{align} x = 20 + \frac{1}{20 + \frac{1}{20....}} \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] x = 20 + \frac{1}{x} \\\\[3pt] Therefore, \ x^2 - 20x - 1 = 0 \\\\[3pt] This \ gives \ x = \frac{20 + \sqrt{404}}{2} = 10 + \sqrt{101} \\\\[3pt] or \\\\[3pt] x = \frac{20 - \sqrt{404}}{2} = 10 - \sqrt{101} \end{align} \)
    Since the given expression can not be negative therefore, we neglect the negative value \( 10 - \sqrt{101} \) . Hence, the desired value of the expression is \( 10 + \sqrt{101} \)

Example 15:

Question:
The roots \( \alpha, \ \beta \) of the equation \( x^3 - 3ax + a^2 = 0 \) are such that \( \alpha^2 + \beta^2 = 1.75 \) . Find the value of a.

Solution:
\( \alpha, \ \beta \) are the roots of \( x^2 - 3ax + a^2 = 0, \) we have
\( \begin{align} \alpha + \beta = 3a \ \ \ \ and \ \ \ \ \alpha \beta = a^2 \\\\[3pt] Now, \\\\[3pt] \alpha^2 + \beta^2 = ( \alpha + \beta)^2 - 2 \alpha \beta = 9a^2 - 2a^2 = 7a^2 \\\\[3pt] 7a^2 = 1.75 \\\\[3pt] Therefore, \ \ 7a^2 = 1.75 \ \ or \ \ a = \pm 0.5 \end{align} \)

Example 16:

Question:
A two - digit number is four times the sum and three times the product of its digits. Find the number.

Solution:
Let x be the digit in ten's place and y be in the unit's place of the number. Then the number is 10x + y. By hypotheses
10x + y = 4(x + y)
and 10x + y = 3xy
The first of these equations gives
6x = 3y 2x = y
Substituting 2x = y is the second equation, we have
\( 10x + 2x = 6x^2 \ \ \ or \ \ \ x^2 - 2x = 0 \)
which gives x = 0 or x = 2. If x = 0, then y = 0 and the number in this case does not have two digits. The solution is therefore x = 2, y = 4. Hence the desired number is 24.

Example 17:

Question:
Find the number which exceeds its positive square root by 20.

Solution:
Let x be the required number. Then, by hypothesis
\( \begin{align} x - \sqrt{x} = 20 \\\\[3pt] or \ \ \ x - 20 = \sqrt{x} \end{align} \)
Squaring on both sides, we get
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow (x - 20)^2 = x \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 - 40x + 400 = x \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x^2 - 41x + 400 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (x - 25)(x - 16) = 0 \\\\[3pt] This \ gives \ x = 25 \ or \ x = 16. \end{align} \)
But x = 16 does not satisfy the given equation. Hence the required number is 25.

Example 18:

Question:
Find the condition that the quadratic equations \( x^2 + ax + b = 0 \) and \( x^2 + bx + a = 0 \) may have a common root.

Solution:
Let \( \alpha \) be a common root of the given equations.
Then \( \alpha^2 + a \alpha + b = 0 \)
and \( \alpha^2 + b \alpha + a = 0 \)
By the method of cross - multiplication, we get
\( \frac{ \alpha^2}{a^2 - b^2} = \frac{ \alpha}{b - a} = \frac{1}{b - a} \)
This give
\( \begin{align} \alpha^2 = \frac{a^2 - b^2}{b - a} = -(a + b) \ and \ \alpha = 1 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (1)^2 = -(a + b) \Rightarrow 1 = - a - b \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a + b + 1 = 0 \ is \ the \ required \ condition \end{align} \)

Example 19:

Question:
For what values of m will the equation
\( (m + 1)x^2 + 2(m + 3)x + (2m + 3) = 0 \)
have equal roots.

Solution:
The given equation will have equal roots, if any only if
\( \begin{align} [2(m + 3)]^2 = 4(m + 1)(2m + 3) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow m^2 - m - 6 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (m - 3)(m + 2) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \end{align} \)
which gives m = 3 or -2

Question:
Show that the equation \( (x - a)(x - b) = h^2 \) has real roots.

Solution:
The given equation can be written as
\( x^2 - (a + b)x + ab - h^2 = 0 \)
The discriminant for this quadratic equation is given by
\( \begin{align} D = (a + b)^2 - 4(ab - h^2) \\\\[3pt] = (a + b)^2 - 4ab + 4h^2 \\\\[3pt] = (a - b)^2 + 4h^2 \end{align} \)
which is always non - negative. Hence, the given equation has real roots.
