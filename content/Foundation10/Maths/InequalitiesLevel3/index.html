The Inequality of the Means

Definition:

Let a, b be two positive numbers. The positive numbers \( A = \frac{a + b}{2}, \ G = \sqrt{ab} \) and \( H = \frac{2ab}{a + b} \) are called respectively the arithmetic mean, the geometric mean and the harmonic mean of a and b.

General case:

Let \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ........a_n \) be n positive numbers. The positive numbers.
\( \begin{align} A = \frac{a_1 + a_2 +......+ a_n}{n} \\\\[3pt] G = (a_1 \ a_2.......a_n)^{ \frac{1}{n}} \end{align} \)
and \( H = \left[ \frac{ \left\lbrace \frac{1}{a_1} + \frac{1}{a_2} +....... + \frac{1}{a^n} \right\rbrace}{n} \right]^{-1} \)
are called respectively the arithmetic mean, the geometric mean and the harmonic mean of \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ........a_n. \)
If A, G and H be respectively the arithmetic mean, the geometric mean and the harmonic mean of n positive numbers \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ........a_n, \) then \( A \geq G \geq H \) . The equality sign holds if \( a_1 = a_2 = ........= a_n. \)
If A and G be respectively the arithmetic mean and the geometric mean of n positive numbers \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ........a_n, \) then \( A \geq G \) . The equality sign holds if \( a_1 = a_2 = ........= a_n. \)
The above inequality is called the 'Inequality of the Means' or 'A.M. - G.M. inequality' and is referred to as \( A.M. \geq G.M. \)
Applying the inequality \( A.M. \geq G.M. \) to the number \( a_1^{-1}, \ a_2^{-1}, \ ....., \ a_n^{-1}, \) we have
\( \frac{1}{n} \lbrace a_1^{-1} + a_2^{-1} + ......... + a_n^{-1} \rbrace \geq (a_1^{-1} \ a_2^{-1}.........a_n^{-1})^{ \frac{1}{n}} \)
or \( (a_1 \ a_2.........a_n)^{ \frac{1}{n}} \geq \left[ \frac{1}{n} \lbrace a_1^{-1} + a_2^{-1} + ......... + a_n^{-1} \rbrace \right]^{-1} \)
is called the harmonic mean of the numbers \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ........a_n \) and is denoted by H. The relation can be written as \( G \geq H \) Together with the relation \( A \geq G \) , we get \( A \geq G \geq H \)

Power Mean Inequality

  1. If a, b are positive reals then for 0 < m < 1.
    \( \frac{a^m + b^m}{2} \geq \left( \frac{a + b}{2} \right)^m \)
    This can be remembered as, when 0 < m < 1 then the Arithmetic mean of the \( m^{th} \) power of a and b is less than or equal to the \( m^{th} \) power of the arithmetic mean of a and b.
    The equality holds when a = b.

  2. If a, b are positive reals then for m < 0 or m > 1.
    \( \frac{a^m + b^m}{2} \geq \left( \frac{a + b}{2} \right)^m \)
    The equality holds when a = b.

Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

If a, b, c and x, y, z are any real numbers (positive, zero or negative), then
\( (ax + by + cz)^2 \leq (a^2 + b^2 + c^2) (x^2 + y^2 + z^2), \) with equality if a:b:c :: x:y:z
Remark:
If none of the numbers \( a_1,.........a_n, \ b_1,........., b_n \) be negative, the inequality can also be established as follows :
Let \( a_1^2 + a_2^2 +..... + a_n^2 = A_1^2, \ b_1^2 + b_2^2 +...... + b_n^2 = B^2 .......(1) \)
Let us now define \( x_1, \ x_2,.........., \ x_n; \ y_1, \ y_2,....., \ y_n \) by the relations
\( \begin{align} x_1 = \frac{a_1}{A},........x_n = \frac{a_n}{A}........(2) \\\\[3pt] y_1 = \frac{b_1}{B},.......,y_n = \frac{b_n}{B}.........(3) \end{align} \)
Then
\( \begin{align} \left. \begin{array}{c} x_1^2 + x_2^2 +.......+ x_n^2 = 1, \\\\ y_1^2 + y_2^2 +......+ y_n^2 = 1 \end{array} \right\rbrace \end{align} \) ..........(4)

Since the geometric mean cannot exceed the arithmetic mean,
\( \begin{align} \begin{array}{ccc} x_1 y_1 \leq \frac{x_1^2 + y_1^2}{2}, \\\\ ................. \\\\ ................. \\\\ x_n y_n \leq \frac{x_n^2 + y_n^2}{2} \end{array} \end{align} \) Adding these inequalities, we obtain
\( x_1 y_1 + x_2 y_2 +....... + x_n y_n \leq \frac{1}{2} (x_1^2 + x_2^2 +..... x_n^2) + \frac{1}{2} (y_1^2 + y_2^2 +......+ y_n^2) \)
On using (4), the above inequality yields
\( x_1 y_1 + x_2 y_2 +....... + x_n y_n \leq 1 ..........(5) \)
Substituting the values of \( x_1,.......x_n, \ y_1, \ y_2,.........y_n \) from (2) and (3), we have
\( \frac{(a_1 b_1 +...... + a_n b_n)}{(AB)} \leq 1 \)
Since AB is positive, we have
\( a_1 b_1 + ....... + a_n b_n \leq AB \)
Squaring both sides (this is permissible since each side in positive), we have
\( (a_1 b_1 +......+ a_n b_n)^2 \leq (a_1^2 +.....+ a_n^2) (b_1^2 + .......+ b_n^2) \)

Tchebychef's Inequality

If \( a_1, \ a_2, \ a_3, \ b_1, \ b_2, \ b_3 \) are any real numbers such that \( a_1 \leq a_2 \leq a_3, \ b_1 \leq b_2 \leq b_3, \) show that
\( 3(a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3) \geq (a_1 + a_2 + a_3) (b_1 + b_2 + b_3) \)
The generalisation, usually called Tchebychefs inequality , is given in the following theorem:

  1. If \( a_1,........ a_n \) and \( b_1,.......b_n \) are any real numbers, such that

    1. \( a_1 \leq ...... a_n, \ b_1 \leq ....... \leq b_n, \) then
      \( n(a_1 b_1 +......+ a_n b_n) \geq (a_1 +......+ a_n) (b_2 +..... + b_n). \)

    2. \( a_1 \geq ...... a_n, \ b_1 \leq ....... \leq b_n, \) then
      \( n(a_1 b_1 +......+ a_n b_n) \leq (a_1 +......+ a_n) (b_2 +..... + b_n). \)

  2. The inequality in (i) above can be put in the following symmetric form:
    \( \frac{a_1 b_1 + ..... + a_n b_n}{n} \geq \frac{a_1 + .... + a_n}{n}. \frac{b_1 +..... b_n}{n} \)
    This form suggests the following generalisation which we state without proof:
    If \( a_1,......., a_n; \ b_1 ,......., b_n; \ k_1 ,......., \ k_n \) are real numbers such that
    If \( a_1 \leq ....... \leq a_n, \ b_1 \leq ....... \leq b_n, \ k_1 \leq ...... \leq k_n, \) then
    \( \frac{a_1 b_1........k_1 + ..... + a_n b_n.......k_n}{n} \)
    \( \geq \frac{a_1 +...... + a_n}{n}. \frac{b_1 + .... b_n}{n}........ \frac{k_1 + ..... + k_n}{n} \)
    We shall refer to this inequality as Generalised Tchebychefs Inequality.

Trigonometric Inequalities

Solution of Trigonometric Inequality

Determination of base or fundamental interval is central to solve trigonometric inequality. The function values in this interval is repeated with a periodicity of trigonometric function. The base interval depends on the nature of trigonometric function and inequality in question. The steps to find solution of trigonometric inequality are:

  1. Convert given inequality to trigonometric equation by replacing inequality sign by equality sign.

  2. Solve resulting equation in the interval \( [0, \ 2 \pi] \) . There are two solutions. They are the angle values at which trigonometric function has the value which is being compared in the given inequality.

  3. Convert positive angle greater than \( \pi \) to equivalent negative value to account for the fact that basic interval being repeated may lie on negative side of the origin (cosine, secant and tangent function).

  4. Construct base interval between two values, keeping in mind the given inequality. It is always advantageous to draw a rough intersection of graphs of each side of given inequality.

  5. If function asymptotes (tangent, cotangent, secant and cosecant) within the interval constructed, then basic interval is limited by the angle value at which function asymptotes.

  6. Generalize solution by extending base interval with the period of the trigonometric function. In order to understand the process, let us solve the inequality given by:
    \( tan x \geq - \sqrt{3} \)
    This example has been selected here as it involves consideration of each step as enumerated above for finding solution of inequality. Corresponding trigonometric equation, in this case, is:
    \( tan x = - \sqrt{3} \)
    The acute angle is \( = \frac{ \pi}{3} \) . Further, tangent function is negative in second and fourth quarter. Using value diagram in conjunction with sign diagram, solution of given equation in \( [0, 2 \pi] \) are:

Figure Tangent function is negative in second and fourth quarter
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow x = \pi - \theta = \pi - \frac{ \pi}{3} = \frac{2 \pi}{3} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x = 2 \pi - \theta = 2 \pi - \frac{ \pi}{3} = \frac{5 \pi}{3} \end{align} \)
Here, second angle is greater than \( \pi \) . Hence, equivalent negative angle is:
\( \Rightarrow y = \frac{5 \pi}{3} - 2 \pi = - \frac{ \pi}{3} \)
Tangent function, however, is not a continuous function between \( \frac{ - \pi}{3} \) and \( \frac{2 \pi}{3} \)
Tangent values are greater than \( - \sqrt{3} \) for angle greater than \( \frac{ - \pi}{3} \) , but value asymptotes to infinity at it \( \frac{ \pi}{2} \) . This can be verified from the intersection graph.

Figure Intervals satisfying inequality, involving tangent function Thus basic interval satisfying inequality is:
\( - \frac{ \pi}{3} \leq x < \frac{ \pi}{2} \)
It is also clear that the solution in this interval is repeated with a period of \( \pi \) , which is the period of tangent function. Hence, solution of given inequality is:
\( n \pi - \frac{ \pi}{3} \leq x < n \pi + \frac{ \pi}{2}; \ n \epsilon Z \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Prove that for different a, b, c positive (a + b) (b + c) (c + a) > 8 abc

Solution:
Applying AM > GM, we have
\( \begin{align} a + b > 2 \sqrt{ab} \\\\[3pt] b + c > 2 \sqrt{bc} \\\\[3pt] c + a > 2 \sqrt{ca} \\\\[3pt] (a + b) (b + c) (c + a) > 8 \sqrt{(ab) (bc) (ca)} \\\\[3pt] (a + b) (b + c) (c + a) > 8 abc \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Question:
If a, b, c, x, y, z are all positive, prove that \( (x + y + z) \left( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z} \right) \geq 9 \)

Solution:
The AM of x, y, z is \( \frac{x + y + z}{3} \)
The GM of x, y, z is \( \sqrt[3]{xyz} \)
The AM of \( \frac{1}{x}, \ \frac{1}{y}, \ \frac{1}{z} \) is \( \frac{ \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z}}{3} \)
The GM of \( \frac{1}{x}, \ \frac{1}{y}, \ \frac{1}{z} \) is \( \sqrt[3]{ \frac{1}{xyz}} \)
Applying AM \( \geq \) GM for the first and second terms we have
\( \begin{align} \frac{x + y + z}{3} \geq \sqrt[3]{ xyz} \\\\[3pt] \frac{ \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z}}{3} \geq \sqrt[3]{ \frac{1}{xyz}} \end{align} \) Multiplying we get
\( \begin{align} \frac{(x + y + z) \left( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z} \right)}{9} \geq \sqrt[3]{xyz \frac{1}{xyz}} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (x + y + z) \left( \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y} + \frac{1}{z} \right) \geq 9 \end{align} \)

Example 3:

Question:
If a, b, c are positive prove that (1 + a) (1 + b) (1 + c) = 8, find maximum of abc

Solution:
\( 1 + a \geq 2 \sqrt{a} \) [A.M - G.M. Law]
\( 1 + b \geq 2 \sqrt{b} \)
\( 1 + b \geq 2 \sqrt{b} \)
Multiplying all above equations
\( \begin{align} (1 + a) (1 + b) (1 + c) \geq 8 \sqrt{abc} \\\\[3pt] 8 \geq 8 \sqrt{abc} \\\\[3pt] 1 \geq \sqrt{abc} \\\\[3pt] \sqrt{abc} \leq 1 \\\\[3pt] abc \leq 1 \end{align} \)
Maximum of abc is 1.

Example 4:

Question:
prove that \( (1^{2007} + 2^{2007} +...... + n^{2007}) \geq n^n (n!)^{2007} \)

Solution:
\( \begin{align} A.M \geq G.M \\\\[3pt] (1^{2007} + 2^{2007} +...... + n^{2007}) \geq n \sqrt[n]{1^{2007}. 2^{2007}.......n^{2007}} \\\\[3pt] \geq n \sqrt[n]{(n!)^{2007}} \\\\[3pt] (1^{2007} + 2^{2007} +...... + n^{2007}) \geq \left[ \left( n(n!)^{2007} \right)^{ \frac{1}{n}} \right]^n \geq n^n (n!)^{2007} \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
If a > 0, b > 0, c > 0, prove that [(1 + a) (1 + b) (1 + c)] > 8 (1 - a) (1 - b) (1 - c) where a + b + c = 1

Solution:
a + b + c = 1
1 + a + b + c = 1 + 1
1 + a = 2 - b - c
\( \begin{align} 1 + a = (1 - b) + (1 - c) \geq 2 \sqrt{(1 - b) (1 - c)} \\\\[3pt] \therefore (1 + a) \geq 2 \sqrt{(1 - b) (1 - c)}........ (1) \end{align} \)
Similarly \( (1 + b) \geq 2 \sqrt{(1 - c) (1 - a)}......(2) \)
\( \begin{align} (1 + c) \geq 2 \sqrt{(1 - a) (1 - b)}........(3) \\\\[3pt] (1) \times (2) \times (3) \\\\[3pt] (1 + a) (1 + b) (1 + c) \geq 8 (1 - a) (1 - b) (1 - c) \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
If sum of any two quantities x, y, z be together greater than third, then prove that \( (x + y + z)^3 > 27 (y + z - x) (z + x - y) (x + y - z) \)

Solution:
x + y > z (given)
(x + y - z) > 0, (z + x - y) > 0 and (y + z - x) > 0
Put y + z - x = a, z + x - y = b, x + y - z = c
\( \begin{align} z = \frac{a + b}{2}, \ x = \frac{b + c}{2}, \ y = \frac{c + a}{2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x + y + z = \frac{a + b + b + c + c + a}{2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow x + y + z = a + b + c \end{align} \)
But \( a + b = c \geq 3 \sqrt[3]{abc} \)
\( x + y + z \geq 3 \sqrt[3]{(y + z - x) (z + x - y) (x + y - z)} \)
Cubing on both sides
\( (x + y + z)^3 \geq 27 (y + z - x) (z + x - y) (x + y - z) \)

Question:
Prove that if n be a positive integer, then
\( \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{n} \right )^n < \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{n + 1} \right )^{n + 1} \)

Solution:
If \( a_1, ..... a_{n + 1} \) be positive number(not all equal), then by the inequality of the means,
we have
\( \begin{align} ( a_1, ..... a_{n + 1}){\frac{1}{(n + 1)}} < \frac{a_1 + .... + a_{n + 1}}{n + 1} ...... (1) \\\\[3pt] Putting \ a_1 = a_2 = ..... a_n = 1 + \frac{1}{n}, \ a_{n + 1} = 1 \end{align} \) ,

Raising both sides to power n + 1, we have
\( \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{n} \right )^n < \left ( 1 + \frac{1}{n + 1} \right )^{n + 1} \)

Applying power mean theorem \( (m = 4 > 1) \) .
\( \begin{align} \frac{a^4 + b^4 + c^4}{3} > \left ( \frac{a + b + c}{3} \right )^4 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ \frac{a^4 + b^4 + c^4}{3} > \left ( \frac{a + b + c}{3} \right ) \left ( \frac{a + b + c}{3} \right )^3 ........ (1) \end{align} \)

The equality holds when a = b = c.
