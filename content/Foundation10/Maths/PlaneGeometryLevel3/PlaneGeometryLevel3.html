Synopsis

Circles

The locus of a point which moves in a plane such that its distance from a fixed point is always constant is called circle.

Note

  1. The fixed point is called centre.

  2. The constant distance is called the radius

Congruent Circles

If two circles are having equal radii then they are said to be congruent to one another.

Parts of Circle

  1. Semi Circle: Each diameter of the circle divides the circle into two segments. segment is called semicircle
    Properties:

    1. The angle in semicircle is \( 90^{\circ} \)

    2. \( Area=\frac{1}{2}r^2 \)

    3. \( Perimeter=\pi r+ 2r \)

  2. Sector: A sector is a part of a circle enclosed by two radii and the are lying them.
    Properties:

    1. Semicircle is one of the sector whose angle is \( 180^{\circ} \)

    2. Circle can be divided into four equal sectors whose angle is \( 90^{\circ} \)

    3. Area of the sector = \( \frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\times \pi r^2 \) (or) \( \frac{1}{2}\times l \times t \) where l = length of the arc

    4. Length of the Arc = \( \frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\times 2 \pi r \)

    5. If the sector of the angle is \( \theta \) , then the remaining sector of the angle is \( 360^{\circ} - \theta \)

  3. Radius: The distance between the centre of the circle and any point on the circle.
    Note: A circle has unlimited number of radii.

  4. Chord: The line segment joining any two points of the circle is called a chord.
    Note: A circle has unlimited number of chord
    Properties:

    1. A chord is a line segment which lies in the interior region of a circle. The can be many chords drawn to a circle. The longest chord is called the diameter of the circle. As the chord approaches the centre, its length increased and as it moves farther from the centre, its length decreases.

    2. The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord.

    3. Conversely, the line-segment joining mid-point of a chord to the centre of the circle is perpendicular to the chord.

    4. Equal chords of a circle are equidistant from the centre.

  5. Diameter: A chord that passes through the centre of a circle is called its diameter.
    Note : A circle has unlimited number of Diameters

  6. Arc: The part of a circle that cuts off by a chord is called an arc of the circle.

  7. Segment of a Circle: A region bounded by an arc and a chord is called the segment of a circle.

  8. Alternate Segments of a circle: The minor and major segments of a circle are called the alternate segments of the circle.

  9. Quadrant of a circle: One fourth of a circle is called a quadrant

  10. Cyclic Quadrilateral: A quadrilateral ABCD is said to be cyclic if all its vertices lie or. a circle.

  11. Tangent of a circle: A line meeting a circle only in one point is called a tangent to the circle at that point. The point at which the tangent line meets the circle is called the point of contact.

Remember

  1. The radius of a circle is perpendicular to the tangent at the point of contact.

  2. Conversely, a perpendicular at the point of tangency passes through the centre of the circle.

  3. A line perpendicular to a radius at its outer end is a tangent to the circle.

  4. One and only one tangent can be drawn to the circle at a given point on the circle.

  5. Tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal in length.

  1. Secant: A line which intersects a circle in two distinct points is called a secant.

  2. Touching Circles : When two circles intersect only in one point, they are said to touch each other. The point at which they touch is called point of contact.

Theorems and Formulae

  1. Equal chords of a circle subtends equal angles at the centre.

  2. If two arcs of a circle are congruent, then the corresponding chords are equal.

  3. The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord.

  4. Two perpendicular bisectors of two chords of a circle intersect at its centre.

  5. Equal chords of a circle are equidistant from the centre.

  6. If three points are non collinear, then there is only one circle passes through that points.

  7. The angle subtended by an arc of a circle at the centre is double the angle subtended by it at any point on the remaining part of the circle.

  8. If one side of a cyclic quadrilateral is produced, then the exterior angle is equal to the interior opposite angles.

  9. Every cyclic parallelogram is a rectangle.

  10. The tangents at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.

  11. If all the sides of a parallelogram touch a circle, then the parallelogram is a rhombus.

  12. If two chords of a circle intersect inside or outside the circle when produced, the rectangle formed by the two segments of one chord is equal in area to the rectangle, formed by the two segments of the other chord.

  13. Tangent secant theorem: If PAB is a secant to a circle, intersecting the circle at A and B and PT is a tangent segment then \( PA \times PB = PT^2 \)

  14. Alternate - segment theorem: If a chord is drawn through the point of a tangent to a circle then the angles in which this chord makes with the given tangent are equal respectively to the angles formed in the corresponding alternate segments.

Cyclic Quadrilateral

A quadrilateral whose vertices are points on the circle is called a cyclic quadrilateral.

  1. Sum of the angles is \( 360^{\circ} \)

  2. The sum of the opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral is \( 180^{\circ} \)

  3. Exterior angle is equal to interior opposite angle.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
If a quadrilateral is cyclic, then sum of each pair of opposite angles is \( 180^{\circ} \) .

Solution:
ABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral with 0 is its centre.
\( \begin{align} \angle DAB = \frac{1}{2} \ reflex \ \angle DOB \\\\[3pt] \angle DCB = \frac{1}{2} \angle DOB \\\\[3pt] Hence, \angle DAB + \angle DCB = \frac{1}{2} \ (reflex \ \angle DAB + \angle DOB ) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{3} \times 360^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \\\\[3pt] i.e., \ \angle A + \angle C = 180^{\circ} \\\\[3pt] since \ \angle A + \angle B + \angle C + \angle D = 360^{\circ} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \angle B + \angle D =180^{\circ} \end{align} \)

Example 3:

Question:
Ptolemy's Theorem: In a cyclic quadrilateral the product of the diagonals is equal to the sum of the products of the pairs of opposite sides.

Solution:
We need to prove that
\( AD \cdot BD = AB \cdot CD + AD \cdot BC \)
Now, ABCD is cyclic quadrilateral choose a point on H on BD such that \( \angle DAH = \angle BAC \)
\( \angle ADH = \angle ADB = \angle ACB \)
So, \( \triangle HAD - \triangle BAC \)
Hence, \( \frac{DH}{BC} = \frac{AD}{AC} \) or \( AD \cdot BC = AC \cdot DH \) -(2)

By (1), \( \angle AHD = \angle ABC \)
Hence, \( \angle AHB = \angle ADC \) (supplementary \( \angle s \) )
Also, \( \angle ABH = \angle ABD = \angle ACD \)
\( \triangle HAB - \triangle DAC \)
Hence, \( \frac{HB}{DC}=\frac{AB}{AC} \)
i.e, \( HB \cdot AC = DC \cdot AB \) -(3)
From (2) and (3)
\( AB \cdot DC = AD \cdot BC = AC \cdot (HB + HD)= AC \cdot BD \)
Note: If the quadrialteral is a rectangle (whose diagonals are equal), then it is cyclic.

In this case Ptolemy's theorem reduces to Pythagoras theorem.
AC = BD (diagonals)
AB = CD and AD = BC
\( AC \cdot BD = AB \cdot CD + AD \cdot BC \)
\( AC^2= AB^2 + BC^2 \)

Example 6:

Question:
ABCD be a circumscribed quadrilateral, then prove that the circles inscribed in two triangles ABC and ADC touch each other.

Solution:
Let the incircle of \( \triangle{ABC} \) be \( Z_1 \) and of \( \triangle{ADC} \) be \( Z_2 \) .
Since \( Z_1 \) and \( Z_2 \) lie on either side of AC, if they touch each other, they touch at a point of AC.
Let \( Z_1 \) touch AC at P and \( Z_2 \) touch AC at Q.
PQ = AQ - AP
\( \begin{align} \frac{1}{2}(AD + AC - DC) - \frac{1}{2}(AB + AC - BC) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2}(AD + BC - AB - CD) = 0 \end{align} \)
Since, the quadrilateral is circumscribed.

Let ABCD be a quadrilateral with an incircle. The sum of the opposite sides are equal

AP = BQ
CR = CQ
DR = DS -(1)

Example 8:

Question:
The radius of a circle is 10 cm and the length of one of its chords is 12 cm. Find the distance of the chord from the centre.

Solution:
Let AB be a chord of circle with centre 0 and radius 10 cm such that AB = 12 cm. AB = 12 cm.

OM is \( \perp \) from 0 on AB. Then \( AM = \frac{1}{2} AB \) = 6 cm.
In right
\( \begin{align} \triangle OAM, OA^2 = OM^2 + AM^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow {(10)}^2= OM^2 + {(6)}^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 100 = OM^2 + 36 \Rightarrow OM^2 = 100 — 36 = 64 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow OM = \sqrt{64} = 8cm . \end{align} \)
Hence, distance of the chord from the centre is 8 cm.

Example 9:

Question:
Find the length of a chord which is at a distance of 12 cm from the centre of a circle of radius 13 cm.

Solution:

Let AB be a chord of a circle with centre O and radius 13 cm. OM is \( \perp \) from O on AB.
\( AM = \frac{1}{2} AB \) and OM= 12 cm (given)
In right
\( \begin{align} \triangle OAM, \ OM^2 + AM^2 = OA^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow {(12)}^2+ AM^2= {(13)}^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 144 + AM^2 = 169 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow AM^2 = 169 — 144 = 25 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow AM = \sqrt{25} = 5cm \\\\[3pt] AB = 2AM = 2 \times 5 cm = 10 cm. \end{align} \)

Example 10:

Question:
AB and CD are two chords of a circle such that AB = 6 cm, CD = 12 cm and AB the distance between AB and CD is 3 cm, find the radius of the circle.

Solution:

Let AB and CD be two parallel chords of a circle with centre O and radius r cm.
AB = 6 cm, CD = 12 cm.
Let \( OM \perp AB \) and \( ON \perp CD \) . Since \( AB \parallel CD \) , therefore, M, N and O are collinear.
Given that distance between AB and CD = 3cm = MN
OA = r cm, OC = r cm.
Let ON = x cm then OM = (x + 3) cm
\( AM = \frac{1}{2} AB = \frac{1}{2} \times 6cm = 3m \)
and \( CN = \frac{1}{2} CD = \frac{1}{2}\times 12cm = 6cm \)
In r.t
\( \begin{align} \triangle{AM}, \ OA^2 = AM^2 + OM^2 \ \Rightarrow \ r^2 = {(3)}^2 + {(x + 3)}^2-(1) \\\\[3pt] and \ in \ rt. \ \triangle OCN, \ OC^2 = CN^2 + ON^2 \ \Rightarrow \ r^2 = {(6)}^2 + x^2 -(2) \\\\[3pt] From \ (i) \ and \ (ii), \ {(3)}^2 + {(x + 3)}^2 = {(6)}^2 + x^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 9 + x^2 + 6x + 9 =36 + x^2 \ \Rightarrow \ 6x = 36 — 9 — 9 = 18 = x = 3 \\\\[3pt] \therefore From \ (ii), \ we \ get \ r^2 = {(6)}^2 + {(3)}^2 = 36 + 9 = 45 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ r = \sqrt{45} \\\\[3pt] or \ r = 3 \sqrt{5}cm = 3 \times 2.236 cm \\\\[3pt] = 6.708 cm = 6.71 cm. \end{align} \)
Hence, radius of the circle is 6.71 cm.

If two circles intersect, prove that the line joining the centres is the perpendicular bisector of the common chord.

AP = AQ (radii of the same circle)
BP = BQ (radii of the same circle)

\( \begin{align} \therefore \Delta APB \cong \Delta AQB (SSS) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \angle{PAB} = \angle{QAB} \\\\[3pt] i.e., \ \angle{PAM} = \angle{QAM} \ ...(i) \end{align} \)
