Progressions

Sequences which follow a definite pattern are called progressions. Eg:

  1. 1, 2, 4, 8, ......

  2. 3, -2, -7, -12, ......

Arithmetic Progressions(A.P)

Arithmetic progression is a sequence in which each term, except the first term is obtained by adding a fixed number to the term immediately preceeding it.
Eg:

  1. 3, 5, 7, 9, ......

  2. 4, 1, -2, -5 ......

This fixed number is the difference of two successive terms. It is called the common difference usually denoted by "d".

General term of an A.P.

\( \begin{align} t_n = a + (n - 1)d \\\\[3pt] t_n \rightarrow n^{th} term \\\\[3pt] a \rightarrow first \ term \\\\[3pt] d \rightarrow common \ difference \end{align} \)

The sum of the first "n" terms of an A.P.

\( \begin{align} S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n - 1)d] \ or \ S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a + l) \\\\[3pt] a \rightarrow first \ term, \ d \rightarrow common \ difference \\\\[3pt] l \rightarrow last \ term, \ S_n \rightarrow sum \ of \ the \ first \ n \ terms \end{align} \)

Important points to remember:

  1. Assume the three terms in an A.P. as a - d, a, a + d.

  2. Assume the four terms in an A.P. as a - 3d, a - d, a + d, a + 3d.

  3. Assume the five terms in an A.P. as a - 2d, a - d, a, a + d, a + 2d.

  4. Assume the six terms in an A.P. as a - 5d, a - 3d, a - d, a + d, a + 3d, a + 5d

  5. \( t_n = S_n - S_{n - 1} \)

  6. If the three terms a, b, c are in A.P. then 2b = a + c

  7. Arithmetic mean of two numbers "a" and "b" is \( \frac{a + b}{2} \)

  8. If there are "n" A.M.'s between "a" and "b" then \( d = \frac{b - a}{n + 1} \)

Important formula:

  1. Sum of the first "n" natural numbers
    \( = 1 + 2 + 3 + ..... + n = \sum n = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2} \)

  2. Sum of the squares of the "n" natural numbers
    \( = 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + ..... + n^2 = \sum n^2 = \frac{n(n + 1)(2n + 1)}{6} \)

  3. Sum of the cubes of the "n" natural numbers
    \( = 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + ..... + n^3 = \sum n^3 = \frac{n^2(n + 1)^2}{4} = \left[ \sum n \right]^2 \)

Properties of A.P.

  1. If a fixed term is added or subtracted from each term of an A.P., then the resulting series is also an A.P.

  2. If each term of an A.P. is multiplied or divided by a fixed term, then the resulting series is also an A.P.

Geometric Progressions(G.P)

G.P. is a series in which the ratio of each term(except the first) to the preceeding term is constant. This constant is called the common ratio(r).
Eg:

  1. \( \frac{-9}{2}, \ \frac{9}{4}, \ \frac{-9}{8}, \ \frac{9}{16}, ..... \)

  2. \( 0.01, \ 0.0001, \ 0.000001, ..... \)

General term of a G.P.

\( \begin{align} t_n = a.r^{n - 1} \\\\[3pt] t_n \rightarrow n^{th} term \\\\[3pt] a \rightarrow first \ term \\\\[3pt] r \rightarrow common \ ratio \end{align} \)

Important points to remember:

  1. Assume the three terms in a G.P. as \( \frac{a}{r}, \ a, \ ar \)

  2. Assume the four terms in a G.P. as \( \frac{a}{r^3}, \ \frac{a}{r}, \ ar, \ ar^3 \)

  3. Assume the five terms in G.P. as \( \frac{a}{r^2}, \ \frac{a}{r}, \ a, \ ar, \ ar^2 \)

  4. If the three terms a, b, c are in G.P., then \( b^2 = ac \)

  5. Geometric mean of two numbers "a" and "b" is \( \sqrt{ab} \)

  6. If three are "n" G.M's between "a" and "b" then \( r = \left( \frac{b}{a} \right)^{ \frac{1}{n + 1}} \)

Sum of the first "n" terms of a G.P.

\( \begin{align} S_n = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}, if r > 1 \\\\[3pt] S_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}, if r < 1 \\\\[3pt] S_n = na \ \ \ \ If \ r = 1 \\\\[3pt] S_n \rightarrow sum \ of \ ''n'' \ terms \\\\[3pt] a \rightarrow first \ term \\\\[3pt] r \rightarrow common \ ratio \end{align} \)

Sum of the infinite Geometric progression

If |r| < 1 then
Sum to infinity of the G.P. \( (S_{ \infty}) = \frac{a}{1 - r} \)

Properties of G.P.

  1. If every term of a G.P. is multiplied or divided by a fixed term, then the resulting series is also a G.P.

  2. If every term of a G.P. is raised to the same power, the resulting series is also a G.P.

  3. The reciprocals of a G.P. is also a G.P.

Harmonic Progressions(H.P)

A progression is said to be Harmonic Progression, if the reciprocals of these terms form an arithmetic progression.
Eg:
\( \frac{1}{3}, \ \frac{1}{5}, \ \frac{1}{7}, \ \frac{1}{9}, ..... \) is a Harmonic Progression since their reciprocals 3, 5, 7, 9,..... are in A.P.

General term of an H.P.

\( \begin{align} t_n = \frac{1}{a + (n - 1)d} \\\\[3pt] Where, \ t_n \rightarrow n^{th} term \\\\[3pt] \frac{1}{a} \rightarrow first \ term \end{align} \)

Important points to remember:

  1. If a, b, c are in H.P. then \( \frac{1}{a}, \ \frac{1}{b}, \ \frac{1}{c} \) are in A.P.

  2. If a, b, c are in H.P. then \( b = \frac{2ac}{a + c} \)

  3. Harmonic mean between "a" and "b" is \( \frac{2ab}{a + b} \)

Relationship between three progressions

If A, G, H are the arithmetic mean, geometric mean, and harmonic mean of two positive nimbers "a" and "b", then

  1. \( G^2 = A.H. \)

  2. \( A = \frac{a + b}{2} , \ G = \sqrt{ab}, \ H = \frac{2ab}{a + b} \)

  3. \( A \geq G \geq H \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
If the first term and common difference in an A.P are 8 and -1 respectively, then find:

  1. General term

  2. The progression

  3. The 10th term

  4. The expression for sum to n terms and hence sum to 10 terms

Solution:
Given the first term \Rightarrow a = 8 and
The common difference \Rightarrow d = -1

  1. The nth term of an A.P. \( \Rightarrow t_n = a + (n - 1)d \)
    Substituting a and d in \( t_n \) .
    \( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_n = 8 + (n - 1)(-1) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t_n = 8 - n + 1 = 9 - n \end{align} \)

  2. By substituting n = 1, 2, 3, ... in the general term \( t_n = 9 - n \) we can generate the arithmetic progression
    \( \begin{align} n = 1 \Rightarrow t_1 = 9 - 1 = 8 \\\\[3pt] n = 2 \Rightarrow t_2 = 9 - 2 = 7 \\\\[3pt] n = 3 \Rightarrow t_3 = 9 - 3 = 6 \\\\[3pt] n = 4 \Rightarrow t_4 = 9 - 4 = 5 \end{align} \)
    Hence the progression is 8, 7, 6, 5,....

  3. The 10th term of the A.P. can be calculated by substituting n = 10 in the nth term \( \Rightarrow t_{10} = 9 - 10 = -1 \)

  4. The sum to 'n' terms of an A.P. \( = S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n - 1)d] \)
    Substituting 'a' and 'd' in \( S_n \) we get
    \( \begin{align} S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2 \times 8 + (n - 1)(-1)] = \frac{n}{2}[16 - (n - 1)] \\\\[3pt] = \frac{n}{2}[16 + n + 1] = \frac{n}{2}[16 - (n - 1)] \\\\[3pt] = \frac{n}{2}[17 - n] \end{align} \)
    We get the sum to 10 terms by substituting n = 10 is the above expression
    \( \Rightarrow S_{10} = \frac{10}{2}[17 - 10] = 35 \)

Example 2:

Question:
If the first term and common ratio in an G.P are 1 and \( \sqrt{3} \) respectively, find:

  1. The general term

  2. The progression

  3. The 12th term

  4. The expression for sum to n terms and hence sum to 10 terms

Solution:
Given the first term \Rightarrow a = 1 and
The common ratio \( \Rightarrow r = \sqrt{3} \)

  1. The nth term of an G.P.
    \( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_n = ar^{n - 1} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t_n = 1 \times [3]^{n - 1} = [ \sqrt{3}]^{n - 1} \end{align} \) .

  2. By substituting n = 1, 2, 3, ... in the general term \( t_n = [ \sqrt{3}]^{n - 1} \) we can generate the geometric progression
    \( \begin{align} n = 1 \Rightarrow t_1 = [ \sqrt{3}]^{1 - 1} = 1 \\\\[3pt] n = 2 \Rightarrow t_2 = [ \sqrt{3}]^{2 - 1} = \sqrt{3} \\\\[3pt] n = 3 \Rightarrow t_3 = [ \sqrt{3}]^{3 - 1} = 3 \\\\[3pt] n = 4 \Rightarrow t_4 = [ \sqrt{3}]^{4 - 1} = 3 \sqrt{3} \end{align} \)
    Hence the progression is \( 1, \ \sqrt{3}, \ 3, \ 3 \sqrt{3}, ..... \)

  3. The 12th term of the G.P. can be calculated by substituting n = 12 in the nth term of the G.P.
    \( \Rightarrow t_{12} = [ \sqrt{3}]^{12 - 1} = [ \sqrt{3}]^{11} = 24 \ 3 \sqrt{3} \)

  4. The sum to 'n' terms of an G.P. \( = S_n = \frac{a[r^n - 1]}{r - 1} \)
    Substituting 'a' and 'r' in \( S_n \) we get
    \( S_n = \frac{1[( \sqrt{3})^n - 1]}{[ \sqrt{3} - 1]} = \frac{[ \sqrt{3}]^n - 1}{[ \sqrt{3} - 1]} \)
    We get the sum to 10 terms by substituting n = 10 is the above expression
    \( \Rightarrow S_{10} = \frac{[\sqrt{3}]^{10} - 1}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} = \frac{242}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} = 121[ \sqrt{3} + 1] \)

Example 4:

Question:
Find the

  1. first term and common difference

  2. general term

  3. sum to 'n' terms and hence sum to 10 terms of the A.P., x - 2b, x + 2b, x + 6b, ....

Solution:
Given the A.P., x - 2b, x + 2b, x + 6b,....

  1. The first term \( = t_1 = a = x - 2b \)
    Common difference \( = d = t_2 - t_1 = x + 2b - (x - 2b) = 4b \)

  2. The general term of the A.P., \( t_n = a + (n - 1)d, \)
    Substituting 'a' and 'd' in \( t_n \)
    \( \Rightarrow t_n = [x - 2b] + [n - 1]4b = x - 6b + 4nb \)

  3. Sum to 'n' terms of an A.P. \( = S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n - 1)d] \)
    Substituting a and d in \( S_n \)
    \( \begin{align} \Rightarrow S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2(x - 2b) + (n - 1)4b] \\\\[3pt] = n[x - 2b + (n - 1)2b] = n[x + 2nb - 4b] ......(1) \end{align} \)
    Substituting n = 10 in (1) we get,
    \( S_{10} = 10x + 160b \)

Question:
Find the

  1. The first term

  2. Common ratio

  3. General term

  4. Sum to 'n' terms of the G.P. \( \frac{x}{y}, \ \frac{1}{x}, \ \frac{y}{x^3} \)

  3. General term of the G.P. \( = t_n = ar^{n - 1} \)
    Substituting 'a' nd 'r' in \( t_n \)

Example 6:

Question:
Find the sum to infinity of the G.P. \( \frac{-3}{4}, \ \frac{3}{16}, \ \frac{-3}{64},...... \)

Solution:
From the given geometric progression the first term \( \Rightarrow a = \frac{-3}{4} \)
Common ratio \( \Rightarrow r = \frac{t_2}{t_1} = \frac{ \frac{3}{16}}{- \frac{3}{4}} = \frac{3}{16} \times \frac{4}{-3} = - \frac{1}{4} \)
The sum to infinity of a G.P. \( = S_{ \infty} = \frac{a}{1 - r} \)
By substituting the values of 'a' and 'r' in the formula, we get,
\( S_{ \infty} = \frac{ \frac{-3}{4}}{1 - \left[- \frac{1}{4} \right]} = \frac{ \frac{-3}{4}}{1 + \frac{1}{4}} = \frac{ \frac{-3}{4}}{ \frac{5}{4}} `\frac{-3}{4} \times \frac{4}{5} = \frac{-3}{5} \)

Example 7:

Question:
If the sum of 'n' terms of an A.P. is \( 2n + 3n^2 \) , generate the progression and find the \( n^{th} \) term.

Solution:
Given \( S_n = 2n + 3n^2 \)
Substitute,
\( \begin{align} n = 1 \Rightarrow S_1 = 2(1) + 3(1)^2 = 5 \\\\[3pt] n = 2 \Rightarrow S_2 = 2(2) + 3(2)^2 = 16 \\\\[3pt] n = 3 \Rightarrow S_3 = 2(3) + 3(3)^2 = 33 \\\\[3pt] n = 4 \Rightarrow S_4 = 2(4) + 3(4)^2 = 56 \\\\[3pt] S_1 = Sum \ of \ 1^{st} \ term \ is \ nothing \ but \ the \ first \ term \ itself \ (t_1) \\\\[3pt] S_2 = Sum \ of \ first \ two \ terms \ t_1 and t_2 \\\\[3pt] S_3 = Sum \ of \ first \ three \ terms \ t_1, \ t_2 \ and \ t_3 \\\\[3pt] S_4 = Sum \ of \ first \ four \ terms \ t_1, \ t_2, \ t_3 \ and \ t_4 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow S_1 = t_1 = 5 \\\\[3pt] S_2 = t_1 + t_2 = 16 \\\\[3pt] S_3 = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 = 33 \\\\[3pt] S_4 = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + t_4 = 56 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow S_2 - S_1 = [t_1 + t_2] - t_1 = 16 - 5 = 11 = t_2 \\\\[3pt] S_3 - S_2 = [t_1 + t_2 + t_3] - [t_1 + t_2] = 33 - 16 = 17 = t_3 \end{align} \)
Hence the sequence is 5, 11, 17, ....
where a = 5 and d = 6
The general term
\( \begin{align} t_n = a + (n - 1)d \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t_n = 5 + (n - 1)6 = 6n - 1 \end{align} \)

Question:
If the sum of 'n' terms of an G.P. is \( 5(2^n - 1) \) , generate the progression and find the general term.

Given \( S_n = 5(2^n - 1) \)

\( \begin{align} n = 1 \Rightarrow S_1 = 5(2^1 - 1) = 5 \\\\[3pt] n = 2 \Rightarrow S_2 = 5(2^2 - 1) - 15 \\\\[3pt] n = 3 \Rightarrow S_3 = 5(2^3 - 1) = 35 \\\\[3pt] n = 4 \Rightarrow S_4 = 5(2^4 - 1) = 75 \\\\[3pt] S_1 = Sum \ of \ 1^{st} \ term \ is \ nothing \ but \ the \ first \ term \ itself \ (t_1) \\\\[3pt] S_2 = Sum \ of \ first \ two \ terms \ t_1 and \ t_2 \\\\[3pt] S_3 = Sum \ of \ first \ three \ terms \ t_1, \ t_2 \ and \ t_3 \\\\[3pt] S_4 = Sum \ of \ first \ four \ terms \ t_1, \ t_2, \ t_3 \ and \ t_4 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow S_1 = t_1 = 5 \\\\[3pt] S_2 = t_1 + t_2 = 15 \\\\[3pt] S_3 = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 = 35 \\\\[3pt] S_4 = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 + t_4 = 75 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow S_2 - S_1 = [t_1 + t_2] - t_1 = 15 - 5 = 10 = t_2 \\\\[3pt] S_3 - S_2 = [t_1 + t_2 + t_3] - [t_1 + t_2] = 35 - 15 = 20 = t_3 \end{align} \)
Hence the sequence is 5, 10, 20, ....
where a = 5 and r = 2
The general term
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_n = ar^{n - 1} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t_5 = 5 \times (2)^{n - 1} \end{align} \)

Example 9:

Question:
Which term in the A.P. 5, 2, -1,.... is -22? Find the sum of these terms.

Solution:
In the given A.P. the first term \Rightarrow a = 5 and
the common difference \Rightarrow d = 2 - 5 = -3
Let the \( n^{th} \) term of A.P. be -22
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t _n = a + (n - 1)d = -22 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 5 + (n - 1)(-3) = -22 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 5 + (-3n + 3) = -22 \Rightarrow 8 - 3n = -22 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 3n = 22 + 8 \Rightarrow n = \frac{30}{3} = 10 \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) The \( 10^{th} \) term of the A.P is -22.
Substituting a = 5, d = -3, and n = 10 in the formula,
\( S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n - 1)d] \) we get the sum of the 10 terms
\( \Rightarrow S_{10} = \frac{10}{2}[2 \times 5 + (10 - 1)(-3)] = -85 \)

Example 10:

Question:
Which term of the G.P. \( \sqrt{2}, \ \sqrt{6}, \ 3 \sqrt{2}, \ 3 \sqrt{6},...... is \ 243 \sqrt{3} \) ? Find the sum of these terms.

Solution:
In the given G.P., the first term \( \Rightarrow a = \sqrt{2} \) and
The common ratio \( = r = \frac{ \sqrt{6}}{ \sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{3}. \)
Let the \( n^{th} \) term of the G.P. be \( 243 \sqrt{2} \)
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_n = ar^{n - 1} = 243 \sqrt{2} \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{2}][ \sqrt{3}]^{n - 1} = 243 \sqrt{2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{3}]^{n - 1} = 243 \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{3}]^{n - 1} = [ \sqrt{3}]^{10} \Rightarrow n - 1 = 10 \Rightarrow n = 11 \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) The \( 11^{th} \) term of the G.P. is \( 243 \sqrt{2}. \)
Substituting \( a = \sqrt{2}, \ r = \sqrt{3} \) and n = 11 in the formula
\( S_n = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}. \) We get the sum of the 11 terms
\( \Rightarrow S_{11} = \frac{ \sqrt{2}(( \sqrt{3})^{11}) - 1}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} = \frac{\sqrt{2}[3^5 \sqrt{3} - 1]}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} \Rightarrow \frac{\sqrt{2}[243 \sqrt{2} - 1]}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} \)

Example 11:

Question:
How many odd integers beginning will 15 must be taken for their sum to be equal to 975?

Solution:
The odd intergers beginning with 15 are as follows 15, 17, 19,....
This forms an A.P. with first term, a = 15 and the common difference, d = 17 - 15 = 2
Let 'n' terms of the A.P be taken to make the sum 975
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow 15 + 17 + 19 + .....n \ terms = 975 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a + (n - 1)d] = 975 \end{align} \)
Substituting the value of 'a' and 'd' in \( S_n \)
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \frac{n}{2}[2 \times 15 + (n - 1)2] = 975 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 15n + (n - 1)n = 975 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 15n + n^2 - n = 975 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n^2 + 14n - 975 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n^2 + 39n - 25n - 975 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n(n + 39) - 25(n + 39) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (n - 25)(n + 39) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n = 25 \ or \ n = -39 \end{align} \)
But n = -39 is rejected since number of terms cannot be negative
\( \therefore \) Number of odd integers beginning with 15 to make the sum equal to 975 = 25.

Example 12:

Question:
How many terms of the geometricic sequence \( \sqrt{3}, \ 3, \ 3 \sqrt{3}.... \) must be taken to make the sum \( 39 + 13 \sqrt{3} \) ?

Solution:
The sum of the terms is \( \sqrt{3} + 3 + 3 \sqrt{3} +..... \) which forms a G.P. with the first term \( \Rightarrow a = \sqrt{3} \)
and the common ratio \( \Rightarrow r = \frac{t_2}{t_1} = \frac{3}{ \sqrt{3}} = \sqrt{3} \)
Let the 'n' terms of the G.P. be taken to make the sum \( 39 + 13 \sqrt{3} \)
\( \Rightarrow S_n = \sqrt{3} + 3 + 3 \sqrt{3} + ..... = 39 + 13 \sqrt{3} \)
Substituting the values of 'a' and 'r' in \( S_n \)
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \frac{\sqrt{3}[( \sqrt{3})^n - 1]}{ \sqrt{3} - 1} = 39 + 13 \sqrt{3} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \sqrt{3}[( \sqrt{3})^n - 1] = [39 + 13 \sqrt{3}][ \sqrt{3} - 1] \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \sqrt{3}[( \sqrt{3})^n - 1] = 39 \sqrt{3} - 39 + 39 - 13 \sqrt{3} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow ( \sqrt{3})^{n + 1} - \sqrt{3} = 26 \sqrt{3} \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{3}]^{n + 1} = 27 \sqrt{3} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{3}]^{n + 1} = [ \sqrt{3}]^6. \sqrt{3} \Rightarrow [ \sqrt{3}]^{n + 1} = [ \sqrt{3}]^7 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n + 1 = 7 \Rightarrow n = 6 \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) The number of terms required to make the sum \( 39 + 13 \sqrt{3} = 6. \)

Example 13:

Question:
Find the value of 'k' if 2k + 7, 6k - 2, 8k - 4 are in A.P. Also find the sequence.

Solution:
Given that 2k + 7, 6k - 2 and 8k - 4 are in A.P. the difference between successive terms in an A.P is same.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_2 - t_1 = t_3 - t_2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow [6k - 2] - [2k + 7] = [8k - 4] - [6k - 2] \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 4k - 9 = 2k - 2 \Rightarrow 2k = 7 \Rightarrow k = \frac{7}{2} \end{align} \)
Substituting the value of 'k' in 2k + 7, 6k - 2, 8k - 4 we get,
\( 2 \times \frac{7}{2} + 7, \ 6 \times \frac{7}{2} - 2, \ 8 \times \frac{7}{2} - 4 \ i.e, \ 14, \ 19, \ 24 \)
\( \therefore \) The sequence is 14, 19, 24,.....

Example 14:

Question:
Find the value of 'p' if the sum to infinity of \( P, \ 1, \ \frac{1}{p},..... is \ \frac{25}{4} \)

Solution:
Given that the sum of the terms \( P, \ 1, \ \frac{1}{p},.....\infty \ is \ \frac{25}{4} \) the first term \Rightarrow a = p, then common difference \( \Rightarrow r = \frac{1}{p} \ and \ S_{ \infty} = \frac{25}{4} \)
The sum to infinity of a G.P \( = S_{ \infty} = \frac{a}{1 - r} \)
Substituting 'a', 'r' and \( S_{ \infty} \) in the above equation.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \frac{25}{4} = \frac{p}{1 - \frac{1}{p}} \Rightarrow \frac{25}{4} = \frac{p^2}{p - 1} \Rightarrow 4p^2 = 25(p - 1) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 4p^2 - 25p + 25 = 0 \Rightarrow 4p^2 - 20p - 5p + 25 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 4p(p - 5) - 5(p - 5) = 0 \Rightarrow [4p - 5][p - 5] = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow p = \frac{5}{4} \ or \ 5 \end{align} \)

Example 15:

This can be done by subtracting equation (1) from (2) where the variable 'a' is eliminated as follows.
\( \begin{align} (2) - (1) = (a + 18d) - (a + 7d) = 39 - 17 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 11d = 22 ......(3) \end{align} \)
From equation(3). It is an equation in single variable 'd' and can be found out very easily.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow 11d = 22 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow d = 2 \end{align} \)
We can find the value of 'a' by substituting the value of 'd' in equation(1)
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow a + 7d = 17 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a + 7 \times 2 = 17 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a = 3 \end{align} \)

\( \begin{align} t_n = a + (n - 1)d \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t_{25} = 3 + (25 - 1)2 = 51 \\\\[3pt] \therefore t_{25} \ of \ the \ A.P. \ is \ 51. \end{align} \)

Example 17:

Question:
The number of terms in A.P is even. The sum of odd and even numbers are 24 and 30, respectively. If the last term exceeds the first term by 10.5, the number of terms in the A.P is _____.

Solution:
Let the number of terms in the A.P be 'n' the two conditions given in the problem are:
The sum of the odd numbered terms in an A.P is \( S_o = 24 \) and the sum of even numbered terms is \( S_e = 30 \)
The last terms exceeds first term by 10.5.
We know that the general representation of an A.P is
a, a + d, a + 2d, a + 3d,....
Sum of odd numbers
\( \begin{align} S_o = t_1 + t_3 + t_5 + t_7 + ...... = 24 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a + a + 2d + a + 4d + ........ = 24 .......(1) \end{align} \)
This is in A.P with a common difference '2d' and there will be \( \frac{n}{2} \) terms in the above A.P since we have even number of terms in the A.P.
Similarly the sum of even numbered terms,
\( \begin{align} S_e = t_2 + t_4 + t_6 + t_8 + ...... = 30 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a + d + a + 3d + a + 5d + ........ = 30 .......(2) \end{align} \)
This will be in A.P with a common difference 2d and there will be \( \frac{n}{2} \) terms in the above A.P since we have even number of terms in the A.P.
Subtracting (1) from (2),
i.e, [a + d + a + 3d + a + 5d + ....] - [a + a + 2d + a + 4d +......] = 30 - 24
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \left[d + d + d + .... \frac{n}{2} \ terms \right] = 6 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{n}{2} \times d = 6 \Rightarrow nd = 12..........(3) \end{align} \)
The other condition given in the problem is that the last term exceeds the first term by 10.5. Let \( n^{th} \) term be the last term.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow t_n - t_1 = 10.5 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a + (n - 1)d - a = 10.5 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (n - 1)d = 10.5.......(4) \end{align} \)
With the two conditions in the problem, we got two equations (3) and (4). On solving these equations we can find the variables 'd' and 'n'. i.e., subtracting (4) and (3), we get
\( \begin{align} nd - (n - 1)d = 12 - 10.5 \Rightarrow nd - nd + d = 1.5 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow d = 1.5 \\\\[3pt] \therefore n = \frac{12}{1.5} = 8[using(3)] \end{align} \)
Number of terms in A.P is 8

Example 18:

Question:
In a G.P of even number of distinct terms, the sum of all terms is 5 times the sum of odd terms. Then the common ratio of G.P is _____.

Solution:
The sum of all the terms in the G.P is,
\( \begin{align} S = a + ar + ar^2 + ar^3 + ....... + ar^n = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} .......(1) \\\\[3pt] Sum \ of \ odd \ terms \ S_o = t_1 + t_3 + t_5 + ....... +t_{n - 1} \\\\[3pt] = a + ar^2 + ar^4 +.......+ ar^{n - 2} \ is \ also \ in \ G.P. \end{align} \)
With the first term, \( t_1 = a \) and common ratio \( = r^2 \) and there will be \( \frac{n}{2} \) terms, since there are even number of distinct terms.
\( \therefore S_o = \frac{a \left[(r^2)^{ \frac{n}{2}} - 1 \right]}{r^2 - 1} = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r^2 - 1}.....(2) \)
Given condition in the problem is \( S_n = 5S_o \)
Using (1) and (2),
\( \begin{align} \frac{a[r^n - 1]}{r - 1} = \frac{5a[r^n - 1]}{r^2 - 1} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r^2 - 1 = 5(r - 1) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r^2 - 1 = 5r - 5 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r^2 - 5r + 4 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r^2 - 4r - r + 4 = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r(r - 4) - 1(r - 4) = 0 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r = 1 \ or \ 4 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r \neq 1, \ as \ all \ the \ terms \ will \ become \ equal \ if \ r = 1 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r = 4 \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) The common ratio of the G.P is '4'.

Example 21:

Question:
If (b + c), (c + a) and (a + b) are in H.P. then
S.T. \( \frac{1}{a^2}, \ \frac{1}{b^2}, \ \frac{1}{c^2} \) will also be in H.P.

Solution:
Given that,
(b + c), (c + a) and (a + b) are in H.P.
\( \frac{1}{b + c}, \ \frac{1}{c + a}, \ \frac{1}{a + b} \) are in A.P.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{c + a} - \frac{1}{b + c} = \frac{1}{a + b} - \frac{1}{c + a} \\\\[3pt] \frac{b + c - c - a}{(c + a)(b + c)} = \frac{c + a - a - b}{(a + b)(c + a)} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{(b - a)}{b + c} = \frac{c - b}{a + b} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow ab - a^2 + b^2 - ab = bc + c^2 - b^2 - bc \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow b^2 - a^2 = c^2 - b^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 2b^2 = a^2 + c^2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow a^2, \ b^2, \ c^2 \ are \ in \ A.P. \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \frac{1}{a^2}, \ \frac{1}{b^2}, \ \frac{1}{c^2} \ are \ in \ H.P. \end{align} \)

Example 22:

Question:
If \( a^2, \ b^2, \ c^2 \) are in A.P, show that b + c, c + a, a + b are in H.P.

Solution:
By adding ab + ac + bc to each term, we see that
\( a^2 + ab + ac + bc, \ b^2 + ab + bc + ac, \ c^2 + ca + cb + ab \) are in A.P.
that is (a + b)(a + c), (b + c)(b + a), (c + a)(c + b) are in A.P.
\( \therefore \) Dividing each term by (a + b)(b + c)(c + a),
\( \frac{1}{b + c}, \ \frac{1}{c + a}, \ \frac{1}{a + b} \) are in A.P.
that is, b + c, c + a, a + b are in H.P.
