Introduction

Reasoning can be classified into two categories.

  1. Inductive reasoning

  2. Deductive reasoning

Inductive Reasoning

Here, the conclusion is made on the basis of a particular observation or experience.
For example: A person touches an electric wire carrying current, gets shocked. Then, he forbades others to do so, on behalf of his experience.

Deductive Reasoning

Here, conclusions are drawn from assumptions, rather than experience. These assumptions are proved mathematically.
For example: By drawing a number of circles and measuring their circumferences with the help of thread, he finds that the length of circumference is \( 2 \pi r \)
Conjecture: Induction begins by observations. And from observations we arrive at some tentative conclusions, called conjectures. A conjecture may be true or false. The principles of mathematical induction helps us in proving conjectures.
For example:

  1. n(n + 1) is divisible by 2

  2. \( 3^{2n} - 1 \) is divisible by 8.

Inductive Method

A person measures the angles of triangles and he finds that the sum of the angles of a triangle is \( 180^{\circ} \) . The experimenter concludes that, "Sum of the angles of a triangle is always equal to \( 180^{\circ} \) ". Thus, he arrived at a generalisation from particular cases. This is called an inductive method.

Deductive Method

We know that the sum of n odd natural numbers is \( n^2 \) . This is a general result. From this general result we deduce a particular case that the sum of first 10 odd natural numbers is \( 10^2 = 100 \) . This process of deducting particular result from general result is called the process of deduction.
Statement : A sentence is called a statement, if it can be adjudged as true or false.
For example:

  1. "3 divides 6" is a statement, because it is true.

  2. "Shahrukh Khan is an actor" is a statement, as it is true.

  3. "There are 6 days in a week" is a statement, as it is false.

A sentence, which can be both true and false, cannot be a statement.
e.g.:

  1. The water is cold.

  2. The boy is tall

Here, the above sentences can be both true and false, hence they are not statements.

Mathematical Statement

A statement having mathematical relations is known as the mathematical statement.

For example: Following are the mathematical statements.

  1. "35 is a multiple of 5"

  2. (x + 1) is a factor of \( x^2 - 3x + 2 \)

  3. \( A \cap B = B \cap A \)

Principle of Mathematical Induction

Consider the set N of natural numbers. N has two characteristic properties contained in the axiom of induction :

  1. N contains the natural number 1.

  2. N is closed with respect to addition of 1 to each of its members.

Thus to determine whether a set K consisting of natural numbers is set of all natural numbers, we have to verify the following two conditions on K:

  1. Does 1 \( \epsilon \) K ?

  2. For each natural number k \( \epsilon \) K , is it true that k + 1 \( \epsilon \) K ?
    When the answer to both the questions is 'yes', then (and then only) K is N.

The above test has several important consequences. It gives (at least three important principles for establishing the truth of certain classes of propositions. The first of these is the following:

First Principle of Induction

Let \( \{ T(n) : n \epsilon N \} \) be a set of statements, one for each natural number n. If T(1) is true and the truth of T(k) implies that of T(k + 1) then T(n) is true for all n.

Example:
Let T(k) be the statement
\( 1 + 3 + 5 + ..... + (2k - 1) = k^2+ 10. \)
Show that T(k) is true \( \Rightarrow \) T(k + 1) is true. Can we conclude by applying FPI that T(n) is true for all n?

Solution:
Assume that T(k) is true
Then [1 + 3 +.....+ (2k - 1)] + (2k + 1)
\( \begin{align} = (k^2 + 10) + (2k + 1) \\\\[12pt] = (k + 1)^2 + 10 \end{align} \)
so that T(k + 1) is true.
We have proved that for the given statement, the truth of T(k) implies that of T(k + 1). But what about T(1)? Obviously, T(1) is not true. Hence we cannot conclude by applying FPI that T(n) is true for all n. In fact, in this case T(n) is not true for any n.

Second Principle of Induction

Let \( \{ T(n) : n \epsilon N \} \) be a set of statements, one for each natural number n. If (i) T(1) is true, and (ii) if for each natural number k, the truth of T(m) for all m < k implies the truth of T(k), then T(n) is true for all n.

Third Principle of Induction

Let \( \{ (T(n) : n \epsilon N \} \) be a set of statements, one for each natural number n. If (i) T(a) is true for some a \( \epsilon \) N and (ii) T(k) is true implies T(k + 1) is true for all \( k \geq a \) , then T(n) is true for all \( n \geq a \) .
Working rule to prove that a statement is true for all natural numbers using Principle of Mathematical Induction:
Step 1: Show that the given statement is true for n = 1.
Step 2: Assume that the statement is true for n = k.
Step 3: Using the assumption made in step 2, show that the statement is true for n = k + 1.
Step 4: This completes the Principle of Mathematical Induction.
Thus, we conclude that the given statement is true for all \( n \epsilon N \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Let P(n) be the statement \( '3^n > n' \) . If P(n) is true, prove that P(n + 1) is true.

Solution:
We are given that P(n) is true i.e., \( 3^n > n \) , and we wish to prove that P(n + 1) is true
i.e., \( 3^{(n + 1)} > (n + 1) \) .
Now, P(n) is true \( \Rightarrow 3^n > n \Rightarrow 3.3^n > 3 . n \) [Multiplying both sides by 3]
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow 3^{n + 1} > n + 2n \\\\[12pt] \Rightarrow 3^{n + 1} > n + 1 \end{align} \)
[ 2n > 1 for every n \( \epsilon N \Rightarrow \) 2n + n > n + 1 for every n \( \epsilon \) N ]
\( \Rightarrow \) P(n + 1) is true.

Example 2:

Question:
If P(n) is the statement \( '2^{3n} - 1 \) is an integral multiple of 7', and if P(r) is true, prove that P(r + 1) is true.

Solution:
Let P(r) be true. Then, \( 2^{3r} - 1 \) is an integral multiple of 7. We wish to prove that P(r + 1) is true i.e.,
\( 2^{3(r + 1)} — 1 \) is an integral multiple of 7. Now,
P(r) is true \( \Rightarrow 2^{3r} — 1 \) is an integral multiple of 7
\( 2^{3r} — 1 = 7 \lambda, \) for some \( \lambda \epsilon N \)
\( 2^{3r} = 7 \lambda + 1 \)
Now, \( 2^{3(r + 1)} — 1 = 2^{3r}.2^3 — 1 = (7 \lambda + 1) \times 8 — 1 \)
\( \begin{align} = 56 \lambda + 8 - 1 = 56 \lambda + 7 = 7( 8 \lambda + 1) \\\\[12pt] = 7 \mu, \ where \ \mu = 8 \lambda + 1 \epsilon N \\\\[12pt] \Rightarrow 2^{3(r + 1)} - 1 \end{align} \)
is an integral part of 7 \( \Rightarrow \) P(r + 1) is true

Example 3:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that for all \( n \epsilon N \) .
\( n^2 + n \) is an even natural number

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement ' \( n^2 + n \) is even'.
Step - I: We have, \( P(n): n^2 + n \) is even
\( 1^2 + 1 = 2 \) which is even
\( \therefore \) P(1) is true
Step-II: Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( m^2 + m \) is even \( \Rightarrow m^2 + m = 2 \lambda \) , for some \( \lambda \epsilon N .....(i) \)
Now we shall show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we have to show that \( (m + 1)^2 + (m + 1) \) is an even natural number.
We have,
\( (m + 1)^2 + (m + 1) = (m^2 + 2m + 1) + (m + 1) = (m^2 + m) + (2m + 2) \)
\( \begin{align} = m^2 + m + 2 (m + 1) = 2 \lambda + 2(m + 1) [Using (i)] \\\\[12pt] = 2(\lambda + m + 1) = 2 \mu, \ where \ \mu = \lambda + m + 1 \epsilon N \end{align} \)
\( \Rightarrow (m + 1)^2 + (m + 1) \) is an even natural number
\( \Rightarrow P(m + 1) \) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, P(n) is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) i.e, \( n^2 + n \) is even for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 4:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that n(n + 1)(2n + 1) is divisible by 6 for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement 'n(n + 1)(2n + 1) is divisible by 6'
i.e., P(n) : n(n + 1)(2n + 1) is divisible by 6
Step - I: We have P(1) : 1(1 + 1) (2 + 1) is divisible by 6.
\( \therefore \) 1(1 + 1) (2 + 1) = 6 which is divisible by 6
P(1) is true.
Step - II: Let P(m) be true. Then, m(m + 1) (2m + 1) is divisible by 6.
\( \Rightarrow m(m + 1) (2m + 1) = 6 \lambda \) , for some \( \lambda \epsilon N......(i) \)
Now, we shall show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we have to show that (m + 1) (m + 1 + 1) [2(m + 1) + 1] is divisible by 6.
We have, (m + 1) (m + 1 + 1) [2(m + 1) + 1] = (m + 1) (m + 2) [(2m + 1) + 2]
=(m + 1)(m + 2)(2m + 1) + 2(m + 1)(m + 2)
= m(m + 1)(2m + 1) + 2(m + 1)(2m + 1) + 2(m + 1)(m + 2)
= m(m + 1)(2m + 1) + 2(m + 1)(2m + 1 + m + 2)
= m(m + 1) (2m + 1) + 2 (m + 1) (3m + 3)
\( = m(m + 1) (2m + 1) + 6 (m + 1)^ 2 = 6 \lambda + 6 (m + 1)^2 \) [Using (i)]
\( = 6[ \lambda + (m + 1)^2] \) , which is divisible by 6 \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true.
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the given statement is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 5:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that for all \( n \epsilon N \)
1 + 4 + 7 + ....... + (3n - 2) = \( \frac{1}{2} \) n(3n - 1)

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by,
\( P(n): 1 + 4 + 7 + ...... + (3n - 2) = \frac{1}{2} n(3n - 1) \)
step - I : we have P(1) : 1 = \( \frac{1}{2} \times (1) \times (3 \times 1 - 1) \)
since 1 = \( \frac{1}{2} \times (1) \times (3 \times 1 - 1) \)
so, P(1) is true
step - II : Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( 1 + 4 + 7 + ...... + (3m - 2) = \frac{1}{2} m(3m - 1) \)
we wish to show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we have to show that,
1 + 4 + 7 + ..... + (3m - 2) + [3(m + 1) - 2] = \( \frac{1}{2} \) (m + 1) [3(m + 1) - 1]
Now, 1 + 4 + 7 + ..... + (3m - 2) + [3(m + 1) - 2]
\( = \frac{1}{2} m (3m - 1) + [3 (m + 1) - 2] \) [using (i)]
\( \begin{align} = \frac{1}{2} m (3m - 1) + (3m + 1) = \frac{1}{2} [ 3m^2 - m + 6m + 2] \\\\[12pt] = \frac{1}{2} [ 3m^2 + 5m + 2] = \frac{1}{2} (m + 1) (3m + 2) = \frac{1}{2} (m + 1)[3 (m + 1) - 1] \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) P(m + 1) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true.
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the given result is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 6:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that for all \( n \epsilon N \)
\( \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ....... + \frac{1}{n (n + 1)} = \frac{n}{n + 1} \)

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by,
\( P(n) : \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ....... + \frac{1}{n (n + 1)} = \frac{n}{n + 1} \)
Step - I : we have \( P(1) : \frac{1}{1.2} = \frac{1}{1 + 1} \)
since, \( \frac{1}{1.2} = \frac{1}{1 + 1} = \frac{1}{2}. \) so P(1) is true
Step - II : Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ..... + \frac{1}{m (m + 1)} = \frac{m}{m + 1} \)
we shall now show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we have to show that
\( \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ..... + \frac{1}{m (m + 1)} + \frac{1}{(m + 1)(m + 1 + 1)} = \frac{m + 1}{( m + 1) + 1} \)
Now, \( \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ..... + \frac{1}{m (m + 1)} + \frac{1}{(m + 1)((m + 1) + 1)} \)
\( \begin{align} = \left [ \frac{1}{1.2} + \frac{1}{2.3} + \frac{1}{3.4} + ..... + \frac{1}{m (m + 1)} \right ] + \frac{1}{(m + 1)((m + 1) + 1)} \\\\[12pt] = \frac{m}{m + 1} + \frac{1}{(m + 1)((m + 1) + 1)} \\\\[12pt] = \frac{1}{(m + 1)} \left \{ \frac{m}{1} + \frac{1}{m + 2} \right \} = \frac{1}{(m + 1)} \frac{(m^2 + 2m + 1)}{(m + 2)} = \frac{(m + 1)^2}{(m + 1)(m + 2)} \\\\[12pt] = \frac{m + 1}{m + 2} = \frac{(m + 1)}{(m + 1) + 1} \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) P(m + 1) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the given statement is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 7:

Question:
Prove by induction that the sum \( S_n = n^3 + 3n^2 + 5n + 3 \) is divisible by 3 for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by
\( P(n) : S_n = n^3 + 3n^2 + 5n + 3 \) is divisible by 3
Step - I: We have \( P(1) : S_1 = 1^3 + 3(1)^2 + 5(1) + 3 \) is divisible by 3
Since, \( 1^3 + 3(1)^2 + 5(1) + 3 = 12 \) , which is divisible by 3.
\( \therefore \) P(1) is true
Step - II : Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( S_m = m^3 + 3m^2 + 5m + 3 \) is divisible by 3
\( \Rightarrow S_m = m^3 + 3m^2 + 5m + 3 = 3 \lambda \) , for some \( \lambda \epsilon N.....(i) \)
We now wish to show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we have to show that \( (m + 1)^3 + 3(m + 1)^2 + 5(m + 1) + 3 \) is divisible by 3.
Now, \( (m + 1)^3 + 3(m + 1)^2 + 5(m + 1) + 3 = (m^3 + 3m^2 + 5m + 3) + 3m^2 + 9m + 9 \)
\( = 3 \lambda + 3(m^2 + 3m + 3) = 3[ \lambda + m^2 + 3m + 3] \) [using (i)]
\( = 3 \mu, \ where \ \mu = \lambda + m^2 + 3m + 3 \epsilon N \)
\( \therefore \) P(m + 1) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the given statement is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 8:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that for all \( n \epsilon N \) .
\( \frac{1}{1.3} + \frac{1}{3.5} + \frac{1}{5.7} + ...... + \frac{1}{(2n - 1)(2n + 1)} = \frac{n}{2n + 1} \)

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by
\( P(n) : \frac{1}{1.3} + \frac{1}{3.5} + \frac{1}{5.7} + ..... + \frac{1}{(2n - 1)(2n + 1)} = \frac{n}{2n + 1} \)
Step - I : We have \( P(1) : \frac{1}{1.3} = \frac{1}{(2 \times 1 + 1)}. \ clearly, \ \frac{1}{1.3} = \frac{1}{(2 \times 1 + 1)}. \) So, P(1) is true.
Step - II : Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( \frac{1}{1.3} + \frac{1}{3.5} + \frac{1}{5.7} + ..... + \frac{1}{(2m - 1)(2m + 1)} = \frac{m}{2m + 1}......(i) \)
We shall now show that P(m + 1) is true. For this we shall show that
\( \frac{1}{1.3} + \frac{1}{3.5} + \frac{1}{5.7} + ..... + \frac{1}{(2m - 1)(2m + 1)} + \frac{1}{(2m + 1)(2m + 3)} = \frac{m + 1}{3m + 3}.....(ii) \)
Now, \( \frac{1}{1.3} + \frac{1}{3.5} + \frac{1}{5.7} + ..... + \frac{1}{(2m - 1)(2m + 1)} + \frac{1}{(2m + 1)(2m + 3)} \)
\( = \frac{m}{2m + 1} + \frac{1}{(2m + 1)(2m + 3)} \) [using (i)]
\( = \frac{2m^2 + 3m + 1}{(2m + 1)(2m + 3)} = \frac{(2m + 1)(m + 1)}{(2m + 1)(2m + 3)} = \frac{m + 1}{2m + 3} \)
\( \therefore \) P(m + 1) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true.
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, the given result is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Example 9:

Question:
If x and y are any two distinct integers, then prove by mathematical induction that \( (x^n - y^n) \) is divisible by (x - y) for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by
P(n): \( (x^n - y^n) \) is divisible by (x - y)
Step - I : P(1) : \( (x^1 - y^1) \) is divisible by (x - y)
\( (x^1 - y^1) \) = (x - y) is divisible by (x - y)
\( \therefore \) P(1) is true.
Step - II : Let P(m) be true. Then, \( (x^m - y^m) \) is divisible by (x - y)
\( \Rightarrow (x^m - y^m) = \lambda(x - y) \) , for some \( \lambda \epsilon Z .......(i) \)
we shall now show that P(m + 1) is true. For this it is sufficient to show that \( \left ( x^{m + 1} - y^{m + 1} \right ) \) is divisible by (x - y).
Now, \( x^{m + 1} - y^{m + 1} = x^{m + 1} - x^m y + x^m y - y^{m + 1} = x^m(x - y) + y(x^m - y^m) \)
\( = x^m(x - y) + y \lambda(x - y) \) [ using (i) ]
\( = (x - y)(x^m + y \lambda ) \) , which is divisible by (x - y)
So, P(m + 1) is true.
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true.
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction, P(n) is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) i.e., \( (x^n - y^n) \) is divisible by (x - y) for all \( n \epsilon N \)

Example 10:

Question:
Prove by the principle of mathematical induction that for all \( n \epsilon N \) , \( 3^{2n} \) when divided by 8, the remainder is always 1.

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by
P(n) : \( 3^{2n} \) when divided by 8, the remainder is 1
or p(n) : \( 3^{2n} = 8 \lambda + 1 \) for some \( \lambda \epsilon N \)
Step - I: \( P(1) : 3^2 = 8 \lambda + 1 \) for some \( \lambda \epsilon N \)
\( 3^2 = 8 \times 1 + 1 = 8 \lambda + 1, \ where \ \lambda = 1 \)
\( \therefore \) P(1) is true.
Step - II: Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( 3^{2m} = 8 \lambda + 1 \) for some \( \lambda \epsilon N \)
We shall now show that P(m + 1) is true for which we have to show that \( 3^{2(m + 1)} \) when divided by 8, the remainder is 1 i.e., \( 3^{2(m + 1)} = 8 \mu + 1 \) for some \( \mu \epsilon N \) .
Now, \( 3^{2(m + 1)} = 3^{2m}.3^2 = (8 \lambda + 1) \times 9 \) [Using (i)]
\( = 72 \lambda + 9 = 72 \lambda + 8 + 1 = 8(9 \lambda + 1) + 1 = 8 \mu + 1, \ where \ \mu = 9 \lambda + 1 \epsilon N \)
\( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Thus, P(m) is true \( \Rightarrow \) P(m + 1) is true
Hence, by the principle of mathematical induction P(n) is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) i.e., \( 3^{2n} \) when divided by 8 the remainder is always 1.

since \( 1 < 2^1 \ \therefore \) P(1) is true

We shall not show that P(m + 1) is true for which we will have to prove that \( (m + 1) < 2^{m + 1} \) .

Example 12:

Question:
Prove by induction the inequality \( (1 + x)^n \geq 1 + nx \) whenever 'x' is positive and 'n' is a positive integer.

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by P(n) : \( (1 + x)^n \geq 1 + nx \)
Step - I: P(1) : \( (1 + x)^1 \geq 1 + 1 (x) \)
since \( (1 + x)^1 \geq 1 + 1 (x) \ \therefore \) P(1) is true
Step - II: Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( (1 + x)^m \geq 1 + mx....... (i) \)
We shall now prove that P(m + 1) is true whenever P(m) is true. For this we have to show that \( (1 + x)^{m + 1} \geq 1 + (m + 1)x. \)
Now,
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow P(m) is true \Rightarrow (1 + x)^m \geq 1 + mx \\\\[12pt] \Rightarrow (1 + x) (1 + x)^m \geq (1 + x) (1 + mx) \end{align} \)
Multiplying both sides by (1 + x)
\( \begin{align} (1 + x)^{m + 1} \geq 1 + (m + 1) x + mx^2 \\\\[12pt] \Rightarrow (1 + x)^{m + 1} \geq 1 + (m + 1) x + mx^2 \geq 1 + (m + 1)x \\\\[12pt] \Rightarrow (1 + x)^{m + 1} \geq 1 + (m + 1) x \end{align} \)
P(m + 1) is true
Hence by the principle of induction, P(n) is true for all \( n \epsilon N \) i.e., \( (1 + x)^n \geq 1 + nx \) for all \( n \epsilon N \)

Example 13:

Question:
Prove by induction that \( (2n + 7) < (n + 3)^2 \) for all natural numbers n. Using this, prove by induction that \( (n + 3)^2 \leq 2^{n + 3} \) for all \( n \epsilon N \) .

Solution:
Let P(n) be the statement given by
P(n) : \( (2n + 7) < (n + 3)^2 \)
Step - I: \( P(1) : (2 \times 1 + 7) < (1 + 3)^2 \)
\( since \ (2 \times 1 + 7) = 9 < (1 + 3)^2 \)
\( \therefore \) P(1) is true
Step - II: Let P(m) be true. Then,
\( 2m + 7 < (m + 3)^2 \)
