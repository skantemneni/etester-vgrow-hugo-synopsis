Important Formulae

In a triangle ABC, the angles are denoted by the capital letters A, B, C and the length of the sides opposite to these angles are denoted by a, b, c, respectively (Fig. 1)

Some important formulae regarding the sides and angles of a triangle are given below.

  1. The law of sines in any triangle ABC
    \( \frac{a}{sin \ A} = \frac{b}{sin \ B} = \frac{c}{sin \ C} = 2R \)
    where R is the radius of the circumcircle of the triangle ABC. R is also known as the circumradius of the triangle.

  2. The law of cosines in any triangle ABC
    \( cos \ A = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} \ or \ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \ cos \ A \)
    Similar formulae for cos B, cos C exist.

  3. Trigonometric ratios of half-angles in terms of the sides. Let 2s = a + b + c, so that s is the semi-perimeter of \( \triangle \) ABC. Then
    \( sin \frac{A}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{(s - b)(s - c)}}{bc}, \)
    \( cos \frac{A}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{s(s - a)}}{bc}, \)
    \( tan \frac{A}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{(s - b)(s - c)}}{s(s - a)} \)
    similar formulae for \( sin \left(\frac{B}{2}\right), cos \left(\frac{B}{2}\right), tan \left(\frac{B}{2}\right) \) and \( sin \left(\frac{C}{2}\right), cos \left(\frac{C}{2}\right), tan \left(\frac{C}{2}\right) \) exist.
    Note that
    \( tan \left(\frac{A}{2}\right) = \frac{\triangle}{bc}, \)
    \( tan \left(\frac{B}{2}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{s(s - a)}}{bc}, \)
    \( tan \left(\frac{C}{2}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{(s - b)(s - c)}}{s(s - a)} \)
    where \( \triangle \) is the area of the \( \triangle \) ABC.

  4. Projection rule in any triangle ABC
    a = b cos C + c cos B,
    b = c cos A + a cos C,
    and c = a cos B + b cos A

  5. Napier's analogy in any trianlge ABC
    \( tan \frac{B - C}{2} = \frac{b - c}{b + c}cot \frac{A}{2}, \)
    \( tan \frac{C - A}{2} = \frac{c - a}{c + a}cot \frac{B}{2} \ etc \)

  6. Area of a triangle if \( \triangle \) represents the area of a triangle ABC, then
    \( \triangle = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) bc \ sin \ A = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \ ca \ sin \ B \)
    \( = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) ab \ sin \ C = \frac{abc}{4R} = rs \)
    \( = \sqrt{s(s - a)(s - b)(s - c)} \)
    where 2s = a + b + c, R is the radius of the circumcircle of triangle ABC and r is the radius of the circle inscribed in triangle ABC.

Solution Of Triangles

The three sides a, b, c and the three angles A. B, C are called the elements of the triangle ABC. When any three of these six elements (except the three angles) of a triangle are given, the triangle is known completely; that is, the other three elements can be expressed in terms of the given elements and hence evaluated. This process is called the solution of the triangle.

Let ABC be a right-angled triangle, right-angled at C (Fig. 2)

The triangle can be solved as follows:

  1. If the hypotenuse c and one side, say b, are given, then \( sin B = \frac{b}{c} \) gives the value of B, \( A = 90^{\circ} - B \) and \( a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}. \)

  2. If two sides a and b are given, then \( tan B = \frac{b}{a} \) gives the value of B, \( A = 90^{\circ} - B \) and \( c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}. \)

  3. If an angle B and a side a are given, then \( A = 90^{\circ} - B \) , side b is obtained from the relation \( tan B = \frac{b}{a} \) and side c from the relation \( cos B = \frac{a}{c}. \)

  4. If an angle B and the hypotenuse c are given, then \( A = 90^{\circ} - B, \) a = c cos B and b = c sin B.

If the triangle is not right-angled, the different cases to be considered are as follows:

  1. If the three sides a, b and c are given, angle A can be found from the formulae \( tan \frac{A}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{(s - b)(s - c)}{s(s - a)}} \ or \ cos A = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} \)
    Angles B and C can be found using the corresponding formulae.

  2. If two sides b and c and the included angle A are given, and if b > c then \( tan \frac{B - C}{2} = \frac{b - c}{b + c} cot \frac{A}{2} \)
    gives the value of \( \frac{(B - C)}{2}, \) while \( \frac{B + C}{2} = 90^{\circ} - \frac{A}{2} \)
    so that B and C can be evaluated. The third side a is given by
    \( a = b \ \frac{sin A}{sin B} \ or \ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \ cos \ A \)

  3. If two sides b and c and the angle B opposite one of them are given, then angle C is given by \( sin C = \left ( \frac{c}{b} \right ) sin B, \ A = 180^{\circ} - (B + C) \) and the third side a is given by \( a = b \left ( \frac{sin A}{sin B} \right ) \)
    If b < c and B is an acute angle then there are two values of A, so that there are two values of A, so there are two triangles having the given elements. This is called an ambiguous case in the solution of triangles.

  4. If one side a and two angles B and C are given, then \( A = 180^{\circ} - (B + C) \) and the sides b, c are obtained from
    \( b = \frac{a \ sin B}{sin A} \ and \ c = \frac{a \ sin C}{sin A}. \)

  5. If the three angles A, B and C are given, we can only find the ratios of the sides a, b and c, using
    \( \frac{a}{sin A} = \frac{b}{sin B} = \frac{c}{sin C} \)

Arithmetic Progression

A sequence of numbers \( (t_n) \) is said to be in arithmetic progression (A.P.) when the difference \( t_n - t_{n - 1} \) is a constant for all \( n \epsilon N, \ n > 1. \) This constant is called the common difference of the A.P., and it is usually denoted by the letter d.

If a is the first term and d is the common difference of the A.P., then its nth term \( t_n \) is given by
\( t_n = a + (n - 1)d \)
The sum \( S_n \) of the first n terms of such an A.P. is given by \( S_n = \frac{n}{2} [2a + (n - 1)d] = 2 (a + l) \)
where l is the last term, that is, the nth term of the A.P.

Geometrical Progression

A sequence is said to be in geometric progression (G.P. when its first term is non-zero and each of its succeeding terms is r times the preceding term, where r is some fixe non-zero number. The fixed number r is known as th common ratio of the G.P. It is clear from the definitio that no term of a G.P. is zero. If a is the first term of a G.F and r is its common ratio, then its nth term, \( t_n, \) is given by

\( t_n = ar^{n - 1} \)

The sum \( S_n \) of the first n terms of such a G.P. is given by \( \begin{align} S_n = \left\{ \begin{array}{rcl} \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} & If \ r \neq 1 \\\\ na & if \ r = 1 \end{array}\right. \end{align} \)

Harmonic Progression

The sequence \( a_1, \ a_2, \ ....., \ a_n,.....\ where \ a_i \neq 0 \) for each i is said to be in harmonic progression (H.P.) if the sequence \( \frac{1}{a_1}, \ \frac{1}{a_2}, \ ...... \ \frac{1}{a_n}....... \) is in A.P. Note that \( a_n, \) the nth term of the H.P., is given by
\( a_n = \frac{1}{a + (n + 1)d} \ where \ a = \frac{1}{a_1} \ and \ d = \frac{1}{a_2} - \frac{1}{a_1}. \)

Summary

  1. By a sequence, we mean an arrangement of a number in a definite order according to some rule. Also, we define a sequence as a function whose domain is the set of natural numbers or some subsets of the type {1, 2, 3......k). A sequence containing a finite number of terms is called a finite sequence . A sequence is called infinite if it is not a finite sequence.

  2. Let \( a_1, \ a_2, \ a_3, \) ..... be the sequence, then the sum expressed as \( a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \) ... is called series. A series is called finite series if it has got finite number of terms.

  3. An arithmetic progression (A.P.) is a sequence in which terms increase or decrease regularly by the same constant. This constant is called common difference of the A.P. Usually, we denote the first terms of A.P. by a, the common difference by d and the last term by l. The general term or the nth term of the A.P. is given by an = a + (n - 1) d. The sum \( S_n \) of the first n terms of an A.P. is given by
    \( S_n = \frac{n}{2} [2a + (n - 1) d ] = \frac{n}{2} (a + 1) \)

  4. The arithmetic mean A of any two numbers a and b is given by \( \frac{a + b}{2} \) i.e., the sequence a, A, b is in A.P.

  5. A sequence is said to be a geometric progression or G.P., if the ratio of any term to its preceding term is same throughout. This constant factor is called the common ratio . Usually, we denote the first term of a G.P. by a and its common ratio by r. The general or the \( n^{th} \) term of G.P. is given by \( a_n = ar^{n - 1} \) . The sum \( S_n \) of the first n terms of G.P. is given by
    \( S_n = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} \ or \ \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}, \ if \ r \ \neq \ 1 \)

  6. The geometric mean (G.M.) of any two positive numbers a and b is given by \( \sqrt{ab} \) i.e., the sequence a, G, b is G.P.

Solved examples

Example 1:

In an A.P. if \( m^{th} \) term is n and the \( n^{th} \) term is m, where \( m \neq n \) , find the pth term.
Solution
We have \( \begin{align} a_m = a + (m - 1) d = n, \ ... \ (1) \\\\[3pt] and \ a_n = a + (n - 1) d = m \ ... \ (2) \end{align} \)
Solving (1) and (2), we get
(m - n) d = n - m, or d = - 1, ... (3)
and a = n + m - 1 ... (4)
Therefore \( a_p \) = a + (p - 1)d
= n + m - 1 + ( p - 1) (-1) = n + m - p
Hence, the pth term is n + m - p.

Example 2:

If the sum of n terms of an A.P. is \( nP + \frac{1}{2} n(n - 1) Q \) where P and Q are constants, find the common difference.
Solution
\( Let \ a_1, \ a_2, \ ...... \ a_n \) be the given A.P. Then
\( \begin{align} S_n = a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + .... + a_{n - 1} + a_n = nP + \frac{1}{2} n(n - 1)Q \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ S_1 = a_1 = P, \ S_2 = a_1 + a_2 + 2P + Q \\\\[3pt] So \ that \ a_2 = S_2 - S_1 = P + Q \end{align} \)
Hence, the common difference is given by \( d = a_2 - a_1 = (P + Q) - P = Q \) .

Example 3:

The sum of n terms of two arithmetic progressions are in the ratio (3n + 8) : (7n + 15). Find the ratio of their \( 12^{th} \) terms.
Solution
Let \( a_1, \ a_2 \) and \( d_1, \ d_2 \) be the first terms and common difference of the first and second arithmetic progression, respectively. According to the given condition, we have
\( \begin{align} \frac{\text{Sum to terms of first A.P.}}{\text{Sumto terms of second A.P.}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{3n + 8}{7n + 15} \\\\[3pt] or \ \frac{\frac{n}{2}[2 a_1 + (n - 1) d_1}{\frac{n}{2}[2a_1 + (n - 1) d_2]} = \frac{3n + 8}{7n + 15} \\\\[3pt] or \ \frac{2a-1 + (n - 1)d_1}{2a_1 + (n - 1)d_2} = \frac{3n + 8}{7n + 15} \ .... \ (1) \\\\[3pt] Now \ \frac{\text{12 th term of first A.P.}}{\text{12 term of second A.P}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{a_1 + 11 d_1}{a_2 + 11 d_2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{2a_1 + 22 d_1}{2a_2 + 22 d_2} = \frac{3 \times 23 + 8}{7 \times 23 + 15} \ \text{[By putting n = 23 in (1)]} \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ \frac{a_1 + 11 d_1}{a_2 + 11 d_2} = \frac{\text{12 th term of first A.P.}}{\text{12 term of second A.P}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{7}{6} \end{align} \)

Example 4:

The income of a person is Rs. 3,00,000, in the first year and he receives an increase of Rs.10,000 to his income per year for the next 19 years. Find the total amount, he received in 20 years.
Solution
Here, we have an A.P. with a = 3,00,000, d = 10,000, and n = 20. Using the sum formula, we get,
\( S_{20} = \frac{20}{2} [600000 + 19 \times 10000] \)
= 10(790000) = 79,00,000.

Example 5:

Find the 10th and \( n^{th} \) terms of the G.P. 5, 25,125,......
Solution
Here a = 5 and r = 5. Thus, \( a_{10} = 5(5)^{10 - 1} = 5(5)^9 = 5^{10} \ and \ a_n = ar^{n - 1} = 5(5)^{n - 1} = 5n \)

Example 6:

Which term of the G.P., 2,8,32, ... up to n terms is 131072?
Solution
Let 131072 be the \( n^{th} \) term of the given G.P. Here a = 2 and r = 4.
Therefore 131072 = \( a_n = 2(4)^{n - 1} \) or \( 65536 = 4^{n - 1} \) This gives \( 4^8 = 4^{n - 1} \) .
So that n - 1 = 8, i.e., n = 9. Hence, 131072 is the \( 9^{th} \) term of the G.P.

Example 7:

Find the sum of first n terms and the sum of first 5 terms of the geometric series \( 1 + \frac{2}{3} + \frac{4}{9} + ..... \)
Solution
Here a = 1 and \( r = \frac{2}{3} \) . Therefore
\( \begin{align} S_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{\left [ 1 - \left ( \frac{2}{3} \right )^n \right ]}{1 - \frac{2}{3}} \\\\[3pt] = 3 \left [ 1 - \left ( \frac{2}{3} \right )^n \right ] \\\\[3pt] In \ particular, \ S_5 = 3 \left [ 1 - \left ( \frac{2}{3} \right )^5 \right ] \\\\[3pt] = 3 \times \frac{211}{243} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{211}{81} \end{align} \)

Example 8:

A person has 2 parents, 4 grandparents, 8 great grandparents, and so on. Find the number of his ancestors during the ten generations preceding his own.
Solution
Here a = 2, r = 2 and n = 10
Using the sum formula \( S_n = \frac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} \)
We have \( S_{10} = 2(2^{10} - 1) = 2046} \)
Hence, the number of ancestors preceding the person is 2046.

Example 9:

Insert three numbers between 1 and 256 so that the resulting sequence is a G.P.
Solution
Let \( G_1, \ G_2, \ G_3 \) be three numbers between 1 and 256 such that
\( 1, \ G_1, \ G_2, \ G_3, \ 256 \) is a G.P.
Therefore 256 = \( r_4 \) giving \( r = \pm 4 \) (Taking real roots only)
For r = 4, we have \( G_1 = ar = 4, \ G_2 = ar^2 = 16, \ G_3 = ar^3 = 64 \)
Similarly, for r = - 4, numbers are - 4,16 and - 64.
Hence, we can insert, 4, 16, 64 or - 4, 16, -64, between 1 and 256 so that the resulting sequences are in G.P.

Example 10:

Find the sum to n terms of the series whose nth term is n(n + 3).
Solution
Given that \( a_n = n(n + 3) = n^2 + 3n \)
Thus, the sum to n terms is given by
\( \begin{align} S_n = \sum \limits_{k = 1}^{n} a_k \\\\[3pt] \sum \limits_{k = 1}^{n} k^2 + 3 \sum \limits_{k = 1}^{n} k \\\\[3pt] = \frac{n(n + 1)(2n + 1)}{6} + \frac{3n(n + 1)}{2} \\\\[3pt] \frac{n(n + 1)(n + 5)}{3} \end{align} \)
