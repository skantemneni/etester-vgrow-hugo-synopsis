Bionomial Theorem

Properties of The Bionomial Expansion:

  1. There are (n + 1) terms in the expansion \( (x + y)^n = \ ^nC_0 x^n + \ ^nC_1 x^{n - 1} y + \ ^nC_2 x^{n - 2} y^2 +...... \)
    \( + \ ^nC_r x^{n - r} y^r + ......+ \ ^nC_{n - 1} x y^{n - 1} + \ ^nC_n y^n (1) \) .

  2. In any term of (1), the sum of the exponents of x and y is always n.

  3. The binomial coefficients of terms equidistant from the beginning and the end are equal,
    since \( ^nC_r = \ ^nC_{n - r} (0 \leq r \leq n). \)

  4. The term \( ^nC_r x^{n - r} y^r \) is the (r + 1)th term from the beginning of the expansion. It is usually denoted by \( T_{r + 1} \) and is called the general term of the expansion.

Bionomial Theorem(For a Positive Integral Index)

If n is a positive integer and x, y are two complex numbers, then
\( \begin{align} (x + y)^n = \ ^nC_0 x^n + \ ^nC_1 x^{n - 1} y + \ ^nC_2 x^{n - 2} y^2 +...... \\\\[6pt] + \ ^nC_r x^{n - r} y^r + ......+ \ ^nC_{n - 1} x y^{n - 1} + \ ^nC_n y^n \end{align} \)
The coefficients \( ^nC_0, \ ^nC_1, \ ^nC_2,......, \ ^nC_n \) are called binomial coefficients.

Properties of The Bionomial Coefficients:

  1. \( C_0 + C_1 + C_2 + ...... + C_n = 2^n \)

  2. \( C_0 + C_2 + C_4 + ...... = C_1 + C_3 + C_5 + ..... = 2^{n - 1} \)

  3. \( C_0 - C_1 + C_2 - ...... + (-1)^n C_n = 0 \)

  4. for n > 1,
    \( C_1 - 2C_2 + 3C_3 - ...... + (-1)^{n - 1} ^nC_n = 0 \)

Middle term

If n is even, then the expansion (1) has just one middle term, viz . \( \left ( \frac{n}{2} + 1 \right ) \) th term. It is given by \( ^nC_{ \frac{n}{2}} x^{ \frac{n}{2}}y^{ \frac{n}{2}} \) .
If n is odd, then the expansion (1) has two middle terms, viz. \( \left ( \frac{n + 1}{2} \right ) \) th term and \( \left ( \frac{n + 1}{2} + 1 \right ) \) th term. These are given by
\( ^nC_{\frac{(n - 1)}{2}} x^{\frac{(n - 1)}{2}} y^{\frac{(n + 1)}{2}} \)
and \( ^nC_{\frac{(n + 1)}{2}} x^{\frac{(n + 1)}{2}} y^{\frac{(n - 1)}{2}} \)

The Greatest Coefficient

n is even, the greatest coefficient in the expansion of \( (x + y)^n \) is \( ^nC_{\frac{n}{2}} \) .
If n is odd, there are two greatest coefficients in the expansion of \( (x + y)^n \) . These are \( ^nC_{\frac{(n - 1)}{2}} \) and \( ^nC_{\frac{(n + 1)}{2}} \)

The Greatest Term

To find the greatest term in the expansion of \( (x + y)^n \) , x, y > 0, \( n \epsilon N \) put
\( m = \frac{(n + 1) y}{x + y} \)
If m is not an integer, then the expansion of \( (x + y)^n \) has just one greatest term and it is the (k + 1)th term where k = [m] where [m] denotes the greatest integer \( \leq m \) .
If m is an integer, then expansion of \( (x + y)^n \) has two greatest terms viz. mth and (m + 1)th terms.

Example 1:

Expand \( \left ( x^2 + \frac{3}{x} \right )^4, \ x \ \neq \ 0 \)
Solution
\( \begin{align} \left ( x^2 + \frac{3}{x} \right )^4 = ^4C_0 (x^2)^4 + ^4C_1 (x^2)^3 \left ( \frac{3}{x} \right ) + ^4C_2 (x^2)^2 \left (\frac{3}{x} \right )^2 + ^4C_3 (x^2) \left ( \frac{3}{x} \right )^3 + ^4C_4 \left ( \frac{3}{x} \right )^4 \\\\[3pt] = x^8 + 4x^6 . \frac{3}{x} + 6x^4. \frac{9}{x^2} + 4x^2 . \frac{27}{x^3} + \frac{81}{x^4} \\\\[3pt] = x^8 + 12x^5 + 54x^2 + \frac{108}{x} + \frac{81}{x^4} \end{align} \) .

\( \begin{align} Therefore, \ T_{17} = T_{16 + 1} = \ ^{50}C_{16} (2)^{50 - 16} a^{16} \\\\[3pt] = \ ^{50} C_{16} 2^{34} a^{16} \\\\[3pt] Similarly, \ T_{18} = \ ^{50} C_{17} 2^{33} a^{17} \\\\[3pt] Given \ that = T_{17} = T_{18} \\\\[3pt] So \ ^{50}C_{16} (2)^{34} a^{16} = \ ^{50} C_{17} (2)^{33} a^{17} \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ \frac{^{50}C_{16} \times 2}{^{50}C_{17}} = \frac{50 !}{16! 34!} \times \frac{17! . 33!}{50!} \times 2 = 1 \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Find the coefficient of \( x^6y^3 \) in the expansion of \( (x + 2y)^9 \) .
Solution
Suppose \( x^6y^3 \) occurs in the \( (r + 1)^{th} \) term of the expansion \( (x + 2y)^9 \) .
Now \( T_{r + 1} = \ ^9C_r x^{9 - r} (2y)^r = \ ^9C_r 2^r. x^{9 - r} . y^r. \)
Comparing the indices of x as well as y in \( x^6y^3 \) and in \( T_{r + 1} \) , we get r = 3. Thus, the coefficient of \( x^6y^3 \) is
\( ^9C_3 2^3 = \frac{9!}{3! 6!}. 2^3 = \frac{9.8.7}{3.3}. 2^3 = 672 \) .

Example 9:

Find the rth term from the end in the expansion of \( (x + a)^n \) .
Solution
There are (n + 1) terms in the expansion of \( (x + a)^n \) . Observing the terms we can say that the first term from the end is the last term, i.e., \( (n + 1)^{th} \) term of the expansion and n + 1 = (n + 1) - (1 - 1).
The second term from the end is the nth term of the expansion, and n = (n + 1) - (2 - 1).
The third term from the end is the \( (n - 1)^{th} \) term of the expansion and n - 1 = (n + 1) - (3 - 1) and so on.
Thus rth term from the end will be term number (n + 1) - (r - 1) = (n - r + 2) of the expansion.
And the \( (n - r + 2)^{th} \) term is \( ^nC_{n - r + 1} \ x^{r - 1} a^{n - r + 1} \)

Example 10:

If the coefficients of \( (r - 5)^{th} \) and \( (2r - 1)^{th} \) terms in the expansion of \( (1 + x)^{34} \) are equal, find r.
Solution
The coefficients of \( (r - 5)^{th} \) and \( (2r - 1)^{th} \) terms of the expansion \( (1 + x)^{34} \) are \( ^{34}C_{r - 6} \) and \( ^{34}C_{2r - 2} \) , respectively. Since they are equal so \( ^{34}C_{r - 6} = ^{34}C_{2r - 2} \)
Therefore, either r - 6 = 2r - 2 or r - 6 = 34 - (2r - 2)
[Using the fact that if \( ^nC_r = ^nC_p \) , then either r = p or r = n - p]
So, we get r = - 4 or r = 14.
r being a natural number, r = - 4 is not possible. So, r = 14.
