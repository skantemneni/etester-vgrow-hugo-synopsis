Complex Number - Definition

A number of the form a + ib where \( a, \ b \epsilon R \) , the set of real numbers, and \( i = \sqrt{-1} \) is called a complex number. A complex number can also be defined as an ordered pair of real numbers a and b, and may be written as (a, b), where the first number denotes the real part and the second number denotes the imaginary part. If z = a + ib, then the real part of z is denoted by Re(z) and the imaginary part of z is denoted by Im(z).

A complex number z is said to be purely real if Im(z)= 0 and is said to be purely imaginary if Re(z) = 0. Note that the complex number 0 = 0 + i0 is both purely real and purely imaginary. It is the only complex number with this property.

We denote the set of all complex numbers by C. That is, \( C = \{ a + ib | a, \ b \epsilon R \} \) . Two complex numbers \( z_1 = a_1 + i b_1 \) and \( z_2 = a_2 + ib_2 \) , are said to be equal if \( a_1 = a_2 \) and \( b_1 = b_2 \) .

Algebraic Operations With Complex Numbers

  1. Addition : \( (a + ib) + (c + id) = (a + c) + i(b + d) \)

  2. Subtraction : \( (a + ib) - (c + id) = (a - c) + i(b - d) \)

  3. Multiplication : \( (a + ib)(c + id) = (ac - bd) + i(ad + bc) \)

  4. Reciprocal : If at least one of a, b is non-zero then the reciprocal of a + bi is given by
    \( \frac{1}{a + ib} = \frac{a - ib}{(a + ib)(a - ib)} = \frac{a}{a^2 + b^2} - i \frac{b}{a^2 + b^2} \)

  5. Quotient : If at least one of c, d is non-zero, then quotient of a + bi and c + di is given by
    \( \begin{align} \frac{a + bi}{c + di} = \frac{(a + ib)(c - id)}{(c + id) (c - id)} = \frac{(ac + bd) + i(bc - ad)}{c^2 + d^2} \\\\[12pt] = \frac{ac + bd}{c^2 + d^2} + i \frac{bc - ad}{c^2 + d^2} \end{align} \)

Conjugate Of Complex Number

Let z = a + ib be a complex number. We define conjugate of z, denoted by \( \overline{z} \) . to be the complex number a - ib. That is, if z = a + ib, then \( \overline{z} \) = a - ib.

Properties of Conjugate of a Complex Number

  1. \( z_1 = z_2 \Leftrightarrow \overline{z_1} = \overline{z_2} \)

  2. \( \overline{ ( \overline{z} )} = z \)

  3. \( z + \overline{z} = 2 \ Re(z) \)

  4. \( z - \overline{z} = 2i \ Im(z) \)

  5. \( z = \overline{z} \Leftrightarrow z \) is purely real

  6. \( z + \overline{z} = 0 \Leftrightarrow z \) is purely imaginary.

  7. \( z \overline{z} = [Re(z)]^2 + [Im(z)^2] \)

  8. \( \overline{z_1 + z_2} = \overline{z_1} + \overline{z_2} \)

  9. \( \overline{z_1 - z_2} = \overline{z_1} - \overline{z_2} \)

  10. \( \overline{z_1 z_2} = \overline{z_1} \overline{z_2} \)

  11. \( \overline{ \left ( \frac{z_1}{z_2} \right )} = \frac{ \overline{z_1}}{ \overline{z_2}} \) if \( z_2 \neq 0 \)

  12. If \( P(z) = a_0 + a_1 z + a_2 z^2 + ..... + a_n z^n \)
    where \( a_0, \ a_1, \ ..... \ a_n \) and z are complex number, then
    \( \begin{align} \overline{P(z)} = \overline{a_0} + \overline{a_1} (\overline{z}) + \overline{a_2}(\overline{z})^2 + ....... + \overline{a_n}(\overline{z})^n = \overline{P}(\overline{z}) \\\\[12pt] where \ \overline{P}(z) = \overline{a_0} + \overline{a_1}z + \overline{a_2}z^2 + .......+ \overline{a_n} z^n \end{align} \)

  13. If \( R(z) = \frac{P(z)}{Q(z)} \) where P(z) and Q(z) are polynomials in z, and \( Q(z) \neq 0 \) , then
    for instance
    \( \overline{ \left ( \frac{z + 3z^2}{z - 1} \right ) } = \frac{\overline{z} + 3 \overline{z}^2}{\overline{z} - 1} \)

  14. If \( \begin{align} z = \left | \begin{array}{ccc} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\\\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\\\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{array} \right | \end{align} \) , then \( \begin{align} \overline{z} = \left | \begin{array}{ccc} \overline{a_1} & \overline{a_2} & \overline{a_3} \\\\ \overline{b_1} & \overline{b_2} & \overline{b_3} \\\\ \overline{c_1} & \overline{c_2} & \overline{c_3} \end{array} \right | \end{align} \) where \( a_i, \ b_i, \ c_i(i = 1, \ 2, \ 3) \) are complex numbers.

Modulus Of Complex Number

Let z = a + ib be a complex number. We define the modulus or the absolute value of z to be the real number \( \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \) and we denote it by |z|.
Note that \( |z| \geq 0 \forall z \epsilon C \)

Properties of Modulus

If z is a complex number, then

  1. \( |z| = 0 \Leftrightarrow z = 0 \)

  2. \( |z| = | \overline{z} | = |â€” z| = | - \overline{z} | \)

  3. \( -|z| \leq Re(z) \leq |z| \)

  4. \( -|z| \leq Im(z) \leq |z| \)

  5. \( z \overline{z} = |z|^2 \)

    6. If \( z_1, \ z_2 \) are two complex numbers, then

  6. \( |z_1 z_2| = |z_1| |z_2| \)

  7. \( \left | \frac{z_1}{z_2} \right | = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|}, \ if \ z_2 \neq 0 \)

  8. \( |z_1 + z_2|^2 = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 + \overline{z_1}z_2 + z_1 \overline{z_2} \)
    \( |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 + 2Re(z_1 \overline{z_2}) \)

  9. \( |z_1 - z_2|^2 = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 - \overline{z_1}z_2 - z_1 \overline{z_2} \)
    \( |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 - 2Re(z_1 \overline{z_2}) \)

  10. \( | z_1 + z_2 |^2 + | z_1 - z_2 |^2 = 2(|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2) \)

  11. If a and b are real numbers and \( z_1, \ z_2 \) , are complex numbers, then
    \( |az_1 + bz_2|^2 + |bz_1 - az_2|^2 = (a^2 + b^2)(|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2) \)

  12. If \( z_1, \ z_2 \neq 0 \) ,then \( |z_1 + z_2|^2 = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 \Leftrightarrow \frac{z_1}{z_2} \) is purely imaginary.

  13. \( |z_1 - z_2| \leq |z_1| + |z_2| \)

  14. \( ||z_1| - |z_2|| \leq |z_1| + |z_2| \)

  15. \( |z_1 - z_2| \geq ||z_1| - |z_2|| \)

Polar Form Of A Complex Number

Let z be a non zero complex number, then we can write
\( z = r(cos \theta + i sin \theta) \)
where r = |z| and \( \theta = arg(z). \)
for instance,
\( \begin{align} -1 + i = \sqrt{2} \left ( cos \frac{3 \pi}{4} + i sin \frac{3 \pi}{4} \right ) \\\\[12pt] and \ -1 - i = \sqrt{2} \left \{ cos \left ( - \frac{3 \pi}{4} \right ) + sin \left ( - \frac{3 \pi}{4} \right ) \right \} \end{align} \)
In fact, if \( z = r(cos \ \theta + i sin \ \theta) \) then z is also given by \( z = r [ cos(2 k \pi + \theta) + i sin(2 k \pi + \theta)] \)
where k is any integer.

Euler's Formula

The complex number \( cos \ \theta + i sin \ \theta \) is denoted by \( e^{i \theta} \) or \( cis \ \theta \) That is
\( e^{i \theta} = cis \ \theta = cos \theta + i sin \theta \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

If 4x + i(3x - y) = 3 + i (-6), where x and y are real numbers, then find the values of x and y.
Solution
We have
4x + i(3x - y) = 3 + i(-6) ... (1)
Equating the real and the imaginary parts of (1), we get
4x = 3, 3x - y = - 6,
which, on solving simultaneously, give \( x = \frac{3}{4} \ and \ y = \frac{33}{4} \)

Example 2:

Express \( (5 - 3i)^3 \) in the form a + ib.
Solution
We have, \( (5 - 3i)^3 = 5^3 - 3 \times 5^2 \times (3i) + 3 \times 5(3i)^2 - (3i)^3 \)
= 125 - 225i - 135 + 27i = - 10 - 198i.

Example 3:

Find the multiplicative inverse of 2 - 3i.
Solution
Let z = 2 - 3i
Then \( \overline{z} = 2 + 3i \) aand \( |z|^2 = 2^2 + (-3)^2 = 13 \)
Therefore, the multiplicative inverse of 2 - 3i is given by
\( z^{-1} = \frac{\overline{z}}{|z|^2} = \frac{2 + 3i}{13} = \frac{2}{13} + \frac{3}{13}i \)
The above working can be reproduced in the following manner also,
\( \begin{align} z^{-1} = \frac{1}{2 - 3i} = \frac{2 + 3i}{(2 - 3i)(2 + 3i)} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{2 + 3i}{2^2 - (3i)^2} = \frac{2 + 3i}{13} = \frac{2}{13} + \frac{3}{13} i \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Represent the complex number \( z = 1 + i \sqrt{3} \) in the polar form
Solution

Let \( 1 = r \ cos \ \theta, \ i \sqrt{3} = r \ sin \ \theta \)
By squaring and adding, we get
\( \begin{align} r^2(cos^2 \ \theta + sin^2 \theta) = 4 \\\\[3pt] i.e., \ r = \sqrt{4} = (conventionally, \ r > 0 ) \\\\[3pt] Therefore, \ cos \ \theta = \frac{1}{2}, \ sin \ \theta = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \ which \ gives \ \theta = \frac{\pi}{3} \\\\[3pt] \text{Therefore, required polar form is} \ z = 2 \left ( cos \frac{\pi}{3} + i sin \ \frac{\pi}{3} \right ) \\\\[3pt] \text{The complex number} \ z = 1 + 1 \sqrt{3} \ \text{is represented as shown in Fig} \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Convert the complex number \( \frac{-16}{1 + i \sqrt{3}} \) into polar form.
Solution

The given complex number \( \frac{-16}{1 + i \sqrt{3}} = \frac{-16}{1 + i \sqrt{3}} \times \frac{1 - i \sqrt{3}}{1 - i \sqrt{3}} \)
\( \begin{align} \frac{-16(1 - i \sqrt{3})}{1 - (i \sqrt{3})^2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{-16(1 - i \sqrt{3})}{1 + 3} \\\\[3pt] = -4(1 - i \sqrt{3}) = - 4 + i 4 \sqrt{3} \\\\[3pt] Let \ -4 = r \ cos \ \theta, \ 4 \sqrt{3} = r \ sin \ \theta \\\\[3pt] \text{By squaring and adding, we get} \\\\[3pt] 16 + 48 = r^2(cos^2 \ \theta + sin^2 \ \theta) \\\\[3pt] which \ gives \ r^2 = 64, \ i.e. \ r = 8 \\\\[3pt] Hence \ cos \ \theta = - \frac{1}{2}, \ sin \ \theta = = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\\\[3pt] = \theta = \pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2 \pi}{3} \\\\[3pt] \text{Thus, the required polar form is} \ 8 \left ( cos \frac{2 \pi}{3} + i \ sin \frac{2 \pi}{3} \right ) \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Express the following in the form a + ib
\( \frac{5 + \sqrt{2} i}{1 - \sqrt{2} i} \)
Solution
We have,
\( \begin{align} \frac{5 + \sqrt{2} i}{1 - \sqrt{2} i} = \frac{5 + \sqrt{2} i}{1 - \sqrt{2} i} \times \frac{1 + \sqrt{2} i}{1 + \sqrt{2} i} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{5 + 5 \sqrt{2} i + \sqrt{2}i - 1}{1 - ( \sqrt{2} i)^2 } \\\\[3pt] = \frac{3 + 6 \sqrt{2} i}{1 + 2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{3(1 + 2 \sqrt{2} i)}{3} = 1 + 2 \sqrt{2}i \end{align} \)

Example 7:

Express the following in the form a + ib
\( i^{-35} \)
Solution
\( \begin{align} i^{-35} = \frac{1}{i^{35}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{i^{35}} = \frac{1}{(i^2)^{17} i} = \frac{1}{-i} \times \frac{i}{i} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{i}{- i^2} = i \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Express \( (-\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{-2})( 2 \sqrt{3} - i) \) in the form of a + ib
Solution
We have,
\( \begin{align} (-\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{-2})( 2 \sqrt{3} - i) \\\\[3pt] = (-\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{2} i )( 2 \sqrt{3} - i) \\\\[3pt] = -6 \sqrt{3} i + 2 \sqrt{6} i - \sqrt{2} i^2 = (-6 + \sqrt{2}) + \sqrt{3} (1 + 2 \sqrt{2}) i \end{align} \)
