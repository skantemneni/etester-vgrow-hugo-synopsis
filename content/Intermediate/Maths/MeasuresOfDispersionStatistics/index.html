Definitions And Formulae

  1. A table showing the distribution of frequencies in the different classes is called a frequency table or frequency distribution.

  2. The number of items in a class interval is called frequency .

  3. Each class interval is bounded by two numbers called class limits . The first number 'l' is known as lower limit and the second number 'U' is known as upper limit .

  4. If the upper limits of a class intervals are equal to the lower limits of successive class intervals,then the limits are called exact limits or exact bounds .

  5. The difference between the upper and lower limits of a class interval is called width of the class interval. The average of lower and upper limits is called midvalue of a class interval.

  6. Arithmetic mean, Median, Mode, Geometric mean and Harmonic mean are called Measure of Central values .

  7. Arithmetic mean for an ungrouped data = \( \frac{ \text{Sum of the items}}{ \text{Number of the items}} \)

  8. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ...... \ x_n \) are the midvalues of the classes and \( f_1, \ f_2, \ ..... \ f_n \) are frequencies of a grouped data,
    then Arithmetic Mean = \( \frac{ \sum f_i x_i}{ \sum f_i}. \)

  9. In the step deviation method A.M. = A + C \( \frac{ \sum f_i x_i}{ \sum f_i}. \) where A = Assumed mean.
    C = width of the classes, \( d_i = \frac{x_i - A}{C}. \)

  10. If \( \bar{X_1} \) and \( \bar{X_2} \) are the arithmetic means of two datas having \( n_1 \) and \( n_2 \) items respectively then the mean of the combined data is given by \( \bar{x} = \frac{n_1 \bar{x_1} + n_2 \bar{x_2}}{n_1 + n_2} \)

  11. When arranging the data in the ascending or in descending order of magnitude, the item which divides the data into two equal parts called Median . If the number of items is even then the average of middle two terms is called median.

  12. The class corresponding to the cumulative frequency just greater than \( \frac{N}{2} \) is called the median class.
    Median = \( l + \frac{ \frac{N}{2} - m}{f} \times C \) , where
    l = lower limit of the median class
    m = Cumulative frequency of the class preceeding the median class
    N = Total frequency
    f = Frequency of the median class
    C = Width of the median class

  13. The item which occurs most frequently in an ungrouped data is called Mode .

  14. The class having maximum frequency is called model class.
    Mode = \( I + \frac{f - f_1}{2f - f_1 - f_2} \times C \) , where
    l = lower limit of the model class
    f = Frequency of the model class
    \( f_1 \) = Frequency of the class preceeding the model class
    \( f_2 \) = Frequency of the class succeeding the model class
    C = Width of the model class

  15. Arithmetic mean - Mode = 3 (Arithmetic mean - Median) or Mode = 3 Mean - 2 Arithmetic mean.

  16. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ....... \ x_n \) are the n items in the given data, then \( (x_1, \ x_2, \ .... \ x_n)^{ \frac{1}{n}} \) is called geometric mean.

  17. If \( g_1 \) and \( g_2 \) are the geometric means of two datas containing \( n_1 \) and \( n_2 \) , items respectively then the geometric mean g of combined data is given by \( log \ g = \frac{n_1 \ log \ g_1 + n_2 \ log \ g_2}{n_1 + n_2} \)

  18. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ....... \ x_n \) are the n items(non zero) in the given data, then \( \frac{n}{ \frac{1}{x_1} + \frac{1}{x_2} + ..... + \frac{1}{x_n}} \) is called harmonic mean.

  19. The measures which determine the dispersion of the items in the data from the measures of central tendency are called Measure of dispersion.

  20. The measures of dispersion are

    1. Range

    2. Quartile deviation

    3. Mean deviation

    4. Standard deviation

  21. The difference between the greatest and smallest values in the data is called Range .

  22. The values of the items which divide the data into four equal parts are called Quartiles . When arrange the data containing n items in ascending order or discending order of magnitude, the value of \( \frac{(n + 1)}{4} \) th term is called Lower Quartile \( (Q_1) \) and the value of \( \frac{3 (n + 1)}{4} \) th item is called Upper Quartile \( (Q_3) \) .

  23. In a grouped data the class containing \( \frac{N}{4} \) in the cumulative frequency is called \( (Q_1) \) and the class containing \( \frac{3N}{4} \) in the cumulative frequency is called \( (Q_3) \) class.
    \( Q_1 = l_1 + \frac{ \frac{N}{4} - m_1}{f} \times C, \ Q_3 = l_3 + \frac{ \frac{3N}{4} - m_3}{f_3} \times C \) , where
    \( l_1 \) = Lower limit of \( (Q_1) \) - class
    \( l_3 \) = Lower limit of \( (Q_3) \) - class
    \( m_1 \) = Cumulative frequency of the class preceeding the \( (Q_1) \) - class
    \( m_3 \) = Cumulative frequency of the class preceeding the \( (Q_3) \) - class
    \( f_1 \) = Frequency of \( (Q_1) \) - class
    \( f_3 \) = Frequency of \( (Q_3) \) - class
    C = Width of the class interval
    N = Total Frequency

  24. Quartile deviation = \( \frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{2} \)

  25. The average of absolute values of the deviations of the items measured from a central value(mean, median, mode) is called Mean deviation (M.D) .

  26. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ....... \ x_n \) are n items in an ungrouped data then mean deviation, M.D. = \( \frac{ \sum | x_i - \bar{x}|}{n} \) where \( \bar{x} \) is a central value

  27. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ....... \ x_n \) are the midvalues of n classes with frequencies \( f_1, \ f_2, \ ..... \ f_n \) in a grouped data and \( \bar{x} \) is a central value, then mean deviation M.D = \( \frac{ \sum f_i | x_i - \bar{x} |}{ \sum f_i} \)

  28. The mean of the squares of deviations of the values from their arithmetic mean is called variance \( ({\sigma}^2) \) . The positive square root is called standard deviation \( (\sigma) \) .

  29. If \( x_1, \ x_2, \ ....... \ x_n \) are n items and \( \bar{x} \) is their arithmetic mean, then varience \( {\sigma}^2 = \frac{ \sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n} = \frac{ \sum x_i^2}{n} - {\bar{x}}^2 \) .

  30. If \( \bar{x_1}, \ \bar{x_2} \) are the arithmetic means and \( {\sigma_1}^2, \ {\sigma_2}^2 \) are the variances of two datas containing \( n_1, \ n_2 \) items respectively, then the variance \( {\sigma}^2 \) of combined data is given by
    \( {\sigma}^2 = \frac{n_1 ({\sigma_1}^2 + d_1^2) + n_2 ({\sigma_2}^2 + d_2^2)}{n_1 + n_2} \) where \( \bar{x} \) is the arithmatic mean of combined data, \( d_1 = \bar{x_1} - \bar{x}, \ d_2 = \bar{x_2} - \bar{x} \) .

  31. The relationship between two variables can be found by rank correlation .

  32. Spearman's Rank Correlation \( \rho = 1 - \frac{6 \sum d^2}{n (n^2 - 1)} \) , where n = number of items,
    d = difference in the ranks.

  33. \( \rho \) lies between - 1 and 1.

  34. For perfect positive correlation \( \rho \) = 1, for perfect negative correlation \( \rho \) = - 1 and for complete absence of correlation \( \rho \) = 0.

  35. If \( | \rho | \) < 0.2, the relationship is negligible.
    If 0.2 < \( | \rho | \) < 0.4, the relationship is light.
    If 0.4 < \( | \rho | \) < 0.7, the relationship is substantial.
    If 0.7 < \( | \rho | \) < 1, the relationship is very high.

  36. Coefficient of range = \( \frac{A - B}{A + B} \) where A and B are the greatest and smallest items.

  37. Coefficient of quartile deviation = \( \frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{Q_3 + Q_1} \)

  38. Coefficient of mean deviation = \( \frac{M.D.}{average} \)

  39. Coefficient of standard deviation = \( \frac{\sigma}{\overline{x}} \)

  40. Coefficient of skewness = \( \frac{Mean - Mode}{\sigma} \)

  41. Coefficient of skewness (based on quartiles) = \( \frac{Q_3 + Q_1 - 2Q_2}{Q_3 - Q_1} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Find the mean deviation about the median for the following data:
3, 9, 5, 3, 12, 10, 18, 4, 7, 19, 21.
Solution
Here the number of observations is 11 which is odd. Arranging the data into ascending order, we have 3, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9, 10, 12, 18, 19, 21
Now Median = \( \left ( \frac{11 + 1}{2} \right )^{th} \ or \ 6^{th} \ observation = 9 \)
The absolute values of the respective deviations from the median, i.e., \( |x_1 - M | \) are 6, 6, 5, 4, 2, 0, 1, 3, 9, 10, 12
Therefore \( \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{11} | x_1 - M | = 58 \)
and \( M.D.(M) = \frac{1}{11} | x_1 - M| = \frac{1}{11} \times 58 = 5.27 \)

Example 5:

Two plants A and B of a factory show following results about the number of workers and the wages paid to them.

A B
No. of workers 5000 6000
Average monthly wages Rs 2500 Rs 2500
50-60 12
Variance of distribution of wages 81 100

In which plant, A or B is there greater variability in individual wages?
Solution
The variance of the distribution of wages in plant \( A({\sigma_1})^2 = 81 \)
Therefore, standard deviation of the distribution of wages in plant \( A({\sigma_1}) = 9 \)
Also, the variance of the distribution of wages in plant \( B({\sigma_1})^2 = 100 \)
Therefore, standard deviation of the distribution of wages in plant \( B({\sigma_1}) = 10 \)
Since the average monthly wages in both the plants is same, i.e., Rs.2500, therefore, the plant with greater standard deviation will have more variability.
Thus, the plant B has greater variability in the individual wages.

Example 6:

Coefficient of variation of two distributions are 60 and 70, and their standard deviations are 21 and 16, respectively. What are their arithmetic means.
Solution
Given C.V. (1st distribution) = 60, \( \sigma_1 = 21 \)
C.V. (2nd distribution) = \( \sigma_2 = 16 \)
Let \( \overline{x_1} \) and \( \overline{x_2} \) be the means of 1st and 2nd distribution, respectively. Then
C.V. (1st distribution) = \( \frac{\sigma_1}{\overline{x_1}} \times 100 \)
Therefore \( 60 = \frac{21}{\overline{x_1}} \times 100 \ or \overline{x_1} = \frac{21}{60} \times 100 = 35 \)
and C.V. (2nd distribution) = \( \frac{\sigma_2}{\overline{x_2}} \times 100 \)
\( 70 = \frac{16}{\overline{x_2}} \times 100 \ or \overline{x_2} = \frac{16}{70} \times 100 = 22.85 \)

Example 7:

The mean of 5 observations is 4.4 and their variance is 8.24. If three of the observations are 1, 2 and 6, find the other two observations.
Solution
Let the other two observations be x and y. Therefore, the series is 1, 2, 6, x, y.
Now Mean \( \overline{x_1} = 4.4 = \frac{1 + 2 + 6+ x + y}{5} \)
or 22 = 9 + x + y
Therefore x + y = 13 ...... (1)
Also variance = \( 8.24 = \frac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{5} (x_i - \overline{x_1})^2 \)
\( \begin{align} i.e. \ 8.24 = \frac{1}{5} \left [ (3.4)^2 + (2.4)^2 + (1.6)^2 + x^2 + y^2 - 2 \times 4.4(x + y) + 2 \times (4.4)^2 \right ] \\\\[3pt] or \ 41.20 = 11.56 + 5.76 + 2.56 + x^2 + y^2 -8.8 \times 13 + 38.72 \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ x^2 + y^2 = 97 \ ..... \ (2) \\\\[3pt] \text{But from (1), we have} \\\\[3pt] x^2 + y^2 + 2xy = 169 \ ...... \ (3) \end{align} \) From (2) and (3), we have
2xy = 72 ..... (4)
Subtracting (4) from (2), we get
\( \begin{align} x^2 + y^2 - 2xy = 97 \ i.e. \ (x - y)^2 = 25 \\\\[3pt] or \ x - y = \pm 5 \ ..... \ (5) \end{align} \)
So, from (1) and (5), we get
x = 9, y = 4 when x - y = 5
or x = 4, y = 9 when x - y = - 5
Thus, the remaining observations are 4 and 9.

Example 8:

The mean and standard deviation of 100 observations were calculated as 40 and 5.1, respectively by a student who took by mistake 50 instead of 40 for one observation. What are the correct mean and standard deviation?
Solution
Given that number of observations (n) = 100
Incorrect mean \( (\overline{x}) = 40 \) ,
Incorrect standard deviation \( (\sigma) = 5.1 \) ,
\( \begin{align} We \ know \ that \ \overline{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i = 1}^n x_i \\\\[3pt] i.e. \ 40 = \frac{1}{100} \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{100} x_i \ or \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{100} x_i = 4000 \end{align} \)
i.e. Incorrect sum of observations = 4000
Thus the correct sum of observations = Incorrect sum - 50 + 40
= 4000 - 50 + 40 = 3990
\( \begin{align} \text{Hence correct mean} = \frac{correct \ sum}{100} = \frac{3990}{100} = 39.9 \\\\[3pt] \text{Also Standard deviation} \ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i = 1}^n {x_i}^2 - \frac{1}{n^2} \left ( \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} \right )^2 } \\\\[3pt] = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} {x_i}^2 - ( \overline{x})^2} \\\\[3pt] i.e. \ 5.1 = \sqrt{\frac{1}{100} \times Incorrect \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} {x_i}^2 - (40)^2} \\\\[3pt] or \ 26.01 = \frac{1}{100} \times Incorrect \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{5} {x_i}^2 - 1600 \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ Incorrect \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} {x_i}^2 = 100(26.01 + 1600) = 162601 \\\\[3pt] Now \ Correct \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} {x_i}^2 = Incorrect \ \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} {x_i}^2 - (50)^2 + (40)^2 \\\\[3pt] = 162601 - 2500 + 1600 = 161701 \\\\[3pt] \text{Therefore Correct standard deviation} \\\\[3pt] = \sqrt{\frac{Correct \ \ sum \ {x_i}^2}{n} - (Correct \ mean)^2} \\\\[3pt] = \sqrt{\frac{161701}{100} - (39.9)^2} \\\\[3pt] = \sqrt{1617.01 - 1592.01} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \end{align} \)
