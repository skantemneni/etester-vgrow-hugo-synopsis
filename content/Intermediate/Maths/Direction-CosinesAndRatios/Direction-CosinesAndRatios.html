Direction-Cosines

\( \alpha, \ \beta, \ \gamma \) are the angles that a given directed line makes with the positive directions X'OX, Y'OY, Z'OZ of the coordinate axes, then \( cos \ \alpha, \ cos \ \beta, \ cos \gamma \) are called the direction-cosines of the line.

Let P(x, y, z) be any point on A'OA. and let the length of OP be r. Let PN be the perpendicular from P on to the plane XOY, and NM be the perpendicular from N on OX. Then
\( x = r \ cos \ \alpha, \ y = r \ cos \ \beta, \ z = r \ cos \ \gamma \) and \( cos^2 \alpha + cos^2 \beta + cos^2 \gamma = 1 \) .

Direction - Ratios

If l, m, n be the direction-cosines of a line OP and a, b, c be numbers proportional to l, m, n respectively, then a, b, c are called the direction ratios of OP. If l, m, n be the direction-cosines of a line and \( (k \neq 0) \) be any number, then kl, km. kn are the direction-ratios of OP.

If a, b, c are the direction-ratios of a line, the actual direction - cosines \( cos \ \alpha, \ cos \ \beta, \ cos \gamma \) are obtained from the relations
\( \begin{align} \frac{cos \ \alpha}{a} = \frac{cos \ \beta}{b} = \frac{cos \ \gamma}{c} \\\\[3pt] = \pm \frac{\sqrt{cos^2 \alpha + cos^2 \beta + cos^2 \gamma}}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{\pm 1}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}} \end{align} \)
If P be the point (a, b, c), and \( cos \ \alpha, \ cos \ \beta, \ cos \gamma \) are the direction cosines of the directed line OP, then
\( cos \ \alpha = \frac{a}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}}, \ cos \ \beta = \frac{b}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}}, \ cos \gamma = \frac{a}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}} \)
The direction - cosines of PO are
\( - \frac{a}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}}, \ - \frac{b}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}}, \ - \frac{c}{\sqrt{(a^2 + b^2 + c^2)}} \)

Angle Between Two Lines

  1. If \( \theta \) is an angle between two lines whose direction-cosines are \( (l_1, \ m_1, \ n_1) \ and \ (l_2, \ m_2, \ n_2) \) then
    \( cos \ \theta = l_1 l_2 + m_1 m_2 + n_1 n_2. \)
    The expressions for \( sin \ \theta, \ tan \ \theta \) are given below:
    \( \begin{align} sin^2 \theta = \left| \begin{array}{cc} m_1 & n_1 \\\\ m_2 & n_2 \end{array} \right|^2 + \left| \begin{array}{cc} n_1 & l_1 \\\\ n_2 & l_2 \end{array} \right|^2 + \left| \begin{array}{cc} l_1 & m_1 \\\\ l_2 & m_2 \end{array} \right|^2 \\\\[3pt] and \ tan \theta = \pm \frac{\left [ \sum (l_1 m_2 - l_2 m_1)^2 \right ]^{\frac{1}{2}}}{(l_1 l_2 + m_1 m_2 + n_1 n_2)} \end{align} \)
    The lines are parallel to each other if and only if
    \( \frac{l_1}{l_2} = \frac{m_1}{m_2} = \frac{n_1}{n_2} \) .
    Also the lines are perpendicular to each other provided
    \( l_1 l_2 + m_1 m_2 + n_1 n_2 = 0 \)

  2. Angle \( \theta \) between two lines whose direction-ratios are \( a_1, \ b_1, \ c_1 \) and \( a_2, \ b_2, \ c_2 \) is given by
    \( \begin{align} cos \ \theta = \pm \frac{a_1 a_2 + b_1 b_2 + c_1 c_2}{\sqrt{{a_1}^2 + {b_1}^2 + {c_1}^2}\sqrt{{a_2}^2 + {b_2}^2 + {c_2}^2}} \\\\[3pt] sin \ \theta = \pm \frac{\left [ (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)^2 + (b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)^2 + (c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1)^2 \right ]^{\frac{1}{2}}}{\sqrt{{a_1}^2 + {b_1}^2 + {c_1}^2}\sqrt{{a_2}^2 + {b_2}^2 + {c_2}^2}} \\\\[3pt] tan \ \theta = \pm \frac{\left [ (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)^2 + (b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)^2 + (c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1)^2 \right ]^{\frac{1}{2}}}{a_1 a_2 + b_1 b_2 + c_1 c_2} \end{align} \)
    Also, the lines are perpendicular to each other if and only if
    \( a_1 a_2 + b_1 b_2 + c_1 c_2 = 0 \) .

Solved examples

Example 1:

If a line makes angle \( 90^{\circ}, \ 60^{\circ} \ and \ 30^{\circ} \) with the positive direction of x, y and z-axis respectively, find its direction cosines.
Solution
Let the d.c.'s of the lines be l , m, n. Then \( l = cos \ 90^{\circ}, \ m = cos \ 60^{\circ} = \frac{1}{2}, \ n = cos \ 30^{\circ} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \) .

Example 2:

If a line has direction ratios 2, -1, -2, determine its direction cosines.
Solution
Direction cosines are
\( \frac{2}{\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + (-2)^2}}, \ \frac{- 1}{\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + (-2)^2}}, \ \frac{- 2}{\sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + (-2)^2}} \ or \ \frac{2}{3}, \ \frac{-1}{3}, \ \frac{-2}{3} \)

Example 3:

Find the direction cosines of the line passing through the two points (- 2, 4, - 5) and (1, 2, 3).
Solution
We know the direction cosines of the line passing through two points \( P(x_1, \ y_1, \ z_1) \ and \ Q(x_2, \ y_2, \ z_2) \) are given by
\( \begin{align} \frac{x_2 - x_1}{PQ}, \ \frac{y_2 - y_1}{PQ}, \ \frac{z_2 - z_1}{PQ} \\\\[3pt] Where \ PQ = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (Z_2 - Z_1)^2} \end{align} \)
Here P is (- 2, 4, - 5) and Q is (1, 2, 3).
So \ \( PQ = \sqrt{ (1 - (-2))^2 + (2 - 4)^2 + (3 - (-5))^2} = \sqrt{77} \)
Thus, the direction cosines of the line joining two points is
\( \frac{3}{\sqrt{77}}, \ \frac{-2}{\sqrt{77}}, \ \frac{8}{\sqrt{77}} \)

Example 4:

Find the direction cosines of x, y and z-axis.
Solution
The x-axis makes angles \( 0^{\circ}, \ 90^{\circ} \ and \ 90^{\circ} \) respectively with x, y and z-axis.
Therefore, the direction cosines of x-axis are \( cos \ 0^{\circ}, \ cos \ 90^{\circ}, \ cos \ 90^{\circ} \) i.e., 1,0,0. Similarly, direction cosines of y-axis and z-axis are 0, 1, 0 and 0, 0, 1 respectively.

Example 5:

Show that the points A (2, 3, - 4), B (1, - 2, 3) and C (3, 8, - 11) are collinear.
Solution
Direction ratios of line joining A and B are 1 - 2, - 2 - 3, 3 + 4 i.e., - 1, - 5, 7.
The direction ratios of line joining B and C are
3 -1, 8 + 2, - 11 - 3, i.e., 2, 10, - 14.
It is clear that direction ratios of AB and BC are proportional, hence, AB is parallel to BC.
But point B is common to both AB and BC. Therefore, A, B, C are collinear points.

Example 6:

Find the vector and the Cartesian equations of the line through the point (5, 2, - 4) and which is parallel to the vector \( 3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 8 \hat{k} \) .
Solution
We have
\( \overrightarrow{a} = 5 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} \ and \ \overrightarrow{b} = 3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 8 \hat{k} \)
Therefore, the vector equation of the line is
\( \overrightarrow{r} = 5 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} + \lambda(3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 8 \hat{k}) \)
Now, \( \overrightarrow{r} \) is the position vector of any point P(x, y, z) on the line.
Therefore,
\( \begin{align} x \hat{i} + y \hat{j} + z \hat{k} = 5 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} + \lambda(3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 8 \hat{k}) \\\\[3pt] = (5 + 5 \lambda) \hat{i} + (2 + 2 \lambda) \hat{j} + (-4 - 8 \lambda) \hat{k} \\\\[3pt] Eliminating \ \lambda, \ we \ get \\\\[3pt] \frac{x - 5}{3} = \frac{y - 2}{2} = \frac{z + 4}{-8} \end{align} \)
which is the equation of the line in Cartesian form.

Example 7:

The Cartesian equation of a line is
\( \frac{x + 3}{2} = \frac{y - 5}{4} = \frac{z + 6}{2} \)
Find the vector equation for the line.
Solution
Comparing the given equation with the standard form
\( \frac{x - x_1}{a} = \frac{y - y_1}{b} = \frac{z - z_1}{c} \)
We observe that x_1 = - 3, \ y_1 = 5, \ z_1 = - 6; \ a = 2, \ b = 4, \ c = 2.
Thus, the required line passes through the point (- 3, 5, -6) and is parallel to the vector \( 2 \hat{i} + 4 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k} \) .
Let \( \overrightarrow{r} \) be the position vector of any point on the line, then the vector equation of the line is given by
\( \overrightarrow{r} = (- 3 \hat{i} + 5 \hat{j} - 6 \hat{k}) + \lambda (2 \hat{i} + 4 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k}) \)

Example 8:

Find the angle between the pair of lines given by
\( \overrightarrow{r} = 3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} + \lambda ( \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k}) \ and \ \overrightarrow{r} = 5 \hat{i} - 2 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} + \mu ( 3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 6 \hat{k}) \)
Solution
\( Here \ \overrightarrow{b_1} = \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k} \ and \ \overrightarrow{b_2} = 3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 6 \hat{k} \)
The angle \( \theta \) between the two lines is given by
\( \begin{align} cos \ \theta = \left | \frac{\overrightarrow{b_1} . \overrightarrow{b_2}}{|\overrightarrow{b_1}||\overrightarrow{b_2}|} \right | \\\\[3pt] = \left | \frac{(\hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k}).(3 \hat{i} + 2 \hat{j} + 6 \hat{k})}{\sqrt{1 + 4 + 4} \sqrt{9 + 4 + 36}} \right | \\\\[3pt] \left | \frac{3 + 4 + 12}{3 \times 7} \right | = \frac{19}{27} \\\\[3pt] Hence \ \theta = cos^{-1} \left ( \frac{19}{21} \right ) \end{align} \)

Example 9:

Find the angle between the pair of lines
\( \frac{x + 3}{3} = \frac{y - 1}{5} = \frac{z + 3}{4} \ and \ \frac{x + 1}{1} = \frac{y - 4}{1} = \frac{z - 5}{2} \)
Solution
The direction ratios of the first line are 3, 5, 4 and the direction ratios of the second line are 1, 1, 2. If \( \theta \) is the angle between them, then
\( \begin{align} cos \ \theta = \left | \frac{3.1 + 5.1 + 4.2}{\sqrt{3^2 + 5^2 + 4^2} \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + 2^2}} \right | \\\\[3pt] = \frac{16}{\sqrt{50} \sqrt{6}} = \frac{16}{5 \sqrt{2} \sqrt{6}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{8 \sqrt{3}}{15} \end{align} \)
Hence, the required angle is \( cos^{-1} = \left ( \frac{8 \sqrt{3}}{15} \right ) \)

Example 10:

Find the shortest distance between the lines \( l_1 \) and \( l_2 \) whose vector equations are
\( \begin{align} \overrightarrow{r} = \hat{i} + \hat{j} + \lambda(2 \hat{i} - \hat{j} + \hat{k}) \ ....... \ (1) \\\\[3pt] and \ \overrightarrow{r} = 2 \hat{i} + \hat{j} - \hat{k} = \mu(3 \hat{i} - 5 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k}) \ ..... \ (2) \end{align} \)
Solution
Comparing (1) and (2) with
\( \begin{align} \overrightarrow{r} = \overrightarrow{a_1} + \lambda \overrightarrow{b_1} \ and \ \overrightarrow{r} = \overrightarrow{a_2} + \mu \overrightarrow{b_2} \ respectively, \\\\[3pt] we \ get \ \overrightarrow{a_1} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}, \ \overrightarrow{b_1} = 2 \hat{i} - \hat{j} + \hat{k} \\\\[3pt] \overrightarrow{a_2} = 2 \hat{i} + \hat{j} - \hat{k} \ and \ \overrightarrow{b_2} = 3 \hat{i} - 5 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k} \\\\[3pt] Therefore \ \overrightarrow{a_2} - \overrightarrow{a_1} = \hat{i} - \hat{k} \\\\[3pt] and \ \overrightarrow{b_1} \times \overrightarrow{b_2} = ( 2 \hat{i} - \hat{j} + \hat{k}) \times ( 3 \hat{i} - 5 \hat{j} + 2 \hat{k}) \\\\[3pt] = \left| \begin{array}{ccc} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\\\ 2 & - 1 & 1 \\\\ 3 & -5 & 2 \end{array} \right| = 3 \hat{i} - \hat{j} - 7 \hat{k} \\\\[3pt] So \ | \overrightarrow{b_1} \times \overrightarrow{b_2}| = \sqrt{9 + 1 + 49} = \sqrt{59} \end{align} \)
Hence, the shortest distance between the given lines is given by
\( \begin{align} d = \left | \frac{(\overrightarrow{b_1} \times \overrightarrow{b_2}).(\overrightarrow{a_2} - \overrightarrow{a_1})}{|\overrightarrow{b_1} \times \overrightarrow{b_2}|} \right | \\\\[3pt] = \frac{|3 - 0 + 7|}{\sqrt{59}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{59}} \end{align} \)
