Thermometry

The branch dealing with measurement of temperature is called thermometry and the devices used to measure temperature are called thermometers.
\( \begin{align} \frac{X_t - X_0}{t} = \frac{X_{100} - X_0}{100} \ or \ \frac{X_t - X_0}{X_{100} - X_0} = \frac{t}{100} \\\\[3pt] Thus, \ t = \left( \frac{X_t - X_0}{X_{100} - X_0} \right) \times 100^{ \circ} C \end{align} \)

Expansion of Solids

On heating a body if its length increases we call it linear expansion if its surface area increases we call it superficial expansion and if its volume increases we call it volume expansion or cubical expansion
Linear expansion(expansion in length of a solid). Consider a rod of length \( l_1 \) at a temperature \( \theta_1 \) . Let it be heated to a temperature \( \theta_2 \) and the increased length of the rod be \( l_2 \) , then
\( l_2 = l_1(1 + \alpha \theta) \)
\( \alpha \) = coefficient of linear expansion and \( \theta = \theta_2 - \theta_1 \)
Superficial expansion(expansion in surface area). If \( A_1 \) is the area of solid at \( \theta_1^{ \circ} C \) and \( A_2 \) is the area at \( \theta_2^{ \circ} C \) , then
\( A_2 = A_1(1 + \beta \theta) \)
\( \beta \) = coefficient of superficial(areal) expansion and \( \theta = \theta_2 - \theta_1 \)
Volume expansion(expansion in volume). If \( V_1 \) is the volume of solid at \( \theta_1^{ \circ} C \) and \( V_2 \) is the volume at \( \theta_2^{ \circ} C \) , then
\( V_2 = V_1(1 + \gamma \theta) \)
\( \gamma \) = coefficient of cubical(volume) expansion, and
\( \theta = \theta_2 - \theta_1 \)

Expansion of Liquids

Thermal expansion in liquids is identical to that of volume expansion in solids and is governed by the relationship which is \( V = V_0(1 + \gamma \Delta \theta), \ \gamma \) for liquids is generally higher than that of solids.

  1. Coefficient of real expansion \( \gamma_r \) , which is due to the actual increase in volume of liquid due to heating.

  2. Coefficient of apparent expansion \( \gamma_a \) , which is apparent increase in the volume of liquid if expansion of vessel containing the liquid is not taken into account.
    \( \therefore \gamma_r = \gamma_a + \gamma_V \)
    Here \( \gamma_V \) = expansion of vessel.

Expansion of Gases

On heating gases expand more than solids or liquids and equal volume of different gases expand equally when heated by the same amount.
All gases have coefficient of volume expansion \( \gamma_V \) with volume variation given by
\( V = V_0(1 + \gamma_V \Delta \theta) \)
and pressure variation given by \( P = P_0(1 + \gamma_P \Delta \theta) \)

Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the science or act of measuring changes in state variables of a body for the purpose of deriving the heat transfer associated with changes of its state due for example to chemical reactions, physical changes, or phase transitions under specified constraints. Calorimetry is performed with a calorimeter

Conduction

It is process by which the heat is transferred in solid. In coduction, molecules vibrate about a fixed location and transfer the heat by collision.

Radiation

This mode of heat transfer doesn't require any medium, and it is the fastest mode of heat transfer.
At microscopic level, thermal radiation arises in a body at any temperature, when atoms of the substance are raised to excited state due to thermal collision and, then they de-excite to lower energy level to emit electromagnetic radiations. Part of electromagnetic radiation emitted by a body which arises due to thermal collisions constitutes the thermal radiation.

Newton's Law of Cooling

According to this law, if the temperature T of the body is not very different from that of the surroundings \( T_0 \) , then rate of cooling \( - \frac{dT}{dt} \) is proportional to the temperature difference between them. To prove it let us assume that
\( T = T_0 + \Delta T \)
So that
\( \begin{align} T^4 = (T_0 + \Delta T)^4 = T_0^4 \left(1 + \frac{ \Delta T}{T_0} \right)^4 \\\\[3pt] \approx T_0^4 \left(1 + \frac{4 \Delta T}{T_0} \right) (from \ binomial \ expansion) \\\\[3pt] \therefore (T^4 - T_0^4) = 4T_0^3( \Delta T) \\\\[3pt] or \ \ (T^4 - T_0^4) \propto \Delta T .......(i) \\\\[3pt] (T_0 = constant) \end{align} \)
Rate at which heat is emitted,
\( \frac{dQ_1}{dt} = \sigma \epsilon AT^4 \)
Rate at which radiations is absorbed,
\( \frac{dQ_2}{dt} = \sigma \epsilon AT_0^4 \)
Net rate of heat loss,
\( H = \sigma \epsilon A[T^4 - T_0^4] \ \ [\therefore e \epsilon = a] \)
Rate of cooling
\( \frac{dT}{dt} = - \frac{ \sigma \epsilon A}{ms}[T^4 - T_0^4] \)
where, m is mass of the body and s its specific heat capacity. Negative sign is there because temperature is falling with time.
Now, \( \frac{dT}{dt} = - K \Delta T \) where, \( K = \frac{ \sigma \epsilon A \times 4 T_0^3}{ms} \)
Solving above equation, we can have
\( T(t) = T_0 + (T_1 - T_0)e^{-kt} \)
where \( T_1 \) is the temperature of body at t = 0.
Approximate solution for Newton's law of cooling is \( \frac{T_1 - T_2}{t} = K \left[ \frac{T_1 + T_2}{2} - T_0 \right] \) , where t is the time in which temperature of body changes from \( T_1 \) to \( T_2 \) .

Newton's Law of Cooling

  1. Heat is a form of energy that flows between a body and its surrounding medium by virtue of temperature difference between them. The degree of hotness of the body is quantitatively represented by temperature.

  2. A temperature-measuring device (thermometer) makes use of some measurable property (called thermometric property) that changes with temperature. Different thermometers lead to different temperature scales. To construct a temperature scale, two fixed points are chosen and assigned some arbitrary values of temperature. The two numbers fix the origin of the scale and the size of its unit.

  3. The Celsius temperature \( (t_C) \) and the Farenheit temperare \( (t_F) \) are related by
    \( t_F = \left ( \frac{9}{5} \right )t_C + 32 \)

  4. The ideal gas equation connecting pressure (P), volume (V) and absolute temperature (T) is :
    \( PV = \mu RT \)
    where \( \mu \) is the number of moles and R is the universal gas constant.

  5. In the absolute temperature scale, the zero of the scale is the absolute zero of temperature the temperature where every substance in nature has the least possible molecular activity. The Kelvin absolute temperature scale (T) has the same unit size as the Celsius scale \( (T_c ) \) , but differs in the origin :
    \( T_C = T - 273.15 \)

  6. The coefficient of linear expansion \( (\alpha_l) \) and volume expansion \( (\alpha_v) \) are defined by the relations :
    \( \begin{align} \frac{l}{l} \propto \alpha_l \ T \\\\[3pt] \frac{V}{V} \propto \\\\alpha_v \ T \end{align} \)
    where \( triangle{l} \) and \( triangle{v} \) denote the change in length l and volume V for a change of temperature \( triangle{T} \) . The relation between them is :
    \( \alpha_v = 3 \alpha_l \)

  7. The specific heat capacity of a substance is defined by
    \( S = \frac{l}{m} \frac{\triangle{Q}}{\triangle{T}} \)
    where m is the mass of the substance and \( triangle{Q} \) is the heat required to change its temperature by \( triangle{T} \) . The molar specific heat capacity of a substance is defined by
    \( C \propto \frac{l}{v} \frac{Q}{T} \)
    where \( \mu \) is the number of moles of the substance

  8. The latent heat of fusion ( \( L_f \) ) is the heat per unit mass required to change a substance from solid into liquid at the same temperature and pressure. The latent heat of vaporisation ( \( L_v \) ) is the heat per unit mass required to change a substance from liquid to the vapour state without change in the temperature and pressure.

  9. The three modes of heat transfer are conduction, convection and radiation.

  10. In conduction, heat is transferred between neighbouring parts of a body through molecular collisions, without any flow of matter. For a bar of length L and uniform cross section A with its ends maintained at temperatures \( T_C \) and \( T_D \) , the rate of flow of heat H is :
    \( H = KA \frac{T_C - T_D}{L} \)
    where K is the thermal conductivity of the material of the bar

  11. Newton's Law of Cooling says that the rate of cooling of a body is proportional to the excess temperature of the body over the surroundings :
    \( \frac{dQ}{dt} = - k(T_2 - T_1) \)
    Where \( T_1 \) is the temperature of the surrounding medium and \( T_2 \) is the temperature of the body.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Show that the coefficient of area expansions, \( \frac{ \left \frac{(\triangle{A}}{A} \right )}{\triangle{T}} \) , of a rectangular sheet of the solid is twice its linear expansivity, \( \alpha_l \) .
Solution

Consider a rectangular sheet of the solid material of length a and breadth b (Fig.). When the temperature increases by \( \triangle{T}, \ \alpha \) increases by \( \triangle{a} = \alpha_1 a \ \triangle{T} \) and b increases by \( \triangle{b} = \alpha_1 b \ \triangle{T} \) the increase in area
\( \begin{align} \triangle{A} = \triangle{A_1} + \triangle{A_2} + \triangle{A_3} \\\\[3pt] \triangle{A} = a \triangle{b} \triangle{A}+ b \ (\triangle{A})(\triangle{b}) + \\\\[3pt] = a \alpha_1 \ b \triangle{T} + b \alpha_1 a \triangle{T} + (\alpha_1)^2 \ ab \ (\triangle{T})^2 \\\\[3pt] = \alpha_1 \ ab \ \triangle{T} (2 + \alpha_1 \triangle{T}) = \alpha_1 \ A \ \triangle{T} (2 + \alpha_1 \ \triangle{T}) \end{align} \)
Since \( \alpha_1: 10^{-5} \ k^{-1} \) the product \( \alpha_1 \ \triangle{T} \) for fractional temperature is small in comparision with 2 and may be neglected. Hence,
\( \left ( \frac{\triangle{A}}{A} \right ) \frac{1}{ \triangle{T}} \cong 2 \alpha_1 \)

Example 2:

A blacksmith fixes iron ring on the rim of the wooden wheel of a bullock cart. The diameter of the rim and the iron ring are 5.243 m and 5.231 m respectively at \( 27^{\circ} C \) . To what temperature should the ring be heated so as to fit the rim of the wheel?
Solution
Given
\( \begin{align} T_1 = 27^{\circ} \ C \\\\[3pt] L_{T_1} = 5.231 \ m \\\\[3pt] L_{T_2} = 5.243 \ m \\\\[3pt] So, \ \\\\[3pt] L_{T_2} = L_{T_1} [1 + \alpha_1 (T_2 - T_1)] \\\\[3pt] 5.243 \ m = 5.231 \ m [ 1 + 1.20 \times 10^{-5} \ k^{-1} (T_2 - 27^{\circ} \ C)] \ or \ T_2 = 218^{\circ} \ C \end{align} \)

Example 3:

When 0.15 kg of ice of \( 0^{\circ} \ C \) mixed with 0.30 kg of water at \( 50^{\circ} \ C \) in a container, the resulting temperature is \( 6.7^{\circ} \ C \) . Calculate the heat of fusion of ice. ( \( S_{water} = 4186 \ J \ kg^{-1} \ K^{-1} \) )
Solution
Heat lost by water
\( \begin{align} = ms_w (\theta_f - \theta_i)_w \\\\[3pt] = (0.30 \ kg)(4186 \ J \ kg^{-1} \ k^{-1} )(50.0^{\circ} C - 6.7^{\circ} C ) \\\\[3pt] = 54376.14 \ J \end{align} \)
Heat required to melt ice
\( = m_2 L_f = (0.15 \ kg) L_f \)
Heat required to raise temperature of ice water to final temperature
\( \begin{align} = m_1 s_w (\theta_f - \theta_i)_I \\\\[3pt] = (0.15 \ kg)(4186 \ J \ kg^{-1} \ K^{-1}) (6.7^{\circ} - 0^{\circ} ) \\\\[3pt] = 4206.93 \ J \end{align} \)
Heat lost = heat gained
\( \begin{align} 54376.14 \ J = (0.15 \ kg) L_f + 4206.93 \ J \\\\[3pt] L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5 \ J \ kg^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 4:

What is the temperature of the steel-copper junction in the steady state of the system shown in Fig. Length of the steel rod = 15.0 cm, length of the copper rod = 10.0 cm, temperature of the furnace \( = 300^{\circ} C \) , temperature of the other end = \( 0^{\circ} \ C \) . The area of cross section of the steel rod is twice that of the copper rod. (Thermal conductivity of steel = \( 50.2 \ J \ s^{-1} \ m^{-1} \ K^{-1} \) ; and of copper = \( 385 \ J \ s^{-1} \ m^{-1} \ K^{-1} \) ).

Solution
The insulating material around the rods reduces heat loss from the sides of the rods. Therefore, heat flows only along the length of the rods. Consider any cross section of the rod. In the steady state, heat flowing into the element must equal the heat flowing out of it; otherwise there would be a net gain or loss of heat by the element and its temperature would not be steady. Thus in the steady state, rate of heat flowing across a cross section of the rod is the same at every point along the length of the combined steel-copper rod. Let T be the temperature of the steel-copper junction in the steady state. Then,
\( \frac{K_1 \times A_1 \times 300 \times T}{L_1} = \frac{K_2 \times A_2 \times T - 0}{L_2} \)
where 1 and 2 refer to the steel and copper rod respectively. For \( A_1 = 2 \ A_2, \ L_1 = 15.0 \ cm, \ L_2 = 10.0 \ cm, \ K_1 = 50.2 \ J \ s^{-1} \ m^{-1} \ K^{-1}, \ K^2 = 385 \ J \ s^{-1} \ m^{-1} \ K^{-1} \) , we have
\( \begin{align} \frac{50.2 \times 2(300 - T)}{15} = \frac{385 \ T}{10} \\\\[3pt] which \ gives \ T = 44.4^{\circ} \end{align} \)

Example 5:

A pan filled with hot food cools from \( 94^{\circ} \ C \ to \ 86^{\circ} \ C \) in 2 minutes when the room temperature is at \( 20^{\circ} \ C \) . How long will it take to cool from \( 71^{\circ} \ C \) to \( 69^{\circ} \ C \) ?

Solution
The average temperature of \( 94^{\circ} \ C \) and \( 86^{\circ} \ C \) is \( 90^{\circ} \ C \) , which is \( 70^{\circ} \ C \) above the room temperature. Under these conditions the pan cools \( 8^{\circ} \ C \) in 2 minutes.
\( \begin{align} \frac{\text{Change in temperature}}{Time} \propto \ K T \\\\[3pt] \frac{8^{\circ} \ C}{2 \ min} = K(70^{\circ} \ C) \end{align} \)
The average of \( 69^{\circ} \ C \) and \( 71^{\circ} \ C \) is \( 70^{\circ} \ C \) , which is \( 50^{\circ} \ C \) above room temperature. K is the same for this situation as for the original.
\( \frac{2^{\circ} \ C}{Time} = K (50^{\circ} \ C) \)
When we divide above two equations, we have
\( \frac{\fraac{8 C}{2 \ min}}{\frac{2C}{time}} = \frac{K(70 \ C)}{K (50 \ C)} \)
Time = 0.7 min = 42 s
