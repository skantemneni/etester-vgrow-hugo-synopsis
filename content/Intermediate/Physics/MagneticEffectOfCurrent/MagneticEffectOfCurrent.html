Biot-Savart's law and its applications

Biot-Savart's Law

According to this law, the magnetic field \( \overrightarrow{dB} \) at the point P due to the small current element of length \( \overrightarrow{dI} \) is given by
\( dB = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \frac{idl sin \theta}{r^2} Wb/m^2 \) or tesla
When \( \mu_0 \) is a constant and is called, permeability of free space.
\( \mu_0 = 4 \pi \times 10^{-7} Wb/A-m \)

If the medium is other than air or vacuum, the magnetic eduction is
\( \overrightarrow{dB} = \frac{\mu_r \mu_0}{4 \pi} \frac{i(\overrightarrow{dI} \times \overrightarrow{r})}{r^3} \)
where \( \mu_r \) is the relative permeability of the medium and is a imensionless quantity.

Applications of Biot-Savart's Law

Let us consider few applications of Biot-Savart's law

  1. Magnetic field due to a straight thin conductor is
    \( B = \frac{\mu_0 i}{4 \pi d}(sin \ \theta_1 + sin \ \theta_2) \)

    1. For an infinitely long straight wire,
      \( \begin{align} \theta_1 = \theta_2 = 90^{\circ} \\\\[3pt] B = \frac{\mu_0 i}{2 \pi d} \end{align} \)

    2. When wire is semi-infinite,
      \( \begin{align} \theta_1 = 0^{\circ} \ and \ \theta_2 = \frac{\pi}{2} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ B = \frac{\mu_0 i}{4 \pi d}\left ( sin \ 0^{\circ} + sin \ \frac{\pi}{2} \right ) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{\mu_0 i}{4 \pi d} \end{align} \)

    3. \( B \propto \frac{1}{d} \) , ie, B-d graph for an infinitely long straight wire is a rectangular hyperbola as shown in figure.

  2. Magnetic field on the axis of a circular coil having N turns is

    \( B = \frac{\mu_0 NiR^2}{2(R^2 + x^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}} \)
    Here, R = radius of the coil
    x = the distance of point P from centre
    and i = current in the coil.

    1. At the centre of the loop, x = 0
      \( \therefore \ B = \frac{\mu_0 Ni}{2 R} \)

    2. For \( \begin{align} x \gg R, \ x^2 + R^2 \simeq x^2 \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ B = \frac{\mu_0 NiR^2}{2x^3} \\\\[3pt] = \left ( \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \right ) \left ( \frac{2 Ni \pi R^2}{x^3} \right ) \\\\[3pt] = \left ( \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \right ) \left ( \frac{2M}{x^3} \right ) \end{align} \)
      Here, M = Magnetic moment of the loop
      \( = NiA = Ni \pi R^2 \)
      Note:
      This result was expected since, the magnetic field on the axis of dipole is
      \( \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \frac{2M}{x^3} \) .

    3. Magnetic field due to an arc of a circle at the centre is

      \( \begin{align} B = \left ( \frac{\theta}{2 \pi} \right ) \frac{\mu_0 i}{2R} \ or \ \left ( \frac{ \\\\mu_0}{4 \pi} \right ) \theta \end{align} \)

  3. Field along the axis of a solenoid is

    \( B = \frac{\mu_0 Ni}{2} (sin \ \alpha + sin \ \beta) \)

    1. For a long solenoid
      \( \begin{align} (L \gg R). \\\\[3pt] ie, \ \alpha = \beta = \frac{\pi}{2} \\\\[3pt] B = \mu_0 Ni \end{align} \)

    2. At the ends of solenoid.
      \( \begin{align} \alpha = 0, \ \beta = \frac{\pi}{2} \\\\[3pt] we \ get, \ B = \frac{1}{2} \mu_0 Ni \ \ \ \ \ \ (for L \gg R) \end{align} \) .

Ampere's law and its applications

Ampere's Circuital Law

It states that the line integral of \( \overrightarrow{B} \) around any closed path or circuit is equal to \( \mu_0 \) times the total current crossing the area bounded by the closed path provided the electric field inside till loop remains constant. Thus,
\( \int \overrightarrow{B}. \overrightarrow{dI} = \mu_0(i_{net}) \)
Its simplified form is \( Bl = \mu_0 i_{net} \)
This equation can be used only under following conditions

  1. at every point of the closed path \( Bl ||| \overrightarrow{dI} \)

  2. magnetic field has the same magnitude B at all place: on the closed path.

Applications of Ampere's Circuital Law

  1. Magnetic field due to a long metal rod of radius I carrying a current i :

    1. if \( r >, \ B = \left ( \frac{\mu_0 i}{2 \pi R^2} \right )r, \ i.e, \ B \ \propto \ r \)

    2. If r = R(i.e, at the surface)
      \( B = \left ( \frac{\mu_0 i}{2 \pi R} \right ) \)

Magnetic field of a solenoid wounded in the form of a helix is
\( B = \mu_0 ni \ \ \ \ \ \left ( n = \frac{N}{L} \right ) \)

Note:

Ampere s circuital law is valid only for steady currents. Further more, it is only for calculating the magnetic fields of current configurations with high degrees of symmetry, just as Gauss's law is useful only for calculating the electric fields of highly symmetric charge distributions.

Lorentz force, force on current carrying conductors in a magnetic field

Lorentz Forces

  1. If a charge q is moving with velocity \( \overrightarrow{V} \) enters in a region in which electric field \( \overrightarrow{E} \) and magnetic field \( \overrightarrow{B} \) both are present. it experiences force due to both fields simultaneously. The force experienced by the charged particle is given by the expression
    \( \overrightarrow{F} = q(\overrightarrow{v} \times \overrightarrow{B}) + q \overrightarrow{E} \)
    Here magnetic force \( \overrightarrow{F_m} = q(\overrightarrow{v} \times \overrightarrow{B} = Bqv \ sin \ \theta \)
    and electric force \( \overrightarrow{F_e} = q \overrightarrow{E} \) .

  2. The direction of magnetic force is same as \( \overrightarrow{v} \times \overrightarrow{B} \) if charge is positive and opposite to \( \overrightarrow{v} \times \overrightarrow{B} \) if charge q is negative.

Case

  1. If v = 0, then F = 0 ie, no force is exerted on a stationary charge. in a magnetic field,

  2. If \( \theta = 0 \) , then F = 0 ie, when the charge is moving parallel to the field then no force will be exerted by the field.

  3. If \( \theta = 90^{\circ} \) , then
    \( sin \ \theta = sin \ 90^{\circ} = 1 \)
    or \( F = qvB \times 1 = gvB \)
    ie, when the charged particle is moving perpendicular to the field, the force exerted by the field will be maximum.

Force on a Current Currying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

When a current carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, the conductor experience a force in a directior perpendicular to both the direction of magnetic field and the direction of current flowing in the conductor.

The direction of this force can be found out either by Fleming's left hand rule or by right hand palm rule.
The magnetic force is
\( F = ilB \ sin \ \theta \)
In vector form, \( \overrightarrow{F} = i(\overrightarrow{I} \times \overrightarrow{B}) \)
where B = intensity of magnetic field
i = current in the conductor
l = length of the conductor
and \( \theta \) = angle between the length of conductor and direction of magnetic field.

Case

  1. If \( \theta = 90^{\circ} \ or \ sin \ \theta = 1 \) then F = ilB(maximum)
    Therefore, force will be maximum when the conductor carrying current is perpendicular to magnetic field.

  2. If \( \theta = 0^{\circ} \ or \ sin \ \theta = 0 \)
    then \( F = ilB \times 0 = 0 \)
    Thus, the force will be zero, when the current carrying conductors is parallel to the field.

Magnetic moment of a current loop, torque on a current loop, Galvanometer and its conversion to voltmeter and ammeter

Behaviour of a Current Loop in a Magnetic Field

Consider a rectangular coil PQRS carrying a current placed uniform magnetic field \( \overrightarrow{B} \) in such a way that the normal \( \hat{n} \) to the coil makes an angle \( \begin{align} \\\\theta \end{align} \) with the direction of \( \overrightarrow{B} \) . Then the coil does not experience a net linear force but experiences a torque given by
or \ \( \overrightarrow{\tau} - Ni(\overrightarrow{A} \times \overrightarrow{B}) \)

As current carrying coil has a magnetic moment \( \overrightarrow{M} = Ni \overrightarrow{A} \) , hence, \( \overrightarrow{\tau} = \overrightarrow{M} \times \overrightarrow{B}) \) .

The Moving Coil Galvanometer (MCG)

It is a device whose principle is based on the torque on a current carrying loop. The MCG consists of a multi-turn coil free to rotate about a vertical axis, in a uniform radial magnetic field. There is a cylindrical soft iron core to increase the sensitivity of the MCG. When a current flows through the coil, a torque acts on it. This torque is given by \( \tau = NiAB \) .

where, the symbols have their usual meaning, since the field is radial by design, we have taken \( sin \ \theta \approx 1 \) in the above expression for the torque.
A spring \( S_0 \) , provides a counter torque resulting in a steady angular deflections \( \phi \) . In equilibrium, \( k \phi = NiAB \)
where k is the torsional constant of the spring. The deflection \( \phi \) is indicated on the scale by a pointer attached to the spring.
\( \phi = \left ( \frac{NAB}{k} \right ) i \)
The current sensitivity
\( \frac{\phi}{i} = \frac{NAB}{k} \)
If a pulse of current carrying charge \( \triangle{q} \) is passed through the galvanometer, then angular impulse applied by the magnetic field on the current carrying loop = \( \int \tau dt = \frac{NAB \triangle{q}}{k} \) which would provide an instantaneous angular velocity about the pivot to the coil.
