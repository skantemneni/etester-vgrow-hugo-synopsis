Kepler's Law

Based on trial and error, observation and using already compiled data by earlier physicists, Kepler discovered three empirical laws which accurately describe the motion of the planets. These laws are

  1. Kepler's First Law or Law of Orbits It states "All the planets move around the sun in an elliptical orbit with sun at one of the focus of ellipse". The point when the planet is nearest to sun is termed as perihelion and the farthest one is known as aphelion.

  2. Kepler's Second Law or Law of Areas It states "The line joining sun and planet sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time ie, areal velocity of planet w.r.t. su n is constant".

    According to second law, Area of region I = Area of region [I where, the planet takes same time to move from A to B and From C to D.]

  3. Keplar's Third Law or Law of Periods It states "The time period of revolution of planet around the sun is directly proportional to \( \frac{3}{2} \) power of the semi-major axis of elliptical orbit".
    \( T^2 \propto a^3 \) where a is the semi-major axis.
    Kepler's didn't have any explanation for his laws, neither he know why planets move in such way. But later Newton found that all three Kepler's laws can be derived using Newton's laws of motion and the law of gravitation.

Universal Law Of Gravitation

In this universe each body attracts other body with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
Let \( m_1 \) , and \( m_2 \) be the masses of two bodies and r be the separation between them.
\( F \propto \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \Rightarrow F = \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2} \)
Here, G is the constant of proportionality which is called 'Universal gravitational constant'. The value of G is \( 6.67 \times 10^{-11} N-m^2 kg^{-2} \) .
The direction of the force F is along the line joining the two particles. Regarding gravitational force, following points should be noted.

  1. The gravitational force between two particles is independent of the presence of other bodies or the properties of the intervening medium.

  2. Gravitational force is a conservative force therefore work done in displacing a body from one place to another is independent of the path followed: It depends only on the initial and final positions.

  3. The gravitational force obeys Newton's third Law ie.
    \( \vec{F}_{12} = - \vec{F}_{21} \)

Acceleration Due To Gravity

If M is the mass of earth and R is the radius, the earth attracts a mass m on its surface with a force F given by
\( F = \frac{GMm}{R^2} \)

This force imparts an acceleration to the mass m which is known as acceleration due to gravity (g).
By Newton's Law, we have
\( \begin{align} Acceleration = \frac{Force}{Mass} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow acceleration (g) = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{ \frac{GMm}{R^2}}{m} = \frac{GM}{R^2} \end{align} \)
On the surface of earth, \( g = \frac{G M}{R^2} \)
Substituting the values of G, M, R, we get \( g = 9.81 ms^{-2} \) Mass of the earth \( m = 6 \times 10^{24} kg \) and radius of the earth \( R = 6.4 \times 10^6 m \) .

Acceleration Due To Gravity Below And Above the Surface Of the Earth

  1. Above the surface of the earth At a point which is located at a height h above the earth's surface, acceleration due to gravity (g) is given by

    \( \begin{align} g' = \frac{Force}{mass} \\\\[6pt] = \frac{ \frac{GMm}{(R + h)^2}}{m} = \frac{GM}{(R + h)^2} \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow g' = g \left ( \frac{R}{R + h} \right )^2 \end{align} \)
    where g is the acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the earth.

  2. Below the surface of the earth Acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the earth is
    \( g = \frac{GM}{R^2} = \frac{4}{3} \pi \rho G R ........(i) \)

    where \( \rho \) is the density of earth.
    Acceleration due to gravity at depth d from the surface of the earth is
    \( g' = \frac{4}{3} \pi \rho G(R - d)........ (ii) \)
    From Eqs. (i) and (ii), \( g' = g \left [ 1 - \frac{d}{R} \right ] \)
    where g is the acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the earth.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy can be defined in two ways. These are

  1. Interaction energy

  2. Self energy

Interaction Energy

This energy exists in a system of particles or bodies due to ie force of interaction between the particles or bodies. This is defined as the -ve of work done by the gravitational force in assembling the system from some reference configuration to the desired configuration.

  1. For two particle system,

    \( U = - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r} \)
    Here, the reference position is taken when particles are at infinite separation.

  2. For three particles system as shown in the figure.

    \( U = - \left [ \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r_{12}} + \frac{Gm_1 m_3}{r_{13}} + \frac{Gm_2 m_3}{r_{23}} \right ] \)

  3. For n particle system, \( \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} \) pairs are formed and total interaction energy of the system is sum of all such pairs.

Self Energy

It is the energy possessed by a body due to forces of interaction acting inside the body. For individual particles, there is no self energy is there.

  1. For a hollow sphere, self energy, \( U_s = - \frac{Gm^2}{2R} \)

  2. For a solid sphere, self energy, \( U_s = - \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{G m^2}{R} \)

  3. For a system of bodies, the total gravitational potential energy is the sum of self energies of each body and the interaction energy between them.

Earth Satellites And Escape Speed

It is the minimum velocity with which a body must be projected from the surface of earth so that it escapes the gravitational field of the earth. We can also say that a body, projected with escape velocity, will be able to go to a point which is at infinite distance from the earth.

Let us imagine what happens to a body of mass m if it is thrown from the earth with a velocity \( v_e \) (escape velocity). As the body moves away from the earth, it slows down (due to gravitational pull of the earth) and hence, its kinetic energy is converted into gravitational potential energy of the mass-earth system. Let us imagine that it is just able to reach upto infinity (where gravitational potential energy is zero)
KE lost by mass m = gain in gravitational potential energy of mass-earth system
\( \frac{1}{2} mv_e^2 = U_f - U_i \)
\( \begin{align} \frac{1}{2} mv_e^2 = 0 - \left ( - \frac{GMm}{R} \right ) ( \because \text{ Final Potential energy is zero}) \\\\[6pt] \Rightarrow v_e = \sqrt{2GM}{R} \ or \ v_e = \sqrt{2gR} \end{align} \)
Substituting the values of \( g = 9.81 ms^{-2} \) and R = 6400 km. we get,
\( v_e = 11.3 kms^{-1} \)
Hence, any object thrown with a velocity of \( 11.3 kms^{-1} \) or more will escape the gravitational field of the earth and will never come back to the earth.

Summary

  1. Newton's law of universal gravitation states that the gravitational force of attraction between any two particles of masses m1 and m2 separated by a distance r has the magnitude
    \( F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \)
    where G is the universal gravitational constant, which has the value \( 6.672 \times 10^{-11} \ N \ m^2 \ kg^{-2} \)

  2. If we have to find the resultant gravitational force acting on the particle m due to a number of masses \( M_1, \ M_2, \ .... \ M_n \) etc. we use the principle of superposition. Let \( F_1, \ F_2, \ .... \ F_n \) be the individual forces due to \( M_1, \ M_2, \ .... \ M_n \) , each given by the law of gravitation. From the principle of superposition each force acts independently and uninfluenced by the other bodies. The resultant force FR is then found by vector addition
    \( F_R = F_1 + F_2 + .... + F_N = \sum \limits_{i = 1}^{n} F_i \)
    where the symbol \( ' \sum ' \) stands for summation

  3. Kepler's laws of planetary motion state that

    1. All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one of the focal points

    2. The radius vector drawn from the sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal time intervals. This follows from the fact that the force of gravitation on the planet is central and hence angular momentum is conserved.

    3. The square of the orbital period of a planet is proportional to the cube of the semimajor axis of the elliptical orbit of the planet

    The period T and radius R of the circular orbit of a planet about the Sun are related by
    \( T^2 = \left ( \frac{4 \pi^2}{G \ M_s} \right ) R^3 \)
    where \( M_s \) is the mass of the Sun. Most planets have nearly circular orbits about the Sun. For elliptical orbits, the above equation is valid if R is replaced by the semi-major axis, a.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Let the speed of the planet at the perihelion P in Fig.(a) be \( v_P \) and the Sun-planet distance SP be \( r_P \) . Relate { \( r_P, \ v_P \) } to the corresponding quantities at the aphelion { \( r_A, \ v_A \) }. Will the planet take equal times to traverse BAC and CPB?
Solution

The magnitude of the angular momentum at P is \( L_P = m_p \ r_p \ v_p \) , since inspection tells us that \( r_P \) and \( v_P \) are mutually perpendicular. Similarly, \( L_A = m_P \ r_A \ v_A \) From angular momentum conservation
\( m_p \ r_p \ v_p = m_P \ r_A \ or \ \frac{v_P}{v_A} = \frac{r_A}{r_P} \)

Example 2:

Find the potential energy of a system of four particles placed at the vertices of a square of side l. Also obtain the potential at the centre of the square.
Solution

Consider four masses each of mass m at the corners of a square of side l ; See Fig. We have four mass pairs at distance l and two
diagonal pairs at distance \( \sqrt{2} l \)
Hence,
\( \begin{align} W(r) = - 4 \frac{G m^2}{l} - 2 \frac{G m^2}{\sqrt{2} l} \\\\[3pt] = - \frac{2 G m^2}{l} \left ( 2 + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right ) = - 5.41 \frac{G m^2}{l} \end{align} \)
The gravitational potential at the centre of the square \( \left ( r = \frac{\sqrt{2}l}{2} \right ) \ is \ U(r) = - 4 \sqrt{2} \frac{Gm}{l} \)

Example 3:

Two uniform solid spheres of equal radii R, but mass M and 4 M have a center to centre separation 6 R, as shown in Fig. The two spheres are held fixed. A projectile of mass m is projected from the surface of the sphere of mass M directly towards the centre of the second sphere. Obtain an expression for the minimum speed v of the projectile so that it reaches the surface of the second sphere.

Solution
The projectile is acted upon by two mutually opposing gravitational forces of the two spheres. The neutral point N (see Fig. ) is defined as the position where the two forces cancel each other exactly. If ON = r, we have
\( \begin{align} \frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{4 G M m}{(6R - r)^2} = (6R - r)^2 = 4r^2 \\\\[3pt] 6R - r = \pm 2r = r = 2R \ or \ - 6R \end{align} \) .

The neutral point r = - 6R does not concern us in this example. Thus ON = r = 2R. It is sufficient to project the particle with a speed which would enable it to reach N. Thereafter, the greater gravitational pull of 4M would suffice. The mechanical energy at the surface of M is
\( E_t = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} - \frac{4GMm}{5R} \)
At the neutral point N, the speed approaches zero. The mechanical energy at N is purely potential.
\( \begin{align} \frac{1}{2} v^2 - \frac{GM}{R} - \frac{4GM}{5R} = - \frac{GM}{2R} - \frac{GM}{R} \\\\[3pt] v^2 = \frac{2 GM}{R} \left ( \frac{4}{5} - \frac{1}{2} \right ) \\\\[3pt] v = \left ( \frac{3 GM}{5R} \right )^{\frac{1}{2}} \end{align} \)
A point to note is that the speed of the projectile is zero at N, but is nonzero when it strikes the heavier sphere 4 M. The calculation of this speed is left as an exercise to the students.

Example 4:

The planet Mars has two moons, phobos and delmos.

  1. phobos has a period 7 hours, 39 minutes and an orbital radius of \( 9.4 \times 10^3 \ km \) . Calculate the mass of mars. ,

  2. Assume that earth and mars move in circular orbits around the sun, with the martian orbit being 1.52 times the orbital radius of the earth. What is the length of the martian year in days?

Solution

  1. the sun's mass replaced by the martian mass \( M_m \)
    \( \begin{align} T^2 = \frac{4 \pi^2}{GM_m} R^3 \\\\[3pt] M_m = \frac{4 \pi^2}{G} \frac{R^3}{T^2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{4 \times (3.14)^2 \times (9.4)^3 \times 10^{18}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times (459 \times 6)^2 \times 10^{-5}} \\\\[3pt] = 6.48 \times 10^{23} \ kg \end{align} \)

  2. Once again Kepler's third law comes to our aid,
    \( \frac{{T_M}^2}{{T_E}^2} = \frac{{R_{MS}}^3}{{R_{ES}}^3} \)
    where \( R_{MS} \) is the mars-sun distance and \( R_{ES} \) is the earth-sun distance.
    \( \therefore \ T_m = (1.52)^{\frac{3}{2}} \times 365 = 684 \ days \)
    We note that the orbits of all planets except Mercury, Mars and Pluto are very close to being circular. For example, the ratio of the semiminor to semi-major axis for our Earth is, \( \frac{b}{a} \) = 0.99986.

Example 5:

A 400 kg satellite is in a circular orbit of radius \( 2R_E \) about the Earth. How much energy is required to transfer it to a circular orbit of radius \( 4R_E \) ? What are the changes in the kinetic and potential energies?
Solution Initially,
\( \begin{align} E_i = \frac{G M_k m}{4 R_E} \\\\[3pt] \text{While finally} \\\\[3pt] E_f = - \frac{G M_e m}{8 R_E} \\\\[3pt] \text{The change in the total energy is} \\\\[3pt] \triangle{E} = E_f - E_i \\\\[3pt] = \frac{G M_E m}{8 R_E} = \left ( \frac{G M_E}{{R_E}^2} \right ) \frac{m R_E}{8} \\\\[3pt] \triangle{E} = \frac{g m R_E}{8} = \frac{9.81 \times 400 \times 6.37 \times 10^6}{8} = 3.13 \times 10^9 \ J \end{align} \)
The kinetic energy is reduced and it mimics
\( \triangle{E} \) , namely, \( \triangle{K} = K_f - K_i = - 3.13 \times 10^9 \ J \)
The change in potential energy is twice the change in the total energy, namely
\( \triangle{V} = V_f - V_i = - 6.25 \times 10^9 \ J \)
