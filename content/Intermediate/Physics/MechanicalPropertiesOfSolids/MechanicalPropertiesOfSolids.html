Youngs Modulus

If a body is deformed by applying forces along one dimension only, then within the elastic limit, the ratio of normal stress (either compressive or tensile) to the longitudinal strain is called the Young's modulus of the material of the body and is represented by the letter Y ie,
\( Y = \frac{linear \ stress}{longitudinal \ strain} \)

Consider a metal wire AB of length l, radius r and of uniform area of cross-section A. Let it be suspended from a rigid support at P. Let the stretching force F be applied normally at its free end Q and let its length increase by \( \Delta l(= QQ') \)
Then longitudinal strain = \( \frac{\Delta l}{l} \)
Normal stress = \( \frac{F}{A} = \frac{F}{ \pi r^2} \ \ \ \ (\because \ A = \pi r^2) \)
\( \therefore Y = \frac{ \frac{F}{\pi r^2}}{ \frac{\Delta l}{l}} = \frac{Fl}{\pi r^2 \Delta l} \)
The unit of Young's modulus of elasticity in SI is \( Nm^{-2} \) or Pascal (denoted by Pa) and in CGS system is \( dyne \ cm^{-2} \) . Its dimensional formula is \( [ML^{-1}T^{-2}] \)

Rigidity Modulus And Bulk Modulus

Bulk Modulus Of Elasticity

It is defined as the ratio of the normal stress to the volumetric strain. It is denoted by K. Thus,
\( K = \frac{normal \ stress}{volumetric \ strain} \)
Suppose a force F acts uniformly over the whole surface of the sphere (shown), decreasing its volume by \( \Delta V \) .Then,

\( k = \frac{ \frac{F}{A}}{ - \frac{\Delta V}{V}} \)
or \( k = \frac{- FV}{A \Delta V} = - \frac{pV}{\Delta V} \)
The negative sign indicates that on increasing stress the volume of the sphere decreases. The units of bulk modulus are Pa or \( Nm^{-2} \) in SI system.
For gases, bulk modulus is of two types:

  1. Isothermal bulk modulus of elasticity \( (K_i) \) which is equal to the pressure of gas (p).

  2. Adiabatic bulk modulus of elasticity \( K_a \) which is equal to \( \gamma p \) ,
    where \( \gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v} \)
    whereas isothermal elasticity \( (K_i) \) , is given by
    \( \begin{align} K_i = p \\\\[6pt] \therefore \frac{K_a}{K_i} = \gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v} > 1 \end{align} \)
    As \( C_p \> C_v \) . So, \( K_a \> K_i \)
    K is maximum for solids, less for liquids and least for gases.

Compressibility The reciprocal of the bulk modulus of the material of the body is called the compressibility of the material. Thus,
Compressibility = \( \frac{1}{K} = - \frac{1}{V} \left ( \frac{ \Delta V}{\Delta p} \right ) \) Its unit is \( N^{-1} m^2 \) or \( Pa^{-1} \) in SI system.

Modulus Of Rigidity

It is defined as the ratio of tangential stress to shearing strain. It is also called shear modulus. It is denoted by Greek letter \( \eta \) (eta). Thus,
\( \eta = \frac{Tangential \ stress}{Shearing \ strain} \)
Here a body (shown) is acted upon by an external force tangential to the surface of the body, the opposite face being kept fixed, its volume remaining unchanged. Then,

\( \eta = \frac{ \frac{F}{A}}{ \frac{DD'}{AD}} \)
Here, \( tan \phi \approx \phi = \frac{DD'}{AD} \)
\( \eta = \frac{ \frac{F}{A}}{\phi} \)
or \( \eta = \frac{F}{A \phi} \)
The units of modulus of rigidity are Pa or \( Nm^{-2} \) in SI system.

Strain Energy

When the size or shape of a body is changed under an external force, the body is said to be strained. The change occurred in the unit size of the body is called strain. Usually it is denoted by \( \epsilon \) .
\( Strain = \frac{\text{change in dimension}}{\text{original dimension}} = \frac{\Delta x}{x} \) Here, \( \Delta x \) is the change (may be in length, in volume etc) and x the original value of quantity in which change has occurred.
It has no dimension as it is a pure number.
Since a body may have three types of deformation. ie. in length, in volume or in shape, likewise there are following three types of strains.

Longitudinal Strain

It is defined as the change in length per unit origin of the body under deformation by the external force. Then

\( \begin{align} longitudinal \ strain = \frac{\text{change in length}}{\text{original length}} \\\\[6pt] or \ \epsilon = \frac{\Delta l}{l} \end{align} \)
It is of two types:

  1. Tensile strain If on applying a deforming force there is an increase of \( \Delta l \) in length of a rod, then strain produced in the rod is called tensile strain.

  2. Compressive strain If on applying a deforming force there is decrease of \( \Delta l \) in length of a rod, then strain produced in the rod is called compressive strain.

Volumetric Strain

It is defined as the change in volume per unit original volume of the body under deformation by the external force.

\( Volumetric \ strain = \frac{\text{change in volume}}{\text{original volume}} \)
or \( \epsilon_V = \frac{\Delta V}{V} \)

Shearing Strain

This type of strain is produced when a shearing stress is present.

It is defined as the angle in radians through which a plane perpendicular to the fixed surface of the cubical body is turned under the effect of tangential force, \( \phi = \frac{x}{l} \)

Summary

  1. Stress is the restoring force per unit area and strain is the fractional change in dimension. In general there are three types of stresses

    1. tensile stress - longitudinal stress (associated with stretching) or compressive stress (associated with compression),

    2. shearing stress, and

    3. hydraulic stress.

  2. For small deformations, stress is directly proportional to the strain for many materials. This is known as Hooke's law. The constant of proportionality is called modulus of elasticity. Three elastic moduli viz., Young's modulus, shear modulus and bulk modulus are used to describe the elastic behaviour of objects as they respond to deforming forces that act on them.
    A class of solids called elastomers does not obey Hooke's law.

  3. When an object is under tension or compression, the Hooke's law takes the form
    \( \frac{F}{A} = Y \frac{\triangle {L}}{L} \)
    where \( \frac{F}{A} = Y \frac{\triangle {L}}{L} \) is the tensile or compressive strain of the object, F is the magnitude of the applied force causing the strain, A is the cross-sectional area over which F is applied (perpendicular to A) and Y is the Young's modulus for the object. The stress is \( \frac{F}{A} \)

  4. A pair of forces when applied parallel to the upper and lower faces, the solid deforms so that the upper face moves sideways with respect to the lower. The horizontal displacement \( \triangle {L} \) of the upper face is perpendicular to the vertical height L. This type of deformation is called shear and the corresponding stress is the shearing stress. This type of stress is possible only in solids In this kind of deformation the Hooke's law takes the form
    \( \frac{F}{A} = Y G \times \frac{\triangle {L}}{L} \)
    where \( \triangle {L} \) is the displacement of one end of object in the direction of the applied force F, and G is the shear modulus.

  5. When an object undergoes hydraulic compression due to a stress exerted by a surrounding fluid, the Hooke's law takes the form
    \( p = B \left ( \frac{\triangle{V}}{V} \right ) \) .
    where p is the pressure (hydraulic stress) on the object due to the fluid, \( \frac{\triangle{V}}{V} \) (the volume strain) is the absolute fractional change in the object's volume due to that pressure and B is the bulk modulus of the object.

Solved examples

Example 1:

A structural steel rod has a radius of 10 mm and a length of 1.0 m. A 100 kN force stretches it along its length. Calculate

  1. stress,

  2. elongation, and

  3. strain on the rod. Young's modulus, of structural steel is \( 2.0 \times 10^{11} \ N \ m^{-2} \) .

Solution
We assume that the rod is held by a clamp at one end, and the force F is applied at the other end, parallel to the length of the rod. Then the stress on the rod is given by
\( \begin{align} Stress = \frac{F}{A} - \frac{F}{\pi r^2} \\\\[3pt] \frac{100 \times 10^3 \ N}{3.14 \times 10^{-2}m^{-2}} \\\\[3pt] = 3.18 \times 10^8 \ N \ M^{-2} \\\\[3pt] \text{The elongation,} \\\\[3pt] \triangle{L} = \frac{ \left ( \frac{F}{A} \right ) }{Y} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{3.18 \times 10^8 \ N \ M^{-2} \times 1 m}{2 \times 10^{11} \ N \ M^{-2}} \\\\[3pt] = 1.59 \times 10^{-3} \ m \\\\[3pt] = 1.59 \ mm \\\\[3pt] \text{The strain is given by} \\\\[3pt] Strain = \frac{\triangle{L}}{L} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{(1.59 \times 10^{-9} \ m}{(1m)} \\\\[3pt] = 1.59 \times 10^{-3} \\\\[3pt] = 0.16 \ \% \end{align} \)

Example 2:

A copper wire of length 2.2 m and a steel wire of length 1.6 m, both of diameter 3.0 mm, are connected end to end. When stretched by a load, the net elongation is found to be 0.70 mm. Obtain the load applied.
Solution
The copper and steel wires are under a tensile stress because they have the same tension (equal to the load W) and the same area of cross-section A. From Eq. we have stress = strain \( \times \) Young's modulus. Therefore
\( \frac{W}{A} = Y_c \times \left ( \frac{\triangle{L_c}}{L_c} \right ) = Y_s \times \left ( \frac{\triangle{L_s}}{L_s} \right ) \)
where the subscripts c and s refer to copper and stainless steel respectively. Or,
\( \begin{align} \frac{\triangle{L_c}}{\triangle{L_s}} = \left ( \frac{Y_s}{Y_c} \right ) \times \left ( \frac{L_c}{L_s} \right ) \\\\[3pt] Given \ L_c = 2.2 \ m, \ L_s = 1.6 \ m, \ \\\\[3pt] Y_c = 1.1 \times 10^{11} \ N.m^{-2}, \ and \ Y_s = 2.0 \times 10^{11} \ N. m^{-2} \\\\[3pt] \left ( \frac{\triangle{L_c}}{\triangle{L_s}} \right ) = \left ( \frac{2.0 \times 10{11}}{1.1 \times 10^{11}} \right ) \times \left ( \frac{2.2}{1.6} \right ) = 2.5. \end{align} \)
The total elongation is given to be
\( \triangle{L_c} + \triangle{L_s} = 7.0 \times 10^{-4} \ m \)
Solving the above equations,
\( \begin{align} \triangle{L_c} = 5.0 \times 10^{-4} \ m, \ and \ \triangle{L_s} = 2.0 \times 10^{-4} \ m \\\\[3pt] Therefore \\\\[3pt] W = \frac{\left ( A \times Y_c \times \triangle{L_c} \right )}{L_c} \\\\[3pt] = \pi (1.5 \times 10^{-3})^2 \times \left [ \frac{5.0 \times 10^{-4} \times 1.1 \times 10^{11}}{2.2} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = 1.8 \times 10^2 N \end{align} \)

Example 3:

In a human pyramid in a circus, the entire weight of the balanced group is supported by the legs of a performer who is lying on his back (as shown in Fig.). The combined mass of all the persons performing the act, and the tables, plaques etc. involved is 280 kg. The mass of the performer lying on his back at the bottom of the pyramid is 60 kg. Each thighbone (femur) of this performer has a length of 50 cm and an effective radius of 2.0 cm. Determine the amount by which each thighbone gets compressed under the extra load.

Solution
Total mass of all the performers, tables, plaques etc. = 280 kg
Mass of the performer = 60 kg
Mass supported by the legs of the performer at the bottom of the pyramid
= 280 - 60 = 220 kg
Weight of this supported mass
\( = 220 \ kg \ wt. = 220 \times 9.8 \ N = 2156 \ N. \)
Weight supported by each thighbone of the performer = \( \frac{1}{2} (2156)N = 1078 \ N \) .
From, the Young's modulus for bone is given by
\( Y = 9.4 \times 10^{9} \ N \ m^{-2} \)
. Length of each thighbone L = 0.5 m
the radius of thighbone = 2.0 cm
Thus the cross-sectional area of the thighbone
\( A = \pi \times (2 \times 10^{-2})^2 m^2 = 1.26 \times 10^{-3} \ m^2 \) .
the compression in each thighbone \( (\triangle{L}) \) can be computed as
\( \begin{align} (\triangle{L}) = \left[ \frac{(F \times L)}{(Y \times A)} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = \left [ \frac{ (1078 \times 0.5)}{(9.4 \times 10^9 \times 1.26 \times 10^{-3})} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = 4.55 \times 10^{-5} \ m \ or \ 4.55 \times 10^{-3} \ cm \end{align} \) .
This is a very small change! The fractional decrease in the thighbone is \( \frac{\triangle{L}}{L} \) = 0.000091 or 0.0091%.

Example 4:

A square lead slab of side 50 cm and thickness 10 cm is subject to a shearing force (on its narrow face) of \( 9.0 \times 10^4 \ N \) . The lower edge is riveted to the floor. How much will the upper edge be displaced?

Solution
The lead slab is fixed and the force is applied parallel to the narrow face as shown in Fig.The area of the face parallel to which this force is applied is
\( \begin{align} A = 50 \ cm \times 10 \ cm \\\\[3pt] = 0.5 \ m \times 0.1 \ m \\\\[3pt] = 0.05 \ m^2 \end{align} \)
Therefore, the stress applied is
\( \begin{align} = \frac{\left(9.4 \times 10^4 N \right )}{0.05 \ m^2} \\\\[3pt] = 1.80 \times 10^6 \ N.m^2 \end{align} \)
We know that shearing strain \( = \left ( \frac{\triangle{x}}{L} \right ) = \frac{Stress}{G}. \)
Therefore the displacement \( \triangle{x} = \frac{(Stress \times L)}{G} \)
\( \begin{align} \frac{(1.8 \times 10^6 \ N \ m^{-2} \times 0.5 m)}{5.6 \times 10^9 \ N \ m^{-2}} \\\\[3pt] = 1.6 \times 10^{-4} m = 0.16 \ mm \end{align} \)

Example 5:

The average depth of Indian Ocean is about 3000 m. Calculate the fractional compression, \( \frac{\triangle{V}}{V} \) , of water at the bottom of the ocean, given that the bulk modulus of water is \( 2.2 \times 10^9 \ N \ m^{-2} \) . (Take \( g = 10 \ m \ s^{-2} \) )
Solution
The pressure exerted by a 3000 m column of water on the bottom layer
\( \begin{align} p = hp g = 3000 \ m \times 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3} \times 10 \ m \ s^{-2} \\\\[3pt] = 3 \times 10^7 \ kg \ m^{-1} s^{-2} \\\\[3pt] = 3 \times 10^7 \ N \ m^{-2} \end{align} \)
Fractional compression \( \frac{\triangle{V}}{V} \) , is \( \begin{align} \frac{\triangle{V}}{V} = \frac{(3 \times 10^7 \ N \ m^{-2})}{(2.2 \times 10^9 \ N \ m^{-2})} \\\\[3pt] = 1.36 \times 10^{-2} \ or \ 1.36 \% \end{align} \)
