Laws of Chemical Combinations

Lavoisier established the "law of conservation of mass" according to which the matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

  1. Lavoisier's law of conservation of mass helped in establishing the law of constant composition or the law of definite proportions (given by J. Proust) which states, "A sample of a pure compound always consists of the same elements combined in same proportions by mass, whatever be its source. eg, ammonia always has the formula \( NH_3 \) ie, one molecule of \( NH_3 \) always contains one atom of nitrogen and three atoms of hydrogen or 17.0 g of \( NH_3 \) always contains 14 g of \( N_2 \) and g of hydrogen. These findings always remain the same for \( NH_3. \)

  2. John Dalton observed that an element may form more than one compound with another element. He note that for a given mass of an element, the masses of other elements (in 2 or more compounds) come in the ratio of small integers. This is called "law of multiple proportions". eg, in \( NH_3, \) 14 g of nitrogen requires 3 g of hydrogen and in hydrazine \( (N_2H_4) \) 14 g of nitrogen requires 2 g of hydrogen. Hence, fixed mass of nitrogen requires hydrogen in the ratio 3 : 2 in two different compounds (3 : 2 is a simple ratio). Thus, this is in agreement with "law of multiple proportions".

  3. "The volume of reactants and products in a large number of chemical reactions are related to each other by small integers, provided the volumes are measured at same temperature and pressure". These lines are considered as "the law of definite proportions by volume" given by Gay-Lussac, a French chemist. eg, in the reaction of hydrogen with oxygen to produce water it was found that 2 vol of \( H_2 \) combines with 1 vol of \( O_2 \) to form 2 vol of \( H_2O \) (steam). This simply means that 100 mL of \( H_2 \) gas combines with 50 mL of \( O_2 \) to produce exactly 100 mL of steam (if all the gases are measured at same temperature and pressure).

  4. "The volume of a gas (at constant pressure and temperature) is proportional to the number of moles (or molecules) of gas present". This is called Avogadro's law. Hence, according to this law :
    \( V \propto n \)
    where, n = number of moles of gas.

Mole Concept

The word 'mole' was introduced around 1896 by W. Ostwald who derived it from Latin word 'moles' means a 'heap' or 'pile'. In 1967 this word was accepted as a unit of chemical substances under SI system. It is represented by the symbol 'mol'.
One mole of any substance is defined as :

  1. The amount which weighs exactly same as its formula weight in gram or atomic mass in gram or molecular mass in gram.

  2. The amount which has same number of entities (atoms, molecules or other particles) as there are atoms in exactly 0.012 kg (or 12 g) of carbon -12 isotope (ie, \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) entities). Here, \( 6.023 \times 10^{23} \) is called Avogadro's number and denoted by \( N_A . \)

  3. The amount which occupies 22.4 L at STP (if it is taken for a gas).

Formulae Used to Calculate the Number of Moles

  1. Number of moles of molecules = \( \frac{weight \ in \ gram}{molecular \ weight} \)

  2. Number of moles of atoms = \( \frac{weight \ in \ gram}{atomic \ weight} \)

  3. Number of moles of gases = \( \frac{volume \ at \ NTP}{standard \ molar \ volume} \)
    (Standard molar volume = volume occupied by 1 mole of any gas at NTP ie, 22.4 L).

  4. Number of moles of atoms/molecules/ions/electrons no. of atoms/ molecules/ ions/ electrons = \( \begin{align} \frac{no. \ of \ atoms/molecules/ions/electrons}{Avogadro' s number (N_A) } \\\\[3pt] N_A = 6.023 \times 10^{23} \end{align} \)

  5. Number of moles of solute = \( molarity \times volume \ of \ solution \ in \ litres \)
    or Number of millimoles = \( molarity \times volume \ in \ mL \)
    \( Moles = \frac{millimoles}{100} \)

  6. Number of equivalents of a substance = \( \frac{weight(g)}{equivalent \ weight} \)

  7. Number of milli - equivalent (Meq) = \( \frac{1000 \times weight(g)}{equivalent \ weight} = normality \times volume (mL) \)

  8. Normality = molarity \( \times \) balance factor (y).
    where, y = acidity/basicity/number of replaceable H-atoms/change in oxidation number.

  9. Number of equivalents = y \( \times \) number of moles

  10. Number of milli - equivalents = y \( \times \) number of millimoles where, y is same as for formula (8).

  11. Mole fraction of solute in the solution = \( \frac{n}{n + N} \)
    where, n = moles of solute, N = moles of solvent

  12. For very dilute solution,
    Mole fraction of solute in solution = \( \frac{Mm}{1000} \)
    where, M = molecular weight of solvent, m = molality

  13. Density of solution
    \( = molarity \left ( \frac{1}{molality} + \frac{molecular \ weight \ of \ solute }{1000} \right) \)

    1. % by weight of solute in solution = \( \frac{weight \ of \ solute(g) \times 100}{weight \ of \ solution(g)} \)

    2. % by volume of solute in solution = \( \frac{weight \ of \ solute(g) \times 100}{volume \ of \ solution(mL)} \)

  14. \( 1 mL, \ 1 N \ KMnO_4 \equiv 1 mL, \ 1 N \ H_2O_2, \)

  15. \( 1 mL, \ 1 N \ Na_2S_2O_3 \equiv 1 mL, \ 1 N \ I_2, \ solution \)

  16. \( 1 volume \ H_2O_2 = 0.1785 \ N \ H_2O_2 \)

  17. If two compounds A and B neutralise completely each other then
    milli - equivalent of A = milli - equivalents of B

  18. Molecular weight = 2 \( \times \) vapour density (for gaseous phase).

Empirical and Molecular Formulae

The chemical formula of a compound which shows relative number of atoms in its simplest ratio is called empirical formula.

  1. The chemical formula of a compound which gives the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule is called its molecular formula. It is important to note that the molecular formula is generally an integral multiple of the empirical formula, ie,
    Molecular formula = (empirical formula) \( \times \) n where, n = 1, 2, 3, ... etc

  2. The molecular formula conveys two informations mainly :

  1. The relative number of each type of atoms in a molecule,

  2. The total of atoms of each element in the molecule.

Stoichiometry

The methods used to describe the atomic and molecular masses constitute the generalised approach to chemical calculations and quantitative methods called chemical stoichiometry. (Stoichiometry from Greek words stoichion = element; metron = measure). Solving of stoichiometric problems require a firm grasp of mole concept, balancing chemical equations and care in consistent use of units.

Limiting Reactant or Limiting Reagent

If a reaction involves two or more reactants, the reactant that is consumed first is called limiting reagent or limiting reactant as it limits the amount of product formed. eg, If 2 moles each of hydrogen and oxygen is ignited, the limiting reactant is hydrogen. This is found as :

  1. First we write the balance equation of formation of water.
    \( \underset{1 \ mole}{H_2} + \underset{\frac{1}{2} mol} {\frac{1}{2} O_2 } \rightarrow \underset{1 \ mole}{H_2O} \)

  2. From the equation, it is clear that for 1 mole of hydrogen half mole oxygen is required. Hence, for 2 mol hydrogen, oxygen required =1 mol. Thus, for this process, oxygen is in excess and hydrogen is limited, so hydrogen is the limiting reagent.

Balancing of Chemical Equations

A balanced chemical equation gives a lot of quantitative information about a chemical reaction. It provides the quantitative relationship between the masses of reactants and the products, ie, the quantity of substance consumed and produced can be determined by it only.
Hence, here first of all we are describing the steps of simplest and generalised method of balancing the equation, ie, the trial and error method.
For the reaction:
\( C_3H_8(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 3CO_2(g) + H+2O(l) \)

Step 1 Balance the number of C-atoms :

Since 3 C-atoms are present in reactants, hence \( 3CO_2 \) molecules are required on product side, ie,
\( C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow 3CO_2 + H_2O \)

Step 2 Balance the number of hydrogen atoms :

As 8 hydrogen atoms are present in reactants and one molecule of water contains 2H atoms in it, hence the product side should contain \( 4H_2O \) molecules, ie,
\( C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow 3CO_2 + H_2O \)

Step 3 Balance the number of oxygen atoms :

As 10 oxygen atoms are present on the product side and reactant side have only 2 oxygen atoms, hence the \( O_2 \) molecule on the reactant side should be multiplied by 5 ie,
\( C_3H_8 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 3CO_2 + 4H_2O \)

Step 4 Verify that the number of atoms of each element is balanced in the final equation.

(The equation shows 3 C - atoms, 8 H - atoms and 10 O - atoms on both the sides of arrow.)

  1. The above trial and error method is also called POAC (principle of atomic conservation) method.

  2. Another method for balancing the equation is called equivalent (or milliequivalent) method. This method is based on the fact that for the different amounts of reactants and products involved in a reaction, the number of equivalents or milliequivalents are equal for each reactant and each product.
    eg, for the reaction
    \( \begin{align} VO + Fe_2O_3 \rightarrow FeO + V_2O_5 \\\\[3pt] Equivalent \ or \ meq. \ of \ VO \ = \ equivalent \ or \ meq. \ of \ Fe_2O_3 \ = \ equivalent \ or \ meq. \ of \ FeO \ = \ equivalent \ or \ men. \ of \ V_2O_5. \end{align} \)
    This method is generally applied in volumetric stiochiometric analysis
