III A - General Properties, Important Compounds of Boron

Boron (B) is the first member of 13th group and occurs in two isotopic forms ie, \( ^{10}B (19 \%) \) and \( ^{11}B (81 \%) \) . The abundance of B in the earth crust is less than \( 0.001 \% \) by mass. B occurs mainly as the salts of boric acid.

Extraction of Boron

Ores of boron The important ores are
Borax : \( Na_2B_4O_7 . 10H_2O \)
Colemanite : \( Ca_2B_6O_{11} . 5H_2O \)
Panderinite : \( Ca_2B_6O_{11} . 3H_2O \)
Boracite : \( 2Mg_3B_8O_{15} . MgCl_2 \)
Kernite : \( Na_2B_4O_5(OH)_4 \)

Preparation of Boron

The outline of preparation of elemental B involves any one of the following methods

  1. Reduction of boron oxide by an electropositive metal.
    \( B_2O_3(s) + 3Mg(s) \longrightarrow 2B(s) + 3MgO(s) \)

  2. Electrolytic reduction of fused borates or tetra-fluoroborates \( (KBF_4) \) in molten KCl/KF at 1073 K.

  3. By reduction (hydrogenation) of a volatile boron compound with \( H_2 \) at high temperature on a heated tantalum filament as,
    \( 2BCl_3(g) + 3H_2(g) \xrightarrow{1270 K} 2B(s) + 6HCl(g) \)
    Boron obtained by this method is of high purity (almost 99.9%).

  4. By thermal decomposition of boranes and boron halides at temperatures above than \( 900^{\circ}C \)

Physical Properties of Boron

Boron is a non-metallic element. It is poor conductor of electricity and is non-fusible under ordinary pressure. Its ionisation energy is highest among the group. It is an extremely hard refractory solid

Uses of Boron

  1. Boron is used for making bullet-proof vests and light composite material for aircrafts.

  2. It is used in nuclear industry as protective shields and control rods.

  3. It is used in steel industry to increase the hardness of steel.

  4. It is used as a semiconductor for making electronic devices.

  5. Many important compounds like borax, boric acid etc, are synthesized from it.

  6. It is used as a deoxidiser in casting of iron.

IV A - General Properties, Important Compounds of Carbon and Silicon

Carbon shows allotropism due to the property of catenation and \( p \pi - p \pi \) bond formation. It exists in two allotropic forms

Crystalline Forms

It is transparent to X-rays. It is a three dimensional giant molecule of carbon in which C-atoms \( (sp^3-hybridised) \) are arranged tetrahedrally by utilising their \( sp^3 \) hybrid orbitals. Carbon

It is one of the most widely distributed element. It occurs naturally in pure forms like diamond and graphite and more abundantly as coal. But coal is not as pure as diamond and graphite. All the naturally occurring forms are recovered as such from their respective mines.
Method of preparation Graphite and diamond can be prepared artificially also.
Artificial diamonds are prepared by Moisson's method where pure sugar charcoal and iron are heated in a graphite crucible to a temperature of about \( 3000^{\circ}C \) in an electric arc furnace (At this temperature iron dissolves an appreciable amount of carbon). The molten mass of graphite crucible is then cooled rapidly by placing it in a bath of molten lead (temperature \( 327^{\circ}C \) ). As a result of solidification of external surface of iron, an enormous pressure is exerted on the interior mass (cast iron is expanded on solidification). Under these conditions, the carbon is thrown in the form of minute diamonds.

Properties

  1. Of all the allotropic forms, charcoal is most reactive. It readily burns in air to give \( CO_2 \) . On reaction with conc \( H_2SO_4 \) and conc \( HNO_3 \) , it reduces them to \( SO_2 \) and \( NO_2 \) respectively. It combines with various metals to give carbides like \( CaC_2, Al_4C_3 \) etc. When heated with sulphur, it forms \( CS_2 \)

  2. Chemically, diamond is resistant towards almost all chemical reagents. It burns in air when heated at \( 900^{\circ}C \) giving \( CO_2 \) . it is also slowly oxidised to \( CO_2 \) by a mixture of \( K_2Cr_2O_7 \) and \( H_2SO_4 (ie, acidic \ K_2Cr_2O_7) \) at \( 200^{\circ}C \) . It is stable in vacuum up to \( 1500^{\circ}C \) but change to graphite at \( 1800^{\circ}C \) and more rapidly at \( 2000^{\circ}C \)

  3. Graphite like diamond is not attacked by dilute acids. It is oxidised slowly to \( CO_2 \) by chromic acid. It is oxidised by conc \( HNO_3 \) to give graphite acid, \( C_{11}H_4O_5 \) , an insoluble yellowish green substance. The reaction occur in the - presence of a little potassium chlorate. With alkaline \( KMnO_4 \) , it is oxidised to oxalic acid and mellitic acid \( C_6(COOH)_6 \) (ie, benzenehexa carboxylic acid)

Uses

Uses of C and its different allotropes include

  1. Coal is used extensively as fuel along with in the manufacture of coal gas, producer gas, water gas and synthetic petrol.

  2. Anthracite is used as a reducing agent in metallurgis al processes and for graphite manufacture.

  3. Activated charcoal is used as an excellent adsorbent in gas masks, in air conditioners, in decolourising sugar syrup, oil refining, and in the treatment of drinking water after chlorination to adsorb excess chlorine.

Silicon

Si occurs abundantly \( (26\%) \) but does not occur free in nature. As oxide \( (SiO_2) \) , it is found in sand, quartz of various forms, flint opal etc. It is also found as silicates eg. feldspar, \( (KAlSi_2O_8) \) mica \( [KAl_2(Si_3AlO_{10})_3(OH)_2] \) etc, in rocks and clay.
Methods of preparation Si is prepared by following methods

  1. Elemental Si is commercially produced by the reaction of sand with coke in an electric furnace as
    \( SiO_2 + 2C \xrightarrow{2273 - 2773K} Si + 2CO \)
    Si obtained by this method is generally \( 96-98\% \) pure and is mostly used in metallurgical industry to produce ferrosilicon and other alloys.

  2. Semiconductor grade silicon is prepared by the reduction of highly pure \( SiCl_4 / SiHCl \) , with \( H_2 \) or by the pyrolysis of \( SiH_4 \) mainly.

Physical properties

Silicon is a hard and brittle solid with a high melting point \( (1410^{\circ}C) \) . It has the same appearance as graphite. Its electrical conductivity is also similar to that of graphite. It exists in two allotropic forms namely a brown amorphous powder and a grey crystalline mass . These forms differ in particle size and content of impurities. The amorphous variety fuses on strong heating and solidifies on cooling to give the crystalline form.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Standard electrode potential values, \( E^{\circleddash} \ for \ Al^{3+}/Al \ is \ - 1.66 V \) and that of \( Tl^{3+}/Tl \ is \ +1.26 \ V \) . Predict about the formation of \( M^{3+} \) ion in solution and compare the electropositive character of the two metals.
Solution
Standard electrode potential values for two half cell reactions suggest that aluminium has high tendency to make \( Al^{3+} \) (aq) ions, whereas \( Tl^{3+} \) is not only unstable in solution but is a powerful oxidising agent also. Thus \( Tl^{+} \) is more stable in solution than \( Tl^{3+} \) . Aluminium being able to form +3 ions easily, is more electropositive than thallium.

Example 2:

White fumes appear around the bottle of anhydrous aluminium chloride. Give reason.
Solution
Anhydrous aluminium chloride is partially hydrolysed with atmospheric moisture to liberate HCl gas. Moist HCl appears white in colour.

Example 3:

Boron is unable to form \( B{F_6}^{3-} \) ion. Explain.
Solution
Due to non-availability of d orbitals, boron is unable to expand its octet. Therefore, the maximum covalence of boron cannot exceed 4.

Example 4:

Why is boric acid considered as a weak acid?
Solution
Because it is not able to release \( H^+ \) ions on its own. It receives \( OH^{-} \) ions from water molecule to complete its octet and in turn releases \( H^+ \) ions.

Example 5:

Select the member(s) of group 14 that

  1. forms the most acidic dioxide,

  2. is commonly found in +2 oxidation state,

  3. used as semiconductor

Solution

  1. carbon

  2. lead

  3. silicon and germanium

Example 6:

\( [SiF_6]^{2-} \) is known whereas \( [SiCl_6]^{2-} \) not.
Give possible reasons. Solution
The main reasons are :

  1. six large chloride ions cannot be accommodated around \( Si^{4+} \) due to limitation of its size.

  2. interaction between lone pair of chloride ion and \( Si^{4+} \) is not very strong.

Example 7:

Diamond is covalent, yet it has high melting point. Why ?
Solution
Diamond has a three-dimensional network involving strong C-C bonds, which are very difficult to break and, in turn has high melting point.
