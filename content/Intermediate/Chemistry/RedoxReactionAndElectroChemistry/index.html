Electronic Concepts Of Oxidation And Reduction

Redox Reductions

The reaction which involves oxidation and reduction as its two half-reactions is called redox reaction . Normally it is found that oxidation and reductions reaction are complementary to each other and occur simultaneously. Their simultaneous occurrence is seen in the form of redox reaction.

Oxidation And Reduction

The term oxidation was first used for those chemical reactions in which oxygen is added to an element or a compound. The reverse of such a process can be defined as reduction . Both the above written terms can be defined in many ways as :

Definitions of Oxidation and Reduction

According to the modern concept, loss of electrons (ie, increase in the oxidation number) is called oxidation while gain of electrons (ie, decrease in the oxidation number) is called reduction.

  1. The redox reactions, in which the same element is oxidised and reduced, are called disproportionation reactions

  2. A substance which undergoes oxidation acts as reducing agent and the other one which undergoes reduction acts as oxidising agent.
    \( Eg. mass \ of \ oxidising/reducing \ agent = \frac{mol. mass \ of \ oxidising/reducing \ agent}{no. of \ electrons \ taking \ part \ in \ the \ reaction} \)

Redox Reactions, Oxidation Number

Oxidation Number

The real or imaginary charge which an atom appears to have in its combined state, is called oxidation state or oxidation number of that atom.
Rules for assigning oxidation number The oxidation number for an element or atom can be calculated with the help of following rules :

  1. The oxidation number of an atom in its free or elemental state is zero, eg, oxidation number of H in \( H_2, \ S \) in \( S_8 \) , and P in \( P_4, \ O \) in \( O_2 \ or \ O_3 \) is zero.

  2. Oxidation number of fluorine is always -1 in all of its compounds.

  3. Oxidation number of oxygen is always -2 in all of its compounds except peroxides, superoxides and oxygen fluorides. In peroxides its oxidation number is -1, in superoxides its oxidation number is \( \frac{1}{2} \) while in \( OF_2 \) its oxidation number is +2

  4. The oxidation number of hydrogen in metallic hydrides is -1 while in rest of the compounds it has +1 oxidation number.

  5. The oxidation number of an ion is equal to the electrical charge present on it.

  6. For complex ions, the algebraic sum of oxidation numbers of all the atoms is equal to the net charge on the ion.

  7. In case of neutral molecules, the algebraic sum of the oxidation numbers of all the atoms present in the molecules is zero.

Balancing Of Redox Reactions

The redox reactions can be balanced through :

  1. Ion-electron method

  2. Oxidation number method

  1. Ion-electron method The ion-electron method for balancing redox reactions was given by Jette and Lamev in 1927. The method involves following steps:

    1. Write the redox reaction in ionic form.

    2. Split the redox reaction into its two half-reactions, ie, the oxidation half and the reduction-half.

    3. Balance atoms of each half-reaction by using simple multiples (not applidable for hydrogen and oxygen )

    4. For balancing H and O :

      1. Use simple multiples if possible.

      2. For acidic and neutral solutions add water molecules to the side deficient in oxygen and \( H^{+} \) to the side deficient in hydrogen.

      3. In case of for alkaline solution for each excess of oxygen add one water molecule to the same side and two \( OH^{-} \) ions on the other side. If hydrogen is still unbalanced add one \( OH^{-} \) ion for each excess hydrogen on the same side and one water molecule to the other side.

    5. Add electrons to the side deficient in electrons, so as to equalise the charge on both the sides.

    6. Multiply one or both the half-reactions by a suitable number so that the numbers of electrons become equal in both the equations.

    7. Add the two balance half-reactions and cancel any term common to both the sides.
      eg, in the reaction,
      \( Cu + HNO_3 \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2 + NO + H_2O \)

  2. Oxidation number method This method is based upo3r-the fact that total increase in oxidation number must be equal to total decrease in oxidation number. The stepwise approach is

    1. Write the skeleton equation (if not given) representing the chemical change

    2. Assign oxidation number to the atoms in the equation and find out which atoms are undergoing oxidation and reduction. Write separate equations for the atoms undergoing oxidation and reduction.

    3. Find the change in oxidation number in each equation. Make the change equal in both the equations by multiplying with suitable integers. Add both the equations

    4. Complete the balancing. First balance those substance which undergone change in oxidation number and then other atoms except H and O. Lastly balance H and O by putting \( H_2O \) molecules whenever needed.
      eg, balance the equation,
      \( Cu + HNO_3 \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2 + NO_2 + H_2O \)

Summary

  1. Redox reactions form an important class of reactions in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously. Three tier conceptualisation viz, classical, electronic and oxidation number, which is usually available in the texts, has been presented in detail.

  2. Oxidation, reduction, oxidising agent (oxidant) and reducing agent (reductant) have been viewed according to each conceptualisation. Oxidation numbers are assigned in accordance with a consistent set of rules.

  3. Oxidation number and ion-electron method both are useful means in writing equations for the redox reactions. Redox reactions are classified into four categories: combination, decomposition displacement and disproportionation reactions.

  4. The concept of redox couple and electrode processes is introduced here. The redox reactions find wide applications in the study of electrode processes and cells.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Justify that the reaction :
\( 2 Na(s) + H_2 (g) \rightarrow 2 NaH(s) \) is a redox change.
Solution
Since in the above reaction the compound formed is an ionic compound, which may also be represented as \( Na^{+}H^{-} (s) \) , this suggests that one half reaction in this process is :
\( 2 Na (s) \rightarrow \ 2 Na^+(g) + 2e^{-} \)
and the other half reaction is:
\( H_2 (g) + 2e^{-} \rightarrow \ 2 H^{-}(g) \)
This splitting of the reaction under examination into two half reactions automatically reveals that here sodium is oxidised and hydrogen is reduced, therefore, the complete reaction is a redox change.

Example 2:

Using Stock notation, represent the following compounds : \( HAuCl_4, \ Tl_2O, \ FeO, \ Fe_2O_3, \ CuI, \ CuO, \ MnO \ and \ MnO_2 \) .
Solution
By applying various rules of calculating the oxidation number of the desired element in a compound, the oxidation number of each metallic element in its
compound is as follows: \( \begin{align} HAuCl_4 \rightarrow Au \ has \ 3 \\\\[3pt] Tl_2O \rightarrow T_l \ has \ 1 \\\\[3pt] FeO \rightarrow Fe \ has \ 2 \\\\[3pt] Fe_2O_3 \rightarrow Fe \ has \ 3 \\\\[3pt] CuI \rightarrow \ Cu \ has \ 1 \\\\[3pt] CuO \rightarrow Cu \ has \ 2 \\\\[3pt] MnO \rightarrow Mn \ has \ 2 \\\\[3pt] MnO_2 \rightarrow Mn \ has \ 4 \end{align} \)
Therefore, these compounds may be represented as: \( HAu(III)Cl_4, \ Tl_2(I)O, \ Fe(II)O, \ Fe_2(III)O_3, \ Cu(I)I, \ Cu(II)O, \ Mn(II)O, \ Mn(IV)O_2 \) .

Example 3:

Which of the following species, do not show disproportionation reaction and why?
\( ClO^{-}, \ Cl{O_2}^{-}, \ Cl{O_3)^{-} \ and \ Cl{O_4}^{-} \)
Also write reaction for each of the species that disproportionates.
Solution
Among the oxoanions of chlorine listed above, \( Cl{O_4}^{-} \) does not disproportionate because in this oxoanion chlorine is present in its highest oxidation state that is, +7. The disproportionation reactions for the other three oxoanions of chlorine are as follows:
\( \begin{align} \overset{+1}{3ClO^{-}} \rightarrow \overset{-1}{2Cl^{-}} + \overset{+5}{Cl{O_3}^{-}} \\\\[3pt] \overset{+3}{6Cl{O_2}^{-}} \xrightarrow{hv} \ \overset{+5} {4Cl{O_3}^{-}} + \overset{-1}{Cl^{-}} \\\\[3pt] \overset{+5}{3Cl{O_3}^{-}} \rightarrow \overset{-1}{Cl^{-}} + \overset{+7}{3 Cl{O_4}^{-}} \end{align} \)
