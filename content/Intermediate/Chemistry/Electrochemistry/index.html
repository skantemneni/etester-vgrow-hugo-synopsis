Cells, Nernst Equation

Electrochemical Cell

The device through which chemical energy changes to electrical energy is called electrochemical cell or voltaic cell or galvanic cell.
The change of chemical energy to electrical energy causes lowering of free energy, which appears as electrical energy and measured in terms of emf.

Representation of a Electrochemical Cell

The following conventions are used in representing an electrochemical cell :

  1. The cell representation contains anodic half on the left hand side and cathodic half on the right hand side.

  2. The anodic side of the cell is represented by writing metal or solid phase first and then the electrolyte (infact cation of electrolyte).

  3. The cathodic side, on the other hand, is represented by writing the electrolyte first (anion) and then the metal or solid phase

Daniell Cell

Among the galvanic cells when cell is designed in such a manner to make the use of spontaneous reaction between Zn and Cu ions to produce an electric current, the cell is called Daniell cell.

Electrode Potential

When a metallic electrode is dipped in its salt solution, a potential difference is developed between the electrode and its salt solution.
The reason for the development of such a potential difference is exchange of electrons between the atoms of electrode and cations in its salt solution.
Such a potential difference is called electrode potential

EMF of Cell

In electrochemical cell, the electrodes in different half-cells have different reduction potential. As the result of this, different flow of electrons is seen from the electrode with higher tendency to lose electrons to other electrode.
This difference in electrode potential of electrodes is called electromotive force or cell potential of a cell.

Nernst Equation

A relationship between electrode potential and concentration of solution is called Nernst equation . For a general electrode reaction, ie
\( M^{n+} (aq) + ne^{-} \rightarrow M (s) \)
This equation can be given as
\( E_{(M^{n+} | M)} = E^{ \circ}_{ (M^{n+}, | M)} - \frac{RT}{nF} ln \frac{ [M(s)]}{[M^{n+} (aq)]} \)
or \( E_{(M^{n+} | M)} = E^{ \circ}_{ (M^{n+}, | M)} - \frac{2.303 RT}{nF} log \frac{ [M(s)]}{[M^{n+} (aq)]} \)
(as \( log_e 10 = 2.303 \ log 10 \) )
where \( E_{(M^{n+} | M)} \) = electrode potential, ie, \( E_{cell} \)
\( E_{(M^{n+} / M)} \) = standard electrode potential, ie, \( E_{cell} \)
R = gas constant
T = temperature
F = Faraday constant
n = number of electrons gained during electrode reaction
\( [M(s)]}{[M^{n+} (aq)] \) = concentration of ions
[M(s)] = concentration of metal
Since, the concentration of solid phase is taken as unity, the Nernst equation will become
\( E_{(M^{n+} / M)}^{ \circ} = E_{(M^{n+} / M)}^{ \circ} - \frac{2.303 \ RT}{nF} log \frac{1}{[M^{n+} (aq)]} \)

Conductance of Electrolytic Solutions

Effect of Nature of Electrolyte over Conductivity

The conductivity of an electrolyte depends upon the nature of electrolyte as the conductivity of strong electrolytes is higher in comparison to weak electrolytes.
The reason for such a difference is difference in availability of ions present in their solutions. Strong electrolytes dissociate completely, hence more ions are present in their solutions resulting to higher conductivity.
Weak electrolytes, on the other hand, dissociate less, hence less ions are present in their solutions resulting to lesser conductivity.

Effect of Temperature over Conductivity

The conductance of an electrolyte increases with increase in the temperature of solution. It is due to the reason that on increasing the temperature of the solution,
the kinetic energy of ions increases resulting to increase in the movement of ions. Over a small range of temperature, the temperature dependence of molar conductivity is given by the equation of the type
\( \mu_t = \mu_{25} [1 + \beta (t - 2b)] \)
where \( \beta \) = conductivity in dilute solutions near room temperature changes 2% per degree change in temperature

Batteries, Fuel Cells, Corrosion and Possivity

Battery

A cell or a battery (arrangement of 1 or more cells connected in series) is basically a galvanic cell and used where the chemical energy of redox reaction is converted into electrical energy. The batteries are of two types basically :

Battery

The primary batteries are those in which the cell reaction occurs only once and the battery becomes dead after use over a period of time and cannot be reused again, eg, dry cells like Leclanche cell, mercury cell etc.
Leclanche cell The electrode reaction for Leclanche cell is
Anode \( Zn(s) \rightarrow Zn^{2+} + 2e^{-} \)
Cathode \( MnO_2 + NH_4^{+} + e^{-} \rightarrow MnO(OH) + NH_3 \)

A commercial dry cell containing of a graphite (carbon) cathode in a zinc container; the latter acts as the anode. (Leclanche cell)

Secondary Batteries

A secondary battery can be recharged by passing current through it in opposite direction, so that it can be used again.
Lead storage battery The most important secondary cell is lead storage battery, commonly used in automobiles and invertors

Fuel Cells

Galvanic cells which use energy of combustion of fuels like \( H_2, \ CH_4, \ CH_3OH \) , etc as the source to produce electrical energy are called fuel cells.
One of the most successful fuel cells use reaction of \( H_2 \) and \( O_2 \) in gaseous state to form water.

The electrode reactions for the cell are:
Cathode \( O_2 (g) + 2 H_2O (l) + 4e^{-} \rightarrow 4 HO^{-} (aq) \)
Cathode \( 2H_2 (g) + 4 OH^{-} (aq) \rightarrow 4 H_2O (l) + 4e^{-} \)
With overall reaction being
\( 2H_2 (g) + O_2 (g) \rightarrow 2H_2O (l) \)
These types of cells run continuously as long as the fuels are supplied and produce electricity with approximately 70% efficiency. The advantage of fuel cells over batteries is that these are pollution free and highly efficient.

Corrosion

"It is the oxidative deterioration of a metal surface with the action of its environment to form unwanted corrosion products". This type of damage usually affects metallic substances and typically produces oxide(s) and/or salt(s) of metal,
eg, conversion of iron to rust \( [Fe_2O_3 . xH_2O], \) the tarnishing of silver (due tc formation of \( Ag_2O \) ), development of a green coating on copper and bronze.
Corrosion is basically an electrochemical phenomenon. A metal is oxidised by loss of electrons to oxygen, and form metal oxide.
Corrosion of iron (rusting) occurs in the presence of water and oxygen (air). The reaction which describes rusting of iron is
\( \underset{iron}{4 Fe (s)} + \underset{oxygen}{3O_2 (g)} + \underset{water \ from \ air}{x H_2O} \rightarrow \underset{ hydrated \ ferric \ oxide (rust)}{Fe_2 O_3. xH_2O (s)} \)

Summary

  1. An electrochemical cell consists of two metallic electrodes dipping in electrolytic solution(s). Thus an important component of the electrochemical cell is the ionic conductor or electrolyte. Electrochemical cells are of two types.

  2. In galvanic cell, the chemical energy of a spontaneous redox reaction is converted into electrical work, whereas in an electrolytic cell, electrical energy is used to carry out a non-spontaneous redox reaction. The standard electrode potential for any electrode dipping in an appropriate solution is defined with respect to standard electrode potential of hydrogen electrode taken as zero.

  3. The standard potential of the cell can be obtained by taking the difference of the standard potentials of cathode and anode \( ({E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)}} = {E^{\circleddash}_{cathode}} - {E^{\circleddash}_{anode}}) \) The standard potential of the cells are related to standard Gibbs energy \( (\ddot{A_r} G^{\circleddash} = - nF \ E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)}) \) and equilibrium constant \( (\ddot{A_r} = - RT \ In \ K) \) of the reaction taking place in the cell. Concentration dependence of the potentials of the electrodes and the cells are given by Nernst equation.

  4. The conductivity k, of an electrolytic solution depends on the concentration of the electrolyte, nature of solvent and temperature. Molar conductivity, \( (\ddot{E_m} \) , is defined by = k/c where c is the concentration.

  5. Conductivity decreases but molar conductivity increases with decrease in concentration. It increases slowly with decrease in concentration for strong electrolytes while the increase is very steep for weak electrolytes in very dilute solutions.

  6. Kohlrausch found that molar conductivity at infinite dilution, for an electrolyte is sum of the contribution of the molar conductivity of the ions in which it dissociates. It is known as law of independent migration of ions and has many applications.

  7. Ions conduct electricity through the solution but oxidation and reduction of the ions take place at the electrodes in an electrochemical cell. Batteries and fuel cells are very useful forms of galvanic cell. Corrosion of metals is essentially an electrochemical phenomenon. Electrochemical principles are relevant to the Hydrogen Economy .

Solved examples

Example 1:

Represent the cell in which the following reaction takes place \( Mg(s) + 2 Ag^{+} (0.0001 M) \ \rightarrow \ Mg^{2+} (0.130 M) + 2Ag(s) \ Calculate \ its \ E_{(cell)} \ if \ E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} = 3.17 \ V \) .
Solution
The cell can be written as \( Mg|Mg^{2+} (0.130 M) || Ag^{+}(0.0001 M)|Ag \)
\( \begin{align} E_{(cell)}= E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} - \frac{RT}{2F} In \frac{[Mg^{2+}]}{[Ag^{+}]^2} \\\\[3pt] = 3.17 \ V - \frac{0.059 \ V}{2} log \frac{0.130}{(0.0001)^2} = 3.17 \ V - 0.21 \ V \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Calculate the equilibrium constant of the reaction:
\( Cu(s) + 2 Ag^{+} (aq) \rightarrow Cu^{2+} (aq) + 2 Ag(s) \ E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} = 0.46 \ V \)
Solution
\( \begin{align} E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} = \frac{0.059 \ V}{2} log \ K_c = 0.46 \ or \ log \ K_c = \frac{0.46 \ V \times 2}{0.059 \ V} = 15.6 \\\\[3pt] K_c = 3.92 \times 10^{15} \end{align} \)

Example 3:

The standard electrode potential for Daniell cell is I.IV. Calculate the standard Gibbs energy for the reaction:
\( Zn(s) + Cu^{2+} (aq) \rightarrow Zn^{2+} (aq) + Cu(s) \)
Solution
\( \triangle_r G^{\circleddash} = - nF E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} \)
n in the above equation is 2, \( F = 96487 C mol^{-1} \) and \( E^{\circleddash}_{(cell)} = 1.1 \ V \)
\( \begin{align} Therfore \ \triangle_r G^{\circleddash} = - 2 \times 1.1 V \times 96487 \ C \ mol^{-1} \\\\[3pt] = - 21227 \ J \ mol^{-1} \\\\[3pt] = - 21.227 \ kJ \ mol^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Resistance of a conductivity cell filled with \( 0.1 \ mol \ L^{-1} \ KCl \) solution is \( 100 \ \Omega \) . If the resistance of the same cell when filled with \( 0.02 \ mol \ L^{-1} \ KCl \) solution is \( 520 \ \omega \) , calculate the conductivity and molar conductivity of \( 0.02 \ mol \ L^{-1} \ KCl \) solution. The conductivity of \( 0.1 \ mol \ L^{-1} \ KCl \) solution is 1.29 s/m.
Solution
The cell constant is given by the equation:
\( \begin{align} Cell constant = G* = conductivity \times resistance \\\\[3pt] = 1.29 \ S/m \times \ \Omega = 129 \ m^{-1} = 1.29 \ cm^{-1} \\\\[3pt] Conductivity \ of \ 0.02 \ mol \ L^{-1} \ KCl \ solution = \frac{cell \ constant}{resistance} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{G*}{R} = \frac{129 \ m^{-1}}{520 \ \Omega} = 0.248 \ S \ m^{-1} \\\\[3pt] Concentration = 0.02 \ mol \ L^{-1} \\\\[3pt] = 1000 \times 0.02 \ mol \ m^{-3} \\\\[3pt] = 20 \ mol \ m^{-3} \\\\[3pt] Molar \ conductivity = \wedge_m = \frac{k}{c} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{248 \times 10^{-3} \ S \ m^{-1}}{20 \ mol \ m^{-3}} \\\\[3pt] = 124 \times 10^{-4} \ S \ m^2 \ mol^{-1} \\\\[3pt] Alternatively, \ k = \frac{1.29 \ cm^{-1}}{520 \ \Omega} = 0.248 \times 10^{-2} \ S \ cm^{-1} \\\\[3pt] and \ \wedge_m = k \times 1000 \ cm^3 \ L^{-1} \ molarity^{-1} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{0.248 \times 10^{-2} \ S \ cm^{-1} \times 1000 \ cm^3 \ L^{-1}}{0.02 \ mol \ L^{-1}} = 124 \ S \ cm^2 \ mol^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 6:

A solution of \( CuSO_4 \) is electrolysed for 10 minutes with a current of 1.5 amperes. What is the mass of copper deposited at the cathode?
Solution
t = 600 s charge = \( current \times time = 1.5 A \times 600 \ s = 900 \ C \)
According to the reaction:
\( Cu^{2+} (aq) + 2^{e-} = Cu(s) \)
We require 2F or \( 2 \times 96487 \ C \) to deposit 1 mol or 63 g of Cu.
For 900 C, the mass of Cu deposited = \( \frac{(63 \ g \ mol^{-1} \times 900 C)}{(2 \times 96487 \ C \ mol^{-1})} \)
= 0.2938 g.
