Rate of Chemical Reactions and Factors, Order and Molecularity of the reaction

Rate of Reaction

The rate or speed or velocity of a reaction is the rate of change of concentration of reactants or products in unit time. When a reaction occurs, the concentration of reactants starts decreasing while the concentration of products starts increasing.
Therefore,
Rate of a reaction \( = \frac{decrease \ in \ the \ concentration \ of \ reactants}{time \ interval} \)
or Rate of a reaction \( = \frac{increase \ in \ the \ concentration \ of \ products}{time \ interval} \)

Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

The factors which affect the rate of reaction are as follows

  1. Concentration of reactants An increase in concentration of reactants increases the reaction rate.

  2. Nature of reactants The nature of bonds in a molecule influences the rate of reaction at which it changes into products

  3. Temperature Reaction rates are normally favoured by increase of temperature. The temperature effect is expressed in terms of temperature coefficient which is the ratio of the velocity constant of a reaction at two temperature differing by \( 10^{\circ} C. \)
    Temperature coefficient \( = \frac{K_{(t +10)^{\circ} C}}{K_{t^{\circ} C}} \)

  4. Presence of catalyst Presence of catalyst increases the rate of reaction by lowering the energy barrier.

  5. Surface area of reactants The rate of reaction increases as the surface area of the reactant increases.

  6. Presence of light Reaction rate normally becomes faster in presence of light. Light gives the necessary activation energy for starting the reaction.

Order of Reaction

In chemical kinetics the reactions are generally classified in terms of their order. The order of a reaction is defined as "The sum of the concentration terms on which the rate of a reaction actually depends as observed experimentally".

Zero Order Reactions

Reactions in which the concentration of reactants don't change with time and the concentration rates remain constant throughout are said to be zero order reactions.
Thus, for zero order reactions,
\( - \frac{\triangle[R]}{\triangle t} = k \) or rate = k

First Order Reactions

The first order reaction is defined as "The reaction in which the reaction rate is determined by the change of one concentration term of the reactant only", eg, In the reaction
\( \underset{ \underset{(a - x)}a}A \rightarrow \underset{ \underset{x}0}B + \underset{ \underset{x}0}C \)
Suppose initial concentration of A is a mol per litre. Rate constant, k for the first order reaction will be given as
\( k = \frac{2.303}{t}log_{10} \frac{a}{a - x} \)

Second Order Reactions

The reaction is said to be of second order if its reaction rate is determined by the variation of two concentration terms of reactants. It can be represented as :
\( - \frac{\triangle R}{\triangle t} \ or \ \frac{\triangle p}{\triangle t} = K[A]^2 \ ( For \ 2A \rightarrow product), \ or \ - \frac{\triangle R}{\triangle t} \ or \ \frac{\triangle p}{\triangle t} = K[A][B] \ ( For \ A + B \rightarrow product) \)

Third Order Reactions

A reaction is said to be of third order if its rate is determined by the variation of three concentration terms.
\( - \frac{\triangle R}{\triangle t} \ or \ \frac{\triangle p}{\triangle t} = K[A]^3 \ ( For \ 3A \rightarrow product), , \ or \ - \frac{\triangle R}{\triangle t} \ or \ \frac{\triangle p}{\triangle t} = K[A][B][C] \ ( For \ A + B + C \rightarrow product) \)

Collision Theory

Collision Theory of Big molecular Reactions

The collision theory states that

  1. If two molecules are to react together, they must collide with each other.

  2. The collision between all molecules do not lead to chemical reaction.

  3. The collisions in which the molecules acquire energy greater than the activation energy, results in chemical reaction.

Temperature Dependence of Rate Constant

Arrhenius Equation

Arrhenius proposed a quantitative relationship between rate constant and temperature as
\( k = Ae^{-E_a/RT} \)
here, A = a constant called frequency factor (because it gives the frequency of binary collisions of the reacting molecules per second per litre). It is also called collision number or pre-exponential factor
R = gas constant
T = absolute temperature
\( E_a \) = energy of activation.
(the minimum energy required over and above the energy possessed by the reactants in order to cross the energy barrier).
\( E_a = E_{th} - E_R \)
Where,
\( E_{th} \) = threshold energy
\( E_R \) = energy possessed by the reactant.
The factor \( e^{-E_a/RT} \) is referred as Boltzmann factor and gives the fraction of molecules having energy equal to or greater than activation energy

Activation energy

The activation energy can also be defined as "The energy that activates passive or non-active molecules after its adsorption or minimum energy in excess to normal energy of molecules, which molecules must possess in order to form product on collision.
The concept of activation energy gives us an idea whether a given reaction is slow or fast at a given temperature. A reaction which has lower activation energy proceed at a faster rate at a given temperature or vice-versa. The differences in activation energy are mainly responsible for observed difference in rates of reactions. Catalyst

A catalyst is a substance which alters the reaction rate but itself remain unchanged in amount and chemical composition at the end of the reaction. Actually the reactants in a reaction have to cross an energy barrier to form the product. The higher the energy barrier. the slower is the reaction rate. Summary

  1. Chemical kinetics is the study of chemical reactions with respect to reaction rates, effect of various variables, rearrangement of atoms and formation of intermediates.

  2. The rate of a reaction is concerned with decrease in concentration of reactants or increase in the concentration of products per unit time. It can be expressed as instantaneous rate at a particular instant of time and average rate over a large interval of time.

  3. A number of factors such as temperature, concentration of reactants, catalyst, affect the rate of a reaction. Mathematical representation of rate of a reaction is given by rate law. It has to be determined experimentally and cannot be predicted.

  4. Order of a reaction with respect to a reactant is the power of its concentration which appears in the rate law equation. The order of a reaction is the sum of all such powers of concentration of terms for different reactants.

  5. Rate constant is the proportionality factor in the rate law. Rate constant and order of a reaction can be determined from rate law or its integrated rate equation. Molecularity is defined only for an elementary reaction.

  6. Its values are limited from 1 to 3 whereas order can be 0, 1, 2, 3 or even a fraction. Molecularity and order of an elementary reaction are same.

  7. Temperature dependence of rate constants is described by Arrhenius equation \( \left (k = Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{RT}} \right ) \) . \( E_a \) corresponds to the activation energy and is given by the energy difference between activated complex and the reactant molecules, and A (Arrhenius factor or pre-exponential factor) corresponds to the collision frequency.

  8. The equation clearly shows that increase of temperature or lowering of \( E_a \) will lead to an increase in the rate of reaction and presence of a catalyst lowers the activation energy by providing an alternate path for the reaction.

  9. According to collision theory, another factor P called steric factor which refers to the orientation of molecules which collide, is important and contributes to effective collisions, thus, modifying the Arrhenius equation to \( = PZ_{AAB} e^{- \frac{E_a}{RT}} \) .

Solved examples

Example 1:

The decomposition of N2O5 in \( CCl_4 \) at 318K has been studied by monitoring the concentration of \( N_2O_5 \) in the solution. Initially the concentration of N2O5 is \( 2.33 \ mol \ L^{-1} \) and after 184 minutes, it is reduced to \( 2.08 \ mol \ L^{-1} \) . The reaction takes place according to the equation
\( 2 N_2 O_5 (g) \rightarrow 4 NO_2 (g) + O_2 (g) \)
Calculate the average rate of this reaction in terms of hours, minutes and seconds. What is the rate of production of \( NO_2 \) during this period?
Solution
\( \begin{align} Average \ Rate = \frac{1}{2} \left \lbrace - \frac{\triangle[N_2 O_5]}{\triangle{t}} \right \rbrace = - \frac{1}{2} \left [ \frac{(2.08 - 2.33) mol \ L^{-1}}{184 \ min} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = 6.79 \times 10^{-4} \ mol \ L^{-1}/min = (6.79 \times 10^{-4} \ mol \ L^{-1} \ min^{-1}) \times (60 min/1h) \\\\[3pt] = 4.07 \times 10^{-2} \ mol \ L^{-1}/h \\\\[3pt] = 6.79 \times 10^{-4} \ mol \ L^{-1} \times 1 \ min/ 60s \\\\[3pt] = 1.13 \times 10^{-5} \ mol \ L^{-1} \ s^{-1} \end{align} \)
It may be remembered that
\( \begin{align} Rate = \frac{1}{4} \left \lbrace - \frac{\triangle[N_2 O_2]}{\triangle{t}} \right \rbrace \\\\[3pt] = \frac{\triangle[NO_2]}{\triangle{t}} = 6.79 \times 10^{-4} \times 4 \ mol\ L^{-1} \ min^{-1} = 2.72 \times 10^{-3} \ mol \ L^{-1} \ min^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Calculate the overall order of a reaction which has the rate expression

  1. Rate = \( k[A]^{\frac{1}{2}} [B]^{\frac{3}{2}} \)

  2. Rate = \( [A]^{\frac{3}{2}} [B]^{-1} \)

Solution

  1. Rate = \( k[A]^x [B]^y \)
    order = x + y
    So order = \( \frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{2} = 2 \) , i.e., second order

  2. \( \frac{3}{2} + (-1) = \frac{1}{2} \) , i.e., half order.

Example 3:

Identify the reaction order from each of the following rate constants

  1. \( k = 2.3 \times 10^{-5} \ L \mol^{-1} \ s^{-1} \)

  2. \( k = 3 \times 10^{-4} \ s^{-1} \)

Solution

  1. The unit of second order rate constant is \( L \mol^{-1} \ s^{-1} \) , therefore \( k = 2.3 \times 10^{-5} \ L \mol^{-1} \ s^{-1} \) represents a second order reaction

  2. The unit of a first order rate constant is \( s^{-1} \) therefore \( k = 3 \times 10^{-4} \ s^{-1} \) represents a first order reaction.

Example 4:

The initial concentration of N2O5 in the following first order reaction \( N_2 O_5 (g) \rightarrow 2 NO_2(g) + \frac{1}{2} O_2 (g) \) was \( 1.24 \times 10^{-2} \ mol \ L^{-1} \) at 318 K. The concentration of \( N_2O_5 \) after 60 minutes was \( 0.20 \times 10^{-2} \ mol \ L^{-1} \) . Calculate the rate constant of the reaction at 318 K.
Solution
For a first order reaction
\( \begin{align} log \frac{[R]_1}{[R]_2} = \frac{k(t_2 - t_1)}{2.303} \\\\[3pt] k = \frac{2.303}{(t_2 - t_1)} log \frac{[R]_1}{[R]_2} \\\\[3pt] = frac{2.303}{(60 \ min - 0 \ min)} log \frac{1.24 \times 10^{-2} \ mol \ L^{-1}}{0.20 \times 10^{-2} \ mol \ L^{-1}} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{2.303}{60} log \ 6.2 \ min^{-1} \\\\[3pt] k = 0.0304 \ min^{-1} \end{align} \)

Example 5:

A first order reaction is found to have a rate constant, \( k = 5.5 \times 10^{-14} \ s^{-1} \) . Find the half-life of the reaction.
Solution
Half-life for a first order reaction is
\( \begin{align} t_{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{0.693}{k} \\\\[3pt] t_{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{0.693}{5.5 \times 10^{-14}} \ s^{-1} = 1.26 \times 10^{14} \ s \end{align} \)
Show that in a first order reaction, time required for completion of 99.9% is 10 times of half-life ( \( t_{\frac{1}{2}} \) ) of the reaction.

Example 6:

When reaction is completed 99.9%, \( [R]_n = [R]_0 - 0.999[R]_0 \) .
Solution
\( \begin{align} k = \frac{2.303}{t} log \frac{[R]_0}{[R]} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{2.303}{t} log \frac{[R]_0}{[R]_0 - 0.999[R]_0} = \frac{2.303}{t} log 10^{3} \\\\[3pt] t = \frac{6.909}{k} \\\\[3pt] \text{For half-life of the reaction} \\\\[3pt] t_{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{0.693}{k} \\\\[3pt] \frac{t}{t_{\frac{1}{2}}} = \frac{6.909}{k} \times \frac{k}{0.983} = 10 \end{align} \)

Example 7:

The rate constants of a reaction at 500K and 700K are \( 0.02s^{-1} \) and \( 0.07 s^{-1} \) respectively. Calculate the values of \( E_a \) and A.
Solution
\( \begin{align} log \frac{k_2}{k_1} = \frac{E_a}{2.303 \ R} \ \left [ \frac{T_2 - T_1}{T_1 T_2} \right ] \\\\[3pt] log \frac{0.07}{0.02} = \left ( \frac{E_a}{2.303 \times 8.314 \ JK^{-1} \ mol^{-1}} \right ) \left [ \frac{700 - 500}{700 \times 500} \right ] \\\\[3pt] = 0.544 = E_a \times 5.714 \times 10^{-14} / 19.5 \\\\[3pt] E_a = 0.544 \times 19.15/5.714 \times 10^{-4} = 18230.8 \ J \\\\[3pt] Since \ k = Ae^{-E_a/RT} \\\\[3pt] = 0.02 = Ae^{\frac{- 18230.8}{8.314} \times 500} \\\\[3pt] = A = \frac{0.02}{0.012} = 1.61 \end{align} \)
