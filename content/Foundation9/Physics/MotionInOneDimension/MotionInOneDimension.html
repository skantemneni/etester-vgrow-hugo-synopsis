Common misconception Fact
1. A body moving with uniform motion, moves with uniform acceleration. 1. A body moving with uniform motion has uniform speed but not uniform acceleration.
2. A body having zero velocity has no acceleration 2. When a body is thrown vertically up. At its highest point, the body possess zero velocity and finite acceleration.

Introduction

In our daily life, we observe a mosquito flying, the cars moving on a road, the motion of the blades of a fan, the motion of our hand etc., In these examples, we observe that the motion of these objects can be either in one dimension or more than one dimension.
In general, the study of the bodies under motion in a straight line can be in one dimension.
One dimensional motion refers to motion of a body in either horizontal or in vertical direction is learnt under the topic motion in one dimension.

Rest

The two most basic concepts in physics (and in science) are space and time. We live in space to experience time. What is space? What is time? These are very difficult questions to answer. It suffices our purpose to assume that space is where all physical objects stay and interact. Time is our way of describing how these objects change. It is interesting to note that neither space nor time can be measured directly; it is the interval in space or time that can be measured. Therefore, metre and second represent length (interval in space) and duration (interval in time) respectively.
A body is said to be at rest if it does not change its position with respect to the surroundings and time. For example a book on the table, a house, a tree etc.,

Motion

A body is said to be in motion if it changes its position with respect to the surroundings and time. For example a moving car water flowing in a river.
Rest and motion are relative. For example a man on the earth is at rest with respect to the earth. But he is in motion with respect to Sun.

Types of Motion

A body under motion can exhibit mainly three types of motion.

Translatory motion

A body moving in a straight line is said to undergo translatory motion. A translatory motion may be a straight line (rectilinear) motion or a curvilinear motion.
An apple falling from a tree is an example of rectilinear motion.
A car moving along a curved road is an example of curvilinear motion.

Rotatory motion

A body cannot be treated as a point and all the particles move simultaneously along circles (whose centres lie on a line called axis of rotation) by shifting through an angle in a given time, the motion is rotatory.

Oscillatory motion

If a periodic motion is within certain limits or if it is to and fro or up and down, it is said to be oscillatory motion.

Distance

It is a scalar quantity and is generally represented by the letters
The total length of the path covered by the body is called distance. It is a scalar quantity

Units

C.G.S : centimetre (cm),
S.I : metre (m),

Displacement

The shortest possible distance from the initial and the final positions of the body is called displacement. It is a vector quantity.
. It is a vector quantity and is represented by the symbol \( \overrightarrow{S} \)

Units

C.G.S : centimetre (cm),
S.I : metre (m),
Example

If a body starts from A and travels along the path ACDEF and reaches B, then:
Distance travelled by the body = length of the curve ACDEFB
Displacement of the body = AB

Speed

Speed is the rate of change of position with time independent of direction:
In physics, the phrase 'rate of change' usually means 'change with respect to time. Speed is a scalar quantity and is generally denoted by the fetter u or v.
The distance travelled by the body in unit time is called speed. It is a scalar quantity. Speed can either be positive or zero.
\( \Rightarrow Speed = \frac{Distance \ travelled}{Time \ taken} \)

Units

C.G.S : \( cm \ s^{-1} \)
S.I : \( ms^{-1} \)
Other units of speed are \( km \ h^{-1}, \ miles \ h^{-1} \)
\( 1 \ km \ h^{-1} = \frac{1000 \ m}{3600 \ s} = \frac{5}{18} \ ms^{-1} \)

Types of Speed

Uniform Speed

If a body travels equal distances in equal intervals of time, however small these intervals may be, then it is said to be moving with uniform speed.

Non-Uniform Speed

If a body travels unequal distances in equal intervals of time or equal distances in unequal intervals of time, the body is said to be travelling with non-uniform or variable speed.

Average Speed

The ratio of the total distance travelled to the total time of travel is called average speed.
\( Average speed = \frac{total \ distance}{total \ time} \)

instantaneous Speed

When the speed of a body is changing at every instant of time, its speed at any instant is known as the instantaneous speed. The speedometer of a vehicle measures the instantaneous speed.

Velocity

The rate of change of displacement is called velocity. Velocity can be either positive or zero or negative. Velocity is a vector quantity.
\( Velocity = \frac{displacement}{time} \)

Units

C.G.S : \( cm \ s^{-1} \)
S.I : \( ms^{-1} \)

Types of Velocity

Uniform velocity

If a body travels equal displacements in equal intervals of time, however small these time intervals may be, the body is said to be travelling with uniform velocity.

Non-Uniform velocity or variable velocity

If a body covers unequal displacements in equal intervals of time or equal displacements in unequal intervals of time, then it is said to be travelling with non-uniform velocity.

Average velocity

The ratio of the total displacement to the total time taken by a body is called average velocity.
\( Average velocity = \frac{Total \ displacement}{Total \ time} \)

Instantaneous Velocity

For a body moving with variable velocity, the velocity of the body at any instant is called the instantaneous velocity .

Acceleration

The rate of change of velocity of a body is called acceleration.
\( Acceleration = \frac{change \ in \ velocity}{time} \)

units

C.G.S : \( cm \ s^{-2}, \)
S.I : \( ms^{-2} \)
Note: If a body moves with uniform velocity, then the acceleration is equal to zero.

Types of Acceleration

Uniform acceleration

If equal changes in velocity takes place in equal intervals of time, however small these intervals may be,
the body is said to be travelling with uniform acceleration.
Example
A freely falling body possesses uniform acceleration.

Non-Uniform acceleration

If unequal changes in velocity takes place in equal intervals of time,
then the body is said to be moving with non-uniform acceleration.

Negative acceleration

If the velocity of the body gradually decreases w.r.t time,
the acceleration is said to be negative, which is called deceleration or retardation.

Equation of Motion

Consider a body moving with an initial velocity u. Let it be travelling along a straight line with uniform acceleration a.
Let v be its final velocity after time t. The displacement of the body in this time be S.
\( \begin{align} \begin{array}{cc} v = u + at & S = ut + \frac{1}{2} at^2 \\\\ v^2 - u^2 = 2aS & S_n = u + \frac{a}{2} (2n - 1) \end{array} \end{align} \)

Gravity

The force with which the earth pulls an object towards it is called the gravitational force of earth or gravity of the earth.

The equations of motion for a body falling down under gravity

  1. The earth exerts a gravitational force on all bodies.

  2. At a given place, acceleration due to this force 'g' is a constant that is directed towards the earth.
    Therefore, bodies moving under gravity will be subjected to this uniform acceleration due to gravity 'g'.

Equations of motion for a freely falling body

Consider a body falling from a height 'h', under acceleration due to gravity 'g'. The initial velocity of a freely falling body is 0.
v = gt
\( \begin{align} h = \frac{1}{2} gt^2 \\\\[3pt] v^2 = 2gh \\\\[3pt] S_n = g \left(n - \frac{1}{2} \right) \end{align} \)

Equations of motion for a body thrown vertically upwards

If a body is thrown vertically up with an initial velocity (u).

  1. v = u - gt

  2. \( h = ut - \frac{1}{2} gt^2 \)

  3. \( v^2 - u^2 = - 2gh \)

  4. \( h_n = u - g \left(n - \frac{1}{2} \right) \)

Maximum height reached by the body

\( H = \frac{u^2}{2g} \)
\( \therefore \) Therefore, the maximum height reached by the body is directly proportional to the square of the initial velocity.

Time of Ascent

The time taken by body thrownup to reach maximum height 'h' is called its time of ascent.
\( t_a = \frac{u}{g} \)
Hence time of ascent \( t_a \) is directly proportional to the initial velocity u.

Time of Descent

The time taken by a freely falling body to reach the ground is called the time of descent.
\( t_d = \sqrt{ \frac{2h}{g}} \)

and \( h = \frac{v^2}{2g}, \ t_d = \sqrt{ \frac{2 \times v^2}{2g \times g}} = \frac{v}{g} \)
But, we know that u = v i.e., projected velocity of a body is equal to the velocity of the body on reaching the ground.
\( t_d = \frac{u}{g} \) = time of ascent \( (t_a) \)
\( \therefore \) Time of ascent = Time of descent

Time of Flight

Time of flight is the time for which a body remains in the air and is given by sum of time of ascent and time of descent.
Therefore
\( \begin{align} t = t_a + t_d \\\\[3pt] t = \frac{u}{g} + \frac{u}{g} \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) Time of flight, \( t = \frac{2u}{g} \)

Velocity on reaching ground

When a body is dropped from a height h, its initial velocity is zero.
Let the final velocity on reaching the ground be v. For a freely falling body.
\( v^2 - u^2 = 2gh \)
but u = 0
\( \therefore v^2 - 0 = 2gh; \ v = \sqrt{2gh} \)

Body projected vertically up from the top of a tower

If a body is projected vertically up from the top of a tower of height 'h' with velocity `u'.

  1. displacement after time t is \( S = ut - \frac{1}{2} gt^2 \)

  2. velocity after time t is v = u - gt.

  3. its velocity on reaching the ground is \( \sqrt{u^2 + 2gh} \)

  4. its maximum height above the ground is \( \left\lbrace h + \left( \frac{u^2}{2gh} \right) \right\rbrace \)

Graphs

In physics we often use graphs as important tools for analysing certain concepts.
The linear motion of a (body can be studied with the help of the following given

  1. Position - time graph

  2. Velocity - time graph

  3. Acceleration - time graph

Displacement-time graphs:

These graphs are very useful in studying the linear motion of the body. The displacement is plotted on the Y - axis and the time on X - axis.
These graphs are very helpful in finding the velocity of body, as the slope of graph
\( \left( \frac{Y - axis}{X - axis} \right) \) is equal to \( \frac{Displacement}{Time} \)

  1. Position - time graph for a stationary object is a line parallel to the time axis as shown in Fig. 3

  2. Position - time graph of a body moving with uni velocity.
    The position-time graph is a straight line inc to the time axis for a body moving with uni velocity as shown in Fig. 4 .

  3. Position - time graph of a body moving with variable velocity.
    If a body moves with a variable velocity, the position - time graph is a curve as shown in Fig. 5

    The velocity at any instant can be obtained by finding the slope of the tangent drawn to the curve at the corresponding position

Conclusions from Displacement - Time Graph

  1. If the graph is parallel to time axis, then body is stationary.

  2. If graph is a straight line, then body is moving with a uniform velocity. The velocity can be found out by finding the slope of the graph.

  3. The graph can never be parallel to displacement axis, as it means that displacement increases indefinitely, without any increase in time, which is impossible.

  4. If graph is a curve, it means the body is moving with a variable velocity, and hence, it is some acceleration.

Velocity-Time Graphs

The graph that shows the variation in velocity of a moving object with time is called velocity - time graph.
In the velocity - time graph, time is taken on the X - axis and velocity on the Y - axis.
Since velocity \( \times \) time = displacement, the area enclosed between the velocity - time graph and the X - axis (i.e., time axis) gives the displacement of a body.

  1. . The velocity - time graph is a straight line parallel to the time axis for a body moving with uniform velocity as shown in Fig. 6

    In Fig.6, the slope of the straight line AB is zero. Therefore, its acceleration is zero.

  2. If the body is in motion with uniform acceleration (i.e., equal changes in velocity take place in equal intervals of time), the velocity-time graph is a straight line inclined to the time axis. The slope of the line gives the acceleration.

If the body is in motion with variable acceleration, the velocity - time graph is a curve.
To find the total displacement of a body in a particular time interval from Fig. 8, first, count the number of complete squares as well as the number of squares whose half or more than half portion is within the curve and time axis (the squares with less than half portion within the curve are ignored), and then, multiply it with the area of one square.

Conclusions

  1. If velocity - time graph is a straight line but moving away from velocity time axis, then:
    Body is moving with variable velocity.

  2. It has uniform acceleration, which can be found by the slope of graph.

  3. Displacement can be found, by finding area under the velocity - time graph.

  4. If slope is positive, then the body has positive acceleration and vice - versa.

  5. If the velocity - time graph is a curve, then:

    1. The body has variable velocity and variable acceleration.

    2. Area under the curve represents displacement.

    3. Acceleration at any instant can be found by finding slope at that point.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
A body drops a coin from top of a tower. Coin takes 't' seconds to come down from the top of a tower.
Calculate time taken to cover half the height of the tower.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} \begin{array}{ccc} Case - I & Case -II \\\\ \text{Time taken by coin be} \ t_n & \text{Time taken by coin to cover half height of tower be} \ t^1 \\\\ acceleration \ a_1 = g & acceleration \ a_2 = g \\\\ \text{intial velocity} \ u_1 = 0 & \text{intial velocity} \ u_2 = 0 \\\\ \text{distance travelled by coins} s_1 = h & \text{distance travelled by coins} s_2 = \frac{h}{2} \\\\ h = \frac{1}{2} gt_n^2.......(1) & \therefore \ \frac{h}{2} = \frac{1}{2} gt^{1^2} .......(2) \end{array} \end{align} \)
Dividing (1) by (2) we get
\( \begin{align} \frac{(1)}{(2)} = \frac{h}{ \frac{h}{2}} = \frac{ \frac{1}{2} gt^2}{ \frac{1}{2} gt^{1^2}} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 2 = \frac{t^2}{t^{1^2}} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow t^{1^2} = \frac{t^2}{2} = t^1 \\\\[3pt] = \frac{t}{ \sqrt{2}} s \end{align} \)

for body covers distance \( S_1 = \frac{1}{2} g \times (1)^2 = \frac{1}{2} g \)

  2. If the lift starts moving up with a uniform speed of \( 5 \ ms^{-1} \) , and the boy again throws the ball up with the maximum speed he can, how long does the ball take to return to his hands?

Solution:

  1. \( v(0) = 49 \ ms^{-1}, \ a = - 9.8 \ ms^{-2}, \ t = ?, \ v(t) = 0 \)
    v(t) = v(0) + at
    \( 0 = 49 - 9.8 t \ or \ 9.8 t = 49 \ or \ t = \frac{49}{9.8} s = 5 s \)
    This is the time taken by the ball to reach the maximum height. The time of descent is also 5 s.
    So, the total time after which the ball comes back is 5 s + 5 s i.e., 10 s

  2. The uniform velocity of the lift does not change the relative motion of ball and lift
    So, the ball would take the same total time i.e., it would come back after 10 second
    Aliter: \( 'u' = 49 \ ms^{-1}, \ 'a' = 9.8 \ ms^{-2}, \ t = ?, \ v = 0 \)
    Using v = u + at, 0 = 49 - 9.8 t or 9.8 t = 49 or \( t = \frac{49}{9.8} s = 5s \)
