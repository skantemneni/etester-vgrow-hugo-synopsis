Introduction

Physics is a branch of science that describes the physical state of matter and universe. In describing the physical state, one needs to involve in measuring various physical quantities. Hence, in the study of physics, measurement is one of the inevitable part. Apart from measuring one needs to identity the physical quantities involved, the proper devices used to measure them, the units involved and the techniques for their accurate measurement.
The world we experience can be broadly divided into three domains: micro, macro, and terrestrial.
Physics constitutes the quantitative study of natural phenomena, occurring in all the three domains mentioned above, in terms of physical quantities and the relationships among them. It begins with the definition of physical quantity. Any quantity that can be measured is called a physical quantity.

Physical Quantity

Any quantity which can be measured is called a physical quantity.
Example
length, mass, time, force etc.,

Types of physical quantities

Recall that every measurement is a process of comparison. Every comparison requires two quantities: the quantity to be compared and the standard quantity with which the are the comparison has to be made. The chosen standard quantity is called the unit
We know that a unit is a standard chosen for measurement. Can we choose the length of Sachin Tendulkar's bat as a unit of length? Can we choose the duration of human heartbeat as a unit of time? Can we choose the mass of a coconut as a unit of mass?
The above questions can be answered if we understand what properties a unit must satisfy

  1. It should be precise. This means that the standard is available to the best precision possible technologically.

  2. It should be reproducible. This means that it should of one be possible to make copies for people to use.

  3. It should be invariable. This means that the size of the unit should not depend on the time, place, and physical conditions such as temperature and pressure.

  4. It must be universally acceptable. This is essential because, in scientific work, results of experiments made in different countries need to be compared.

Physical quantities are classified into two types:

Fundamental quantities

The physical quantities which does not depend on other physical quantities are called fundamental quantities. For example length, mass, time etc.,
Fundamental units
The units used to measure fundamental quantities are called fundamental units. For example metre, kilogram, second etc.,

Derived quantities

The physical quantities which are derived from the fundamental quantities are called derived quantities.
Example

  1. Density = \( \frac{mass}{volume} \)

  2. Velocity = \( \frac{displacement}{time} \)

  3. Acceleration = \( \frac{change \ in \ velocity}{time} \) .

Derived unit
The units in which derived quantities are measured are called derived units.
Example
SI unit of density is \( kg \ m^{-3}, \) velocity is \( m \ s^{-1} \) , acceleration is \( m \ s^{-2} \)

Standard unit

To measure a physical quantity, we need a standard of the same quantity called a unit. The measurement of a physical quantity means to express the number of times its standard unit contained in that physical quantity. A standard unit must be possible to be defined without any ambiguity and any change with respect to space and time.

Rule of writing units

  1. Full stop or punctuation mark should not be used within or at the end of symbol of unit.
    Example
    10 kg but not 10 kg.

  2. Plural form should not be used in units.
    Example
    10 kg but not 10 kgs.

  3. Full names of units named after scientists should be written with initial lower case letter.
    Example
    newton but not SIR Isaac Newton or Newton

  4. The capital letter should be taken as a symbol for the unit named after a scientist.
    Example
    A for ampere, N for newton.

  5. Compound prefixes should be avoided.
    Example
    pF for picofarad but not \( \mu \mu F \) .

Measurement of Length

Length is one of the fundamental quantity. It is the distance between any two fixed points either in a straight or a curved path. The SI unit of length is metre. The other units usually used are km, cm, mm etc.,
Metre scale, vernier calipers, screw gauge are common devices used to measure length. A metre scale can measure a length of 1 mm accurately i.e. least count is 1 mm.

Vernier Calipers

Vernier callipers is an instrument used to measure the length of small objects. The instrument was designed in the year 1630 by the French mathematician Vernier.

Principle of vernier callipers

Let the length of one main scale division be 1 M.S.D. Consider 'n' number of divisions on the vernier such that the length of n divisions are equal to (n - 1) M.S.D.
\( \begin{align} n V.S.D = (n - 1) M.S.D \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ \frac{1}{n} \times \text{vales main scale division} \end{align} \) .

Least Count

The Least Count (L.C) or vernier constant of a vernier is the smallest length that can be accurately measured with it and is equal to the difference between a Main Scale Division (M.S.D) and a Vernier Scale Division (V.S.D).
L.C = 1 M.S.D - 1 V.S.D
Least count = 0.1 mm or 0.01 cm

Zero Error

When the two jaws of a vernier calipers are brought in contact with each other, then the zero of the vernier should coincide with the zero of the main scale. Sometimes they may not coincide. In such case, we say that the instrument has a zero error.

There are two kinds of zero errors namely positive zero error and negative zero error.

Positive zero error

If the zero of the vernier scale is to the right of the zero of the main scale, the error is positive. Since the zero error is positive, the zero correction is negative, because measured length is more than the actual length.

If \( n^{th} \) division of vernier coincides with a main scale division then zero error = \( + n \times L.C., \)
\( correction = - (n \times L.C) \)

Negative zero error

If the zero of the vernier scale is to the left of the zero of the main scale, the error is negative. Since the error is negative, the zero correction is positive, because measure length is less than the actual length.

If the \( n^{th} \) division of the vernier coincides with a main scale division, then the zero error
= - (N - n) L.C.
Correction = + (N - n) L.C.

Screw Guage

A screw gauge works on the principle of a screw in a nut.

Pitch of the Screw

It is the distance travelled by the tip of the screw for one complete rotation of its head.
Pitch of the screw,
\( \begin{align} = \frac{distance \ travelled \ by \ the \ screw}{number \ of \ rotations} \\\\[3pt] \frac{x}{n} = P \\\\[3pt] d = P.S.R + [(H.S.R) \times L.C] \end{align} \)
Same units must be used for both the measurements.

Zero error

When the stud and the tip of the screw of a screw gauge are in contact with each other, the zeroes of the head scale should coincide with the index line. If this does not happen, then the instrument is said to have a zero error.

positive zero error

If the zeroth division of the circular scale is 'below' the reference (index) line of the main scale, the error is said to be positive and the correction is negative.

Zero error = \( + n \times \) least count.

Negative zero error

Similarly, if the zeroeth division of the circular scale is 'above' the reference line of the main scale, the error is said to be negative and the correction is positive. If n is the circular scale division coinciding with the index line of the main scale, then the
Zero error = \( -(N - n) \times \) least count and the correction = \( +(N - n) \times \) least count
Where 'N' is the total number of divisions on the circular scale.

True measurement = Observed measurement + Correction for zero error.
Observed measurement = \( (M.S.R.) + (H.S.R. \times L.C.) \)

Back-lash Error

Sometimes the tip of screw does not move backward for a part of rotation of head in reverse direction because of wear and tear of threads. This is called backlash error. The backlash error can be avoided by not rotating the head in the reverse direction once the object is held between the two studs.

Measurement of Area

Area is a derived quantity, that is obtained by multiplying the two measurements of a regular body. For example the area of a rectangle is calculated as the product of its length and breadth. The SI unit of area is \( m^2 \) or square metre. The other units of area are \( cm^2, \ mm^2, \ km^2 \) etc.,

The area of an irregular object can be calculated by drawing the outline of its shape on a graph paper. The number of complete squares are calculated, next the squares greater than half are also counted as a square, leaving the squares less than half. The sum of all the squares gives the area of leaf to be that many \( cm^2 \)

Measurement of Volume

Volume is the space occupied by an object. The SI unit of volume is \( m^3 \) or cubic metre. The other units of volume are \( cm^3 \) , ml, l etc.,
\( \begin{align} 1 \ ml = 1 \ cm^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ ml = 10^{-6} \ m^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^{3} cm^3 = 10^{3} ml \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^{-3} m^3 \end{align} \)
Volume of regular bodies can be calculated by measuring the dimensions necessary for the calculations. The volume of a cube is given by \( (side)^3 \)

Volume of Liquid

Volume of liquids can be measured using the following apparatus.

Measuring Jar

It is graduated in ml from bottom to top and used to measure required amount of liquid.

Pipette

It is filled by sucking the liquid into it. It is used to take up only a fixed amount of liquid.

Burette

It resembles a measuring jar with a pitch cock provided at the bottom. It is used to take desired amount of liquids.

Volume of Irregular Solids

To find the volume of solid insoluble in water but heavier than water

  1. Take some water in a measuring jar.

  2. Let the volume of water taken be \( V_1, \ cm^3 \)

  3. Now immerse the solid completely in water using a string.

  4. Note the level of water, let it be \( V_2 \ cm^3 \)

  5. So, volume of solid = \( (V_2 - V_1)cm^3 \)

To find the volume of solid insoluble in water but lighter than water

  1. Take some water in a measuring jar along with a sinker.

  2. Let the volume of water along with the sinker taken be \( V_1 \ cm^3 \)

  3. Now tie the solid to the sinker and immerse completely into water using a string.

  4. Note the level of water, let it be \( V_2 \ cm^3 \)

  5. So, volume of solid = \( (V_2 - V_1)cm^3 \)

To find the volume of single drop of water

  1. Take a clean burette with water, clamp it upright.

  2. Remove any air bubbles formed.

  3. Now allow water to trickle slowly drop by drop, count number of drops when known volume of water has been drained out.

  4. Therefore, average volume of a single drop = \( \frac{Volume \ of \ water \ drained \ out}{number \ of \ drops} \)

To find the average Volume of lead shots

  1. Take a measuring jar with water of volume \( V_1 \ cm^3 \)

  2. Drop n number of lead shots into the water and note the final level of water as \( V_2 \ cm^3 \)

  3. Therefore, average volume of lead shot = \( \left ( \frac{V_2 - V_2}{n} \right ) c.c \)

Measurement of Mass

Mass is the quantity of matter present in a body. The SI unit of mass is kilogram. The other units used to measure mass are mg, quintal, tonne, etc.,
Common balance, physical balance are commonly used to measure mass of a body which work on the principle of moments.

Weight

The force of gravity with which a body is pulled towards the centre of the earth.
Weight (w) = \( mass (m) \times \) acceleration due to gravity (g)
\( g = 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} \)
The SI unit of weight is newton (N). The other units are kgwt, kgf, dynes.
1 kgwt = 1 kgf = 9.8 N
\( 1 N = 10^3 \ dynes \)
A spring balance is used to measure the weight of a body.

Density

It is defined as mass per unit volume of the substance. Density is a derived unit. It is a scalar quantity.

Measuring formula

\( Density = \frac{mass}{volume} i.e., \ d = \frac{M}{V} \)

Unit

S.I. unit of density is kg \( m^{-3} \)
C.G.S unit of density is g \( cm^{-3} \)
\( 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = 10^{-3} \ g \ cm^{-3} \)

Relation between the SI and CGS units

\( \begin{align} 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1 \ kg}{1 m^3} = \frac{1 \ kg}{1m \times 1m \times 1m} = \frac{1000 g}{100 cm \times 100 cm \times 100 cm} = \frac{1 g}{1000 cm^3} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1}{1000} g cm^{-3} \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) Density of a substance in SI system = 1000 \( \times \) density of the substance in CGS system.

Relative Density

It is the ratio of the density of the substance to the density of water at \( 4^{\circ} \ C \) .
\( \begin{align} Relative \ density (R.D) = \frac{density \ of \ the \ substance}{density \ of \ water \ at \ 4^{\circ} C} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{mass \ of \ thesubstance}{volume \ of \ the \ substance} + \frac{mass \ of \ water \ at \ 4^{\circ} C}{volume \ of water \ at \ 4^{\circ} C} \left[ D = \frac{M}{V} \right] \end{align} \)
Relative density does not have any units.

Measurement of Time

Time is the interval between two events. SI unit of time is second. The other units to measure time are hour, minute, century, decade etc.,
1 h = 3600 s 1 century = 100 years 1 millineium = 1000 years 1 min = 60 s 1 decade = 10 years
Simple pendulum is one of the devices used to measure time.

General terms related to Simple Pendulum:

Length of the bob

The distance between the point of suspension of the bob to the centre of the bob.

Frequency

The number of oscillations made in one second.

Time period

The time taken to complete one oscillation.

Amplitude

The maximum displacement of the bob, from the mean position.

Laws of Simple Pendulum

  1. The time period of a simple pendulum of constant length is independent of the amplitude of the bob (provided the angular amplitude of the bob is very small)

  2. The time period of a simple pendulum of constant length is independent of the size, shape, mass and material of the bob.

  3. The time period of a simple pendulum of constant length is directly proportional to the square root of the length of the simple pendulum.
    Let a simple pendulum of length l executes oscillations whose time period is T then
    \( \begin{align} T \propto \sqrt{l} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ T = 2 \pi \sqrt{\frac{l}{g}} \end{align} \) Where g = acceleration due to gravity of earth.

Significant Figures

In each numerical data either exact numbers or measured numbers (quantities) are specified. Exact numbers are those without any uncertainty or error.
The error or uncertainty of the results of a mathematical operation may be computed by statistical methods. A simple procedure for estimating this uncertainty involves the use of significant figures (sf). The degree of accuracy of a measured quantity depends on how finely divided the measuring scale of the instrument is, e.g., you might measure the length of an object as 3.5 cm with one instrument and 3.54 cm with another; the second instrument provides more significant figures and a greater degree of accuracy.
In general, the number of significant figures of a numerical quantity is the number of reliably known digits it contains.

The following rules are used while writing significant figures

  1. Zeros at the beginning of a number are not significant. They merely locate the decimal point. 0.0254 m three significant figures (2, 5, 4)

  2. Zeros within a number are significant. 104.6 m four significant figures (1, 0, 4, 6)

  3. Zeros at the end of a number after the decimal point are significant. 2705.0 m five significant figures (2, 7, 0, 5, 0)

  4. In whole numbers without a decimal point that end in one or more zeros, e.g., 500 kg the zeros may or may not be significant.

The rules for rounding off numbers are as follows

  1. If the next digit after the last significant figure is 5 or greater, round up : Increase the last significant figure by 1 (e.g., 2.136 becomes 2.14 rounded to 3 significant figures.)

  2. If the next digit after the last significant figure is less than 5, round down : Do not change the last significant figure. (e.g., 2.132 becomes 2.13 rounded to 2 significant figures.)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Find the CGS unit of force

Solution:

  1. \( Force = mass \times acceleration \)

  2. Mass is a fundamental quantity, and acceleration is a derived quantity which can be written as:
    \( acceleration = \frac{change \ in \ Velocity}{time} \)
    for unit purpose, acceleration = \( \frac{length}{(time)^2} \)

  3. Substituting the units of the quantities we have CGS unit of force
    \( \begin{align} = \frac{CGS \ unit \ of \ mass \times \ CGS \ unit \ of \ length}{(CGS \ unit \ of \ time)^2} = \frac{g cm}{s^2} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ CGS \ unit \ of \ force \ is \ g \frac{cm}{s^2} \end{align} \) which is also named as dyne

Example 2:

Question:
If the \( 3^{rd} \) division of the vernier coincides with \( 3^{rd} \) division of a main scale, then find the correction to be made.

Solution:
Zero correction = \( -3 \times 0.01 \ cm = - 0.03 \ cm \)

Example 3:

Question:
A vernier callipers has 20 divisions on the vernier scale. 1 cm on main scale is divided into 20 equal parts. Find the least count of the vernier.

Solution:
Number of divisions on vernier scale = 20 i.e., N = 20
1 cm = 20 divisions,
\( \begin{align} \frac{1 cm}{20} = 1 \ division \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ \text{1 main scale division} = \frac{1}{20} cm = 0.05 \ cm \\\\[3pt] Find L.C = ? \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ Least count (L.C) = \frac{1 MSD}{\text{Number of vernier scale divisions}} = \frac{0.05 cm}{20} = 0.0025 \ cm \end{align} \)

Example 4:

Question:
While measuring the length of a chalkpiece by vernier callipers the main scale reading is 4.6 cm and the \( 6^{th} \) division on vernier scale is found to coincide with a division on the main scale. Find the length of the chalk, if L.C of vernier is 0.01 cm and zero error is -0.02 cm.

Solution:
M.S.R = 4.6 cm, V.S.D = 6, L.0 = 0.01 cm, zero error = -0.02 cm
correct length of chalk = \( M.S.R + (V.S.D \times L.C) \pm error \) In this case error is negative, then the correction will be positive.
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ Correct \ length = M.S.R + (V.S.D \times L.C) + correction \\\\[3pt] = 4.6 cm + (6 \times 0.01 cm) + 0.02 cm \\\\[3pt] = 4.6 cm + 0.06 cm + 0.02 cm = 4.68 cm \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
Find the least count of a screwgauge whose head scale is divided into 100 divisions and moves 10 mm for 10 rotations of the head.

Solution:
pitch of the screw P =
\( \begin{align} \frac{x}{n} = \frac{10 mm}{10} = 1 mm \\\\[3pt] Least \ count = \frac{pitch \ of \ the \ screw}{number \ of \ head \ scale \ divisions} = \frac{1 mm}{100} = 0.01 mm = 0.001 cm \end{align} \)

Example 6:

Question:
Find the least count of a screwgauge whose head scale is divided into 200 divisions and moves 5 mm for 5 rotations of the head.

Solution:
pitch of the screw P =
\( \begin{align} \frac{x}{n} = \frac{5 mm}{5} = 1 \ mm \\\\[3pt] Least \ count = \frac{pitch \ of \ the \ screw}{number \ of \ head \ scale \ divisions} = \frac{1 mm}{200} = 0.005 mm = 0.0005 cm \end{align} \)

Example 7:

While measuring the diameter of a steel ball the following readings were noted :

  1. P.S.R. = 8 mm

  2. H.S.R = 42

  3. L.C. = 0.01 mm

  4. Error = -0.05 mm.

Find out the diameter

Solution:
Correction is + 0.05 mm
Diameter = \( P.S.R. + (H.S.R. + n) L.C. (or) P.S.R + (H.S.R \times L.C.) + Correction \)
\( = 8 mm + (42 \times 0.01 mm) + 0.05 mm = 8.47 mm \)

Example 8:

Head of a screw gauge is divided into 50 divisions. It advances 2 mm when screw is turned though 4 rotations. Find the pitch of the screw and L.C. of the instrument.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Pitch = = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{2 mm}{4} = 0.5 mm \\\\[3pt] L.C = \frac{Pitch \ of \ the \ screw}{no.of \ head \ scale \ divisions} = \frac{0.5 mm}{50} = \frac{1}{100} = 0.01 \ mm \end{align} \)
