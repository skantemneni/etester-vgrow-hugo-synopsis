Common misconception Fact
1 Energy can be created or destroyed Energy can neither be created nor be destroyed. It can only be changed from one form to another.
2 Whenever we carry a load, then we say work has been done. We do work only if we carry a load and move it through a distance. No work is done if the load does not move through a distance.
3 The terms energy and power are same. The term 'energy' is different from 'power: Energy refers to the capacity to perform the work. Power determines the rate of performing the work.Thus, in determining power, time taken to perform the work is significant but it is of no importance for measuring energy of a body.

Introduction

A man is sitting in a chair. Though he does not attend to any work physically, his eyes do their work by seeing, the nose does its work by smelling and breathing. Even the internal organs do their work, the heart goes on beating and pumping blood to various parts of the body, the lungs work by filtering the air breathed by the liver etc. But according to physics, the quantity of work done is a force causing a displacement. Work has nothing to do with the amount of time in which this force acts to cause the displacement
If two people a hiker and a rock climber might do the same amount of work, yet the hiker does the work in considerably less time than the rock climber. The quantity which has to do with the rate at which a certain amount of work is done is known as the power. The hiker has a greater power rating, than the rock climber.
Energy is the word often used in our day to day life. A man does laborious work or a child works energetically, then we say that they have full of energy. Much the similar way, in physics, too anything that has the ability to do work is said to possess energy.

Work

Work is said to be done by a force on a body, when the force displaces the body through a certain distance in the direction or against the direction of the force.
Work is said to be done when force acting on a body displaces it.

Measurement of work

The amount of work done by a body is measured as the product of magnitude of the force applied and the distance moved by the point of application in the direction of force.
Work = force \( \times \) displacement of the body
W = F . S
work is a scalar quantity.

Units

Work done is the dot product of force and displacement. SI units of force and displacement are newton (N) and metre (m), respectively. Therefore, the unit of work is 'newton pc metre' (N m). This unit is called joule (J) in honour of the British scientist, James Prescott Joule.
'Work done is 1 joule if a force of I newton acting on a body displaces it by 1 metre in the direction of the force'.
The CGS unit of work is erg.
'Work done is 1 erg if a force of I dyne acting on a body displaces it by 1 centimetre in the direction of the force'.
The joule and the erg are the absolute units of work.
Absolute units
S.I. unit: newton - meter or joules
C.G.S unit: dyne - cm or ergs
Gravitational units
S.I unit: kgwt - m, 1 kgwt - m = 9.8 newton - m = 9.8 J
C.G.S unit: gwt - cm, 1 gwt - cm = 980 dyne - cm or 980 erg

Work Done in Different Cases

The magnitude of work done mainly depends on the magnitude of force and displacement. So, it can be expressed in following cases.
Case 1:
Displacement in the direction of force:
A force \( '\overrightarrow{F}' \) acting on an object. The object has a displacement 'S' in the direction of force. Then the work done is the product of force and displacement.
Work done (W) = Force (F) \( \times \) displacement in same direction.
Formula: W = F S
Case 2:
Displacement at an angle ( \( \theta \) ) with the force:
When a force \( \overrightarrow{F} \) moves the body by a distance S is a direction at an angle \( \theta \) with the force. Work done is given by:
Work = Horizontal component of force in direction of displacement \( \times \) displacement
\( \begin{align} W = (F \ cos \ \theta )S \\\\[3pt] W = FS \ cos \ \theta \end{align} \)
Note

  1. If \( \theta < 90^{\circ}, \ cos \ \theta \) is positive. So work done is positive.

  2. If \( \theta > 90^{\circ}, \ cos \ \theta \) is negative. So work done is negative.

  3. If \( \theta = 90^{\circ}. cos \ \theta = 0^{\circ} \) So no work is done by the force on the body.

  4. If \( \theta = 180^{\circ}, \ cos \ 180^{\circ} = - 1 \) So work done is negative.

Case 3:
Work done against gravity
Let a body of mass (m) lifted by an upward a force (F = mg) through a height.
Then, work done = force \( \times \) distance
W = FS = mgh ( \( g = 9.8 m s^{-2} \ or \ 10 \ m \ s^{-2} \) )
Case 4:
Displacement and force are in opposite direction
By applying force 'F' a moving car is tried to stop the car. If the car comes to rest after travelling some distance 'S' opposite to the direction of force, then the force is applied opposite to the direction of the displacement. So the displacement is taken as '-S'.
Work done = Force acting \( \times \) displacement in opposite direction.
W = - FS
Case 5:
Where work is not done

  1. Work is zero if applied force is zero (W = 0 if F = 0)

  2. Work is zero if \( cos \theta \) is zero or \( \theta = \frac{ \pi}{2} \) .

  3. Work done is zero when displacement is zero.

Power

Power is the rate of doing work.
\( Power (P) = \frac{work(W)}{time(t)} \)
For a particle acted upon by a constant force \( \overrightarrow{F} \) , causing displacements \( \overrightarrow{s} \) , the work donw is given by \( W = \overrightarrow{F} . \overrightarrow{s} \) .
Hence, the power can be written as
\( \begin{align} P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{\overrightarrow{F} \times \overrightarrow{s}}{t} \\\\[3pt] = \overrightarrow{F} \left ( \frac{\overrightarrow{s}}{t} \right ) = \overrightarrow{F} . \overrightarrow{v} \end{align} \)
Where \( \overrightarrow{v} = \left ( \frac{\overrightarrow{s}}{t} \right ) \)
\( \therefore \ P = \overrightarrow{F} . \overrightarrow{v} \)
The particle moves with a constant velocity when the applied force just overcomes the frictional force. The rate at which energy is porduced or utilized is called power.
Units
The S.I unit of power is watt (W). This is named after James Watt, the British inventor of steam engine. If 1 joule of work is done in 1 second, then the power is said to be 1 watt. The watt is frequently used to measure any kind of power.
In many branches of engineering, the power output of machines is expressed in terms of horsepower which is defined as \( 550 \ ft - lb \ s^{-1} \) (a horse pulling a weight of 550 lb through 1 ft in 1s).
C.G.S unit = erg \( s^{-1} \)
MKS unit of power = \( J \ S^{-1} \)
SI unit of power = watt
\( 1 \ watt = \frac{1 \ joule}{1 \ second} \)
Biggerunits of power are 1 kilowatt (kW) \( = 10^3 \ W \)
1 Megawatt (MW) \( = 10^6 \ W \)

Horse Power

1 horse power (HP) = 745.7 W \( \approx \) 746 W .

Power is calculated using different expressions depending on the situation

1 Power to move a body \( P = \frac{FS}{t} \)
2 Power to pull a body \( P = \frac{-FS}{t} \)
3 Power to pull a body \( P = \frac{FS \ cos \ \theta}{t} \)
4 Power to lift a body against gravity \( P = \frac{mgh}{t} \)

Energy

Energy is the capacity (or) the ability of a body to do work. It is a scalar quantity

Units

S.I unit: joule (J)
CGS unit: erg

units of Energy

  1. Electrical units of energy: watt-hour and kilowatt-hour (kWh).1 kWh = \( 3.6 \times 10^6 \ J \)

  2. Units of heat energy: calorie and kilocalorie. 1 calorie = 4.18 J (or) 4.2 J

  3. Units of atomic energy: electron-volt. \( 1 \ eV = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \ J \)

Different forms of energy

The energy possessed by a body is due to its state of rest (or) of uniform motion is called mechanical energy. The mechanical energy is found in two forms viz., kinetic energy (K.E.) and potential energy (P.E.)

Kinetic Energy

The energy possessed by a body by virtue of its motion is called kinetic energy.
(Or) The work done by a body by virtue of its motion is called kinetic energy.
Examples

  1. Energy possessed by a shooting arrow.

  2. Energy possessed by blowing wind.

  3. Energy possessed by a speeding vehicle

Mathematical expression for K.E

Consider a body of mass (m) at rest. When a force is applied it sets into motion and acquires a velocity after travelling distance of 's'.

K.E. = Force \( \times \) Displacement = ma \( \times \) S ...... (1)
from equation of motion, \( v^2 - u^2 = 2aS \)
\( a = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2S} \)
Substituting above value in equation (1) we get,
Kinetic energy \( = m \times \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2S} \times S \)
\( \begin{align} K.E = m \times \frac{v^2 - 0^2}{2S} \times S \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ K.E = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 \end{align} \)
Kinetic energy = \( \frac{1}{2} \times mass \times (velocity)^2 \)
The larger the mass of the body greater is the K.E. If the mass of the body is doubled, the KE is also doubled. If velocity of a body is doubled, then its KE will be increased by four times.

Potential Energy

Energy possessed by an object by virtue of its relative position (or) state.
Potential energy is stored energy that depends upon the relative position of a system.
Examples

  1. A spring has a more potential energy when it is compressed or stretched.

  2. A ball has more potential energy raised above the ground than it has after falling to the earth.

  3. The energy possessed by a stretched bow.

  4. The energy possessed by water, stored high up in the dam.

Mathematical Expression for Potential Energy

Consider a body of mass 'm' lifted to a height 'h' against gravity. Force applied for lifting the body of mass (m) from A to B = mg.
The workdone (W = mgh) is stored in the form of potential energy.
\( \therefore \) Potential energy = mgh.

Law of conservation of Energy

Energy can neither be created nor destroyed but it can be transformed from one form of energy to another form. The total energy in the universe remains constant. Consider a body of mass (m) falling freely from height 'h'.

The diagram shows that body of mass (m) at rest at a point A at a height (h) from the ground. It falls freely from A and reach B, at a distance x below. A reaches C the ground via B.

At position A

\( \therefore \ KE = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} m(0)^2 = 0 \)
and PE = mgh
Total Energy = KB + P.E
= 0 + mgh = mgh
Therefore at highest point the total energy is stored in the form of potential energy i.e., mgh.

At position B

\( \begin{align} PE = mgh_B = mg(h - x) \\\\[3pt] KE_B = \frac{1}{2} \ mv_{B^2} = \frac{1}{2} m(2gx) \ \ \ \ \ \ [\therefore \ v^2 - u^2 = 2gs, \ v^2u^2 = 2 x, \ v^2 - (0)^2 = 2gx] \end{align} \)
Total energy at \( B = KE_B + PE_B = mgx + mg(h - x) = mgx + mgh - mgx = mgh \) .

At position C

\( KE_c = \frac{1}{2} mv_c = \frac{1}{2} 2mgh = mgh \) .
\( \therefore \) Total energy at \( C = KE_c + PE_c = 0 + mgh = mgh \)
\( \therefore \) Total energy is possessed by the object is always constant

Transformation of Energy

According to the law of conservation of energy, energy can be transferred from one form to another form of energy. This is called the transformation of energy.
For example, when a bullet is fired into a target, the entire of the bullet is lost and converted into heat and sound. This energy cannot be utilized further, and so the energy of the bullet is dissipated.

Laws Relating to Energy

Work - energy theorem:

Whenever work is done on an object, its energy increases. When we push a block kept on a table, it starts moving. We do work on the block and consequently it acquires kinetic energy.
Similarly, when we compress a spring, we exert inward force at the two ends. As the ends move inwards during compression, the force at the two ends does work on the spring. As a result, the spring is compressed and its elastic potential energy is increased, thus we can say that any work done on a system is stored in the form of energy.
When a ball moves on the ground, the force of friction does some negative work on the ball. This is because the force of friction is in the backward direction. Because of the negative work, the kinetic energy of the ball decreases and it comes to a stop. Thus, we can say that any work done on a system brings a change in the energy of the system. This is stated as the work energy theorem given below.
Work done by external forces on a system is equal to the change in the system's energy.
Consider a body of mass m falling from a height h, freely under the force of gravity.

From the figure,

    1. \( U_1 = mgh \)

    2. \( U_2 = mgh_2 \)

    3. \( U_1 > U_2 \ \ \ (since \ h_1 > h_2) \)

    1. \( T_1 = \frac{1}{2} mu^2 \)

    2. \( T_2 = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 \)

    3. \( T_1 < T_2 \ \ \ (since \ u < v ) \)

The work done by gravity in brining the body down by a height h is given by
\( \begin{align} W = \overrightarrow{F} . \overrightarrow{s} = mg(h_1 - h_2) \\\\[3pt] = mgh_1 - mgh_2 \ \ \ ( \because \ a = g) \\\\[3pt] = U_1 - U_2 ........ (1) \end{align} \)
= Change in PE
Considering the equation,
\( \begin{align} W= ma(h_1 - h_2) \\\\[3pt] W = mah \ \ \ \ ( \because \ h = h_1 - h_2) \\\\[3pt] W = m \left ( \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2} \right ) \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \left ( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \ ; \ as = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2} \right ) \\\\[3pt] \left (\therefore \ ah = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2} \right ) \\\\[3pt] W = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 - \frac{1}{2} mu^2 \\\\[3pt] W = T_2 - T_1 ....... (2) \end{align} \)
= Change in KE
Thus, we see that the work done by a force on a system is equal to the change in the energy of the system.
From Eqs. (1) and (2), we get
\( U_1 - U_2 = T_2 - T_1 \) ......(3)
i.e., change in PE = change in. KE
Further, since \( U_1 > U_2 \) and \( T_1 < T_2 \)
Decrease in PE = Increase in KE
Equation (3) can be re-written as
\( \begin{align} U_1 + T_1 = U_2 + T_2 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ E_1 = E_2 \end{align} \) ...... (4)
El = E2 (4) Equation (4) shows that the total energy of the system is conserved. This leads to another law relating to energy, the law of conservation of energy.

Solved examples

Example 1:

How much work is done in ergs in pulling a box 2 m across a table top with a force of 20 N in horizontal direction

Solution:
Force F = 20 N
Displacement of Box, S = 2 m
work done = Force \( \times \) Displacement
\( W = 20 \ N \times 2 \ m = 40 \ J = 40 \times 10^7 \ erg (\because \ 1 \ J = 10^7 \ erg) \)

Example 2:

A car weighing 600 kg travels with \( 72 \ km \ h^{-1} \) stops at a distance of 50 m deccelerating uniformly. What is the force exerted by the brakes? What is the work done by brakes?

Solution:
Mass of the car m = 600 kg
Initial velocity of car = \( u = 72 \ km \ h^{-1} = 72 \times \ m \ s^{-1} = 20 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Final velocity of the car v = 0
Distance covered s = 50
\( \begin{align} W = F \times S \\\\[3pt] F = ma a = \frac{u^2 - v^2}{2s} \\\\[3pt] a = \frac{(0)^2 - (20)^2}{2 \times 50} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ \frac{0 - 400}{100} = - 4 \ m \ s^{-2} \end{align} \)
Retarding force = 600(+ 4) = 2400 N
From formula \( W = F \times S \)
\( W = 2400 \times 50 = 120 \ kJ \)

A man weighing 50 kg supports a man of 20 kg on his hand. What is the work done by him when

  1. he is stationary

  2. he moves a distance of 30 m on a smooth horizontal road.

  4. he moves over a distance of 20 m up an inclined plane rising

1 in 10 ( \( g = 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} \) ) (Hint \( sin \ \theta = \frac{1}{MA} \) )

m = 50 + 20 = 70 kg
s = 30 m

    \( W = F times 0 = 0 \)

  2. When he moves a distance of 30 m on smooth horizontal road.
    Work done = 0

  3. Distance moved in upward direction = 30 m

    = \( 70 \times 9.8 \times 30 \)

  4. The body moves up an inclined plane, so work done against gravity is the component \( mg \ sin \ \theta \) the inclined plane rises by 1 in 10 \( \Rightarrow \ sin \ \theta = \frac{1}{10} = 0.1 \)
    Work done = \( mg \ sin \ A \times S = 60 \times 9.8 \times 0.1 times 30 \)

Example 4:

A car at rest has mass 1000 kg. It is moved by a horizontal force of 50 N on a horizontal surface. Calculate the work done by the force in 8 s

Solution:
Mass of body (m) = 1000 kg
Force (F) = 50 N
Time (t) = 8s
Work done (W) = ?
From equation of motion, \( S = ut + \frac{1}{2} at^2 \)
\( S = \frac{1}{2} at^2 \ \ \ \ \ [\because \ u = 0] \)
From the equation F = ma
\( \begin{align} a = \frac{F}{m}, \ a = \frac{500}{1000} = 0.05 \ m \ s^{-2} \\\\[3pt] S = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.05 \times (8)^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.05 \times 64 = 1.6 m \end{align} \)
s = 1.6 m
From formula \( W = F \times S \)
\( W = 50 \times 1.6 = 80 \)
The work done by the force in 8 s is 80 J

Example 5:

Calculate the displacement caused by the force in horizontal direction when force of 800 N is acting at an angle of \( 30^{\circ} \) to the horizontal and does a work of 80,000 J.

What would be the power of an engine required to lift 9 metric tons of coal per hour from a mine 200 m deep ( \( g = 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} \) )

Example 13:

Calculate the kinetic energy of a body of mass 10 kg moving with a velocity \( 2 \ m \ s^{-1} \) .

Solution:
Mass of the body (m) = 10 kg
Velocity of the body (v) = \( 2 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Kinetic energy (K.E) = ?
\( \begin{align} K.E = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times (2)^2 \\\\[3pt] = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 4 = 20 \ joule \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) The K.E of the body = 20 joule

Example 14:

When the mass and velocity of the body are doubled, what happens to its kinetic energy.

Solution:
Case - I
Mass of body \( (m_1) \) = m
velocity \( v_1 = v \)
Kinetic energy \( (K.E)_1 = K \)
\( KE_1 = K = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 \ ..... \ (1) \)
Dividing (II) by (I) we get
\( \begin{align} \frac{K.E_2}{K.E_1} = \frac{K.E_2}{K} = \frac{4 mv^2}{\frac{1}{2} mv^2} \\\\[3pt] KE_2 = 8 \ K \end{align} \)
Case - II
Doubled mass of the body \( (m)^2 = 2m \)
Doubled velocity of the body \( v^2 = 2v \)
Kinetic energy \( K^2 \) = ?
\( KE_2 = \frac{1}{2} mv_{2^2} = \frac{1}{2} \times 2u^2 = 4mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(8 \ mv^2) \ ..... \ (2) \)
\( \therefore \) When the mass and, velocity of the body are doubled, the kinetic energy becomes 8 times the original.

Example 15:

Two bodies of masses \( m_1 \) , and \( m_2 \) are moving with equal kinetic energies. What is the ratio of their momenta?

Solution:
Let \( p_1 \) and \( p_2 \) be the momenta of first and second body respectively.
Case - I
Mass of first body \( (m_1) \) = \( m_1 \)
Momentum of first body \( P_1 \)
Ratio of their momentum \( \frac{p_1}{p_2} = ? \)
\( \begin{align} KE = \frac{p_2}{2m} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ P = \sqrt{2mKE} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow P \propto \sqrt{m} \ \Rightarrow \ \frac{P_1}{P_2} = \frac{\sqrt{m_1}}{\sqrt{m_2}} \ \ \ \ \ ( \because \ \text{K.E. is same in the both the cases}) \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ P_1 : P_2 = \sqrt{m_1} : \sqrt{m_2} \end{align} \)
Therefore, the ratio of their momentum is \( \sqrt{m_1} : \sqrt{m_2} \)

Example 18:

Height of the wall = 3.6 m
Potential energy of gained by = 2268 J
Gravitational force \( (g) = 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} \)
Mass of the mass (m) = ?
\( \begin{align} P.E = mgh \\\\[3pt] m = \frac{P.E}{gh} \ \Rightarrow \ m = \frac{2268}{9.8 \times 3.6} \\\\[3pt] h = \frac{2268}{35.28} = 64.28 \ kg \end{align} \)

Example 19:

A bag of rice weighs 200 kg. To what height should it be raised, so that its potential energy may be 9800 J (Take \( (g) = 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} \) )

Solution:
Given, mass of the bag, (m) = 200 kg, Potential energy, (P.E) = 9800 J
Height to which it is raised, (h) = ?
Selection of formula : P.E = mgh
Substituting the values, we get
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \ 9800 = 200 \times 9.8 \times h \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ h = \frac{9800}{200 \times \frac{90}{100}} \ \Rightarrow \ h = 5 \ m \end{align} \)
\( \Rightarrow \) The bag of rice should be raised to a height of 5 m
