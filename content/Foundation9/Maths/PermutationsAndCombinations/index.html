Factorial Notation

The continued product of first "n" natural numbers is called "n factorial" or "factorial n" and is denoted by \( \angle n \) or n!
Thus, \( \angle n \) or n! = 1.2.3.4............(n - 1).n
= n.(n - 1).(n - 2)...........3.2.1
(in reverse order)
Note:

  1. When "n" is a negative integer or a fraction, n! is not defined. Thus, n! is defined only for positive integers.

  2. According to the above definition, 0! makes no sense. However, we define 0! = 1.

  3. n! = n (n - 1)!

  4. (2n)! = \( 2^n \) n![1.3.5.7..........(2n - 1)].

Fundamental Principal of counting

Multiplication Principle

If an operation can be performed in 'm' different ways; following which a second opera-tion can be performed in 'n' different ways, then the two operations in succession can be performed in \( m \times n \) different ways.
Example:
How many numbers of two digits can be formed out of the digits 1, 2, 3 and 4 no digit being repeated ?
Sol.
The first digit can be any one of the four digits 1, 2, 3, 4, that is the first digit can be chosen in four ways. Having chosen the first digit, we are left with three digits from which the second digit can be chosen. Therefore, the possible ways of choosing the two digits are 4 \( \times \) 3 = 12.
Since the first digit can be chosen in four ways and for each choice of the first digit there are three ways of choosing the second digit, therefore, there are 4 \( \times \) 3 = ways, that is,

12 ways of choosing both the digits. Thus, 12 numbers can be formed.

Addition Principle

If an operation can be performed in 'm' different ways and another operation, which is independent of the first operation, can be performed in 'n' different ways, then either of the two operations can be performed in (m + n) ways.
Note: The above two principle can be extended for any finite number of operations.
Example:
Suppose there are 5 gates to stadium, 2 on one side and 3 on the other. Mohan has to go out of the stadium. He can go out from any one of the 5 gates.
Thus, the number of ways in which he can go out is 5. Hence, the work of going out through the gates on one side will be done in 2 ways and the work of going out through the gates on other side will be done in 3 ways.
The work of going out will be done when Mohan goes out from side I or side II. Thus the work of going out can be done in (2 + 3) = 5 ways.

Note: Addition theorem of counting is also true for more than two operations.

Permutation

Each of the different arrangements which can be made by taking some or all of given number of things or objects at a time is called a permutation.
Note: Permutation of things means arrangement of things. The word arrangement is used if order of things is taken into account.
Thus, if order of different things changes, then their arrangement also changes.

Notation

Let "r" and "n" be positive integers such that \( 1 \leq r \leq n \) . Then, the number of permutations of "n" different things, taken "r" at a time, is denoted by the symbol \( ^n p_r \) or P (n, r).

Some basic results

\( ^nP_r \ or \ \rho (n, \ r) = \frac{n !}{(n - r) !} = n(n - 1) (n - 2) ........ [n - (r + 1)], \ 0 \leq r \leq n \)
The number of permutations of "n" things, taken all at a time, out of which "p" are alike and are of one type, "q" are alike and are of second type and rest are all different \( = \frac{n !}{p ! q !} \)
Example:
There are 5 red, 4 white and 3 blue marbles in a bag. They are drawn one by one and arranged in a row. Assuming that all the 12 marbles are drawn, determine the number of different arrangements.
sol.
Here n = 12, p = 5, q = 4 and r = 3
\( \therefore \) The required number of different arrangements
\( \begin{align} = \frac{n !}{p_1 ! q_2 ! r_3 !} = \frac{12 !}{5 ! 4 ! 3 !} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{12. 11. 10. 9. 8. 7. 6}{4. 3. 2. 1. 3. 2. 1} = \frac{11. 10. 9. 8. 7}{2} \\\\[3pt] = 990 \times 4 \times 7 = 27720 \end{align} \)
The number of permutations of "n" different things taken "r" at a time when each thing may be repeated any number of times is \( n^r . \)
Example:
In how many ways can 5 apples be distributed among 4 boys, there being no restriction to the number of apples each boy may get ?
sol:
The required number of ways = \( 4^5 \)

Permutations under restrictions:

  1. Number of permutations of "n" different things, taken "r" at a time, when a particuÂ¬lar thing is to be always included in each arrangement, is
    \( r.^{n - 1}P_{r - 1}. \)

  2. Number of permutations of "n" different things, taken "r" at a time, when "s" particular things are to be always included in easy arrangement, is
    \( s!(r - (s - 1)). ^{n - s}P_{r - s}. \)

  3. Number of permutations of "n" different things, taken "r" at a time, when a particular thing is never taken in each arrangement, is
    \( ^{n - 1}P_r . \)

  4. Number of permutations of "n" different things, taken all at a time, when "m" specified things always come together, is \( m! \times (n - m + 1)! \)

  5. Number of permutations of "n" different things, taken all at a time, when "m" specified things never come together, is \( n! - m! \times (n - m + 1)!. \)

Circular Permutations

  1. Number of circular arrangements (permutations) of "n" different things = (n - 1)!.
    Example:
    In how many ways can eight people be seated at a round table ?
    sol:
    Required number of ways = (8 - 1)! = 7! = 5040.

  2. Number of circular arrangements (permutations) of "n" different things when clockwise and anticlockwise arrangements are not different, that is, when observation can be made from both sides = \( \frac{1}{2} (n - 1)! \)
    Required number of arrangements \( = \frac{1}{2}(5 - 1)! = \frac{1}{2} \times 4! = 12. \)

Combination

Each of the different groups or selections which can be made by taking some or all of a number of things (irrespective of order) is called a combination.
Combination of things means selection of things. Obviously, in selection of things order of things has no importance. Thus, with the change of order of things selection of things does not change.

Notations

The number of combinations of "n" different things taken "r" at a time is denoted by "Cr or C (n, r)
Thus,
\( \begin{align} ^nC_r = \frac{n !}{r! (n - r)!} \ \ \ \ (0 \leq r \leq n) \\\\[3pt] = \frac{^nP_r}{r!} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2) .......(n - r + 1)}{r(r - 1) (r - 2).......3.2.1} \end{align} \) If r > n, then \( ^nC_r = 0 \)

Key Points to Remember

  1. \( ^nC_r = ^nC_{n - r} \)

  2. \( ^nC_0 = ^nC_n = 1, \ ^nC_1 = n \)

  3. If \( ^nC_x = ^nC_y, \) then either x = y or x + y = n.

  4. \( ^nC_r + ^nC_{r - 1} = ^{n + 1}C_r \)

  5. \( \frac{^nC_r}{^nC_{r - 1}} = \frac{n - r + 1}{r} \)

  6. If "n" is even, then the greatest value of \( ^nC_r \) is \( \frac{^nC_n}{2} \)

  7. If "n" is odd, then the greatest value of \( ^nC_r \) is \( \frac{^nC_{ \frac{n + 1}{2}}}{2} \) or \( \frac{^nC_{n - 1}}{2} \)

  8. \( ^nC_r = \frac{ r \ decreasing \ numbers \ strating \ with \ 'n'}{r \ increasing \ numbers \ strating \ with \ 'l'} \)
    \( = \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2).......(n - r + 1)}{1.2.3.....r} \)

  9. \( ^nC_0 + ^nC_1 + ^nC_2 + .... + ^nC_n = 2^n \)

  10. \( ^nC_0 + ^nC_2 + ^nC_4 + ......... = ^nC_1 + ^nC_3 + ^nC_5 + ..... = 2^{n - 1}. \)

  11. Number of combinations of "n" different things taken "r" at a time.

    1. When "p" particular things are always included = \( ^{n - p} C_{r - p} \)

    2. When "p" particular things are never included = \( ^{n - p} C_{r} \)

    3. when p particular things are not together in any selection = \( ^nC_r - ^{n - p}C_{r - p} \)
      Example
      In how many ways can 5 members forming a committee out of 10 be selected so that

    1. two particular members must be included.

    2. two particular members must not be included
      Sol

    1. When two particular members are included, then, we have to select 5 - 2 = 3 members out of 10 - 2 = 8
      \( \therefore \) The required no.of ways
      \( = C(8, 3) = \frac{8 !}{5! 3!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{6} = 56 \)

    2. When 2 particular members are not included, then, we have to select 5 members out of 10 - 2 = 8.
      \( \therefore \) The required no.of ways
      \( = C(8, 5) = \frac{8 !}{5! 3!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{6} = 56 \)

    1. Number of selections of "r" consecutive things out of "n" things in a row = n - r + 1.

    2. Number of selections of "r" consecutive things out of "n" things along a circle
      \( \begin{align} = \left\lbrace \begin{array}{cc} n, \ \ \ where \ \ \ r < n \\\\ 1, \ \ \ when \ \ \ r = n \end{array}\right. \end{align} \)

    1. Number of selections of zero or more things out of n different things.
      \( = ^nC_0 + ^nC_1 + ^nC_2 + ...... + ^nC_n = 2^n \)

    2. Number of combinations of "n" different things selecting at least one of them is
      \( = ^nC_1 + ^nC_2 ...... + ^nC_n = 2^n - 1 \)

    3. Number of selections of r things \( (r \leq n) \) out of "n" identical things is 1.

    4. Number of selections of one or more things out of "n" identical things = n + 1.

    5. Number of selections of one or more things out of "n" identical things = n.

    6. If out of (p + q + r + t) things, "p" are alike of one king, "q" are alike of second kind,
      "r" are alike of third kind and "t" are different, then the total number of selections is \( (p + 1) (q + 1) (r + 1) 2^t- 1. \)

    7. If out of (p + q + r + t) things, "p" are alike of one king, "q" are alike of second kind, "r" are alike of third kind and "t" are different, then the total number of selections is \( (p + 1) (q + 1) (r + 1) 2t- 1. \)

    8. The number of ways of selection some or all out of p + q + r items where "p" are alike of one kind, "q" are alike of second kind and rest are alike of third kind is [ (p + 1) (q + 1) (r +1) - 1.

    1. Number of ways of dividing (m + n) different things in two groups containing "m" and "n" things, respectively \( (m \neq n) \) is
      \( ^{m + n}C_m = {(m + n)!}{m ! n !} \)

    2. Number of ways of dividing (m + n + p) different things in two groups containing "m" and "n" things, respectively \( (m \neq n \neq p) \) is :
      \( \frac{(m + n + p)!}{m ! n ! p !} \)

Some useful shortcut Methods

The number of triangle which can be formed by joining the angular points of a polygon of "n" sides as vertices are \( \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2)}{6} \)
Example
Find the number of triangles formed by joining the vertices of an octagon.
sol
The required number of triangles
\( = \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2)}{6} \)
\( = \frac{8(8 - 1) (8 - 2)}{6} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{6} = 56 \)
The number of diagonals which can be formed by joining the vertices of a polygon of "n" sides are
\( \frac{n(n - 3)}{2} \)
Example
How many diagonals are there in a decagon?
sol
The required number of diagonals
\( = \frac{n(n - 3)}{2} = \frac{10(10 - 3)}{2} \)
\( = \frac{10 \times 7}{2} = 35 \)
If there are 'm' horizontal lines and 'n' vertical lines then the number of different rectangles formed are given by \( (^mC_2 \times ^nC_2). \)
Example
In a chess board there are 9 vertical and 9 horizontal lines. Find the number of rectangles formed in the chess board.
sol
The required number of rectangles
\( = ^9C_2 \times ^9C_2 = 36 \times 36 = 1296 \)
There are 'n' points in a plane out of which 'm' points are collinear. The number of triangles formed by the points as vertices are given by \( ^nC_3 - ^mC_3 \)
Example
There are 14 points in a plane out of which 4 are collinear. Find the number of triangles formed by the points as vertices.
sol
The required number of triangles
\( = ^{14}C_3 - ^4C_3 = 364 - 4 = 360. \)
There are 'n' points in a plane out of which 'm' points are collinear. The number of straight lines formed by joining them are given by \( (^nC_2 - ^mC_2 + 1) \)
Example
There are 10 points in a plane out of which 5 are collinear. Find the number of straight lines formed by joining them.
sol
The required number of straight lines
\( \begin{align} = ^nC_2 - ^mC_2 + 1 \\\\[3pt] = ^{10}C_2 - ^5C_2 + 1 = 45 - 10 + 1 = 36 \end{align} \)
If there are 'n' points in a plane and no three points are collinear, then the number of triangles formed with 'n' points are given by \( \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2)}{6} \)
Example
Find the number of triangles that can be formed with 14 points in a plane of which no three points are collinear.
sol
The required number of triangles
\( = \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2)}{6} = \frac{14 \times 13 \times 12}{6} = 364 \)
The number of quadrilaterals that can be formed by joining the vertices of a polygon of "n" sides are given by \( \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2) (n - 3)}{24} \) , where n > 3.
Example
Find the number of quadrilaterals that can be formed by joining the vertices of a septagon.
sol
The required number of quadrilaterals
\( \begin{align} = \frac{n(n - 1) (n - 2) (n - 3)}{24} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{7(7 - 1) (7 - 2) (7 - 3)}{24} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{7 \times 6 \times 5 \times 4}{24} = 35 \end{align} \)
There are "n" points in a plane and no points are collinear, then the number of straight lines that can be drawn using these 'n' points are given by \( \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} \)
Example
How many straight lines can be drawn with 18 points on a plane of which no points are collinear?
sol
The required number of straight lines
\( = \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} = \frac{18(18 - 1)}{2} = \frac{18 \times 17}{2} = 153 \)
In a party, every person shakes hands with every other person. If there was a total of "H" handshakes in the party, then the number of persons "n" who were present in the party can be calculated from the equation: \( \frac{n(n - 2)}{2} = H \)
Example
In a party every person shakes hands with every other person. If there was a total of 105 handshakes in the party, find the number of persons who were present in the party.
sol
Let 'n' be the number of persons present in the party.
We have, the equation
\( \begin{align} \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} = H \Rightarrow \frac{n(n - 1)}{2} = 105 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n(n - 1) = 15 \times (15 - 1) \Rightarrow n = 15 \end{align} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
If \( ^np_4 = 12 \times ^np_2 \) , find the value of "n"

Solution:
Given that
\( ^np_4 = 12 \times ^np_2 \) since \( ^np_r = n(n - 1) (n - 2) ....... \) to r factors,
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow n(n - 1) (n - 2) (n - 3) (n - 4) = 12 \times n(n - 1) (n - 2) \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow (n - 3) (n - 4) = 12 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n^2 - 7n + 12 \\\\[3pt] = 12 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow n = 7 \end{align} \)

Example 2:

Question:
If \( ^{10}p_r = 720 \) , find the value of 'r'.

Solution:
Given that
\( \begin{align} ^{10}p_r = 720 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times ....... to \ r \ factor = 720 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow 10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times ....... to \ r \ factor = 10 \times 9 \times 8 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow r = 3 \end{align} \)
Hence the value of "r" is 3

Example 3:

Question:
A telegraph has 5 arms and each arm is capable of 4 distinct positions, including the position of rest. Find the total number of signals that can be made.

Solution:
Two arms may have the same position, but same arm cannot have two positions at a time.
Hence, in this problem, position is repeatable (R), but arm is non-repeatable (NR). Here, number of distinct positions = R = 4
number of arms = NR = 5
Using the formula,
Number of signals = \( [R]^{NR} \)
\( 4^5 \)
= 1024
But, in one case, when all the 5 arms will be in rest position, no signal will be made. Hence, the required number of signals
= 1024 - 1
= 1023

Example 4:

Question:
How many numbers, each lying between 100 and 1000, can be formed with digits 2, 3, 4, 0, 8, 9. (if repetition of digits not allowed)

Solution:
Here, hundredth place cannot use the digit "0" (zero), but can be filled by any of the other five digits.
So, Permutation of restricted place = \( ^5P_1 \) = 5.
Now, tenth place and unit place can be filled by remaining five (5) digit taking two at a time (repetition of digits not allowed).
So, permutation of non-restricted place (without repetition) = \( ^5P_2 \) = 5 \( \times \) 4 = 20
Now, using the product rule total number of permutations (or required number)
= permutation of restricted place \( \times \) permutation of non-restricted place.
\( 5 \times 20 = 100 \)
Hence the required number is 100.

Example 11:

Question:
Find the total number of signals that can be made by using 5 different coloured flags when any number of them may be hoisted at a time.

Solution:
When one flags is used, the number of signals made = \( 5p_1 \) = 5
When two flags are used, the number of signals made = \( 5p_2 \) = 20
When three flags are used, the number of signals made = \( 5p_3 \) = 60
When four flags are used, the number of signals made = \( 5p_4 \) = 120
When five flags are used, the number of signals made = \( 5p_5 \) = 120

Example 13:

Question:
There are 10 points in a plane, of which 4 are collinear. Find the number of

  1. triangles

  2. straight lines

formed by joining these points

Solution:
Here, order of joining of any two points (for a straight line) and of any three points (for a triangle) out of given 10 points is not important.
So, it is a case of combination.

  1. Now, three different points in a plane will make one combination (or selection) to form a triangle.
    Since no same combination is repeated, the number of combinations = \( ^nc_r . \)
    where n = 10 r = 3
    \( \Rightarrow \) the number of triangle formed = \( ^{10}c_3 \)
    Now, 4 points are collinear. So \( 4_{c_3} \) triangles formed by these 4 points will be lost.
    Hence, the required number of triangles formed
    \( = ^{10}c_3 - ^4c_3 = 120 - 4 = 116 \)

  2. Two different points in a plane will make one combination (or selection) to form a straight line. Since no same combination is repeated, the number of combinations = \( ^nc_r \)
    where n = 10 r = 2
    \( \Rightarrow \) the number of straight lines formed = \( ^{10}c_2 \)
    Now, 4 points are collinear. So \( 4_{c_2} \) straight lines formed by these 4 points will be lost and just one straight line will be obtained by 4 collinear points.
    Hence, the required number of straight lines formed
    \( = ^{10}c_2 - ^4c_2 + 1 \)
    = 45 - 6 + 1 = 40

Example 14:

Question:
There are 7 men and 5 women who are eligible for being included in a committee of 4 men and 3 women. In how many ways can the committee be formed if

  1. one women \( W_1 \) refuses to be in the committee if another particular woman \( W_2 \) be incuded?

  2. one man \( M_1 \) refuses to serve if another woman \( W_2 \) is included?

Solution:

  1. Here \( W_1 \) will not be a member if \( W_2 \) is a member and vice-versa.

    Case (7) Men (5 - 1) Women Required number of ways
    I \( W_1 \) is a member 4 3 \( ^7C_4 \times ^4C_3 \)
    II \( W_2 \) is not a member 4 3 \( ^7C_4 \times ^4C_3 \)

    Both case are mutually exclusive. So, using the Sum Rule, Total no. of ways
    \( = ^7C_4 \times ^4C_3 + ^7C_4 \times ^4C_3 = 280 \)

  2. Here \( M_1 \) will not be a member if \( W_2 \) is a member and vice-versa

    Case Men Women Required number of ways
    I \( W_1 \) is a member \( ^{7 - 1}C_4 \) \( ^{5 - 1}C_{3 - 1} \) \( ^6C_4 \times ^4C_2 = 90 \)
    II \( W_2 \) is not a member \( ^7C_4 \) \( ^{5 - 1}C_{3 - 1} \) \( ^7C_4 \times ^4C_3 = 140 \)

    Both case are mutually exclusive, so, using the Sum Rule, Required no. of ways = 90 + 140 = 230.

    \( = \frac{15 \times 14 \times 13 \times 12 \times 11 \times 10}{6 \times 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2} = 5005 \)
