Ordered Pairs

An ordered pair, written as (a, b), is a pair of elements where one of the elements is designated as first (a in this case) and the other is second (b here).
The notation of an ordered pair differs from that of simply a set of two elements because of the preference in order.
For example: {1, 2} = {2, 1} as sets, because they have same elements.
But, (1, 2) \( \neq \) (2, 1) as ordered pairs, since the order of the elements is different.
Note:
From the graph it is obvious that ordered pairs (1, 2) and (2, 1) represent two different points and hence they are not equal.

Equality of ordered pairs

Two ordered pairs (a, b) and (c, d) are equal if and only if a = c and b = d.

Cartesian Product of Sets

The Cartesian product, named after the french mathematician Rene' Descartes, forms the basis of whole number multiplication and is also useful in probability and geometry.

Definition

The Cartesion product of set A with set B, written " \( A \times B \) "and read as "A cross B", is the set of all possible ordered pairs (a, b) where \( a \epsilon \ A \) and \( b \ \epsilon \ B \) .
In set-builder notation,
\( A \times \ B = \{(a,b) | a \ \epsilon \ A \ and \ b \ \epsilon \ B \} \) .
Note:

  1. If \( A = \phi \) and/or \( B \ = \phi \) the \( A \ \times \ B \) does not contain any ordered pair,
    i.e., \( A \times B \) is empty i.e., \( A \times B \neq \ \phi \) if \( A \neq \phi \) and \( B \neq \phi \)

  2. The Cartesian product of \( B \times A \) of the sets B and A is the set
    \( B \times A = \{(b, a) | \ b \epsilon B, \ a \epsilon A \} \)
    i.e., \( B \times A \neq A \times B \) when \( A \neq B \)

  3. \( A \times B = B \times A \) only when A = B

  4. When B = A, then \( A \times B = A \times A \) is

the set of all ordered pairs of the type (a, b)
\( \begin{align} a \epsilon A, \ b \epsilon A , \ i.e., \\\\[3pt] A \times A = \{a, b) | a, b \epsilon A \} \end{align} \)
A \( \times \) A is usually written as \( A^2 \)
Example:
If A = {2, 4, 6} find \( A \times A \)
Sol:
A \( \times \) A = {2, 4, 6} \( \times \) {2, 4, 6}
= {(2, 2) (2, 4) (2, 6) (4, 2) (4, 4) (4, 6) (6, 2) (6, 4) (6, 6)}

Some Results on Cartesian Product of Sets

In this section, we intend to study some results on cartesian product of sets which are given below:

  1. For any three sets A, B and C

    1. \( A \times (B \cup C) = (A \times B) \cup (A \times C) \)

    2. \( A \times (B \cap C) = (A \times B) \cap (A \times C) \)

  2. For any three sets A, B and C
    \( A \times (B - C)=(A \times B) - (A \times C) \)

  3. If A and B are any two non-empty sets, then
    \( A \times B = B \times A \Leftrightarrow A = B \)

  4. If \( A \subseteq B \) , then \( A \times A \subseteq (A \times B) \cap (B \times A) \) .

  5. If \( A \subseteq B \) , then \( A \times C \subseteq B \times C \) for any set C.

  6. If \( A \subseteq B \) and \( C \subseteq D \) , then \( A \times C \subseteq B \times D \)

  7. For any sets A, B, C and D
    \( (A \times B) \cap (C \times D) = (A \cap C) \times(B \cap D) \)

  8. For any sets three sets A, B and C

    1. \( A \times (B' \cup C')' = (A \times B) \cap ( A \times C) \)

    2. \( A \times (B' \cap C')' = (A \times B) \cup ( A \times C) \)

    3. We have

      1. \( A \times (B' \cup C')' = A \times (B')' \cap (c') = A \times (B \cap C) = (A \times B) \cap (A \times C) \)

      2. \( \begin{align} A \times (B' \cap C')' = A \times (B')' \cup (C')' \\\\[3pt] = A \times (B \cup C) = (A \times B) \cup (A \times C) \end{align} \)

  9. Let A be a non-empty set such that \( A \times B = A \times C \) , then B = C

Cardinal number of cartesian product

The cardinal number of the cartesian product \( A \times B \) , denoted by \( n(A \times B) \) , is the product of the cardinal numbers of the set A and B, that is,
\( n(A \times B)= n(A) \times n(B) \)
Note:

  1. If A and /or B is an infinite set then \( A \times B \) is an infinite set

  2. Number of subsets of \( A \times B \) is \( 2^{n(A \times B)} \) i.e., \( 2^{n(A) \times n(B)} \)

Example:
If the universal set
\( \mu = \{u, v, w, x , y \} \)
and A = {u, v, w } then write the cartesian product \( A \times A' \) in roster form.
Also, find \( n(A \times A') \) .
Sol:
\( \begin{align} A' = \{ x,y \} \\\\[3pt] \therefore A \times A' = \{u,v,w} \times \{x,y \} \\\\[3pt] = {(u,x)(u,y)(v,x)(v,y)(w,x)(w,y)} \\\\[3pt] Now, \ n(A \times A') = n(A) \times n(A') \\\\[3pt] = 3 \times 2 = 6 \end{align} \)

Relations

Relationship between objects or numbers can be analyzed using ideas from set theory. For example on the set {1, 2, 3, 4}
we can express the relationship "a is a divisor of b" by listing all the ordered pairs (a, b) for which the relationship is true, namely
{(1, 1), (1, 2), (1, 3), (1, 4), (2, 2), (2, 4), (3, 3), (4, 4)}
Relations are used in mathematics to represent a relationships between two numbers or objects. For example, when we say, "3 is less then 7", "2 is a factor of 6" and "Triangle ABC is similar to triangle DEF," we are expressing relationship between pairs of numbers in the first two cases and triangles in the last case. More generally, the concept of relation can be applied to arbitrary sets. A set of ordered pairs can be used to show that certain pair of objects are related.
For Example:

  1. Let A and B be the two sets of persons as follows: A = {Dashrath, Ravan};
    B = {Ram, Laxman, Meghnath}.
    There is a relation 'is father of' between the elements of the sets A and B. If we write R for the relation 'is father of, then we have
    Dashrath R Ram, Dashrath R Laxman, Ravan R Meghnath.
    Omitting the letter R between the above pairs of persons and writing these pairs of persons as ordered pairs, the above information can be written as a set R of ordered pairs R where
    R = {(Dashrath, Ram), (Dashrath, Laxman), (Ravan, Meghnath)}
    \( = \{(x, y); \ x \epsilon A, \ y \ \epsilon \ B, \ x \ R \ y \} \) .
    Thus, we find that the relation 'is father of' from the set A to the set B gives rise to a subset R of A x B such that \( (x, y)\epsilon R \) iff x R y.

  2. Let N be the set of natural numbers. There is a relation 'has as its square' from the set N to N. If we write R for the relation 'has as its square', then we have 1 R 1, 2 R 4, 3 R 9, 4 R 16, ...
    This can be written as a set of ordered pairs R where
    R = {(1, 1), (2, 4), (3, 9), (4, 16), ... }
    \( = \{(x, \ y); \ x, y \ \epsilon \ N, \ y = x^2 \} \)
    Thus R is a subset of \( N \times N \) such that \( (x, \ y) \epsilon R \) iff \( y = x^2 \) .

  3. Let A = (1, 3, 5} and B = {9, 11},
    Let \( R = \{(x, y); \ x \epsilon \ A, \ y \epsilon \ B \) and x - y is an odd integer}, then R is the empty subset of \( A \times B \) . We say that R is the empty relation from A to B.
    (Note that 1 - 9 = -8, 1 - 11 = - 10, 5 - 9 = - 4, 3 - 9 = - 6, 3 - 11 = - 8, 5 - 11 = -6 are even integers.
    The above examples lead to the following definition:
    Let A, B be any two non-empty sets, then any subset of \( A \times B \) is called relation from A to B.

Note:

  1. The empty set \( \phi \) of \( A \times B \) is called the empty relation w.r.t which no element of A related to any element of B.

  2. \( A \times B \) (as a subset of itself) is called the universal relation w.r.t which every element of A is related to every element of B.

  3. If A is a non-empty set, then any subset R of \( A \times A \) is called a relation on A.

  4. Let A & B be two non-empty finiate sets consisting of m and n elements respectively. Since each subset of \( A \times B \) defines a relation from A to B, so total number of relations from A to B is \( 2^{mn} \) . Among these \( 2^{mn} \) relations the void relation \( \phi \) and the universal relation \( A \times B \) are trival relations from A to B.

Domain, Codomain and Range of a relation:

Let A, B be any sets and R be a relation from A to B, then the domain of the relation R, denoted by D, is the set of all first entries of the ordered pairs which belong to R, i.e.,
\( D = \{a : \ a \epsilon \ A, \ (a, \ b) \ \epsilon \ R \ for \ some \ b \ \epsilon \ R \} \) and the range of the relation R, denoted by E, is the set of all second entries of the ordered pairs which belong to R.
i.e., \( E = \{b : \ b \epsilon \ B, \ (a, \ b) \epsilon \ R \ for \ some \ a \ \epsilon \ A \} \) . If R is a relation from A to B, then B is called co-domain of R.

Reflexive Property

A relation R on a set A is said to be reflexive if \( (a, \ a) \epsilon R \) for all \( a \epsilon A \) . We say that R is reflexive if every element in A is related to itself.
For example, the relation "is a factor of on the set A = {2, 4, 6, 8} is reflexive, since every number is A is a factor of itself. In general, in an arrow diagram, a relation is reflexive if every element in A has an arrow printing to itself.

Let A be a non-empty set. A relation R on A is said to be reflexive iff a R a for every \( a \epsilon A \) , i.e., (a, a) \( \epsilon \) R for all a \( \epsilon \) A.

Transitive Property

A relation Ron a set A is transitive if whenever (a, b) \( \epsilon \) R and (b, c) \( \epsilon \) R, then (a, c) \( \epsilon \) R. In words, a relation is transitive if for all a, b, c in A, if a is related to c. Consider the relation "is a factor of " on the set {2, 4, 6, 8, 12}. Notice that 2 is a factor of 4 and 4 is a factor of a 8 and 2 is a factor of 8. Also, 2 is a factor of 4 and 4 is a factor of 12 and 2 is a factor of 12. The last case to consider involving 2, 6 and 12 is also true. Thus, "is a factor of" is a transitive relation on the set {2, 4, 6, 8, 12}. In an arrow diagram, a relation is transitive if whenever there is an "a to b" arrow and a "b to c" arrow, there is also an "a to c" arrow.

Transitive

Let A be a non-empty set. A relation R on A is said to be transitive iff a R b, b R c implies a R c for a, b, c \( \epsilon \) R and (b, c) \( \epsilon \) R \Rightarrow (a, c) \( \epsilon \) R.

Example 1:

Determine whether each of the following relations are reflexive, symmetric and transitive.

  1. Relation R in the set N of natural numbers defined as R = {(x, y): y = x + 5 and x < 4}

  2. Relation R in the set A = { 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} as R = {(x, y): y is divisible by x}

  3. Relation R in the set Z of all integers defined as R = {(x, y): x - y is an integer}

Solution:

  1. Here, A = N, the set of natural numbers and
    R = {(x,y): y = x + 5, x < 4 }
    = {(x, x + 5 ) : x \( \epsilon \) N and x < 4}
    = {(1, 6), (2, 7), (3, 8)}
    R is not reflexive as (x, x) \( \not{\epsilon} \) R \( \forall \ x \epsilon \ A \) .
    R is not symmetric as (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R \( \not{\Rightarrow} \) (y, x) \( \epsilon \) R ,
    for example; (1, 6) \( \epsilon \) R but (6, 1) \( \not{\epsilon} \) R .
    R is transitive in this case as transitivity is not contradicted here .

  2. A = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 } and
    R = {(x, y): y is divisible by x }
    Here, R is reflexive as (x, x) \( \epsilon \) R. \( \forall \) x \( \epsilon \) A.
    (since x divides x for every x \( \epsilon \) A)
    R is not symmetric as (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R \( \not{\Rightarrow} \) (y, x) \( \epsilon \) R
    for example,(2, 6) \( \epsilon \) R but (6, 2) \( \not{\epsilon} \) R
    Also (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R and (y, z) \( \epsilon \) R
    \Rightarrow y is divisible by x and z is divisible by y
    \Rightarrow z is divisible by x
    \Rightarrow (x, z) \( \epsilon \) R
    \( \therefore \) R is transitive.

  3. A = Z, the set of integers and R = {(x, y): x - y is an integer}
    R is reflexive as (x, x) \( \epsilon \) R \( \forall \) x \( \epsilon \) A
    (since x - x = 0 is an integer \( \forall \) x \( \epsilon \) A)
    R is symmetric as (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R \Rightarrow (y, x) \( \epsilon \) R
    (since when even x - y is an integer, y - x is also an integer)
    R is transitive as (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R and (y, z) \( \epsilon \) R \Rightarrow (x, z) \( \epsilon \) R
    (since when x - y is an integer and also y - z is an integer, then (x - y) + (y - z) = x - z is also an integer)

Example 5:

Show that the relation R in the set A of all the books in a library of a college, given by R = {(x, y): x and y have same number of pages} , is an equivalence relation.

Solution:

\( \therefore \) R is reflexive.
Also (x, y) \( \epsilon R \)
\Rightarrow x and y have the same number of pages
\Rightarrow y and x have the same number of pages
\Rightarrow (y, x) \( \epsilon \) R
\( \therefore \) R is symmetric.
Next, (x, y) \( \epsilon \) R and (y, z) \( \epsilon \) R.
\Rightarrow x and y have the same number of pages and also y and z have the same number of pages.
\Rightarrow x and z have the same number of pages
\Rightarrow (x, z) \( \epsilon \) R
\( \therefore \) R is transitive.
