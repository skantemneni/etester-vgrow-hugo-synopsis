Introduction

Earth is surrounded by a blanket of air which is a mixture of gases. The study of gaseous state is also very interesting in the sense that the physical properties of all gases is very nearly the same. Unlike solids and liquids, all gases behave in much the same way with changing conditions, such as pressure and temperature.
Of the three states of matter, the gaseous state is the simplest and shows greatest uniformity in behaviour. The behaviour of gases can be described by certain quantitative relationships between mass, pressure, volume and temperature. These relationships are called gas laws.

The Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

  1. Gases are made up of tiny particles (molecules) moving in all possible directions at all possible speeds.

  2. The molecules are negligibly small in size compared to the volume occupied by the gas.

  3. There is negligible force of attraction between gas molecules.

  4. The moving molecules of a gas collide with each other and also with the walls of the container. Due to these collisions, gas molecules exert pressure.

  5. There are large intermolecular spaces between gas molecules, and this accounts for the high compressibility of gases. Gases increase in volume on decreasing pressure and increasing temperature.

  6. The mass of a gas per unit volume is very small due to the large intermolecular spaces between their molecules. Therefore gases have low density.

  7. The intermolecular spaces in a gas are very large. When two gases are brought in contact with each other, their molecules just move into one another, thus forming a homogeneous gaseous mixture.

  8. The average kinetic energy of a molecule is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

Measurable Proerties of Gases

The physical behaviour of gases can be described by four variables : Volume (V), pressure (P), temperature (T), mass (m) known as standard variables.

Volume

The volume (V) of a gas is the space occupied by that gas. The space occupied by a gas is equal to the volume of its container.
Units of volume
Cubic centimeter ( \( cm^3 \) ), millilitre (ml), cubic decimetre ( \( dm^3 \) ), litre (L)
\( 1m^3 = 10^3 dm^3 = 10^3 litres = 10^6 cm^3 = 10^6 ml \)

Pressure

The pressure (P) of a gas is the force that the gas exerts per unit area on the walls of its container.
Units of Pressure
Atmosphere (atm), pascal (Pa)
Height of mercury column:

  1. in centimeter (cm Hg)

  2. in millimeter (mm Hg)

  3. in torr (named after Torricelli)

Relationship between these units
1 atm = 76.0 cm of Hg = 760 mm of Hg = 760 torr
1 atm = 101.325 kPa

Temperature

The temperature (T) of a gas is defined as the degree of hotness or coldness of a body
Units of temperature

  1. Degree celsius ( \( ^{\circ}C \) ) or degree fahrenheit ( \( ^{\circ}F \) ).

  2. kelvin (K) or absolute temperature (T).

Relationship between these units
\( kelvin (K) = ^{\circ}C + 273, \ \frac{F - 32}{180} = \frac{^{\circ}}{100} = \frac{K - 273}{100} \)

Mass

The amount of matter contained
Unit of Mass
kilogram, gram
\( 1 kg = 10^3 g \)
Usually mass ins expressed in the terms of number of moles(n)
\( n = \frac{m(in \ g)}{M(Molar \ Mass)} \)

Gas Laws

The behaviour of a gas under known conditions of pressure, volume and temperature is described by laws known as gas laws.

Boyle's Law

The volume of a given mass of a dry gas is inversely proportional to its pressure at constant temperature.

Mathematical expression of Boyle's Law

according to Boyle's law
V = 1/P where V = volume and P = pressure
or V = K/P or PV = K= constant.
Where K is the constant of proportionality.
Suppose a gas occupies a volume \( V_1 \) when its pressure is \( P_1 \) : then
\( V_1 \propto 1/P_1 \ or \ V_1 = K/P_1 \) or
\( P_1V_1 \) = K = constant.

Note

Boyle's law is applicable only when temperature and mass of the gas are kept constant.

The graph drawn at constant temperature are called Isotherms.

Charle's law

Jacques Charle's worked on the relation between the other two terms, temperature and volume, keeping pressure and amount of gas as constants.

Charle's observation

"At constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of a gas increases (or decreases) by 1/273 of its volume at \( 0^{\circ}C \) for every \( 1^{\circ}C \) rise (or fall) of temperature".

Mathematical expression

Let \( V_{\circ} \) be volume of a gas at \( 0^{\circ}C \) and \( V_t \) be the volume of a gas at \( t^{\circ}C \) at constant pressure
\( \therefore V_t = V_{\circ}+\frac{V_{\circ}}{273}t \)
\( \Rightarrow V_t = V_{\circ}\left(1+\frac{t}{273} \right) \)

Statement

According to Charles' law, the volume (V) of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to absolute temperature (T) at constant pressure.
\( \frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{T_1}{T_2} = \frac{t_1 + 273}{t_2 + 273} \)

Note

Charle's law is applicable only if mass and pressure of the gas are kept constant.

Graph between V and T

Graph V and T at constant pressure is a straight passing through origin.

The graphs drawn at constant pressure are known as isobars.
("lso" means same and "bar" means pressure).

Note

The graph between two terms 'a' and 'b' and if \( a \propto b \) , is always a straight line passing through origin.

Volume occupied by any gas at \( -273 ^{\circ}C \)
If \( t=-273^{\circ}C \) , the volume at \( - 273^{\circ}C \) is given by
\( V_t=V_0\left(1+\frac{t}{273}\right) \Rightarrow V_{-273} = V_0\left(1+\frac{(-273)}{273} \right) \)
\( \Rightarrow V_{-273}=V_0(1-1) \Rightarrow V_{-273} = ZERO \)
This temperature is called absolute zero.

Absolute zero

The hypothetical temperature of \( -273^{\circ}C \) at which a gas will have zero volume is known as absolute zero.
Important observations at absolute zero :

  1. The molecular motion of gases ceases to zero.

  2. All the measurable properties of a gas become zero at this temperature and hence, it is called absolute zero.

Gaylussac's law

Gay Lussac worked on the relationship between pressure and temperature by keeping e volume and mass of a gas as constants.
He observed that for every \( 1^{\circ}C \) rise or fall in temperature, the pressure of the gas increases or decreases respectively by \( {\frac{1}{273}}^{th} \) of its pressure at \( 0^{\circ}C \) .
"At constant volume, the pressure(P) of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (T)".
Mathematically, \( P \propto T \) .

\( \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2} or \frac{P_1}{P_2} = \frac{T_1}{T_2} \Rightarrow \frac{P_1}{P_2} = \frac{t_1 + 273}{t_2 + 273} \)

Where \( P_1 \) and \( P_2 \) are the pressures of the gas at \( {t_1}^{\circ}C \) and \( {t_2}^{\circ}C \) respectively.
Graph between P and T : Graph between pressure (P) and absolute temperature (T) is a straight line passing through the origin.

The graphs drawn at constant volume are called isochors.

Avogadro's law

"At constant temperature and pressure the volume occupied by a gas is directly proportional to the mass or number of moles or number of molecules of the gas".
Mathematically, \( V \propto m \) or n, where V is volume of the gas, m and 'n' are the mass andnumber of moles of the gas respectively.
From Avogadro's law, it is clear that equal volumes of all gases under similar conditions of temperature and pressure contain an equal number of moles.
\( \Rightarrow V \propto n \) -(1)
But we also know that number of moles
\( n = \frac{mass(m)}{Molecular Weight(W)} \) -(2)
Substituting (2) in (1) we have \( V \propto \frac{m}{W} \)
we have Ideal Gas Equation :
It helps us to find the variation in volume, when all the terms P, T and n vary simultaneously.
\( V \propto \frac{1}{P} \)
\( V \propto T \)
\( V \propto n \)
By combining the equations 1, 2 and 3, we get \( V \propto \frac{nT}{P} \)
\( \Rightarrow V = \frac{RnT}{P} \Rightarrow PV = nRT \)
Where 'R' = Universal gas constant
\( R \neq f \) (nature of gas, temperature, amount of gas and pressure of gas)

System Value
SI Value \( 8.314 joules mol^{ -1} K^{ -1} \)
CGS value \( 8.314 \times 10^7 erg mol^{ -1} K^{ -1} \)
Practical Value \( 0.0821 litre atm mol^{ -1} K^{ -1} \)
Other value \( R = 2 cal mol^{ -1} K^{ -1} \)

Graham's law of diffusion

Diffusion : The spontaneous mixing of gases is known as diffusion.
Effusion : Escaping of gas through an aperture.
Condition for diffusion / effusion : The gases should be non - reactive.
Example : CO & \( Cl_2 \) , NO & \( O_2 \) and \( H_2 \) & \( Cl_2 \) .
Rate of diffusion or effusion (r) : Volume of gas escaping per second.
\( \Rightarrow r = \frac{v}{t} \Rightarrow \frac{r_1}{r_2} =\frac{V_1/t}{V_2/t} \) - (1)
At constant temperature and pressure the rate of diffusion and effusion of the gases is inversely proportional to the square roots of their densities.
\( r \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \Rightarrow \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{d_2}{d_1}} \) - (2)
Where \( r_1 \) , \( r_2 \) are the rates of diffusion or (the rate of) effusion and \( d_1 \) , \( d_2 \) are the rates of diffusion or (the rate of) effusion and d1, d2 are the densities of two different gases. The density of a gas is directly proportional to its molecular weight. So the rate of diffusion of a gas becomes inversely proportional to the square roots of the molecular weight of the gas
\( \Rightarrow d \propto M \)
\( \Rightarrow \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{d_1}{d_2}} = \sqrt{\frac{M_1}{M_2}} \Rightarrow \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_1}{M_2}} \) -(2)
Combining (1) , (2) and (3) we can summarize the rate of diffusion as follows :
\( \Rightarrow \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \frac{V_1/t_1}{V_2/t_1} = \sqrt{\frac{d_1}{d_2}} = \sqrt{\frac{M_1}{M_2}} \) -(4)
This is the final equation for Graham's law of diffusion.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
A 8 litre container has gas at 14 atm. Pressure is connected to another empty vessel of volume 20 litres. Find the pressure exerted by the gas in the new system a const of temperature.

Solution:
Before connection
\( V_1=8 \ lts \)
\( P_1=14 atm \)
After connection
\( V_2= 8+20=28 lts \)
\( P_2=? \)
Temperature is constant \( \Rightarrow \) Boyle's law is applicable.
\( \Rightarrow P_1V_1=P_2V_2 \Rightarrow P_2 =\frac{P_1V_1}{V_2}=\frac{14 \times 8}{28}= 4 atm \)

Example 2:

Question:
A steel cylinder of 40 litres capacity contains a gas under 100 atmospheres pressure. Find how many cylinders of \( 400 cm^3 \) capacity could be filled from it at 76 cm of Hg, [Temperature is constant].

Solution:
Let the no. of cylinders be 'n'.
Initial volume ( \( V_1 \) ) = 40 lts.
Initial pressure ( \( P_1 \) ) = 100 atm
Final volume ( \( V_2 \) ) = \( 400 cm^3 \) = 0.4 litres.
Final pressure ( \( P_2 \) ) 76 cm = 1 atm
Temperature constant \( \Rightarrow \) Boyle's law is applicable.
\( P_1V_1 = nP_2 V_2 \)
\( n= \frac{P_1V_1}{P_2V_2}=\frac{100 \times 40}{1 \times 0.4}=10,000 \)
Hence 10,000 cylinders are filled.

Example 3:

Question:
There is a gas in a sphere of radius '2r' at a pressure 'P'. It is connected to another sphere 'r' by means of a tube whose volume is negligible. Find the final pressure exerted by the gas after connection.

Solution:
Case I: Before connection
\( P_1=P \)
\( V_1 =\frac{4}{3}\pi(2r)^3=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \times 8 \)
Case II:
After connection
\( P_2=? \)
\( V_3=\frac{4}{3}\pi 9r^3 \)
\( \left[ \therefore \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 + \frac{4}{3}\pi(2r)^3 = \frac{4}{3} \pi 9 r^3 = \frac{4}{3} \pi 9 r^3 \right ] \)
\( \Rightarrow \) Boyle's law is applicable.
\( P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 \)
\( \Rightarrow P \times \frac{4}{3}\pi 8r^3 = P_2 \times \frac{4}{3} \pi 9r^3 \)
\( \Rightarrow P_2 = \frac{P \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \times 8}{\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \times 9} \)
\( \therefore P_2 = \frac{8P}{9} \)
