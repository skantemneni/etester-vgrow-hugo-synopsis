Introduction

Nitrogen is one of the important component of air. It is also equally important to life next to oxygen. This is because our body proteins are made out of it.
Symbol : N
Atomic mass : 14
Atomic No : 7
Electronic configuration : 2, 5 (K, L)
\( 7^n \rightarrow 1s^2 2s^2 2p^3 \)

Occurrence

Nitrogen occurs both in free as well as in combined state. In free state, it occurs in air. It is 78% by volume of air. In combined state, it occurs in the following forms :

  1. In solid state nitrogen occurs as mineral nitrates.

    1. Potassium nitrate or nitre \( (KNO_3) \)

    2. Sodium nitrate or chile salt petre \( (NaNO_3) \)

    In addition to above compounds, it is an integral part of proteins which may be of plant or animal origin.

  2. In gaseous state, nitrogen is present in the form of free ammonia, in air at such places, where vegetable or animal matter decays. During thunder and lightning, oxides of nitrogen are produced. Exhaust of automobiles, also gives off oxides of nitrogen.

Laboratory Method of Preparation of Nitrogen

From ammonium nitrite \( (NH_4NO_2) \)
Principle : Ammonium nitrite is obtained by heating a mixture of ammonium chloride \( (NH_4Cl) \) and sodium nitrite \( (NaNO_2) \)

  1. \( NH_4 Cl + NaNO_2 \rightarrow NH_4 NO_2 + NaCl \)

  2. \( NH_4 NO_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2H_2O + N_2 (g) \)

Note:

  1. Nitrogen cannot be collected by displacement of air but collected by down ward displacement of water.

  2. Nitrogen obtained from nitrogenous compounds is called chemical nitrogen.

Preparation of Nitrogen from Nitrogen Compounds

  1. From ammonium dichromate and ammonium nitrate:
    Thermal decomposition of nitrogen compounds like ammonium dichromate
    \( [(NH_4)_2 Cr_2 O_7] \) and ammonium nitrite \( (NH_4 NO_2) \) give nitrogen.
    When orange - coloured ammonium dichromate \( [(NH_4)_2 Cr_2 O_7] \) is heated nitrogen and steam escapes leaving behind green chromic oxide \( (Cr_2 O_3) \)
    \( NH_4 NO_2 \xrightarrow{ \Delta} 2H_2O + N_2(g) \)

  2. From ammonium :
    When ammonia gas is passed over the heated oxides of less active metals, such as copper oxide, lead oxide, etc., it reduces them to metals with the liberation of nitrogen gas.
    \( \begin{align} 3CuO (s) + 2NH_3 (g) \xrightarrow{ \Delta} 3Cu(s) + 3 H_2O (l) + N_2 (g) \\\\[3pt] 3PbO (s) + 2NH_3 (g) \xrightarrow{ \Delta} 3Pb(s) + 3 H_2O (l) + N_2 (g) \end{align} \)

Industrial Method

From air

Composition of air :

Nitrogen (78%), Oxygen (21%), CO, (0.03%), Inert gases, Dust particles and Water vapour.

Principle

Nitrogen can be obtained by removal of oxygen, carbon dioxide and dust particles from air.

Removal of different components from air

  1. Dust particles : By passing air through electric precipitator.

  2. Water vapour : By passing through conc. H_2SO_4

  3. \( CO_2 : \) By passing air through concentrated KOH solution \( CO_2 \) is absorbed.
    \( KOH + CO_2 \rightarrow KHCO_3 \)

  4. Oxygen : By passing air over red hot copper.
    Oxygen reacts with heated copper forming black cupric oxide (CuO), leaving behind nitrogen and inert gases.
    \( 2Cu + O_2 \rightarrow 2CuO \)
    By downward displacement of water.
    When a gas under pressure is allowed to expand suddenly through a small orifice to a region of low pressure, its temperature falls.
    This is called Joule Thompson effect.The liquefied air is then subjected to fractional evaporation.

Liquefaction of air by compression and expansion

  1. Purified air is first compressed and then allowed to expand suddenly through a small hole into a region of low pressure.
    This leads to the cooling of air.
    By repeating this process, the temperature of air is lowered sufficiently and it gets liquefied.

  2. Fractional evaporation of liquid air
    The major components of liquid air and their boiling points are as follows.
    \( \begin{align} \begin{array}{ccc} Component & & Boiling \ point \\\\ Liquid \ N_2 & & - 196^{ \circ} C \\\\ Liquid \ \ oxygen & & - 183^{ \circ} C \end{array} \end{align} \)
    Liquid \( N_2 \) has least boiling point on warming liquid \( N_2 \) turns into gaseous nitrogen, leaving behind liquid oxygen.

  3. Collection of \( N_2 \)
    Nitrogen is collected and stored in steel cylinders under pressure as liquid \( N_2 \) .

Physical Properties

  1. Nitrogen is a colourless, odourless and tasteless gas.

  2. Nitrogen has a boiling point of \( - 196^{ \circ} C \) and a melting point of \( - 210^{ \circ} C \) .

  3. Nitrogen is sparingly soluble in water. At ordinary temperatures, 23 ml of nitrogen dissolves in 1 L of water

  4. Nitrogen gas is slightly less dense than air. The density of pure nitrogen gas is \( 1.2505 g cm^{-3} \) , that of atmospheric nitrogen is \( 1.2567 g cm^{-3} \) and that of air is \( 1.2928 g cm^{-3} \)

Chemical Properties

Combustion

Nitrogen is neither combustible nor a supporter of combustion.

Action with litmus

Nitrogen is neutral towards litmus solution, i.e., it neither turns blue litmus red nor red litmus blue.
Thus, it is neither acidic nor basic in nature.

Reaction with litmus

At room temperature, nitrogen reacts only with lithium to form lithium nitride, \( Li_3 N \) .
\( 6 Li + N_2 \rightarrow 2 Li_3 N \)

Reaction with Calcium and magnesium

\( N_2 \) reacts with red-hot Mg, Ca, etc., to form nitrides.
\( \begin{align} 3 Mg + N_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Mg_3 N_2 \\\\[3pt] 3 Ca + N_2 \xrightarrow{\Delta} Ca_3 N_2 \end{align} \)

Reaction with hydrogen

Nitrogen combines with hydrogen to form ammonia \( (NH_3) \) . The reaction is exothermic and reversible.
To get the optimum yield of ammonia, the reaction is carried out under high pressure (200 to 800 atm) and at a temperature of around \( 500^{ \circ} C \) .
At lower temperature, the yield is higher but the rate of reaction is low. Even at \( 500^{ \circ} C \) , a catalyst (iron) is used to speed up the reaction.
\( N_2 + 3H_2 \xrightarrow[500^{ \circ }C, 200 atm]{Fe, catlayst} 2NH_3 \)

Reaction with Oxygen

At \( 3000^{ \circ} C, & N_2 \) combines with \( O_2 \) to form nitric oxide (NO). This is an endothermic reaction
\( N_2 + O_2 \xrightarrow{3000^{ \circ} C} 2NO \)

Reaction with Calcium carbide

At \( 1000^{ \circ} C \) nitrogen reacts with calcium carbide \( (CaC_2) \) to form calcium cyanamide \( (CaCN_2) \)
\( CaC_2 + N_2 \xrightarrow{3000^{ \circ} C} \underset{ \overline{nitroliumum}}{CaCN_2 + C} \)
Note : The mixture of calcium cyanamide and carbon is known as nitrolium and is used as a fertilizer.

Uses

  1. Nitrogen is a relatively inertgas, it is used to protect materials, which are easily oxidised or combustible, from the action of atmospheric oxygen.
    It is also used in food packaging to prevent the growth of microbes.

  2. Nitrogen is used as a refrigerant, eg. In hospitals to store blood etc.,

  3. Nitrogen is mainly used in the production of nitroliumm, ammonia, nitric acid and in the fertilizer industry.

  4. Liquid nitrogen is used for the recovery of steel, copper and aluminium from old cars, refrigerators and other steel or iron items left as scrap.

Tests of Nitrogen

Nitrogen is colourless, odourless and tasteless gas which is neutral towards litmus.
Thus, its unreactive nature makes it very difficult to identify by simple chemical tests.
However, nitrogen can be at the best guessed by its negative response to all the chemical tests for the other gases.

Nitrogen Cycle

The transfer of nitrogen from atmosphere to soil, and then to plants and animals, and then to soil again and finally back to atmosphere is known as the nitrogen cycle.

Step I:

During fixation, nitrogen from air is converted to nitrogen in soil in the form of soluble nitrates. Natural fixation occurs in two ways. During lightening, nitrogen is converted to nitric oxide, which is further oxidized to \( NO_2 \) by oxygen in air. \( NO_2 \) dissolves in rain water to form nitric acid, which reacts with minerals in soil to form soluble nitrates.

Plants

Plants absorb nitrogenous compounds and metabolise them in cells to amino acids which are finally converted to proteins.

Animals

The plant protein enter into herbivorous animals,where they break down into amino acids and are finally converted to animal proteins. The protein from herbivorous animals enter into carnivorous animals.

Step III:

The death and decay of plants and animals converts the proteins to amino acids. Amino acids are converted to nitrates by bacteria in the following sequence.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Determine the oxidation number of nitrogen in

  1. \( N_2O \)

  2. \( NO_2 \)

  3. \( HNO_3 \)

  4. \( NH_3 \)

Solution:

  1. Let oxidation number of nitrogen in \( N_2O \) be x.
    Then 2x - 2 = 0
    2x = 2
    x = + 1
    \( \therefore \) Oxidation number of nitrogen in \( N_2O \) is + 1

  2. Let oxidation number of nitrogen in \( NO_2 \) be x.
    then x + 2(-2) = 0
    x = 4
    \( \therefore \) Oxidation number of nitrogen in \( NO_2 \) is + 4

  3. Let oxidation number of nitrogen in \( HNO_3 \) be x.
    then + 1 + x + 3(-2) = 0
    x = 5
    \( \therefore \) Oxidation number of nitrogen in \( HNO_3 \) is + 5

  4. Let oxidation number of nitrogen in \( NH_3 \) be x.
    then x + 3(+1) = 0
    x = -3
    \( \therefore \) Oxidation number of nitrogen in \( NH_3 \) is -3

Example 4:

Why is \( N_2 \) less reactive at room temperature?

Solution:
In molecular nitrogen, there is a triple bond between two nitrogen atoms \( (N \equiv N) \) and it is nonpolar in character. Due to the presence of a triple bond, it has very high bond dissociation energy \( (941.1 kJ mo1^{-1} ) \) and therefore, it does not react with other elements under normal conditions and is very unreactive. However, it may react at higher temperatures.

Example 5:

Why is it very difficult to identify nitrogen by chemical test?

Solution:
Nitrogen is a colourless, odourless and neutral gas. Secondly, it is unreactive and inert gas at low temperatures. Hence it cannot be identified by direct tests rather than indirect tests are used for its identification.

Example 6:

Why is nitrogen gas filled in sealed food packets?

Solution:
As nitrogen is chemically inert gas it is used in sealed food packets to retard the bacterial growth.
