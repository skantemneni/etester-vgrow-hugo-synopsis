Common misconception Fact
1 Liquid solutions are formed by homogeneous mixture of two or more liquids. No, liquid solutions can be formed eithe by mixing of solids or gases
2 Molarity and molality are same. In molarity, the volume of the solution is considered. In case of molality, mass of the solution is considered.

Introduction

The air we breathe in, the water and milk we drink are not pure substances but these are called as mixtures. A mixture is formed when two or more chemically non reacting substances are mixed. A mixture may be homogeneous or heterogeneous. A heterogeneous mixture has distinctive phases. The observed properties are just the sum of the properties of their individual phases. A homogeneous mixture consists of a single phase which has properties that may differ drastically from those of their individual components.
Homogeneous mixtures are termed as solutions.

Solutions

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances, whose comPosition can be varied within certain limits depending on the total components present in its solution. They are classified as binary sloution (two components), ternary solution (three components), quaternary solution (four components). The components of a binary solution are termed as solute and the solvent. Solute is a component which is present in relatively in excess.

Types of Solution

All the three states of matter mainly solids, liquids and gases may be have either as a solvent or as a solute. Depending on the solute or solvent mainely there may be the following types of binary solution.

Factors of Affecting solubility

Effect of nature of solute and solvent

"Like dissolves like" this means:"

  1. compounds and ionic compounds are soluble in polar solvents.
    Eg. Ionic compounds like common salt (NaCI) dissolve in polar solvents like water.

  2. Non-polar compounds dissolve in non-polar solvents.
    Eg: Covalent compounds like methane \( (CH_4) \) dissolve in non-polar solvents like carbon tetrachloride \( (CCl_4) \) . But, non-polar solutes do not dissolve in polar solvents and polar solutes do not dissolve in non-polar solvents, Hence, no solubility.

As both oil and gasoline are non-polar in nature, they are miscible in each other. As water being polar, oil is immiscible in water.

Classification of solution

Solutions are classified depending upon the amount of solute present in the solution.

  1. When we add little salt to water and stir it, the salt dissolves forming a solution. If some more salt is added, we observe that it gets dissolved. Such a solution which can dissolve some more solute is called an unsaturated solution .

  2. If we go on adding salt to the unsaturated solution, a stage comes where the solution cannot dissolve any more salt. Such solution which cannot dissolve any more solute is called a saturated solution .
    The excess salt in a saturated solution remains undissolved at the bottom of the beaker. This is one of the identifications of a saturated solution.

  3. Increase the temperature of the saturated solution. Some more salt gets dissolved. Let us cool the solution slowly to the original temperature. It is observed that the excess Solute which was dissolved in the solution at an elevated temperature does not separate out and stay in the slowly cooled solution. Such a solution which has excess of solute is called super-saturated solution . However, if a super-saturated solution is disturbed or if a little solute is added, the excess dissolved solute precipitates.

Effect of temperature on saturated solutions

A saturated solution on change of temperature can be changed

On increase of temperature

The solubility increases on increase of temperature and the solution can take some more solute. Such a solution which can dissolve some more solute is an unsaturated solution.

On decrease of temperature

The solubility decreases on decrease of temperature and the resulting solution has extra solute than required for saturation at lower temperature. Such a solution which has extra solute in it is called super-saturated solution.
Thus on decrease of temperature of a saturated solution, it is converted to super-saturated solution. On increase of temperature of a saturated solution, it is converted to unsaturated solution.

Solubility

Solubility of a substance expresses the maximum amount of it which can be dissolved in a specific amount of solvent.

Factors affecting solubility

Nature of Solute or Solvent

When a polar solute is added to a polar solvent, it dissolves and forms a solution an:, there exists solubility. When a non-polar solute is added to a non-polar solvent, it dissolves and forms a solution and there exists solubility.
Note
It also depends on the size of the solute particles and the agigation of mixture.

Effect of temperature on solubility

  1. For exothermic solutions (if heat is realeased in the formation of solution), solubility decreases with increase of temperature.

  2. For endothermic solutions (if heat is absorbed in the formation of solutions), solubility increases with increase of temperature.

Utility of Solubility curves:

  1. The solubility of a given salt at any temperature can be read directly from its solubility curve.

  2. Substances whose solubility decreases with rise in temperature are, NaOH, \( Na_2CO_3, \ Na_2SO_4 \) etc.,

  3. Substances whose solubility fairly increases or no change with rise in temperature are, NaCl, \( LiSO_4 \) . Solubility of solids is independent of change in pressure.

  4. Substances whose solubility increases with rise in temperature are, \( NaNO_3, \ KNO_3, \ KCl, \ NH_4 Cl \) etc.,

  5. The nature of curve gives an idea about the effect of temperature on the change of solubility. A steep curve indicates a rapid change, while a flat one indicates rather a slow change in solubility with rise of temperature.

Concentration

The amount of solute present in a given amountof solution is known as concentration. It can be described quantitatively or qualitattively. Qualitatively it can be described as whether a solution is dilute (relatively small quantity more quantity of solute is present) or concentrated (relatively more quantity of solute is present). To express solution quantitatively different methods need to be used.

Methods of Expressing Concentration:

There are different methods to express the concentration, quantitavely. Weight percentage, volume percentage, weight-volume percentage, molarely, normality, molality, mole fraction.

Weight of percentage:

Weight percentage is defined as the amount of solute in grams present in 100 grams of solution. In older text and references, this is sometimes referred to as weight - weight percentage or mass percentage and abbreviated as 'w/w'
\( Weight \ percentage = \frac{(\text{Weight of solute}) g}{(\text{Weight of solution}) g} \times 100 \)
In a solution, if \( w_1 \) and \( w_2 \) are the weights of solute and solvent in grams respectively,
then \( w/w = \frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2} = 100 \)

Volume percentage:

Volume percentage is defined as the volume of solute (in ml) present in 100 ml of solution.
This method of concentration is generally used for the solutions made by mixing liquids and is abbreviated as v/v.
\( Volume \ percentage = \frac{(\text{Weight of solute}) ml}{(\text{Weight of solution}) ml} \times 100 \)
In a solution, if v is the volume of solute in ml and V is the volume of solution in nil, then, \( \frac{v}{v} = \frac{v}{V} \times 100 \)
Hence, volume percentage decreases with the increase in temperature.

Weight - Volume percentage:

Weight - volume percentage is defined as the amount of solute (in grams) present in 100 ml of solution. This method of concentration is generally used for the solutions made from solid reagents and is abbreviated as ' \( \frac{w}{v}' \)
\( Weight - Volume \ percentage = \frac{(\text{Weight of solute}) g}{(\text{Weight of solution}) g} \times 100 \)
In a solution, if w is the weight of solute in grams and v is the volume of solution in millilitre, then, \( \frac{w}{v} = \frac{w}{v} \times 100 \) .
For example: 5% \( \frac{w}{v} \) NaC1 solution means that 5 g of NaCl is present in 100 ml solution.

Effect of Temperature:

We know that, weight-volume % = \( \frac{w}{v} \times 100 \) . Hence, weight - volume percentage decreases with increase in temperature.

Molarity(M)

Molarity is defined as the number of moles of solute present in 1 litre of solution. It is denoted by M. It is the most common and universally accepted method of expressing concentration of a solution in analytical chemistry.
\( \begin{align} M = \frac{\text{No.of moles of solute (n)}}{\text{Volume of solution in litres (V)}} \\\\[3pt] M = \frac{\text{Weight of the substance (W)}}{\text{Gram molecular weight (GMW)}} \\\\[3pt] M = \frac{w}{GMW} \times \frac{1}{V} \ or \ M = \frac{w}{GMW} \times \frac{1000 (ml)}{V} \end{align} \)
M = No.of milli moles / V (ml)

Units

The units of molarity are moles / litre or millimoles/ milliliter or molar.

Advantage

It is advantageous to express the concentration in terms of molarity because of the use of moles. This enables the measurement of absolute number of particles present in a solution irrespective of their weight and volume.

Molar Solution:

A solution which contains one gram molecular weight or one mole of solute per litre of the solution is known as molar solution.
Eg: NaOH contains 1 mole (40 g) of NaOH in its 1 litre solution.

Effect of Temperature:

We know, \( M = \frac{n}{V} \) . Hence molarity decreases with increase of temperature.

Molality

It is the number of moles of solute dissolved per kg of solvent. It is denoted by m. e nu Mathematically.
\( \begin{align} molality(m) = \frac{\text{Moles of solute}}{\text{Weight of solvent in kg}} \\\\[3pt] or \ = \frac{\text{Moles of solute}}{\text{Mass of solvent in gram}} \times 100 \end{align} \)
Thus, the units of molaiity are moles per kilogram i.e., \( mol \ kg^{-1} \) . It is represented by the symbol m.
If as moles of solute are dissolved in W grams of solvent, then Molality = \( \frac{n_B}{W} \times 100 \) .

Mole fraction

The ratio between number of moles of a component to the total number in the solution is called mole fraction.
\( \begin{align} Mole \ fraction = \frac{\text{Number of moles of the component}}{\text{Total number of moles of all components}} \\\\[3pt] \text{Mole fraction of solute} \ (X_A) = \frac{n_A}{(n_A + n_B)} \\\\[3pt] \text{Mole fraction of solute} \ (X_B) = \frac{n_B}{(n_A + n_B)} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ X_A + X_B = 1 \end{align} \)
The sum of mole fraction of all components in solution is equal to one.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Why are the world's major ocean fisheries located in cold regions?
Solution:
The main source of living for aquatic animals like fishes is the dissolved oxygen present in water. The survival becomes tough if the dissolved oxygen decreases. We know that solubility of a gas in a liquid decreases with increase of temperature or less is the temperature more is the solubility of gas (oxygen) in a liquid (water). Therefore, fishes cannot survive in warm water due to the availability of less amount of dissolved oxygen. The reverse is the reason for the location of major ocean fisheries in cold regions.

Example 2:

16 grams of NaOH is dissolved in 100 grams of water at \( 25^{\circ} C \) to form a saturated solution.Find the solubility and weight percentage of the solution.
Solution:
Weight of the solute (NaOH) = \( w_{solute} = w_l = 16 \ g \)
Weight of the solvent \( (H_2O) = W_{solvent} = w_2 = 100 \ g \)
Solubility (S) = ?
Percentage by weight = ?
We know that solubility
\( \begin{align} S = \frac{w_solute}{w_{solvent}} \times 100 ...... \ (1) \\\\[3pt] S = \frac{16}{100} \times 100 \ \Rightarrow \ S = 16 \end{align} \)
Therefore, solubility is 16.
Percentage by weight
\( \begin{align} w \% = \frac{\text{Weight of solute}(w_1)}{\text{Weight of solution}(w_1 + w_2)} = \frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2} \times 100 \\\\[3pt] Percentage \ by \ weight = \frac{16}{116} \times 100 = 13.79 \end{align} \)
Therefore, percentage by weight of the solution = 13.79.

Example 3:

A student has only 200 g of valuable solvent and wishes to make solution of 20% by weight from solute A. How many grams of 'A' is weighed out?
Solution:
Mass of the solvent = \( w_{solvent} \) = 200 g
Mass of the solute = \( w_{solute} \) = A = ?
Percentage by weight = 20
We know that percentage by
\( \begin{align} w \% = \frac{\text{Weight of solute}(w_1)}{\text{Weight of solution}(w_1 + w_2)} = \frac{w_1}{w_1 + w_2} \times 100 \\\\[3pt] w \ \% = \frac{w_{solute}}{w_{solution}} \times 100 \\\\[3pt] = \frac{w_{solute}}{w_{solute} + w_{solvent}} \times 100 \ .... \ (1) \end{align} \)
Substituting the above values in equation (1), we get
\( 20 = \frac{w_{solute}}{w_{solute} + 200 } \times 100 \)

Example 4:

A solution with weight percentage of sodium hydroxide of 9% is prepared by adding the following mass of water (in grams) to 300 grams of that solution with a weight percent ofNaOH of 15%.
Solution:
Before dilution
Initial weight % = \( w_1 \ \% = 15 \% \)
Mass of initial solution = \( m_1 \) = 300 g
Mass of water added = \( m_2 - m_1 = m - 300 \)
How to get m?
After dilution
Final weight % = \( w_2 \ \% \) = 9%
Mass of final solution \( m_2 = m \)
We know ,
\( W \% = \frac{w_{solute}}{w_{solution}} \times 100 \ \Rightarrow \ w \% \ \propto \frac{1}{w_{solution}} \)
Note: Before and after dilution, the mass of the solute remains constant.
\( \begin{align} \Rightarrow \ \% W \ \propto \ \frac{1}{w_{solution}} \ \Rightarrow \ \frac{w_1 \ \%}{w_2 \ \%} = \frac{m_1}{m_2} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ \frac{15}{9} = \frac{m_2}{300} \ \Rightarrow \ m_2 = 500 \ g \end{align} \)
\( \therefore \) Mass of water added = 500 - 300 = 200 g

Example 5:

8 g of a solute is dissolved in 92 g of solvent. If the density of solution is 2 g \( ml^{-1} \) , find the weight - volume percentage.
Solution:
Weight of the solute = 8 g
Density of the solution = 2 g \( ml^{-1} \)
Weight of the solvent = 92 g
Weight - volume percentage?
The total weight of the solution = weight of the solute + weight of the solvent
= 8 + 92 = 100g
\( \therefore \) The volume of the solution = \( \frac{\text{Mass of the solution}}{\text{density of the solution}} = \frac{100}{2} = 50 \ ml \)
We know, weight - volume percentage = \( \frac{\text{Weight of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}} \times 100 \)
By substituting the values, we get,
Weight - volume %
\( = \frac{8}{50} \times 100 = 16 \)
Therefore, the weight - volume % of the solution is equal to \( 16 \ g \ ml^{-1} \)

Example 8:

The weight percentage and volume percentage (w/v) of a solution are 22 and 44 respectively. What is the density of the solution?
Solution:
Weight percentage of the solution = 22
Weight-volume percentage of the solution = 44
Density of the solution =?
Weight - volume percentage = density of solution \( \times \) percentage by weight.
\( \therefore \) Density of the solution = \( \frac{Weight-volume \%}{Weight \ \%} = \frac{44}{22} = 2 \)

Example 9:

The solubility of a solute is 8. If the density of solution is \( 1.364 \ g \ ml^{-1} \) , find the weight-volume percentage.
Solution:
Solubility of the solute = 8 \( \Rightarrow \) 8 g of the solute is dissolved in 100 g of solvent.
Total mass of the solution = 8 + 100 = 108 g
The density of the solution = \( \frac{Mass \ of \ the \ solution}{Volume \ of \ the \ solution} \)
\( \therefore \) The volume of the solution = \( \frac{108}{1.364} = 79.18 \ ml \)
We know,
\( \begin{align} Weight - volume \% = \frac{Weight \ soliute (g)}{Volume \ of \ the \ solution (ml)} \times 100 \\\\[3pt] = \frac{8}{79.18} \times 100 = 10.103 \ g \ ml^{-1} \end{align} \)
Therefore, the weight-volume percentage of the solution is \( 10.103 \ g \ ml^{-1} \) .
