Introduction

Matter is one of the elementary entities of nature. Elements are the basic building blocks of matter which are made up of atoms. Atoms are the smallest particles of matter that take part in the chemical reactions. Just as the ultimate particle of an element is atom, the ultimate particle of a chemical compound is called a molecule. When two or more atoms of different elements combine, the molecule of a compound is formed.
The molecules are made up of atoms of the same or different elements and are capable of independent existence.

Atomic Mass(A)

An atom of an element is so small particle that its mass cannot be determined even with the help of most sensitive balance. For example, by an indirect method, the absolute mass of hydrogen atom has been found to be \( 1.6736 \times 10^{-24} g \) . Such extremely small number are very inconvenient for calculations. The difficulty was overcome by expressing atomic masses as relative masses.

Pressure

Hydrogen being the lightest element, was first chosen as the reference, and the atomic mass was defined as follows. The relative atomic mass of an element is the number of times an atom of the element is heavier than an atom of hydrogen.
\( Relative \ atomic \ mass = \frac{mass \ of \ 1 \ atom \ of \ the \ element}{mass \ of \ 1 \ atom \ of \ hydrogen} \)
Thus, on the hydrogen scale, a hydrogen atom is assigned a mass of exactly 1 and masses of the atoms of other elements are determined accordingly.

The Carbon - 12 Scale

On this scale, one - twelfth the mass of an atom of the isotope \( ^{12}C \) is treated as the atomic mass unit (amu), and relative atomic masses are determined accordingly. Relative atomic mass is defined. as follows.
The relative atomic mass of an element is the ratio of the mass of an atom element to one - twelfth the mass of an atom of carbon - 12 isotope.
In other words, it is a number that shows how many times an atom of an element heavier than one-twelfth the mass of an atom of carbon - 12 isotope.
\( \frac{mass \ of \ 1 \ atom \ of \ the \ element}{\frac{1}{12} \times mass \ of \ 1 \ atom \ of \ ^{12}C} \)
\( \frac{1}{12} \) of the mass of 1 atom of \( ^{12}C \) , ie., 1 amu = \( 1.66 \times 10^{-24}g \) .

Relative atomic mass

The atomic mass of an element is a relative quantity and it is the mass of one atom of, the element relative to one - twelfth the mass of one atom of the element C-12.

Gram atomic mass

The atomic mass of an element expressed in grams is called the gram atomic mass of the element. Gram atomic mass is the quantity of an element whose mass in gram is numerically equal to its atomic mass. For example, the atomic mass of hydrogen is 1.008 a.m.u. and thus, a gram atom of hydrogen is 1.008 g.
Similarly, the atomic mass of oxygen = 16 a.m.u. Therefore, gram atomic mass of oxygen = 16 g.

Atomic Mass Unit (amu)

The quantity of mass equal to \( \frac{1}{{12}^{th}} \) of the mass of an atom of carbon ( \( C^{12} \) ).

Atomicity

The number of atoms that a molecule of an element contains is called the atomicity of the element. The relative atomic mass of an element is a number which shows how many times an atom of the element is heavier than an atom of a reference elements.

Molecular Mass

Like atoms, the actual masses of molecules are also very small and cannot be measured by actual weighing. Like atomic masses, molecular masses are expressed relative to the stable isotope of carbon ( \( C^{12} \) ) having mass number 12 isotope.

Relative molecular mass

The relative molecular mass of a substance is the mass of a molecule of the substance as compared to one-twelfth the mass of one carbon -12 atom.

Gram molecular mass

The molecular mass of a substance expressed in grams is called the gram-molecular mass of the substance. A quantity of substance whose mass is equal to its molecular mass is called gram molecular mass.
Example

  1. Molecular mass of hydrogen ( \( H_2 \) ) = 2
    Gram-molecular mass of hydrogen ( \( H_2 \) ) = 2 g

  2. Molecular mass of methane ( \( CH_4 \) ) = 16
    Gram-molecular mass of methane ( \( CH_4 \) ) = 16 g

Chemical combinations obey certain laws. Some of them deal with the masses of the reactants and products. However, there is one law, Gay-Lussac's law of combining volumes, that deals with the volumes of the reacting gases, as also of the products, if gaseous.

Gay Lussac's law of combining volumes

In 1781, Cavendish observed that when an electric spark is passed through a mixture containing two volumes of hydrogen and one volume of oxygen, water is formed. Encouraged by this observation, Joseph Louis Gay - Lussac studied a large number of gaseous reactions and came up with the following law. The volume of gases taking part in a chemical reaction and those of the produces, if gaseous, are in a whole - number ratio under the same conditions of temperature and pressure.
Example

1. \( \underset{2H_2}{Hydrogen} \) + \( \underset{O_2}{oxygen} \) \( \rightarrow \) \( \underset{2H_2O}{steam} \) 2:1:2
2 volumes 1 volume 2 volumes
2. \( \underset{N_2}{Nitrogen} \) + \( \underset{3H_2}{hydrogen} \) \( \rightarrow \) \( \underset{2NH_3}{ammonia} \) 1:3:2
1 volume 3 volumes 2 volumes
3. \( \underset{2CO}{Carbon \ monoxide} \) + \( \underset{O_2}{oxygen} \) \( \rightarrow \) \( \underset{2CO_2}{Carbon \ dioxide} \) 2:1:2
2 volumes 1 volume 2 volumes
4. \( \underset{H_2S}{Hydrogen \ sulphide} \) + \( \underset{Cl_2}{chloride} \) \( \rightarrow \) \( \underset{2HCl + S}{hydrogen \ chloride + sulphur \ (solid)} \) 1:1:2
1 volume 1 volume 2 volumes
Note

The laws of chemical combination related to mass were successfully explained by Dalton's atomic theory. But this theory. However, could not explain the law of combining volume.

Avogadro's Law

In order to explain Gay Lussac's law of combining volumes, Avogadro made the following assumption in 1811, known as Avogadro's hypothesis, later called Avogadro's law. Equal volumes of gases, under the same conditions of temperature and pressure, contain an equal number of molecules. According to Avogadro 's law, n molecules of hydrogen react with n molecules of chlorine to give 2n molecules of hydrogen chloride.

Therefore, one molecule of hydrogen reacts with one molecule of chlorine to give two molecules of hydrogen chloride.

Formula Mass

Covalent substances exist as discrete molecules, but ionic solids do not. You have learnt that the formula of an ionic compound represents the ratio of cation (s) and anion (s) in a crystal of the compound. Hence, for an ionic substance, the term relative formula mass should be used rather than relative molecular mass. The formula mass is calculated in the same way as the molecular mass. Sometimes, the term 'relative molecular mass is loosely used for relative formula mass.
Example

2. Calcium Chloride \( CaCl_2 \) \( 40+2 \times 35.5=111 \)

5. Sodium carbonate \( Na_2CO_3 \) \( 2 \times 23 + 12 + 3 \times 16 = 106 \)

The Mole Concept

A mole is the amount of a substance that contains the same number of particles - atoms, molecules or ions - as the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon - 12.
This number of particles (the Presently accepted value of which is \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} \) ), previously called the Avogadro number, is of great fundamental significance. Nowadays, the term Avogadro constant ( \( N_A \) ) is preferred, which is simply the Avogadro number suffixed by the unit of \( mol^{-1} \) .
The Avogadro constant = \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} mol^{-1} \) .
Thus, an assemblage of \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} \) particles is a mole.

A Mole of Ions

A mole of ions is defined as the assemblage of the same number of ions as the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of \( ^C{12} \) .
For example, a mole of \( Na^+ \) ions is a collection of \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} C1^- \) ions, which collectively weighs 35.5 g. 1 mol of NaCl will furnish 1 mol each of \( Na^+ \) and \( Cl^- \) ions.

NaCl \( \rightarrow \) \( Na^+ \) + \( Cl^- \)

1 mole 1 mole 1 mole

Molar volume

The volume occupied by one mole, i.e., one gram - molecular mass of a gas, is called the molar volume of the gas at a given temperature and pressure.
As the volume of a gas is dependent on temperature and pressure, they must be mentioned along with the molar volume. If the molar volume is taken at STP, it is called the standard molar volume or the molar volume at STP.
The standard molar volume of a gas is defined as the volume occupied by one mole, i.e., one gram - molecular mass, of the gas at STP.
It has been found that one mole of gas occupies 22.4 L at STP. In other words, a collection of \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} \) molecules of any gas occupies 22.4 L STP.
So, the standard molar volume of a gas = 22.4 L
Thus as the molecular mass of \( H_2 \) is 2, 2g of \( H_2 \) occupies 22.4 L at STP.
Similarly \( 2 \times 16 = 32 g \) of \( O_2 \) will also occupy 22.4 L at STP.

Molecular Mass from vapour denisy

The density of a gas relative to that of hydrogen at the same temperature is known as the vapour density or the relative density of the gas.
Thus,
the vapour density of a gas = \( \frac{density \ of \ the \ gas \ at \ a \ given \ P \ and \ T}{density \ of \ hydrogen \ at \ the \ same \ P \ and \ T} \)
\( =\frac{mass \ of \ the \ given \ volume \ of \ the \ gas \ at \ a \ given \ P \ and \ T}{mass \ of \ the \ same \ volume \ of \ the \ hydrogen \ at \ the \ same \ P \ and \ T} \)
\( =\frac{mass \ of \ 22.4L \ of \ the \ gas \ at \ STP}{mass \ of \ 22.4L \ of \ hydrogen \ at \ STP} \)
\( =\frac{1 \ gram - molecular \ mass \ of \ the \ gas}{2g} \)
\( \therefore \) The relative molecular mass of a gas = 2 \( \times \) vapour density

Note

\( Atomicity= \frac{relative \ molecular \ mass}{relative \ atomic \ mass}=\frac{2 \times vapour \ density}{relative \ atomic \ mass} \)

Empirical and Molecular Formula

The chemical formula may be of two types

  1. Empirical formula and

  2. Molecular formula

Empirical formula

The formula which gives the simple whole number ratio of the atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound is called empirical formula.

Molecular formula

The formula which gives the actual number of atoms of various elements present in one molecule of the compound is called molecular formula.

Relation between Empirical and Molecular Formula

Molecular formula and empirical formula are related as
Molecular formula = \( {(empirical \ formula)}_n \)
where n is a simple whole number and may have values 1, 2, 3..... It is equal to
n = \( \frac{Molecular \ mass}{Emperical \ Formula \ mass} \)
\( Molecular \ formula \ mass = n \times Emperical \ formula \ mass \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Question:
Determine the molecular mass of water.

Solution:
The molecular formula of water is \( H_2O \)
Atomic mass of H=1
Atomic mass of O = 16
Molecular mass of water \( = (2 \times atomic \ mass \ of \ H) + (1 \times atomic \ mass \ of \ O)= 2 \times 1 + 16 = 18 \)

Example 2:

Question:
Find out the molecular mass of sulphuric acid.

Solution:
The molecular formula of sulphuric acid is \( H_2SO_4 \)
Atomic mass of H = 1
Atomic mass of O = 16
Atomic mass of S = 32
Molecular mass of \( H_2 SO_4 = (2 \times atomic \ mass \ of \ H) + (1 \times atomic \ mass \ of \ S) + (4 \times atomic \ mass \ of \ O)= (2 \times 1) + (1 \times 32) + (4 \times 16) = 98 \)
i.e., molecular mass of \( H_2SO_4 \) = 98 amu.

Example 3:

Question:
How many grams of each of the following elements must be taken to get 1 mole of the element.

  1. sodium

  2. sulphur

  3. copper

Solution:
The mass of 1 mole of an element is its atomic mass expressed in grams. Since the atomic masses sodium, sulphur and copper are 23 g, 32 g and 63.5 g respectively. We get

  1. 23 g

  2. 32 g

  3. 63.5 g

Example 4:

Question:
What is the mass in grams of a single atom of chlorine ? (Atomic mass of chlorine = 35.5)

Solution:
\( \begin{align} 6.022 \times 10^{23} \ atoms \ of \ Cl = 35.5 g / 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 5.9 \times 10^{-23}g \\\\[3pt] 6.022 \times 10^{23} atoms \ of \ Cl = 35.5 \ g \\\\[3pt] Single \ atom \ of \ Cl = x \\\\[3pt] x \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 35.5 \times 1 \\\\[3pt] x = \frac{35.5}{6.022 \times 10^{23}} = 5.9 \times 10^{-23} g \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Question:
The density of liquid mercury is \( 13.6 g cm^{-3} \) . How many moles of mercury are there in 1 litre of the metal. (atomic mass of Hg = 200)

Solution:
Mass of mercury (Hg) in grams = density (g \( cm^{-3} ) \times volume (cm^3 \) )
= \( 13.6 g cm^{-3} \times 1000 cm^3 = 13600 g \)
Number of moles of mercury = mass of mercury in grams / gram - atomic mass of mercury = 13600 g / 200 g = 68 g

Example 6:

Question:
The mass of a single atom of an element M is \( 3.15 \times 10^{-23} g \) . What is its atomic mass? What could the element be?

Solution:
Gram - atomic mass = mass of \( 6.022 \times 10^{23} atoms \)
= \( mass \ of \ 1 \ atom \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \)
= \( (3.15 \times 10^{-23}g) \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \)
= \( 3.15 \times 6.022g = 18.97 g \)
Atomic Mass of the element = 18.87

Example 7:

Question:
An atom of neon has a mass of \( 3.35 \times 10^{-23} g \) . How many atoms of neon are there of the gas in 20g.

Solution:
Number of atoms = total mass / mass of 1 atom = 20 g / \( 3.35 \times 10^{-23} g = 5.97 \times 10^{23} \ atoms. \)

Example 9:

Question:
How many grams of sodium will the same number of atoms as 6 g of magnesium ?
(Na = 23, Mg = 24)

Solution:
No. of gram-atoms of Mg = mass of Mg in grams / gram-atomic mass = 6g / 24g = \( \frac{1}{4} \)

Example 10:

Question:
How many moles of Cr are there in 85 g of \( Cr_2S_3 \) ? (Cr = 52, S = 32)

Solution:
Molecular mass of \( Cr_2S_3 = 2 \times 52 + 3 \times 32 = 104 + 96 = 200 \)
85 g of \( Cr_2S_3 \) contains \( 10^4 \times 85 / 200 g \) of Cr = 44.2 g
Thus, number of moles of Cr = 44.2 / 52 = 0.85
