Probability

Human life is full of uncertaintities. In our day to day life we very often make guess and give statements like : "Possibility of a particular party to win this election is more", "Most probably it will be sunny day today"; "It is almost certain that Ramesh will compete in Bank Clerk Exam." Whenever we use such statements we have intuition which enables us to claim that one event is more likely to happen than the other. In probability theory, the degree of certainty and uncertainty is measured in terms of numbers lying between 0 and 1.

Experiment

An operation which results in some well defined outcomes is called an experiment.

Random Experiment:

An experiment whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty is called a random experiment. In other words if an experiment is performed many times under similar conditions and the outcome each time is not the same, then this experiment is known as a random experiment.
Remark: An experiment whose out - come cannot be predicted before hand is not a random experiment. For example, when we throw an apple upward, it is certain that it will come to the earth falling downward. So, throwing an apple upward is not a random experiment
Examples:

  1. "Tossing of a fair coin" is a random experiment because if we toss a coin either a head or a tail will come up. But if we toss a coin again and again, the outcome in each case will not be the same.

  2. "Drawing a card from a well shuffled pack of 52 playing cards" is a random experiment.

  3. "Throwing an unbiased dice" is a random experiment since when we throw a dice, we cannot say with certainty that a particular number ranging from 1 to 6 will come up.

Trial and Elementary Events :

When an experiment is repeated under similar conditions and it does not give the same result each time but may result in any one of the several possible outcomes, the experiment is called a trial and the outcomes are called cases. The number of times the experiment is repeated is called the number of trials.
For example:

  1. Tossing of a coin is a trial and getting a head or a tail is case or elementary event.

  2. Throwing of a dice is trial and getting 3 on its upper face is an elementary event or case.

  3. One toss of a coin is a trial when the coin is tossed 5 times.

  4. Drawing a card from a pack of a well shuffled cards is a trial and getting a king of diamond is an elementary event.

Exhaustive Number of Cases:

The total number of possible outcomes of a random experiment in a trial is known as the exhaustive number of cases. In other words the total number of elementary events of a random experiment is called the exhaustive number of cases. It is generally denoted by n (S).
For example:

  1. In throwing of a dice the exhaus-tive number of cases is 6, since any one of the six faces marked with 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 may come uppermost

  2. In drawing 2 cards from a well-shuffled pack of 52 cards, the exhuastive number of cases is \( ^{52}C_2 \) since 2 cards can be drawn out of 52 cards in \( ^{52}C_2 \) ways.

  3. In drawing three balls from a bag containing 3 white, 5 black and 3 red balls, the exhaustive number of cases is \( ^{11}C_3 \) since 3 balls can be drawn out of (3 + 5 + 3) = 11 balls in \( ^{11}C_3 \) ways.

Equally Likely Events :

Events are equally likely if there is no reason for an event to occur in preference to any other event.
Thus when an unbiased dice is thrown all the six faces 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6 are equally likely to come up.
Similarly when an unbiased coin is tossed occurrences of head and tail are equally likely cases.

Favourable Number of Cases:

The number of cases favourable to an event in a trial is the number of elementary events such that if any one of them occurs, we say that the event happens.
In other words, the number of cases favourable to an event in a trial is the total number of elementary events such that the occurrence of any one of them ensures the happening of the event. It is generally denoted by n (E).
For example:

  1. In tossing of a dice, the number of cases favourable to the appearance of a multiple of 2 is 3 viz 2. 4, 6.

  2. In drawing two cards from a pack of 52 cards, the number of cases favourable to drawing 2 kings is \( ^4C_2 \) .

Mathematical Definition of Probability

If there are n elementary events associated with a random experiment and m of them are favourable to an event E. then the probability of happening of E is denoted by P(E) and is defined as the ratio \( \begin{align} \frac{m}{n} \\\\[3pt] or, \\\\ P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of cases favourable to event A}}{\text{Total no . of cases}} \\\\[3pt] or, \\\\ P(E) = \frac{\text{No.of desired events}}{\text{Total no.of events}} = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{\text{Favourable number of cases}}{\text{Exhaustive number of cases }} \end{align} \)
Note:

  1. Probability of occurrence of an event is a number lying between 0 and 1.

  2. Probability of a certain event = 1.

  3. Probability of happening an event of not happening an event = 1

Some information about playing cards:

  1. A pack of 52 playing cards has 4 suits.

    1. Spades

    2. Hearts

    3. Diamonds

    4. Clubs.

  2. Spades and clubs are black faced cards.

  3. Hearts and diamonds are red faced cards.

  4. Each suit consists of 13 cards

  5. The aces, kings, queens, jacks are called face cards or honours cards.

Sample Space:

The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment is called the sample space and is denoted by S. No. of events in S = n(S).

sol:

Example 3:

In a bag contains 8 White balls and 6 Black balls from that bag two balls are taken out at random. Find the Probability that they are of the same color?

sol:
Here S be the sample space So,
n(S) = Number of ways of taken out balls of \( ( 8 + 6 ) = \ ^{14}C_2 = \frac{(14 \times 13)}{(2 \times 1)} = \frac{182}{2} = 91. \)
E = Event of getting both balls of the same color.
Then, n(E) = Number of ways of drawing ( 2 ball out of 8) or ( 2 balls out of 6)
\( \begin{align} (^8C_2 + ^6C_2) = \frac{8 \times 7}{2} + \frac{6 \times 5}{2 \times 1} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{56}{2} + \frac{30}{2} = 28 + 15 = 43. \\\\[3pt] P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{43}{91} \end{align} \) .

Example 4:
