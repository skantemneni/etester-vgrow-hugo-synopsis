Simplification

The simplification of an expression containing a number of fractions means to reduce it into one fraction by performing various mathematical operations namely, addition, subtraction, multiplication and division etc.
In this section we will enlarge our horizon of study that will be clear from the discussion of various related terms, definitions and worked out problems that follow :

Fraction :

A fraction is known as a rational number written in the form of \( \frac{m}{n} \) , where m and n are integers and \( n \neq 0 . \)
The lower number 'n' is known as denominator that can never be zero and the upper number 'm' is known as numerator.

Improper Fraction :

The fraction in which numerator is greater than the denominator, is called improper fraction.
For example, \( \frac{5}{4}, \ \frac{8}{7}, \ \frac{17}{7} \) etc

Proper Fraction :

The fraction in which numerator is less than the denominator is called a proper fraction.
For example, \( \frac{1}{2}, \ \frac{1}{3}, \ \frac{3}{7} \) etc

Decimal Fraction :

The fraction whose denominator is 10 and its higher powers: is railed a decimal fraction.
For example, \( \frac{2}{10}, \ \frac{7}{1000}, \ \frac{17}{10000} \) etc
They can be expressed respectively as 0.2, 0.007. 0.0017

Complex Fraction :

A complex fraction is that fraction in which numerator or denominator or both are fractions.
For example \( \frac{ \frac{2}{3}}{4}, \ \frac{ \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6}}{ \frac{2}{5} + \frac{3}{10}} \)

Continued Fraction :

Fractions which contain addition fraction or a series of fractions generally in denominator (sometimes in numerator also) are called continued fractions.
For example \( \frac{2}{2 - \frac{2}{2 + \frac{3}{4}}} \)

Recurring Decimal :

It is one in which a digit or block of digits repeats itself again and again. We represent it by putting a dot or bar on the number (s) repeating again and again.
For example,
\( \begin{align} \frac{1}{3} = 0.333...... = 0.\overline{3} \ or \ 0.3 \\\\[3pt] \frac{135}{99} = 1.363636...... = 1.\overline{36} \ or \ 0.36 \end{align} \)

Pure Recurring Decimal :

A decimal in which all the figures after the decimal point are repeated, is known as a pure recurring decimal.
Thus, \( 0.\overline{3}, \ 0.\overline{57} \) are pure recurring decimals.

Mixed Recurring Decimal :

A decimal in which at least one figure after the decimal point is not repeated and then a figure or set of figures is repeated; is known as a mixed, recurring decimal.
Thus, \( 0.\overline{26}, \ 0.\overline{327} \) are mixed recurring decimals.

Pure Recurring Decimal :

For changing pure recurring decimals into vulgar fractions, put as as many 9's in the denominator as the number of digits under the recurring and remove the recurring sign.
Thus, \( 0.\overline{3} = \frac{3}{9}, \ 0.\overline{45} = \frac{45}{99} = \frac{5}{11} \)
A mixed recurring decimal is equal to a vulgar fraction which has for its numerator the difference between the numbers formed by all the digits to the end of first period and that formed by digits which do not repeat and for its denominator the number formed by as many 9's as there are repeating digits, followed by as many zeros as there are non-recurring digits. It is illustrated below :
\( 0.3 \overline{56} = ? \)
By the rule
Numerator = 356 - 3 = 353
Denominator = 990
\( \therefore 0.3 \overline{56} = \frac{356 - 3}{990} = \frac{353}{990} \)

Simplification:

The simplification of an expression involves various operations. The order of various operations must be strictly maintained in the order of each letter of the word VBODMAS where,

  1. V stands for vinculum or bar,
    For example, \( \overline{- 3 - 8} = -(- 5) = 5 \)

  2. B stands for brackets in the order \( (), \ \lbrace \rbrace \) or []. For example,
    \( \begin{align} [4 - \lbrace 3 - (5 - 2) \rbrace] = [4 - \lbrace 3 - 3 \rbrace] \\\\[3pt] = [4 - 0] = 4 \end{align} \)

  3. O stands for of

  4. D stands for division

  5. M stands for multiplication.

  6. A stands for addition.

  7. S stands for subtraction

Important Formulae:

  1. \( (a + b)^2 = a^2 + b^2 + 2ab = (a - b)^2 + 4ab \)

  2. \( (a - b)^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab = (a + b)^2 - 4ab \)

  3. \( a^2 - b^2 = (a + b) (a - b) \)

  4. \( a^3 + b^3 = (a + b) (a^2 - ab + b^2) \)

  5. \( a^3 - b^3 = (a - b) (a^2 + ab + b^2) \)

  6. If a + b + c = 0, \( a^3 + b^3 + c^3 = 3abc \)

  7. \( \begin{align} a^4 + b^4 + a^2 b^2 \\\\[3pt] = (a^2 - ab + b^2) (a^2 + ab + b^2) \end{align} \)

  8. \( \begin{align} a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc \\\\[3pt] = (a + b + c) (a^2 + b^2 + c^2 - ab - bc - ca) \end{align} \)
    If a + b + c = 0 \( a^3 + b^3 + c^3 = 3abc \)

Mathematical Operations on Signs

  • +(+ 20) = + 20

    1. +(- 20) = - 20

    2. -(+ 20) = - 20

    3. -(- 20) = + 20

    We notice that the operation of plus does not change the sign of the number whereas the operation of minus changes the sign of the number.

    1. \( (+) \times (+) = + \)

    2. \( (-) \times (-) = + \)

    3. \( (+) \div (-) = - \)

    4. \( (-) \div (-) = + \)

    5. \( (+) \times (-) = - \)

    6. \( (+) \div (+) = + \)

    7. \( (-) \div (+) = - \)

    In multiplication and division, when both the numbers carry similar signs, we get positive sign in the result otherwise we get negative sign in the result.
