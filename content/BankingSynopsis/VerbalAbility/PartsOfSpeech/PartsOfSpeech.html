Parts of speech

Noun:

Noun is the name of a person, place or thing
Eg: (Boy, Girl, John, Ram, Lucknow, India, Honesty etc)
Types:
Proper Noun: Used to Name a specific Person, Place or thing (Unique) and Begins with a capital letter.
Eg: Ram, Shyam, John, India, Lucknow, Asia, etc.
Common Noun: Used to name people, place and things in general, it represents the category to which a person, place or thing belongs. (Also called countable noun)
Eg: (Boy, Girl, City, Continent, Animal, Mobile, Book etc.)
Material Noun: Used to name material/ substances with which something is made.
Eg: Gold, Silver, Cotton, Wood, Diamond etc.
(When these things are used as materials)
Abstract Noun: Used to name emotions, ideas, feelings that have no physical existence.
Eg: Honesty, Love, Bravery, Fear, Corruption, etc.
Collective Noun: Refers to a group of people, animals or things
Eg: Jury, Pack, Bunch, team, pile, crowd etc,
Rules
Rule 1: Some nouns always remain in plural form and used with plural verbs.
Alms, cattle, fetters, earnings, entrails, tongs, tidings, rides, wages, valuables, etc.
Rule 2: Some nouns look plural in form but have singular meaning and takes singular verbs.
Eg: News, innings, Politics, Summons, Physics, Economics, Mechanics, Mathematics, measles, Mumps, Billiards etc.
Rule 3: Some nouns are singular in form but take plural verbs
Eg: Cattle, Peasantry, gentry, Children, Police infantry etc.
Rule 4: Some nouns remains in singular form and used with singular verbs in general.
Scenery, Machinery, Poetry, Furniture, News, Information, Hair, Advice, Luggage, Wasteage, Money, Jewelry, Bread, Stationery, Baggage etc.
Rule 5: These Nouns have same form both in singular and in plural.
Eg: Deer, Sheep, Crew, Jury, Vermin, Swine etc.
Rule 6: Dozen, Hundred, Thousand, Million Score, Pair are used in singular forms when numerical adjectives are used before them.
Eg: I have two million Rupees. (not millions) He gave me five Hundred Rupees. (not hundreds)

Pronoun:

Pronoun: Pronouns are, words used in place of nouns.
\( Eg. \) He, She, It, They, I, We etc.
Types
Personal Pronouns: I, me, you, they, us, it, we etc.
Possesslve Pronouns: Mine, Yours, His, Hers, Theirs etc
Demonstrative Pronouns: This, that these those, etc.
lndeflnlte Pronouns: Both, Any, all now, some, one, anybody, somebody, something, anything etc.
Reflexive Pronouns: Himself, herself, themselves, itself, etc.
Relative Pronouns: who, whom, that and which, etc.
Interrogative Pronouns: who, whom, which, what and whose, etc.
Rules:
We, he, they, she, it, you are used, as subjects in sentences and are therefore in subjective/ nominative case.
\( Eg. \) He is nice person (subject)
Him/Her/us. etc. are in objective case. (used as objects)
\( Eg. \) He is nice person (subject)
Rule of 2 + 3 + 1 and 1 + 2 + 3 persons. In simple sentences we used 2 + 3 + 1 order.
You, He and I are friends. (sentence correct)
He, you and I and friends. (sentence wrong)
But with negative words 1 + 2 + 3 Rule applies to singulars (like sorry, guilty, mistake etc.)
\( Eg. \) I, you and he must say sorry (sentence correct)
Let is followed by objective case pronouns.
Let you and he work hard. (sentence wrong)
Let you and him work hard. (sentence correct)
It is followed by subjective/nominative case.
It is him (sentence wrong)
It is he (sentence correct)
Transitive verbs take reflexive pronouns after them (if the object is absent or required)
\( Eg. \) Helped, Hanged, called, played enjoyed etc.
He enjoyed at the party (sentence wrong)
He enjoyed himself at the party (sentence correct)
Use of neutral pronoun "one" with one's (relative pronoun)
one must do his duty (sentence wrong)
one must do one's duty (sentence correct)
who/whom are used for person/people. which is used for things and animals but not for person or people
That - is - used with all (person/place and things)
But with these words: All, much, little, none, no one, etc. that is used.
whose is used before a noun e.g. whose boot is this?
"Neither" means not one of the two and "Either" means any one of the two and both take singular verbs.
\( Eg. \) Either of the books is worth reading.
Neither of the boy is busy.

Adjectives

Adjectives: A word that defines or modifies a noun or pronoun is an adjective.
Eg. Nice, good, bad, ugly etc
He is nice person
She is intelligent.
Types
Possessive: my, your, his, her, their etc.
Eg. This is my book.
Your car is outside.
Descriptive/Quality :
Tall, short, good, beautiful, intelligent
Eg. He is a tall man.
She is a beautiful girl.
Numerical : first, second, third, one, two etc.
Eg. This book has two hundred pages.
This is the first idea and the last idea.
Demonstrative : This, that, these and those etc.
Eg. This man is very honest.
Distributive : each, every, other, another
Eg. Each book is worth reading.
Interrogative: where, what, which etc.
Eg : which books is yours?
Rules:
Rule 1: Many is used for countable nouns while much is used with uncountable nouns
Eg. How many books are there.
How much time do you need.
Rule 2 : Some is used for positive sense while any for negative Or interrogative in general
Eg. There is still some food left.
Are there any books left on the table?
Rule 3 : Latter is used with reference to order (the second one, of the two things mentioned) while former is used for the 1st mentioned thing/person in any order.
Eg. John and Ram are good players, the former is a good batsman but the latter is better.

Eg He is senior to me.

Eg I do not like these kind of shirts.
I do not like this kind of shirts.
Rule 9 "Owing to" means because of while "due to" is used as a reason for happening but in the middle of a sentence.
Eg Owing to his negligence, he failed.
Eg He failed due to his negligence.
Rule 10 Next is used to talk about time or series while near is used to talk about physical closeness.
Eg He lives near the railway station.
She got off at the next shop.

Adverbs

Adverbs: An adverb is a word In a sentence that modiï¬?es the meaning of a verb or an adjective or another adverb in general.
Eg fast, very, slowly, loudly, enough etc.
He speaks very loudly.
She runs very slowly.
Here very slowly and loudly are adverbs.
Types:
Manner: slowly, sadly, quickly, happily etc
Place: there, here, every where, now here etc.
Time: ago, before, tomorrow, today, yesterday etc.
Frequency: often, never, always, seldom, hardly etc.
Rules:
Rule 1 Fairly is used in positive sense while rather is in negative.
Eg. This book is fairly good
That shirt is rather bad.
Rule 2 Hardly/ scarily / often/ never/ always are generally used before the main verb if there is a habitual action in the sentence.
Eg. He hardly comes on time.
She often comes on time.
Rule 3 Uses of very and much
Eg It is a very good book. (sentence correct)
Eg It is a much good book. (sentence wrong)
Eg The law has been much abused. (participle)
Eg His reasons are very convincing. (general)
Rule 4. Uses of enough
Eg He is good enough (used after the adjective)
Eg He has suffered enough (used after the verb)
Rule 5 Adverbs of manner are placed after verbs in general
Eg He wrote quickly
She played beautifully
He left immediately.
Rule 6 Lately means recently while late means opposite of early.
Eg He come late today
I have it seen her lately (recently)
Rule 7 Use of too
Too means excess or more than required.
Eg He is too weak to walk
He is too old to work
It is too late for the shops to open.
Rule 8 Order of different adverbs
Manner + Place + Time
Eg He wrote the entire article quickly, home yesterday.
Rule 9 with verbs like come/go/arrive/enter:
place + manner is followed.
Eg He come here quickly.
Rule 10 Place + Frequency + Time (order of adverbs)
Eg They come here twice last week.

Prepositions

Prepositions: Preposition is usually a word that indicates the realtion between words. (generally nouns and pronouns)
Eg. He wants to help you
The culture of Lucknow .
What are you staring at?
By which train you came?
Types
Simple: in/on/at/about/owe/under/off/for/to etc
Eg. She sat on the sofa.
He fell off the ladder
Compound: without/ within/ inside, between, outside, into, below, behind etc.
Eg. I can't do it within the given time.
There is nothing inside the bucket.
Double: Outside of, out of, from behind, from beneath.
Eg. Suddenly it came out of nowhere.
Participle: Pending, considering, concering, not withstanding.
Eg. You did the job well, concerning your age and experience.
Phrase: because of, by mean of, on behalf of, instead of, on account of for the scale of etc.
Eg. I am standing here on behalf of my friends and colleagues.

Conjunction

Conjunctions: Joining words are called conjunctions.
Eg And, or, because, before, after, when, action, though, yet etc
Types
Co-ordinating : for, and nor, but or, yet so etc.
Subordinating : after, although, as, as if, because, before, though, since, so, unless, until when etc.
Co-relative conjunction : No sooner-than, hardly - when, scarcely - when, either - or, neither - nor etc.
Rules
Rule 1 Either - or. neither - nor.
Eg Either he or his friends are busy.
Neither he nor his friends are busy.
Rule 2 Not only - but also.
Eg She is not only clever but also honest.
Rule 3 Both - and.
Eg Both he and his parents are honest.
Rule 4 No sooner - than
Eg No sooner had I reached the station than the train arrived.
Rule 5 Hardly / scarely - when.
Eg Hardly had he begun his work when it started to rain.
Rule 6 Lest - should.
Eg Work hand lest you should fail in the exam.
Rule 7 As if/ as though - Past Tense. (When referring to imaginary action in past)
Eg He talks as if he had been there himself.
Rule 8 Seldom or never/seldom - if ever.
Eg He seldom or never comes to the institute.
