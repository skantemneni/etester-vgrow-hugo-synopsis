Cosmology

Solar System

  1. Our galaxy is Milky Way Galaxy (or the kash Ganga ).
    It is spiral in shape. Nearest galaxy to ours is Andromeda.

  2. Our solar system consists of a star (sun), 8 planets and countless fragments of left-overs called asteroids, meteors, comets and satellites of the planets.

The Sun

  1. It is one of the stars in the Milky Way Galaxy.

  2. The Sun is 109 times bigger than the earth and weighs \( 2 \times 10^{27} \) tonne.

  3. The energy of sun comes from the fusion of hydrogen into helium.

  4. Sunlight takes 8 minutes 16.6 seconds to reach earth.

  5. The sun's present age is 5 billion years and the total life is 10 billion years.

Planets

  1. Planets are opaque bodies, which continuously revolve around and are lighted by the Sun.

  2. There are eight planets in the solar system.

  3. The sequence of planets according to their size (in descending order) is Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Earth, Venus, Mars and Mercury.
    The sequence of planets according to their distance from the Sun is Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune.

Classification of Planets

  1. Inner Planets Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars

  2. Outer Planets Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune

Mercury

  1. Revolution: 88 days (Fastest revolution in Solar System).

  2. Maximum diurnal range of temperature. Its days are scorching hot and nights are frigid.

  3. It has no atmosphere and no satellite.

Venus

  1. Also called Earth's twin, because it is slightly smaller than earth (500 km less in diameter).

  2. Popularly known as Evening star and Morning star. It is seen in the east in the morning and in the west in the evening.
    It is not possible to see it all over the night.

  3. Brightest Planet (because of 0.65% albedo, the reflecting power).

  4. Closest planet to earth.

  5. It is the hottest planet in Solar System. It is because of the Green house effect as its atmosphere contains 90-95% carbon dioxide. The night and day temperatures are almost the same.

  6. Rotates backward (clockwise) unlike others.

  7. No satellite.

Mars

  1. Also called 'Red Planet'.

  2. Recent explorations have thrown light on the possibilities of existence of life here.

  3. It has 2 satellites- Phobos and Deimos.

Jupiter

  1. Largest of all planets (71% of the total mass of all planets). Called 'Lord of the Heavens'.

  2. It gives off more energy than it receives from the sun. because of the heat inside.

  3. Its atmosphere contains hydrogen, helium, methane and ammonia.

  4. A great red spot is detected on it. It represents a huge storm.

  5. It has the fastest rotation time (9.8 hrs) in the Solar System.

  6. It has 63 satellites (Prominent are Europa, Gannymede and Callisto). Gannymede is the largest satellite of Solar System.

Saturn

  1. Second in size after Jupiter.

  2. Revolution: 29 years. Rotation: 10.3 hrs

  3. Least density of all (30 times less dense than earth).

  4. Unique feature is its system of rings (3 well defined).

  5. 62 satellites (Prominent is Titan).

Uranus

  1. Rotates from north to south as it is inclined at an angle \( 98^{ \circ} \) to its orbit.

  2. Surrounded by a system of 9 faint rings.

  3. Has 27 satellites (prominent are Miranda, Ariel, etc)

Neptune

  1. Revolution: 165 years. Rotation: 15.7 days.

  2. It has 13 satellites (Prominent are triton and nereid)

Moon

  1. Circumference: 11,000 km Diameter: 3475 km Gravitational pull: \( \frac{1}{6}^{th} \) of Earth.

  2. Takes 27 days, 7 hrs, 43 minutes and 11.47 second to complete one revolution around earth.

  3. Rotates on its axis in exactly the same time as that of its revolution. That is why we see only one side of the moon (only 59% of its surface).

  4. Moon has no atmosphere.

  5. Moon-light takes 1.3 seconds to reach earth.

  6. Neil Armstrong and Edwin Aldrin reached moon on July 21, 1969 on Apollo XI (landing spot is called 'Sea of tranquility').

Asteroids

Asteroids are a series of very small planets or fragments of planets lying between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter.
They are to be the debris left over from the formation of inner of planets.

Comets

  1. It has a head and a tail. Its tail originates only when it gets closer to the sun. The tail can be 20-30 million km long.
    It always points away from the sun because of the force exerted by solar wind and radiation on the cometory material.

  2. Hailey's comet: Reappears after 76 years. Last seen in 1986

Meteros (Shooting Stars)

The meteors are probably the remains of comets which are scattered in the inter-planetary space of the Solar System.
On contact with the earth's atmosphere, they burn due to friction.

The Earth

  1. Also called 'Blue Planet' . It is the densest of all planets.

  2. Circumference: 40,232 km Area: 510 million sq km

  3. 2 types of movements

    1. Rotation or daily movement

    2. Revolution or annual movement

Rotation

  1. Spins on its imaginary axis from west to east in 23 hrs, 56 minutes and 40.91 seconds.

  2. Earth's rotation results in

    1. causation of days and nights;

    2. change in the direction of wind and ocean currents;

    3. rise and fall of tides everyday.

  3. The longest day in North Hemisphere is June 21, while shortest day is on 22 December (Vice-versa in S Hemisphere).

  4. Days and nights are almost equal at the equator.

Revolution

  1. It is earths motion in elliptical orbit around the sun. Earth's average orbital velocity is 29.79 \( \frac{km}{s} \) .

  2. Takes 365 days, 5 hrs, 48 minutes and 45.51 seconds. It results in one extra day every fourth year.

  3. Revolution of the earth results in

    1. change of seasons;

    2. variation in the lengths of days and nights at different times of the year;

    3. shifting of wind belts;

    4. determination of latitudes.

Equinoxes

  1. These are dates when days and nights are equal. During these days the sun shines directly over the equator.

  2. March 21 Vernal equinox

  3. September 23 Autumnal equinox

Solstic

  1. The time of the year when the difference between the length of days and the length of nights is the largest.

  2. During these days the sun shines directly over the tropics.

  3. June 21 Summer Solstice

  4. December 22 Winter Solstice

Latiude

  1. Imaginary lines drawn parallel to the equator. Measured as an angle whose apex is at the centre of the earth.

  2. The equator represents \( 0^{ \circ} \) latitude, while the North Pole is \( 90^{ \circ} \) N and the South Pole \( 90^{ \circ} \) S.

  3. \( 23 \frac{1}{2}^{ \circ} \) N represents Tropic of Cancer While \( 23 \frac{1}{2}^{ \circ} \) S represents Tropic of Capricorn

Longitude

  1. It is the angular distance measured from the centre of the earth. On the globe the lines of longitude are drawn as a series of semicircles that extend from the North Pole to the South Pole through the equator.
    They are also called meridians.

  2. India, whose longitudinal extent is approx. \( 30^{ \circ} \) , has adopted only one time zone, selecting the \( 82.5^{ \circ} E \) for the standard time which is 5 hours and 30 minutes ahead of GMT (Greenwich Mean Time).

International Date Line

  1. It is the \( 180^{ \circ} \) meridian running over the Pacific Ocean, deviating at Aleutian Islands, Fiji, Samoa and Gilbert Islands.

  2. Travellers crossing the Date Line from west to east (i.e., from Japan to USA) repeat a day and travellers crossing it from east to west (i.e., from USA to Japan) lose a day.

Eclipse

Lunar Eclipse

  1. When earth comes between sun and moon.

  2. Occurs only on a full moon day. However, it does not occur on every full moon day.

Solar Eclipse

  1. When moon comes between sun and earth.

  2. Can be partial or total.

  3. Occurs only on a new moon day when the moon is in line with the sun.
    However, due to the inclination of the moon's orbit, a solar eclipse doesn't occur on every new moon day.

Internal Structure of Earth

The Crust

  1. It is the outermost and the thinnest layer of the earth's surface, about 8 to 40 km thick.

  2. Thickness of the crust is more under the continents and lesser under the oceans, while denisty of crust is lower than oceans.

  3. It forming the crust of the Earth are rich in lighter minerals like silica and aluminium. Hence, this layer is also called as Sial (silica and aluminium). The average density of this layer is 2.7.

The Mantle

  1. This layer extends up to a depth of 2,900 kms.

  2. Due to presence of minerals like silica and magnesium in the rocks forming this layer of the Earth , the mantle layer is also known as Sima (silica and magnesium). The average density of this layer is about 5.68.

  3. The line of separation between the mantle and the crust is known as Mohoviricic Discontinuity.

The Core

  1. Beyond a depth of 2,900 km lies the core of the earth.

  2. This layer is also known as Nife (nickel and iron), because this layer contain large concentration of iron and nickel.

  3. Temperature of the core is between \( 2200^{ \circ} C \) and \( 2750^{ \circ} C \) .

  4. Density of this part of the Earth is 17.2 and is many times greater than the average density of the Earth (5.53).

  5. The line of separation between the mantle and the core is called Gutenberg-Wiechert Discontinuity.

Earthquakes

  1. It refers to the vibration of the Earth's surface caused by the endogenetic forces of Earth.

  2. Seismology is the special branch of geology that deals with the study of earthquake.

  3. The magnitude or intensity of energy released by an earthquake is measured by the Richter Scale , whereas the damage caused is measured by modified Mercalli Intensity Scale .

  4. The waves generated by earthquake are called seismic waves and they are classified into 3 types such as

Primary Wave (P waves) These are the waves of short wave length and high frequency. They are longitudinal waves and can travel through solid, liquid and gases.
(S Waves) These are the waves of short wave length and high frequency. They are transverse waves, which travel through all solid particles.
Surface Waves or Long Waves (L waves) They are the waves of long wavelength, confined to the skin of the Earth's crust. It causes most of the earthquake's structural damage.

Volcanism

  1. Volcanism includes all phenomena connected with the movement of heated material from the interior to or towards the surface of the Earth.

  2. A volcano is a vent or opening, through which heated materials consisting of gases. water, liquid lava, fragments of rocks are ejected from the highly heated interior to the surface of the Earth.

The Rocks

Any aggregate of material particles that forms part of the earth's crust is called a rock.
There are 3 major types of rock

Igneous Rocks

  1. Formed by the solidification of molten magma from the interior of the earth.

  2. Most abundant of the three (95%).

  3. All other types of rocks originate from these rocks, thus called Primary rocks.

  4. Granite, Basalt. Gabbro are some of the examples of Igneous rocks.

Sedimentary Rocks

  1. Made up of weathered remains of igneous rocks. Also contains fossils of plants and animals.

  2. Comprise only about 5% of the earth's crust but cover about 75% of the total land surface.

  3. Coal and gypsum are some of the examples of sedimentary rocks.

Metamorphic Rocks

When igneous or sedimentary rocks metamorphize or change due to great pressure, intense temperature or the action of water and chemical activity is called Metamorphic Rocks.

Atmosphere

  1. The atmosphere is a mixture of a layer of gases enveloping the earth, held to it by gravitational force. Almost all the atmosphere (97 per cent) lies within 29 kms of the earth's surface.

  2. Up to about 50 kms the atmosphere is composed of

    1. Nitrogen : 78.09%

    2. Oxygen : 20.95%

    3. Argon : 0.93%

    4. Carbondioxide : 0.03%
      (Others are Neon, Helium, Ozone, Hydrogen, etc)

  3. Carbon dioxide is important for absorption of heat from the sun as well as from the earth. A high concentration of carbon dioxide leads to Greenhouse Effect.

Layers of Atmosphere

Troposphere

  1. Layer nearest to earth's surface. Thickness varies from 8 kms at the poles to 16 km at the equator.

  2. All weather phenomenons occur here.

  3. Tropopause separates troposphere from stratosphere.

Stratosphere

  1. Stratosphere provides ideal conditions for flying large airplanes.

  2. Contains ozone (25-30 km from earth's surface), region being called Ozonosphere. It absorbs the ultra-violet rays from the sun.
    This layer has a comparatively higher temperature due to the absorption of ultra-violet radiation from the sun (temperature increases as we go up).

Mesosphere

  1. Up to a height of about 80 km.

  2. In this, the temperature decreases with height and falls to about \( - 100^{ \circ} c \) at 80 km ht.

Ionosphere

  1. Extends to about 500-600 km.

  2. Called so as it contains electrically charged particles (ions) that reflect the radio waves back to the earth thus making radio communication possible.

  3. It also protects earth from falling meteorites, as most of them burn out in this region.

Ionosphere

  1. Here the earth's gravity is extremely weak.

  2. The outer part is called Magnetosphere.

Winds and Their Types

  1. 3 broad categories are

    1. Regular
      (e.g., : Trade winds, Westerlies and Polar Easterlies).

    2. Winds/Prevailing Winds/ Planetary Periodical Winds (which blow seasonally): Monsoons

    3. Variable Winds (cyclones and other local winds).

Trade Winds

  1. Trade in German means 'Track'. To blow 'trade' means 'to blow steadily in the same direction and in a constant course'.

  2. These are steady currents of air blowing from the sub-tropical high pressure belts towards the equatorial low . pressure areas (doldrums).

Westerlies

  1. Blows from subtropical high pressure to sub-polar low pressure belt.

  2. In the northern hemisphere, land masses cause considerable disruption in the westerly wind belt. But between \( 40^{ \circ} \) and \( 60^{ \circ} S \) lies the almost unbroken ocean belt. Westerlies are strong and persistent here, giving rise to mariner's expressions- 'Roaring Forties', 'Furious Fifties' and 'Shrieking Sixties'.

Polar Easterlies

  1. Move from high pressure poles to sub-polar low pressure areas.

  2. These are deflected by the Earth's rotation to become east winds, or the polar easterlies.

Indian Geography

Location

  1. Lying between latitude \( 8^{ \circ} 4' N \) to \( 37^{ \circ} 6' N \) and from longitude \( 68^{ \circ} 7' E \) to \( 97^{ \circ} 25' E \) , the country is divided into almost equal parts by the Tropic of Cancer (passing from Jabalpur in MP).

  2. The southernmost point in Indian territory, (in Great Nicobar Island) is the Indira Point \( (6^{ \circ} 45' ) \) , while Kanyakumari, also known as Cape Comorin, is the southernmost point of Indian mainland.
    The country thus ors wholly in the northern and eastern hemispheres.

  3. The \( 82^{ \circ} 30' E \) longitude is taken as the Standard Time Meridian of India, as it passes through the middle of India from Naini, near Allahabad).

The Climate

  1. India has tropical monsoon type of climate. It is greatly influenced by the presence of the Himalayas in the north as they block the cold air masses from Central Asia. It is because of them only that the monsoons have a watershed in India.

  2. The Tropic of Cancer divides India into two almost equal climatic zones, namely, the northern zone and the southern zone. The warm temperate or the subtropical climate of the northern zone gives it cold winter seasons and the hot summer seasons. The southern tropical climatic zone is warmer than the north and does not have a clear-cut winter season.

  3. The fastest train in India is the Shatabdi Express whose maximum speed is 140 km/h.

  4. The longest railway platform in India is at Gorakhpur ( Uttar Pradesh).

  5. The longest train route is of Vivak Express' from Dibrugarh to Kanyakumari. It covers a distance of 4,286 km.

  6. The first Metro Rail was introduced in Kolkata (Paschim Banga) on October 24, 1984. The two stations connected were Dumdum and Belgachhia.

  7. The Indian Railways operate in three different gauges.

    1. Broad Gauge (Distance between rails is 1.67 m)

    2. Metro Gauge (Distance between rails is 1.00 m)

    3. Narrow Gauge
      (Distance between rails is 0.762 or 0.610 m)

The broad gauge accounts for nearly 50% route followed by metre gauge (43%) and the remaining by narrow gauge.

Waterways

The total length of navigable waterways in India comprising rivers. canals, backwaters, etc., is 14,500 km out of which 3,700 km is navigable by mechanised boats.

Ports

  1. The Waterways Authority in India divides Indian ports into three categories, major, minor and intermediate.

  2. India has about 190 ports in all, with 12 major and the rest intermediate and minor.
