Indian Economy at a Glance

Indian economy is 10th largest in world by nominal GDP and 3rd largest on Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) basis in 2013 (International Monetary Fund).

Purchasing Power Parity

  1. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) is a theory which states that exchange rates between currencies are balanced when their purchasing power is the same in each of the two countries.

  2. India has a share of 17.5% in world population, but accounts for only 2.6% of world's GNI (Gross National Income) on exchange rate basis.

Characteristics of Indian Economy

Main characteristics of Indian economy are

Agrarian Economy

In an agrarian or primary economy, agriculture dominance prevails in both the GNP and employment.

Mixed Economy

It is an economy where both public and private sector co exist. The nature of Indian economy is a mixed economy, coined by JM Keynes.

Developing Economy

Following features shows that Indian economy is a developing economy

  1. Low per capita income

  2. Heavy population pressure.

  3. Prevalence of chronic unemployment and underemployment.

  4. Steadily improving rate of capital formation.

  5. Low human development indicators.

Broad Sectors of Indian Economy

Primary Sector

Agriculture, forestry, fishing.

Secondary Sector

Mining, manufacturing, electricity, gas and water supply, construction.

Tertiary Sector

Business (also called service sector) transport, telecommunication, banking, insurance, real estate, community and personnel services.

National Income of India

  1. National Income is the net value of all the final goods and services produced in a country during a financial year. It is a flow concept. In India the financial year is from 1st April to March . The nationa. income is calculated annually.

  2. National Income is the measurement of the production power of an economic system in a given time period.

National Income Aggregates

Gross National Product (GNP)

GNP refers to the money value of total output of production of final goods and services produced by the nationals of a country during a given period of time, generally a year. Symbolically,
GNP = C + G + I + (X - M)+ (R - P)
Where, C = Consumption expenditure
G = Government expenditure
I = Investment expenditure
(X - M) = Net exports
(R - P) = Net factor income from abroad.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

It is the total money value of all final goods and services produced within the geographical boundaries of the country during a giver period of time.
Symbolically, GDP = GNP - (R - P)
When, R - P = 0 then GDP = GNP

Net National Product (NNP)

NNP = GNP - Depreciation

Personal Income (P1)

Personal Income = National Income - Undistributed profits of corporations - Payment for social security provisions - Corporate taxes + Transfer payments + Net interest paid by the government

CSO and NSSO

  1. In 1949, Central Statistical Organization (CSO) was Constituted to publish national income data.

  2. NSSO (National Sample Survey Organization) was set-up in 1950, for conducting large scale sample survey to meet the data needs of the country for the estimation of national income and other aggregates

Personal Disposable

Income (PDI)

When personal direct taxes are subtracted from personal income, the obtained value is called personal disposable income. Symbolically,
PDI = PI - Direct taxes
PDI = Consumption + Saving

National Income (NI)

When NNP is calculated at factor cost (FC) it is called National Income. This measure is calculated by deducting indirect taxes and adding subsidies in NNP at Market Price (MP).
\( {NNP}_{Fc} = {NNP}_{MP} - Indirect \ Taxes + Subsidies \) or NNPFC = GNPMP - Depreciation - Indirect Taxes + Subsidies.

Estimates of National Income in India

  1. In 1868, the first attempt was made by Dadabhai Naoroji in his book Poverty and Un-British Rule in India. He estimated the per capita annual income to be Rs.20.

  2. The first scientific attempt to measure national income in India was made by Prof VKRV Rao in 1931-32. He divided the Indian economy into 13 sectors.

  3. In 1949, National Income Committee under the Chairmanship of Prof PC Mahalanobis was constituted.

  4. National Statistical Commission (NSO) was set-up on 1st June 1st 2005 for promoting statistical network in the country. It was then headed by Prof SD Tendulkar.

Planning in India

  1. Planning is a conscious attempt on the part of policy makers to achieve multiple and pre-determined objectives of development within a specific period of time in a planned way. First attempt was made was by Sir M Visvesvaraya through his book 'Planned Economy for India' in 1934.

  2. Planning in India derives its objectives and social premises from the Directive Priciples of State Policy mentioned in Indian Constitution.

Planning Commission

  1. National Plannin Committee was set-up under the Chairmanship of Jawaharlal Nehru in 1938.

  2. The Government of India setup the Planning Commission on 15 March, 1950.

  3. It is the central body for making plans in India and Prime Minister is the Ex - officio Chairman. It is a non-statutory, extra - constitutional and advisory body and finds no mention in the Constitution of India.

  4. The first Chairman of the Commission was Jawaharlal Nehru and first Deputy Chairman was Gulzarilal Nanda.

Role of Planning Commission

The Planning Commission has been assigned a number of functions. These were

  1. To make an assessment of the material, capital and human resources of the country and investigate the possibilities to augment these resources.

  2. To formulate a plan for the most effective and balanced utilisation of the country's resources.

  3. To indicate and determine the conditions for the successful execution of the plan

  4. To determine priorities and define stages in which the plan should be divided and propose the allocation of resources.

National Development Council (NDC)

  1. The NDC was Constituted on 6th August, 1952, with Prime Minister as the Ex-officio Chairman and the secretary of the Planning Commission as the Ex-officio Secretary of the NDC.

  2. Chief Ministers of all the states and the members of the Planning Commission are the members of the NDC. It is an extra-constitutional body.

Functions

  1. It aims to make cooperative environment for economic planning between states and the Planning Commission. It gives final approval to the Five Year Plan.

  2. It evaluates the management of plans from time to time.

  3. It analyses the policies affecting development.

  4. It gives suggestions to achieve the fixed aim in the plans.

Poverty and Unemployment

Poverty

  1. Poverty line is defined on the basis of nutritional standards. The least calorie intake is fixed at 2400 cal/person/day for rural area and 2,100 cal/person/day for urban area. The least per capita per month in rural area is fixed at Rs.328 and for urban area is Rs.454. The people below these nutritional and , income standards are considered to be below the poverty line (BPL).

  2. Presently 24.4% population in India is below Poverty Line. It is 24.36% in rural India and 24.50% in urban areas. National Sample Survey Organization (NSSO) conducts this survey.

Unemployment

  1. An economic condition marked by the fact that individual actively seeking jobs remain unemployed. Unemployment is expressed as a percentage of the total available workforce. The level of unemployment varies with economic conditions and other circumstances.

  2. In India, a person working 8 hours a day for 273 days of the year is regarded as employed on a standard person year basis.

Types of Unemployment

  1. Structural Unemployment

  2. Open Unemployment

  3. Frictional Unemployment

  4. Classical Unemployment

  5. Under Employment

  6. Disguised Unemployment

  7. Seasonal Unemployment

  8. Cyclical Unemployment

Agriculture and Industry

  1. Agriculture is the mainstay of Indian economy despite concerted in industrialization in the last six decades.

  2. Although, its share in national income is declining but still it contributed 11.65% to the total GDP in 2012-2013 and provides livelihood to more than half of the population.

Green Revolution

  1. Indian Green Revolution is associated with the use of HYVS (High Yielding Variety Seeds), chemical fertilizers and new technology which led to a sharp rise in agricultural production during the middle of 1960.

  2. The credit of this goes not only to Nobel Laureate Dr Norman Borlaug, but also to Dr MS Swaminathan.

Regional Rural Banks(RRBs)

  1. RRBs formally launched in 2nd October. 1975, at Moradabad and Gorakhpur (Uttar Pradesh), Bhiwani (Haryana), Jaipur (Rajasthan) and Malda (Paschim Banga).

  2. The objective of the RRBs was to provide credit and other facilities particularly to small and marginal farmers, agricultural labourers etc so as to develop agriculture.

NABARD

  1. National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development (NABARD) was set-up in July, 1982, as the Apex Bank with a paid-up capital of Rs.100 crore contributed equally by RBIs and Government of India. Its headquarter is at Mumbai.

  2. The role of NABARD was to act as a refinance institution for all kind of production and investment credit to agricultural and village sector.

  3. Rural Infrastucture Development Fund (RIDE) was set-up in 1995-96, under NABARD for holistic ryral developmemnt.

Jute Industry

  1. Its first modernized industrial unit was established at Reshra in West Bengal in 1855.

  2. The jute industry in the country is traditionally export oriented. India ranks number one in raw jute and jute goods production and number two in export of jute goods in the world.

Cotton and Textile Industry

  1. Oldest industry of India, and employs largest number of workers.

  2. The first Indian modernized cotton cloth mill was established in 1818 at Fort Gloaster near Kolkata but this mill was not successful.

  3. The second mill named 'Bombay Spinning and Weaving Co.' was established in 1854 at Bombay by KGN Daber.

Sugar Industry

  1. Sugar industry is the second largest industry after cotton textile industry among agriculture based industries in the country.

  2. India is now the largest producer and consumer of sugar in the world. Maharashtra contributes over one-third of the total sugar output, followed closely by UP.

Silk Industry

India is the second largest (first being China) country in the world in producing natural silk. At present, India produces about 16 per cent silk of the world.

Petroleum and Natural Gas

  1. First successful oil well was dug in India in 1889 at Digboi, Asom.

  2. For exploration purpose, Oil and Natural Gas Commission (ONGC) was established in 1956 at Dehradun. Uttarakhand.

Navratnas

  1. In 1997, the Government identified nine leading, well performing and high profit making public enterprises as Navratnas (Nine precious jewels), two more were added to the list. In year 2007 (June) three more are about to join the list.

  2. They have been given special powers including freedom to form new joint ventures, make new investments and authorized to raise money.

Miniratnas

In 1997. the Government recognized another 100 profit making public enterprises as Miniratnas and granted them financial, managerial and operational autonomy.

Tax Structure

  1. Tax is a compulsory payment by the citizens to the government to meet the public expenditure.

  2. It is legally imposed by the government on the taxpayer and in no case taxpayer can deny to pay taxes to the government.

  3. Direct Tax A direct tax is that tax which is born by the person on whom it is levied. A direct tax cannot be shifted to other person.

  4. Indirect Tax Indirect taxes are those taxes which have their primary burden or impact on one person.

  5. But that person succeeds in shifting his burden on to others.

Currency and Inflation

Currency

  1. The present monetary system of India is based on inconvertible paper currency and is managed by the RBI.

  2. The present currency system is based on minimum reserve system of note issue.

  3. It was adopted in 1957 under the minimum reserve system, minimum of gold and foreign securities to the extent of Rs.200 crore (of which gold should be of value Rs.115 crore) and the balance in rupee securities is maintained.

  4. Rupee was first minted in India during the reign of Sher Shah Suri around 1542.

  5. All coins and one rupee notes are issued by Govt. of India. That's why one rupee note doesn't bear the signature of Governor of RBI. It bears the signature of Finance Secretary, Government of India.

Inflation

  1. Inflation is that state in which the prices of goods and services rise on the one hand and value of money falls on the other.

  2. When money circulation exceeds the production of goods and services, the state of inflation takes place in the economy.

Banking System in India

The first purely Indian bank was the Punjab National Bank (1894).

Reserve Bank of India

  1. It is the Central Bank of the country.

  2. It was established on April I, 1935 with a capital of Rs 5 crore.

  3. It was nationalized on January 1, 1949 as govt. acquired the private share holdings.

Functions

  1. Issuing of Notes

  2. Banker to the Government

  3. Banker's Bank

  4. Controller of Credit

  5. Custodian of Foreign Reserves

  6. It formulates and administers the monetary policy.

  7. Acts as the agent of the Government of India in respect to India's membership of the IMF and the World Bank.

State Bank of India (SBI)

  1. It was created in January, 1921 by amalgamation of 3 presidency banks.

  2. After nationalization in 1955, its name was changed to State Bank of India.

  3. It is the biggest commercial bank in the public sector of India.

  4. It has the largest number of branches (more than 13000) in the world.

  5. State SBI has 7 subsidiaries.

Share Market in India

  1. Stock exchange or share market deals in shares, debentures and financial securities.

  2. Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) is the oldest stock exchange in Asia which was established in 1875. It is synonymous with Dalai Street. BSE was corporatised and renamed BSE Limited in 2005.

  3. In 1894, the Ahmedabad Stock Exchange was started to facilitate dealing in the shares of textile mills.

  4. There are 23 stock exchanges in India. Among them two are national level stock exchanges namely Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) and National Stock Exchange (NSE). The rest 21 are Regional Stock Exchanges (RSE).

Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)

  1. It was extablished in April, 1988, and awarded statutory status by an Act of Parliament in 1992.

  2. It is the regulatory authority of stock exchanges and protects investors from fraudulent dealings.

Insurance

Insurance industry includes two sectors-Life Insurance and General Insurance.

Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC)

  1. LIC was established on 1st September 1 1956, which set the pace for nationalisation of life insurance under the stewardship of CD Deshmukh.

  2. It has head office at Mumbai and eight zonal offices, the most recent being at Patna.

  3. LIC is also operating inter nationally through branch offices in Fiji, Mauritius, and UK and through joint venture companies in Bahrain, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Kenya and Saudi Arabia. In 2008, its wholly and subsidiary was opened in Singapore.

General Insurance Corporation of India (GIC)

  1. GIC was established on 1st January, 1973 with its four subsidiaries, viz

    1. National Insurance Company Limited, Kolkata;

    2. The New India Assurance Company Limited, Mumbai;

    3. The Oriental Fire and General Insurance Company Limited New Delhi; and

    4. United India General Insurance Company Limited, Chennai.

  2. On 3rd November, 2000, GIC was renamed as GIC Re and approved as Indian Reinsurer and the four subsidiaries of GIC were separated from GIC and are functioning independently under Public Sector General Insurance Companies (GIPSA).
