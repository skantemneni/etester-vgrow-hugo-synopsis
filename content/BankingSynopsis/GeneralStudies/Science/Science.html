Position

The first idea needed in describing the motion of an object is its position relative to some fixed reference point. This entails two ideas: the distance the object is away from the reference point, and also the direction relative to that reference point. Waves

  1. A wave is a disturbance that travels across space and time

  2. Propagation of waves usually involves transfer of energy without transferring mass. This is achieved by oscillations or vibrations around fixed locations

  3. Electromagnetic waves do not require a

  4. Mechanical waves require a medium for transmission (e.g. sound) medium and can travel in vacuum (e.g . light)

  5. Longitudinal waves are those with vibrations parallel to the direction of wave propagation.
    E.g. sound waves

  6. Transverse waves are those with vibrations perpendicular to the direction of travel. E.g. electromagnetic waves including light

  7. Waves on a string are an example of transverse waves

Properties of Waves

  1. Reflection: It is the change in direction of a wave at the interface between two media Examples include reflection of light, sound etc

  2. Refraction: It is the change in direction of a wave due to a change in its speed. Examples: refraction of light when it passes through a lens

  3. Electromagnetic waves do not require a medium and can travel in vacuum (e.g. light)

  4. Diffraction: Bending of waves as they interact with obstacles in their path. Example: rainbow pattern when light falls on a CD or DVD

  5. Interference: Superposition of two waves that come into contact

  6. Dispersion: the splitting up of waves by frequency

  7. Polarization: the oscillation of a wave in only one direction. Exhibited only by transverse waves (like light), not exhibited by longitudinal waves (like sound)

Wave properties in everyday life

  1. The floor of a lake or the ocean appears closer than it actually is. This is because of refraction of light

  2. The red ring around the Sun is due to diffraction of light

  3. We can hear but not see across corners, this is because of diffraction of sound (e.g. we can hear but not see a person in the next room)

  4. The rainbow and the blue colour of sky are both due to dispersion of light

  5. Sunglasses use polarization filters to block glare

The Human Eye and Corrective Lenses
The pupil is a little hole which allows light to pass into the eye. Behind the pupil lies the eye's lens. Muscles in the eye control the size of the pupil and the shape of the lens, thereby adjusting the amount of light that enters the eye and the focus of the lens. The retina is a sensitive layer of nerves at the back of the eyeball; incident light upon the retina is translated into a coherent image by the brain.
Many people do not have perfect vision; that is, a lot of people have eyes whose lenses do not focus light properly on the retina.
Magnifying Glasses
In our study of lenses, we saw that if the source was placed within a focal length of a converging lens, the lens yielded a magniï¬?ed image on the same side of the lens as the source. This is, of course, the detective's best friend, the magnifying glass.
The amount of magniï¬?cation, as we know from our treatment of lenses, depends on the distance of the source from the lens, and the refractive index of the lens material.
Cameras
Cameras, unsurprisingly, work on similar principles as the eye.
The aperture, which lets light into the inside of the camera, corresponds to the pupil. The system of lenses in a camera performs the same function as the lens of the eye. However, whereas the lens of the eye changes shape to change focus, glass lenses are not very forgiving of shape changes. Instead, the lens system can be slide along its Optical axis in order to focus on the ï¬?lm. Of course, the ï¬?lm plays the role of the retina. In addition, cameras have a shutter, which opens and closes quickly so that the film does not get inundated with light. This produces a more or less clear image of the instant that the photographerg shoots.

Environmental Chemistry

Overview

  1. Environmental chemistry is the study of chemical and biochemical phenomena that occur in natural places

  2. Environmental chemistry is used to detect and identify the nature and source of pollutants,including

  3. Heavy metal contamination of land by industry. These can transported to water bodies and taken up ingested by living organisms

  4. Nutrients leaching from agricultural land into water sources

  5. Urban pollutants runoff. Typical pollutants include petrol and other fuel, metals, nutrients and sediments

  6. Common environmental phenomena arising out of contamination include acid rain, soil salination and ocean acidification

Water Quality Parameters

  1. Dissolved Oxygen (Oxygen Saturation)

  2. It is a relative measure of the amount of oxygen dissolved in water.

  3. Supersaturation (excess of oxygen) can be harmful to organisms and also cause decompression sickness

  4. It is expressed in \( \frac{mg}{l} \)

  5. Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)

  6. COD is used to indirectly measure the amount of organic compounds in water

  7. It is expressed in \( \frac{mg}{l} \) , which indicates the amount of oxygen consumed per litre of water

  8. Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)

  9. BOD measures the rate of uptake of oxygen by microorganisms in water

  10. BOD is measured at a temperature of \( 20^{ \circ} C \) and over a period of 5 days in the dark

  11. BOD is widely used to determine the threshold at which treated wastewater can be reintroduced into the environment

  12. Pristine rivers have a BOD of below 1 \( \frac{mg}{l} \) . Municipal sewage treated effectively by a three-stage process would have BOD of 20 \( \frac{mg}{l} \) or less

  13. Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)

  14. TDS is a measure of combined content of all inorganic and organic substances contained in a liquid

  15. TDS is generally not considered a primary pollutant, but is used to indicate the aesthetic characteristics of drinking water

  16. High TDS levels generallyindicate hard water

  17. Drinking water is expected to have a TDS 100 \( \frac{mg}{l} \) or less

Environmental Pollution Phenomena

Acid rain

  1. Acid rain is form of rain that is unusually acidic i.e. has low pH

  2. Acid rain is mostly caused by emission of sulphur, nitrogen and carbon which react with water molecules in the atmosphere to produce acids

  3. The biggest human activity causes of acid rain include coal-based power plants, factories and automobile emissions .

  4. It can also be caused by natural phenomena such as

  5. lightning strikes (which splits nitrogen compounds)

  6. volcanic eruptions (which release large quantities of sulphur dioxide)

  7. Natural (unpolluted) rain is slightly acidic with pH of 5.2 due to the reaction of carbon dioxide with water to produce carbonic acid

  8. Acid rain has many adverse effects including

  9. Damage to aquatic animals

  10. Damage to soil chemistry by killing of essential microbes

  11. Loss of forests and vegetation

  12. Human illnesses such as cancer, asthma and other diseases

  13. Damage to buildings and historical monuments (esp. those made of limestone and marble)

Chemical reactions in explosives

Deflagration

  1. Deï¬‚agration is a term that describes subsonic combustion that propagates through thermal conductivity

  2. Deï¬‚agration is easier to control and so is used when the goal is to move an object with the force of expanding gas

  3. Examples of deï¬‚agration include gas stove, internal combustion engine, gunpowder, pyrotechnics etc

Detonation:

  1. Detonation is a combustion process in which a supersonic shock wave through the body of a material

  2. In detonation, a supersonic shock wave originating at the point of ignition compresses the surrounding material, thus increasing its temperature to the point of ignition

  3. Because detonations generate high pressures, they are much more destructive than deflagrations

  4. Detonations are difficult to control and are used primarily for demolition and in warfare

  5. Examples of detonation includes high explosives, oxygen-methane mixture

Classification of explosives

High explosives

  1. Materials that explode faster than the speed of sound are called high explosives

  2. This type of explosion is known as detonation

  3. Because detonations generate high pressures, they are much more destructive than deflagrations

  4. Detonations are difficult to control and are used primarily for demolition and in warfare

  5. Used in mining, demolition and military applications

Low explosives

  1. Materials that explode slower than the speed of sound are called low explosives.

  2. This type of explosion is known as deflagration

  3. Used as propellants, gun powder, pyrotechnics (such as flares and fireworks)

Primary explosives

  1. A primary explosive is an explosive that is extremely sensitive to stimuli. These stimuli include impact, friction, heat, static electricity and electromagnetic radiation

  2. For primary explosives, a relatively small amount of energy is required for initiation of explosion

  3. In general, primary explosives are considered to be those explosives that are more sensitive than PETN

  4. Used in detonators to trigger larger charges of more stable secondary explosives

  5. E.g.: Mercury fulminate, Nitrogen trichloride, acetone peroxide, ammonium permanganate

Secondary explosives

  1. Secondary explosives are less sensitive than primary explosives and require more energy to be initiated

  2. They are safer to handle and store

  3. In general, secondary explosives are considered to be those explosives that are less sensitive than PETN

  4. Secondary explosives are usually used in large quantities and are initiated by small amounts of primary explosives E.g.: TNT, RDX (Research and Development Xplosive)

Ceramics

Overview

  1. A ceramic is an inorganic, non-metallic solid prepared by the action of heating and subsequent cooling

  2. The earliest ceramic materials were pottery made from clay

  3. Ceramics are resistant to chemical erosion and high temperatures (up to 16000 C)

Properties of Ceramics

  1. Mechanical properties

  2. Ceramic materials are usually formed by ionic or covalent bonds

  3. These materials tend to not be elastic and fracture easily

  4. Ceramics are also porous

  5. In general ceramics have poor toughness and have low tensile strength

  6. Electrical properties

  7. Some ceramics are semiconductors

  8. Semiconducting ceramics are made using zinc oxide

  9. Under extremely low temperatures. some ceramics exhibit superconductivity

  10. Most ceramics exhibit piezoelectricity i.e. the conversion of mechanical stress to electrical signals. This effect is commonly used in quartz watches

  11. Optical properties

  12. Ceramics (esp. those based on aluminium oxide) can be made translucent

  13. This has immediate applications in sodium - vapour lamps and dental restorations

  14. Ceramics can be made transparent with applications in laser technology

Types of Ceramics

  1. structural ceramics such as bricks, pipes, ï¬‚oor, roof tiles etc

  2. Refractory ceramics such as kiln lining, steel and glass making crucibles

  3. Whitewares such as tableware, wall, tiles, pottery, sanitary products

  4. Technical ceramics such as jet engine turbine blades, ballistic protection etc

Manufacture of Ceramics

Milling

  1. Process by which materials are reduced in size

  2. Involves breaking of cemented material or pulverization

  3. Techniques used include ball mill, roll crusher, jaw crusher, wet attrition mills

Batching
Is the process of weighing the oxides according to recipes and preparing them for further processing

Mixing

  1. Involves mixing the various components inthe appropriate proportions

  2. Uses ribbon mixers, Mueller mixers and pug mills

Forming

  1. This is the process of the making the mixed materials into desired shapes such as toilet bowls, spark plugs etc

  2. Forming techniques include extrusion, pressing and slip shape

Drying
Controlled heat is applied to dry the materials and obtain rigid shape

Firing
Dried parts are processed through a controlled heating process and oxides are chemically changed to cause sintering and bonding

Bio-Ceramics

  1. Bacteria, plants and animals exhibit a tendency to form crystalline materials composed of silicon

  2. These bioceramics show exceptional physical properties such as strength, fracture resistance etc

  3. Bio-ceramics are usually made of proteins such as keratin, elastin, chitin and collagen

  4. The mother-of-pearl portion of marine shells exhibit the strongest mechanical strength and fracture toughness of any non-metallic substance known.

Polymers

Overview

  1. A polymer is a large molecule consisting of repeating structural units

  2. The repeating units are usually connected by covalent chemical bonds

  3. Polymers can be of two types

  4. Natural polymers: shellac, amber, rubber, proteins etc

  5. Synthetic polymers: nylon, polyethylene, neoprene, synthetic rubber etc

  6. Synthetic polymers are commonly referred to as plastics

  7. The ï¬?rst plastic based on a synthetic polymer to be created was Bakelite. by Leo Baekeland(Belgium/USA) in 1906

  8. Vulcanization of rubber was invented by Charles Goodyear (USA) in 1839.Vulcanization is the process of making rubber more durable by addition of sulphur

  9. The ï¬?rst plastic to be created was Parkesine (aka celluloid) invented by Alexander Parkes (England) in 1855

Synthesis of polymers

  1. The synthesis of polymers - both natural and synthetic - involves the step called polymerization

  2. Polymerization is the process of combining many small molecules (monomers) into a covalently bonded chain (polymer)

  3. Synthetic polymers are created using of two techniques

  4. Step growth polymerization: chains of monomers are combined directly

  5. Chain growth polymerization: monomers are added to the chain one at a time

  6. Natural polymers are usually created by enzyme-mediated processes. such as the synthesis of proteins from amino acids using DNA and RNA .

Categories of polymers

  1. Organic polymers are polymers that are based on the element carbon. Eg: polyethylene. cellulose etc

  2. Inorganic polymers are polymers that are not based on carbon. Eg: silicone. which uses silicon and oxygen

  3. Copolymer is one that is derived from two or more monomeric units. Eg: ABS plastic

  4. Fluoropolymers are potymers based on ï¬‚uorocarbons. They have high resistance to solvents, acids and bases. Eg: teï¬‚on

  5. Chain growth polymerization: monomers are added to the chain one at a time

  6. Natural polymers are usually created by enzyme-mediated processes. such as the synthesis of proteins from amino acids using DNA and RNA .

Biology : Nutrition

Overview

  1. Nutrition is the supply to cells and organisms, of the materials necessary to support life

  2. Many common health problems can be prevented by a healthy diet

  3. A poor diet can have injurious impact on health, leading to problems such as scurvy, beriberi and kwashiorkor

  4. A healthy diet can also signiï¬?cantly prevent and mitigate systemic diseases like cardiovascular disease, diabetes and osteoporosis

  5. Eating a wide variety of fresh, unprocessed food has proven favourable compared to monotonous diets of processed food

  6. Consumption of whole plant foods slows digestion, allows better absorption and a more favourable balance of nutrients

Nutrients

  1. There are six major classes of nutrients: carbohydrates, fats, minerals, proteins, vitamins and water
    These can be classiï¬?ed into

  2. Macronutrients: nutrients needed in large quantities. These include carbohydrates, fats, proteins and water. Fibre is another macronutrient whose functions have not been fully understood

  3. Micronutrients: nutrients needed in smaller quantities. These include minerals and vitamins. Antioxidants and phytochemicals are micronutrients as well, but their functions are not well understood

  4. Most foods contain a mixture of nutrients

  5. Some nutrients may be stored internally (eg. Fat soluble Vitamins) while others are requrred more or less continuously

Carbohydrates

  1. Carbohydrates are sugars, and are classified as monosaccharides, disaccharides or polysaccharides depending on the number of monomer (sugar) units they contain

  2. Carbohydrates constitute a large part of foods such as rice, noodles, bread and other grain based products

  3. In general, simple saccharides are easier to digest and absorb than polysaccharides

  4. Since they are absorbed more quickly, simple carbohydrates lead to elevated levels of blood glucose Fibre

  5. Dietary ï¬?bre is a carbohydrate (polysaccharide) that is incompletely absorbed in humans and some animals

  6. Like all carbohydrates, when metabolised it produces energy

  7. However, it does not contribute much energy due to limitations on its absorbability and digestion

  8. Dietary fibre consists mainly of cellulose, a polysaccharide that is indigestible in humans

  9. Whole grains, fruits and vegetables are good sources of fibre

  10. Fibre provides bulk to intestinal contents and stimulates peristalsis the rhythmic muscular contractions of the intestines that moves digesta along the digestive tract

  11. For these reasons, fibre is important for digestive health. It helps alleviate constipation and diarrhoea and is said to reduce colon cancer Fats

  12. Fat consists of fatty acids bonded to glycerol. Fatty acids are carboxylic acids that contain long chains of carbon and hydrogen atoms

  13. They are typically found as triglycerides

Fats are classified as

  1. Saturated fats: have all the carbon atoms in the fatty acid chains bonded to hydrogen atoms

  2. Unsaturated fats: have some carbon atoms double bonded to themselves, thereby have fewer hydrogen atoms
    Studies have shown that unsaturated fats are preferable to saturated fats in terms of health effects

  3. Saturated fats are usually solids at room temperature (eg butter) while unsaturated fats are liquids at room temperature (eg olive oil)

  4. Trans fats are a type of unsaturated fat with trans-isomer bonds.
    These are rare in nature and usually created by an industrial process called hydrogenation.
    Trans fats are harmful to health (coronary heart disease) and their use is to be avoided

Proteins

  1. Proteins are the basis of many animal body structures and form enzymes that control chemical reactions in the body

  2. Proteins are composed of amino acids, which contain nitrogen atoms

  3. The body requires amino acids to produce new proteins and replace damaged proteins

  4. Since the body cannot store protein, amino acids must be present in the daily diet

Vaccines

  1. Vaccines made from Horse and Cow

  2. A vaccine is a biological preparation that improves immunity to a particular disease

  3. Vaccines were first used by Edward Jenner (England) in the 1770s to inoculate against small pox using the cow pox microbe

  4. Vaccines have resulted in the eradication of small pox, one of the most contagious and deadly diseases known to man

  5. Other diseases like polio, measles, mumps, typhoid etc are have been signiï¬?cantly reduced. Currently, polio is prevalent only four countries: Afghanistan, Pakistan, Nigeria and India Mechanism of action

  6. A vaccine is usually made from a weakened or dead form of the microbe that it is intended to fight

  7. It stimulates the body's immune system to recognise the microbe as foreign. and destroy it and remember it

  8. When the same microbe re-appears later. the immune system easily recognises and destroys it

  9. When the body recognises the virulent microbe attack, it

  10. Neutralises the target microbe before it can enter body cells

  11. Destroys infected cells before the microbe can spread to other cells and multiply

Types of vaccines

  1. Killed vaccines: these are vaccines that contain micro-organisms that have been killed using chemicals or heat. Eg :inï¬‚uenza, cholera, bubonic plague, polio, hepatitis A

  2. Attenuated vaccines: these contaln live attenuated (numerous) microorganisms.
    These are usually live viruses that have been cultivated under conditions which disable their virulent properties, or use closely-related by less dangerous micro-organisms. These vaccines provide more durable immune response and are preferred type for healthy adults. Eg: yellow fever, measles, rubella, mumps, typhoid

  3. Toxoid vaccines: inactivated toxic compounds that cause illness. Eg: tetanus, diphtheria

  4. Subunit vaccines: these use protein subunits instead of the entire microorganism as a vaccine. Eg: Hepatitis B vaccine (which uses only surface proteins), Human Papilloma Virus (HPV) vaccine (which uses subunits of inï¬‚uenza virus)

Effectiveness of vaccines

  1. Vaccines do not guarantee complete protection from a disease

  2. This could be due to

  3. Host's immune system may not respond adequately

  4. Host may have lowered immunity (such as due to diabetes, HIV, steroid use etc)

  5. Host may not have a B cell capable of producing antibodies to that particular antigen

  6. The efficiency of a vaccine depends on a number of factors

  7. The disease itself

  8. The strain of vaccine

  9. Following the schedule of vaccinations

  10. Individual host factors

  11. Genetic and ethnic predisposition

  12. Most vaccines use adjuvants to boost immune system response. Adjuvants are compounds added to the vaccine that increase the immune response, without having any specific. antigenic effect by themselves.

  13. Aluminum salts like aluminium phosphate and aluminium hydroxide are the most common adjuvants used

  4. Some genetic disorders may also be influenced by non-genetic environmental factors. Eg: cancer

  3. Single gene disorders include sickle cell disease, cystic ï¬?brosis Huntington disease

Sickle cell disease is inherited in the autosomal recessive pattern

  1. Two copies of the gene must be mutated for a person to be affected

  2. An affected person usually has unaffected parents who each have one mutated gene

The circulatory system functions with other body systems to provide the following:

Excretory System

Function of the Excretory System
The excretory system functions in ridding the body of nitrogenous (nitrogen-containing discussed below) and other wastes. It also regulates the amount of water and ions present in the body fluids.
Kidneys
The kidneys of vertebrates (discussed below) function in the removal of nitrogenous and other wastes and in osmotic regulation of the body fluids.
Nitrogenous wastes
Cells use amino acids to construct proteins and other nitrogen-containing molecules. Amino acids can also be oxidized for energy or converted to fats or carbohydrates. When amino acids are oxidized or converted to other kinds of molecules, the amino \( (NH_2) \) group must be removed. The nitrogen. containing compounds produced as a result of protein breakdown are toxic and must be removed by the excretory system. Nitrogenous wastes of animals are excreted in form of ammonia, urea, or uric acid.
Ammonia
Ammonia is formed immediately after the amino group is removed from an amino acid. This process requires very little energy. Ammonia is highly soluble in water but very toxic. Aquatic animals such as bony ï¬?shes. aquatic invertebrates, and amphibians excrete ammonia because it is easily eliminated in the water.
Urea
Terrestrial amphibians and mammals excrete nitrogenous wastes in the form of urea because it is less toxic than ammonia and can be moderately concentrated to conserve water Urea is produced in the liver by a process that requires more energy to produce than ammonia does.
Uric Acid
Insects, reptiles, birds, and some dog (Dalmatians) excrete uric acid. Reptiles and birds eliminate uric acid with their feces. The white material seen in bird droppings is uric acid It is not very toxic and is not very soluble in water. Excretion of wastes in the form of uric acid conserves water because it can be produced in a concentrated form due to its low toxicity. Because it is relatively insoluble and nontoxic, it can accumulate in eggs without damaging the embryos. The synthesis of uric acid requires more energy than urea synthesis.

Respiratory System

Introduction
Energy that we consume in our food is temporarily stored in the bonds of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) before being used by the cell. Cells use ATP for movement and to drive chemical reactions. Normally, after food is consumed, it is digested and broken down into simple compounds including glucose. The energy in glucose is used to synthesize ATP by a process called cellular respiration. Oxygen is needed for cellular respiration because it picks up hydrogen atoms and that were part of the glucose molecule. In this process, it forms water. The remaining carbon and oxygen atoms from glucose form carbon dioxide. The equation below summarizes the process of cellular respiration. \( C_6 H_{12} O_6 + 6O_2 \ 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + 36 \ ATP \) The role of the respiratory system is to provide oxygen so that cellular respiration can occur and to eliminate the carbon dioxide that is produced as a waste product.

The Immune System

A number of mechanisms operate within the body to either prevent infection or fight infection by foreign particles and cells, Nonspecific immunity refers to mechanisms that are generally effective against a variety of infections. Speciï¬?c immunity refers to mechanisms that are specific for one type of infection. Speciï¬?c immunity is generally acquired after exposure to the infecting particles or cells.

Barriers to Entry

The skin is the main barrier preventing the entry of foreign organisms and particles. Skin oils weaken or kill bacteria. Cilia lining the respiratory tract sweep mucus and trapped particles to the throat where they are swallowed. The low pH of the stomach kills microorganisms. Tears wash the eyes. Saliva helps clean teeth, preventing dental caries Urine ï¬‚ow prevents colonization of the urinary tract. Vaginal secretions move microorganisms out of the reproductive tract.
