Synopsis

Natural numbers

The numbers 1, 2, 3,... are used for counting objects and therefore are called 'counting numbers'. They are also called natural numbers. Natural numbers are infinite. The sum of natural numbers is always a natural number and so is the product of natural numbers.

Whole numbers

If we include '0' in the collection of natural numbers, 'we get 0, 1, 2, 3,....... These are called whole numbers. Whole numbers are infinite. The sum of whole numbers is a whole number and the product of whole numbers is also a whole number. If we subtract any whole number from the same number the difference is 0, which is also a whole number.

Integers

Using the concept of opposite numbers, we get directed numbers. Natural numbers, the number 0, and all opposites of natural numbers are together known as integers. Thus ... -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ... are integers. Out of these, 1, 2, 3, ... are positive integers and -1, -2, -3, .... are negative integes. 0 is neither positive nor negative. The product of two integers is an integer. The difference on substracting one integer from another is also an integer. Is the quotient on dividing one integer by another also an integer.
Let us study the following examples.

  1. \( 8 \div 2 = \frac{8}{2} = 4 \)

  2. \( 0 \div 5 = \frac{0}{5} = 0 \)

  3. \( 15 \div (-3) = \frac{15}{-3} = -5 \)

  4. \( (-4) \div (-4) = \frac{-4}{-4} = 1 \)

  5. \( 7 \div 3 = \frac{7}{3} \)

  6. \( 4 \div (-5) = \frac{4}{-5} \)

  7. \( 1 \div 7 = \frac{1}{7} \)

  8. \( 2 \div (-8) = \frac{2}{-8} = 4 \)

We observe that in examples (i) to (iv), there is no remainder and the quotient is an integer. But in each of the examples (v) to (viii), there is a remainder at the end. In other words, we do not obtain '0' as the remainder in these examples if we try any of the integers as the quotient. Therefore, no integer can be the quotient here. This leads us to conclude that the collection of integers is not sufficient to express every possible quotient. In order to express every possible quotient we have to extend the number system. Let us now consider how it is done.

Rational numbers

Rational numbers are expressed in the form of \( \frac{p}{q} \) , where p and q are integers and \( q \neq 0 \) , and p and q do not have common factors.
The set of rational numbers is denoted by 'Q'.
Thus Q = \( \{ \frac{p}{q}/ p,q \epsilon \ Z \ and \ q \neq 0 \}, \ Ex \ Q = \{ - \frac{-5}{2}, \ \frac{-1}{2}, \ \frac{-1}{3}, \ 0, \frac{1}{3}, \ \frac{1}{2}, \ \frac{5}{2},..... \} \)
Let us now see which of the numbers can be said to be rational numbers.

  1. All the integers are rational numbers, because every integer can be expressed in the form \( \frac{p}{q} \) . For example, the integers 4, 0, -23 can be expressed in the form \( \frac{p}{q} \) as follows:
    \( 4 = \frac{4}{1} = \frac{8}{2} = ....., 0 = \frac{0}{1} = \frac{0}{2} = .....,-23 = \frac{-23}{1} = \frac{-92}{4} = .... \)

  2. All the fractions are rational numbers
    It follows from the meaning of the rational number that (i) the proper fraction \( \frac{2}{3} is \) a rational number, (ii) the improper fraction \( \frac{3}{2} \) is also a rational number, (iii) the mixed fraction \( 1 \frac{2}{3} \) can be expressed as \( \frac{5}{3} \) which is an improper fraction and hence \( 1 \frac{2}{3} \) is also a rational number. Opposites of all fractions are also rational numbers. Opposites of \( \frac{2}{3}, \ \frac{3}{2} \) and \( 1 \frac{2}{3} \) are \( - \frac{2}{3}, \ - \frac{3}{2} \) and \( - \frac{2}{3} \) . They are all rational numbers

Clearly

  1. Every natural number is a rational number

  2. Every whole number is a rational number

  3. Every integer is a rational number

  4. Every fraction is a rational number

Existence of a rational number between any two given rational numbers

We can always find many rational numbers between any two given rational numbers. If \( \frac{a}{b} \) and \( \frac{c}{d} \) are two rational numbers, then \( \frac{1}{2} \left ( \frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{d} \right ) \) is a rational number which lies between \( \frac{a}{b} \) and \( \frac{c}{d} \)

Order relation

There are three possibilities:

  1. If the rational numbers \( \frac{p}{q} \) and \( \frac{r}{s} \) correspond to the same point on the number line, the two numbers are equal, i.e., \( \frac{p}{q} = \frac{r}{s} \)

    If \( \frac{p}{q} \neq \frac{r}{s} \) then \( \frac{p}{q} \) will be either to the right or to the left of \( \frac{r}{s} \)

  2. If \( \frac{p}{q} \) lies to the right of \( \frac{r}{s} \) , then \( \frac{p}{q} > \frac{r}{s} \)

  3. If \( \frac{p}{q} \) lies to the right of \( \frac{r}{s} \) , then \( \frac{p}{q} < \frac{r}{s} \)

Comparison of two rational numbers

In earlier classes we have learnt the way to write fractions in an order and to compare one fraction with the other. We also know that the integers have an order relation. In the same way, rational numbers also have an order relation. There are two ways to compare rational numbers.

  1. Using the number line

  2. Using the arithmetical process

Using the number line:

One can compare rational numbers by using a number line easily. We arrange the rational numbers on the number line in ascending order from left to right. For instance.

  2. the rational numbers having 5 as denominator can be represented on the number line as follows.

From the first example it is clear that \( \frac{7}{5} > \frac{5}{4} \) since \( \frac{7}{4} \) is to the right of \( \frac{5}{4} \) .
Of the two rational numbers represented on the number line, the number on the left is smaller than the number to its right. Thus,
\( - \frac{6}{5} < \frac{4}{5}, \ \frac{3}{5}, < \frac{9}{5}, \) \( - \frac{8}{5} < - \frac{2}{5}, \ - \frac{3}{5}, < - \frac{7}{5}, \) etc.
We observe from the above examples that
A positive rational number is always greater than a negative rational number ; zero is greater than each one of the negative rational numbers and less than eah one of the positive rational numbers.

Using the arithmetical process:

  1. Express each rational number with a positive denominator.

  2. Find LCM of the positive denominators.

  3. Express each of the given rational numbers with LCM as the common denominator.

  4. The number having greater numerator is greater.

To compare two rational numbers \( \frac{p}{q} \) and \( \frac{r}{s} \) we compare the products \( s \times p \) and \( q \times r \) and define their inequality as under:

  1. \( \frac{P}{q} > \frac{r}{s}, \ if \ s \times p > q \times r \)

  2. \( \frac{P}{q} < \frac{r}{s}, \ if \ s \times p > q \times r \)

Properties of rational numbers

Addition on rational numbers satisfies the closure property, the commutative law and the associative law.
Zero is the identity element for the addition of rational numbers.
Every rational number \( \frac{p}{q} \) has its additive inverse \( \frac{-p}{q} \) .
Mutliplication on rational numbers satisfies the closure property, the commutative law. the associative law and the distributive law over addition.
One is the multiplicative identity for rational numbers.
Every nonzero rational number \( \frac{p}{q} \) has its multiplicative inverse \( \frac{q}{p} \) .
Zero does not have its multiplicative inverse.

Order properties of rational numbers

Property 1:
For each rational number x, one and only one of the following is true.

  1. x > 0

  2. x = 0

  3. x < 0

Property 2:
For any two rational numbers x and y, one and only one of the following is true.

  1. x > y

  2. x = y

  3. x < y

Property 3:
x, y, z be any three rational numbers such that x > y and y > z; then x > z.

Decimal representation of a rational number

We can represent a rational number as a decimal number by the long division process.
Observe the examples: \( \frac{75}{100} = 0.75, \ \ \ \ \ \frac{1}{2} = 0.5 , \ \ \ \ \frac{-2}{5} = -0.4, \ \ \ \ \ \ \frac{4}{5} = 0.8, \ but \ \frac{10}{3} = 3.333 ......, \ \ \ \ \ \ \frac{2}{3} = 0.6666....., \ \ \ \ \frac{4}{11} = 0.3636...... \)
We have three types of decimals. They are

  1. Terminating decimals

  2. Non-terminating and repeating decimals

  3. Non-terminating and non-repeating decimals

  1. In the above examples, \( \frac{75}{100}, \ \frac{1}{2}, \ \frac{-2}{5}, \ \frac{4}{5} \) terminating decimals, i.e., first type.

  2. \( \frac{10}{3}, \ \frac{2}{3}, \ \frac{4}{11} \) are non-terminating and repeating decimals, i.e., second type.

  3. Non-terminating and non-repeating decimals are known as irrational numbers. We will study about them later. Repeating decimals are also called recurring decimals.

Introduction of irrational numbers

The numbers which are not rational i.e, means not in the p/q form.
We have seen that every rational number can be expressed either in terminating decimal form or non-terminating repeating (or periodic) decimal form. Consider the decimal number 0.202002000200002.... We can see that no group of digits is repeating in a cyclic manner. So we have numbers whose decimal representation is neither terminating nor repeating. There are a nnumber of such decimals. Such numbers are called irrational numbers.
Ex:0.12112111211112..., 0.303003000300003..., 4.73210546..., 2.6457513...
Note:
A number is an irrational number, if it has a non-terminating and non-cyclic repeating decimal representation.
Ex: Show that \( \sqrt{2} \) is not a rational number.
\( \sqrt{2} = 1414215..... \)
The decimal representation of \( \sqrt{2} \) is neither terminating nor repeating. Hence it is an irrational number Similarly \( \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5} \ and \ \sqrt{7} \) are a few examples of irrational numbers. as \( \sqrt{3} = 1.732050807... \)
\( \sqrt{5} = 2.236067977....; \sqrt{7} = 2.64575131..... \)
Let us represent the number \( \sqrt{2} \) on the number line.

  1. Denote the point '0' on the number line l by A and the point 1 as B. The line segment AB is of unit length.

  2. At the point 'B' draw a straight line perpendicular to the line 'l'.

  3. Cut off a segment BC of unit length on the perpendicular line.

  4. With 'A' as centre and AC as radius draw a circular arc to intersect the line 'l' at P.

  5. Now AP = AC. Since ABC is a right angled triangle, with \( \angle{B} = 90^{\circ} \) , it follows from the Pythagoras theorem that \( AC = \sqrt{2} \) . Hence AP = \( \sqrt{2} \) .

What is the Pythagoras theorem?

Theorem states that, 'In any right-angled triangle the square on the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares on the other two sides of the triangle. Which side of a triangle is called hypotenuse? The side opposite to the right angle is the hypotenuse. In A ABC, \( B = 90^{\circ} \) , AC is the hypotenuse. If two sides of a right triangle are represented by x and y and hypotenuse is represented by z, then \( x^2 + y^2 = z^2 \) .
Ex:

  1. \( (3)^2 + (4)^2 = (5)^2 \)

  2. \( (24)^2 + (10)^2 = (26)^2 \)

  3. \( (6)^2 + (8)^2 = (10)^2 \)

  4. \( (5)^2 + (12)^2 = (13)^2 \)

Such triplets are known as Pythagorean Triplets.

Distinction between rationals and irrationals:

We know that every rational number is always expressible in the form of a terminating or a repeating decimal.
Number which when expressed in decimal form are expressible neither in terminating nor in repeating decimals are known as irrational numbers.
Ex:

  1. 0.01001000100001 .... is a nonterminating and nonrepeating decimal and so it is an irrational number.

  2. 0.23233233323333.... is a nonterminating and nonrepeating decimal and so it is an irrational number.

Find four rational numbers equivalent to each of the rational numbers.

  1. \( \frac{-2}{3} \)

  2. \( \frac{7}{-9} \)

Solution:

  1. \( \begin{align} \frac{-2}{3} = \frac{-2 \times 2}{3 \times 2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{-2 \times 3}{3 \times 3} = \frac{-2 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{-2 \times 5}{3 \times 5} \\\\[3pt] i.e., \ \frac{-2}{3} = \frac{-4}{6} = \frac{-6}{9} = \frac{-8}{12} = \frac{-10}{15} \end{align} \) .
    Hence, the four rational numbers equivalent to \( \frac{-2}{3} \ are \ \frac{-4}{6}, \ \frac{-6}{9}, \ \frac{-8}{12} \ and \ \frac{-10}{15} \) .
