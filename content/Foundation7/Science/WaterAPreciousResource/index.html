Common misconception Fact
1 Rain water is not pure. Rain water collected directly is pure water.
2 Water is the largest renewable natural resource on earth. Hence there is no likely shortage of water. The world is likely to face shortage of drinking water due to improper water management and water wastage. (The actual amount of water available for human use is only 0.06% of the total water).
3 The level of water table is fixed. Fact The level of water table is not fixed. It varies from place to place and from season to season, depending on type of soil and amount of rainfall.

Introduction

Water is essential for all living beings, humans, animals, plants. There can be no life without it. All living matter (unicellular or multicellular), plant or animal contain 70% to 90% of water. This water is used by the organism to transport soluble food materials to the cells and for excretion of waste. Water regulates our body temperature. Water is essential for germination of seeds and growth of plant. Water is used for drinking by human beings and animals. It is used for cooking, washing, bathing, cleaning and a number of other activities. Water is used for agriculture and power generations.

Occurance

  1. Occurrence of water in free state:
    In free state water occurs in solid, liquid and gaseous state.

    1. Solid state:
      In solid state, large amount of fresh water is found in the form of snow or ice. Vast regions of Antartica and Arctic circles have frozen water. High peaks of mountains have seasonal/perpetual snow. Frost, hail, snow are some other forms of solid state of water.

    2. Liquid state:
      Most of the water is present in oceans though it is saline and is not fit for drinking. On the surface of land, water is found in streams, rivers, ponds, lakes etc. Water present under surface of earth is called ground water. Well water is example of ground water.

    3. Gaseous state:
      Water mostly occurs as water vapors present in air which on condensation forms clouds. Mist and fog are other examples of water in gaseous state.

  2. Occurrence of water in combined state :

    1. In plants and animals :
      Water occurs in combined state in tissues of plants and animals. It has been estimated that 70% to 80% of body weight of plants and animals is in the form of water in the combined state.

    2. As water of crystallization :
      Crystals of certain salts contain a fixed number of water molecules. Such salts are called hydrated salts and the water molecules are collectively called water of crystallization.
      - Hydrated copper sulphate : \( CuSO_4.5H_2O \)
      - Hydrated ferrous sulphate : \( FeSO_4.7H_2O \)
      - Hydrated sodium sulphate : \( Na_2SO_4.10H_2O \)
      - Bauxite (ore): \( Al_2O_3.2H_2O \)
      - Borax crystals : \( Na_2B_4O_7.10H_2O \)
      - Potash alum : \( K_2SO_4.Al_2(SO_4)_3.24H_2O \)

Sources of water

People in the village may be getting water from a well or a river whereas in a city we get water in our home through taps where water is supplied by the municipality. Each of us may be getting water into their homes in different ways. But finally all of us get the water from some big source; a river, well or a lake.

Water as it occurs in nature is called natural water. The various forms of natural water are rain water; river water, water from ponds, lakes, streams and underground water (water from the well)

Rain water

It is the purest form of water available in nature. The first showers of rain collects the dust; bacteria, germs and dissolves some of the gases present in the atmosphere. Subsequent showers are free from such impurities.

River water

Water which flows on the surface of earth making. a regular course is the river water. It is very impure as it contains both suspended and dissolved impurities. It may also contain sewage, chemical and toxic waste flowing into it from a nearby town, village or an industry (The Ganges river flowing near Kanpur city is polluted because of the chemical wastes being flown into the river from various shoe factories of Kanpur).

See water

Rivers ultimately flow into the sea. So all the impurities are carried into the sea. Sea water is very salty and impure form of natural water. Yet the oceans play an import' ant role in supplying the water we get through water cycle.

Spring or well water (ground water)

Rain water falls into rivers and seas. Some of it evaporates and mixes in the air. The rest is absorbed by the ground and is called ground water. The surface of the ground water is called the water table. The level of the water table is not fixed and varies from place to place depending on the condition of soil and amount of rain fall. This water reappears at several places in the form of spring water. Sometimes this water is obtained by digging wells and called well water. Spring or well water is purer than sea or river water but less purer than rain water. It still contains some impurities from earth's crust and needs to be purified before drinking.

Potable water

The water fit for human consumption is called potable water. It should have the following properties.

  1. Clear, odorless and colorless.

  2. Free from harmful bacteria and suspended impurities.

  3. Contains dissolved gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide as they impart freshness and sparkling appearance.

  4. Contains small amount of mineral salts of sodium, calcium and magnesium as they give special test as well as required by body metabolism. However excess of these minerals can be harmful.

Potable water may be prepared from ordinary water by

  1. Boiling

  2. Chemical treatment

  3. Distillation

Electrolysis of Water

Classifications

The process of breaking down of a compound by passing electric current through it is called electrolysis, The electrolysis of water produces hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio of 2 : 1 by volume.

Physical properties of water

Water is a neutral oxide and has no effect on litmus paper. It is a colorless, transparent liquid at room temperature. Pure water is tasteless and odorless. Pure water boils at \( 100^{\circ} C \) and freezes at \( 0^{\circ} C \) at sea level, where the atmospheric pressure is the greatest. The boiling point of water decreases if the pressure is lowered and increases if the pressure is raised. The melting point of ice is lowered if the pressure is increased.
Impurities lower the freezing point and raise the boiling point of water. Water has the minimum volume and maximum density at \( 4^{\circ} C \) because it starts expanding below \( 4^{\circ} C \) .The specific heat of water is higher than any other liquid. Hence it is used as cooling agent.

Chemical properties of water

  1. Many metals react with water to produce oxides and hydroxides.
    Sodium and potassium react with cold water vigorously.
    \( 2 Na + 2 H_2 O \rightarrow 2 NaOH + H_2 \)
    Magnesium reacts slowly with cold water.
    \( Mg + 2 H_2 O \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2 + H_2 \)
    Zinc reacts with hot water, while iron reacts with steam.

  2. Reaction with oxides - water reacts with metallic oxides to form bases
    \( Na_2 O + H_2 O \rightarrow 2 NaOH \)

Hard water

Hard water has high mineral content consisting bicarbonates, sulphates of calcium \( (Ca^{2+}) \) , and magnesium \( (Mg^{2+}) \) . Hard water is generally not harmful. The simplest way to determine the hardness of water is the lather (or) froth test with soap or tooth paste, when agitated, lathers easily in soft water but not in hard water.
Hard water is not fit for

  1. Drinking as it upsets stomach.

  2. Washing clothes or bathing as it forms sticky and wastes soap.

  3. Forms scales on the walls of boilers.

  4. Dyeing as the colours tend to fade.

Soft water

Contains few or igneous rock sources, such as granite but may also derive from sandstone sources, since such sedimentary rocks are usually low in calcium and magnesium.
There are water - softening devices that are used in houses, industries to remove the minerals causing hardness. Artificially softened water is beneficial as scales are not formed in the pipes, boiler or hot water cylinder which is due to deposition of calcium carbonates.

Temporary hardness

Temporary hardness is caused by combination of calcium ions and bicarbonate ions in the water. Hardness in water is caused by the presence of calcium ions, magnesium ions, or both. As their concentrations increase, water becomes harder. The combined concentration of calcium and magnesium is frequently referred to as total hardness. Hard water containing bicarbonate of calcium and magnesium can be removed on boiling water. The good thing about temporary hardness is that it is easy to remove by boiling or through precipitation with lime (calcium hydroxide). Boiling and adding lime are two of the older methods used to soften water containing temporary hardness.

Permanent hardness

Permanent hardness is hardness (mineral content) that cannot be removed by boiling. It is usually caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium sulphates and/or chlorides in the water, which become more soluble as the temperature rises. They can be removed by several methods. One method is treating with washing Soda.

Solubility of solids

Water is called universal solvent because a large number of substances dissolve in it. Water is an excellent solvent. The solubility of a solute is the amount of it that dissolves in a unit amount of water. The solubility of a solid increases with the temperature.
When a solution contains as much solute as it can hold, it is called saturated. The solubility of a gas increases with the increase in pressure and decreases with the increase in temperature.
Water exists in three states - solid, liquid and gas. The continuous cycling of water among its three forms keeps the total amount of water constant. This is known as water cycle.
The water table goes down if the usage is more than the replenished water-through rains and seepage. Water harvesting is one of the methods of recharging ground water. It is done by constructing pits to which rain water is diverted and deposited. This increases the ground water level. Sewage is liquid waste. The impurities in water are called contaminants. Chemicals used in agriculture, factories and human waste contaminate water. Waste water treatment plants (WWTP) clean water before its disposal by using a series of processes like filtration, sedimentation, skimming, treatment with anaerobic and aerobic bacteria, chlorination, etc. We all must ensure economy in use of water and make all our effort for conservation of water.

Conservation of water

It is very important for all of us to stop waste and conserve water. Careful and economy in use of water will help in conserving water. Water can be conserved in a large scale by a method known as rain water harvesting. This is done by collecting the rain water in huge pits and storing for use later.
In cities rain water flowing from the roof of houses are collected into a storage tank by pipes. Instead of collecting the rain water in a tank, the pipes can go directly into a pit in the ground. The water then seeps into ground soil and restore the ground water level. You can help in conservation of water by paying attention to the following at your home, school and neighborhood.

  1. Don't waste or throw water while taking bath. You can use this water for watering plants.

  2. Don't flush the toilet when not required

  3. Don't leave the tap running. Close water taps while brushing teeth or shaving.

  4. Repair leaky taps immediately.

  5. Advise friends and kids, younger to you about conservation of water.

Water pollution

Pollution means spreading of harmful, unwanted substances. Water is said to be polluted if it is not fit for drinking bathing or washing, Substances which pollute the water are called pollutants. Several chemical substances, sewage, garbage etc., pollute water. Major causes of water pollution are as follows:

  1. Disposal of waste into water:
    If sewage, garbage and liquid wastes from factories and homes are discharged into water sources like river, lake or pond the water gets polluted.

  2. Chemical fertilizers and pesticides:
    Farmers use fertilizers for growth of plants and pesticides to protect crops from harmful insects. Though these chemicals are good for plants, these are harmful for human beings and when get mixed with water pollute the water.

  3. Disposal of human waste and animal dung in water:
    The rain washes away human excreta and animal dung into rivers, ponds and lakes which pollute water.

prevention of water pollution

Clean water is necessary for health. Therefore every one of the community must ensure that pollution of water prevented.

  1. Industrial waste should not be allowed to get mixed with water sources. It should be treated.

  2. Garbage and other waste materials should not be accumulated near any water source. This is to avoid any chance of garbage flowing into the river or water source.

  3. Dead bodies of humans and animals should not be thrown into rivers.

  4. Bathing and washing of clothes near water sources should be avoided.

  5. The water of rivers and lakes etc, should be purified from time to time by treating with chemicals, removal of mud silt etc.,

Example 2:

Compare the density and freezing point of sea water with fresh water. Also reason out.

Solution:
The density of sea water is higher and its freezing point lower than those of fresh water because it contains dissolved salts.

Example 3:

Explain water cycle with help of a diagram.

Solution:
The water cycle is completed in nature in the following manner:

  1. Water evaporates as water vapor from water bodies like lakes, rivers and oceans.

  2. Water vapor cools as it moves up and condenses to form clouds of tiny water droplets.

  3. When the cloud is saturated with water droplets, precipitation occurs in the form of rain or snow.

  4. A part of the rain water percolates through soil to form ground water while another part is absorbed by plants and animals. Rest of the water returns to rivers, lakes and oceans.

  5. Water absorbed by plants is returned to the atmosphere through transpiration while animals excrete excess water as urine or sweat.

Example 4:

Is distilled water potable?

Solution:
Distilled water is pure water, yet it is not suitable for drinking because it has a flat, insipid taste and lacks important salts of nutritional value.

Example 5:

water from river or well can be made safe for drinking?

Solution:
Water obtained from river or well may not be safe for drinking as it contains harmful bacteria and germs. There are two ways to eliminate harmful micro organisms:

  1. Boiling
    Small quantities of water can be made safe for drinking purposes by boiling it for few minutes. In this way, all the germs and bacteria are killed and water becomes safe for drinking.

  2. Chemical Treatment:
    Boiling water is time consuming and expensive. Large amounts of water required to be supplied to people of towns or big cities are treated with chemicals like chlorine which kills germs and bacteria.

Example 6:

Explain the following terms related to water and its sources.

  1. Infiltration

  2. Aquifer

  3. Bawris

  4. Drip irrigation

  5. Aeration

  6. Chlorination

Solution:

  1. Infiltration
    The rainwater and water from other sources such as rivers and ponds seeps through the soil and fills the empty spaces and cracks deep below the ground. This process of seeping water into ground is called infiltration. The ground water thus gets recharged by this process.

  2. Aquifer
    At places deep below ground, the ground water is stored between layers of hard rock below the water table. This is known as an aquifer. Water in the aquifers can easily be pumped out with tube wells or hand pumps.

  3. Bawris
    Bawris was a traditional way of collecting rain water. Small well are dug and the surface near the mouth of the well is cleaned and made sloppy so that rain water can easily flow into the pit. Bawris are still found in village of Rajasthan where rainfall is scanty and there is acute shortage of water.

  4. Drip irrigation
    It is a technique of watering plants by making use of narrow tubes which deliver water water at the base of plant. In this method water is used economically. The loss of er due to evaporation and infiltration is minimized.

  5. Aeration
    In the process of water supply in big cities, the water from sedimentation tank is subjeeto4 to combined action of sunlight and air. This kills micro organisms and improves the taste of water.

  6. Chlorination
    The water after aeration is treated with chlorine to kill any harmful germs or bacteria before supplied for drinking. This process is known as chlorination.

What measures should be adopted to prevent water pollution?

Solution:
Clean water is necessary for good health. Some of the preventive measures to stop pollution of water sources are:

Example 10:

Suggest methods for conservation of water :

Solution:
One of the most important steps in conservation of water is to prevent wastage of precious water. Small effort to prevent wastages like leaking of water, spilling of water will help in water conservation. Some of the other methods are:

  1. Rain water harvesting :
    By making pits near buildings where rain water can be made to flow to recharge the ground water.

  2. Drip irrigation :
    It is a technique by which plants are watered directly at their base reducing wastage of water.

    Waste water can be chemically treated for reuse. If it is not for drinking it can be used for washing clothes and watering plants.

  4. Cleaning existing water reservoirs :
    De-setting of lakes, ponds and cleaning of rivers will help in recharging the ground water.

  5. Afforestation :
    Planting of trees help in increase of rain fall and prevents soil erosion there by recharging ground water.
