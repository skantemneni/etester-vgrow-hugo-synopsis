Common misconception Fact
1 Speed of sound travels slowly in solids compared to liquids and gases. No speed of sound travels faster in solids than in liquids and gases as density is greater for solids and they are relatively more elastic than liquids and gases.
2 All sound waves produced are audible to human beings. Only the sounds produced by vibrating bodies whose frequencies are within audible range i.e.,20 Hzto 20 kHz.

Introduction

Sound is a form of energy. It causes sensation of hearing. Sound is produced by vibrating bodies. When a particle (or body) moves to and fro about its mean position, it is said to vibrate. Every source of sound is a vibrating body. In laboratory, a tuning fork is used to produce sound.

Production of sound

Sound is a mechanical wave and is normally associated with our sense of hearing. Sound is produced by vibrating sources in a material medium. Medium can be any gas, liquid or solid. A tuning fork, a violin string and a loudspeaker are examples that produce sound. This is because they are all in a state of vibration. The energy of the sound waves is propagated and carried by colliding particles of a material medium. Sound travels faster in denser media. It travels faster in liquids than in gases and fastest in solids.

Sound cannot travel through vacuum

Sound is produced by vibrating body. It transmitted in the form of mechanical waves. Thus, sound requires a material medium for its propagation. Sound can travel through solids, liquids and gases. Sound travels much better in solids and at a very high speed as compared to liquids and gases. In case of liquids, sound travels much better than in gases and at a higher speed. From the above discussion, it can be understood that sound cannot travel through vacuum. It can be illustrated using following experiment.

Experiment

Take an electric bell and fit an electric bell through an air tight cork as shown in figure. Place the bell jar on the platform of vacuum pump and apply a little grease at its edges, so as to make it air tight. Now press the key. It is possible to hear the sound of the bell, as its hammer strikes against the gong. Now start the vacuum pump. It will be observed that as the air is withdrawn from the bell jar, the loudness of sound of the bell decreases.Furthermore, when a vacuum is created, no sound is heard, inspite of the fact that hammer is striking against the gong. Stop the vacuum pump and allow the air to enter in bell jar. The sound of the bell is heard again. This experiment proves that sound cannot travel through vacuum and material medium is necessary for the propagation of sound.

Determining the speed of sound in air

The speed of sound can be determined by direct method using only two measurements.

The distance of the sound source from the receiver.
The time taken for the sound wave to travel that distance.
The speed of the sound can then be calculated using the formula:
\( speed = \frac{\text{distance travelled}}{time} \)

Why is the thunder heard after the flash of lightening?

Light travels with a gpeed of \( 3 \times 10^8 \ m \ s^{-1} \) whereas the sound travels in air at \( 330 m s^{-1} \) . Thus, whenever lightening takes place, it can be seen first, whereas sound will take some to reach us, depending upon the height of cloud. This fact can be used for finding the height of a cloud where lightening takes place.
Hold a stopwatch in your hand on a rainy day. As soon as you see the flash of lightening, start the stopwatch. The moment you hear the sound of thunder, stop the stopwatch. Let us imagine that time gap in seeing the lightening flash and hearing the sound of thunder is T s. Knowing the velocity of sound in air as 330 \ m \ s^{-1} we can find the height of cloud as under:
Height of the cloud = Speed of sound in air \times Time.

some Common Terms used in Sound

Amplitude

The maximum displacement of a vibrating body from its mean position is called amplitude. If a stationary body is struck slowly or with less force, it has less amplitude. However, if same body is struck hard, it has a large amplitude.

Frequency

The number of vibrations produced by a vibrating body in one second is called frequency.

Units of frequency

The unit of frequency in SI system is hertz (Symbol Hz). When a vibrating body produces one vibration in one second, then its frequency is said to be one hertz. If the frequency of a tuning fork is 256 Hz, it means it is producing 256 vibrations in one second.

Bigger Units of Frequency

Bigger units of frequency are kilohertz (symbol kHz) and megahertz (MHz).

  1. 1 kHz = 1000 Hz

  2. 1 MHz = 1000,000 Hz

Time period

The time in which a vibrating body completes one vibration is called time period. It is denoted by letter T.
If 'f' is frequency of vibrating body and 'T' the time period, then:
f vibrations are completed in = 1 s.
\( \therefore \) one vibration is completed in \( \frac{1}{f} s \)
But time taken to complete one vibration is its time period (T).
\( \therefore \ T = \frac{1}{f} \) i.e., Time period = \( \frac{1}{Frequency} \)

Do All vibrations produce sound

Human ear is not sensitive to vibrations of all frequencies. If the frequency of the vibrations is less than 20 Hz, then human ear cannot perceive them. Such vibrations are called subsonic vibrations or infrasonic vibrations.
If the frequency of vibrations is between 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz, then they, can be perceived by human ear. Such vibrations are called sonic vibrations or sound vibrations. It has been further found that the higher the frequency, the sharper or shriller is the effect of sound on the ears.
However, if the frequency of vibrations is above, 20,000 Hz, then they can be Perceived by human ear However, if the frequency of vibrations is above 20,000 Hz, then the human ear cannot perceive them. Such vibrations are called ultrasonic vibrations or ultrasonics.

Application of Ultrasonic Sounds

  1. These are used for the treatment of inoperable brain tumors.

  2. It is used to detect flaws and blowholes in metal casting.

  3. In the welding together of two pieces of metals. The heat produced by friction between the metals due to the vibrations of one of them at ultrasonic frequency fuses them and welds them together.

  4. It is used to kill bacteria in milk.

  5. Ultrasonic sound is used to control smoke and clear fog.

  6. Special whistles using ultrasonic wave which are inaudible to the human ear are used to call dogs.

Reflection

Like light, sound also obeys the laws of reflection when sound is reflected from hard smooth surfaces. The following experiment given below is used to illustrate reflection of sound.
Take two metal tubes place them inclined to each other such that they point towards a vertical flat surface of a drawing board. At one end of the tube place a ticking watch. At the other end place your ear by adjusting the position we hear the maximum loudness of the sound. Thus on measuring angle i and r shown in the figure it is observed that they are equal proving that sound obeys laws of reflection.

Absorption of Sound (Sound Insulation)

It has been found that shining objects like mirrors, metals or hard objects like buildings or stones reflect sound. However, materials with loose texture absorb sound. For example, curtains, gunny bags, straw, carpets, etc., absorb sound.
Big cinema half or auditoriums are carpeted and their walls are coated with some rough materials, so that they do not reflect any sound. It is because, such places are very big and therefore, echoes are formed. Thus, the quality of sound heard by people becomes poor.
However, in ordinary rooms in which we live, we do not have this problem. It is because they are seldom 17 m or more long or wide. Thus, the original sound and reflected sound reach the ears almost at the same time, and hence, no echo is formed.
Note:

  1. Reflection of sound takes place only from hard and polished surface.

  2. The angle of incidence is always equal to angle ofreflection.

  3. Sound energy is absorbed by soft and loose surfaces, such as cotton, wool, jute, etc.,

Echoes

If you stand in front of a high building or a hillock, such that you are at a distance of more than 20 m and shout loudly, you will notice that you can hear your own sound. It is because the sound is reflected from the building. This hearing of reflected sound is called echo.
Human ear can hear two sounds separately only, if they reach the ear after an interval of \( \frac{1}{10} \) of a second. This is natural feature of any human ear. The average speed of sound is 332 m s-'. Thus, in \( \frac{1}{10} \) of a second the sound will travel a distance of \( 332 \ m s^{-1} \div \frac{1}{10} = 33.2 \ m \) . This means if a sound which starts from a point, on reflection from some hard object comes back to same point, then the distance between the reflecting body and source of sound should be \( 33.2 \ m \div 2 \) = 16.6 m or 17 m (approx). This hearing of a reflected sound from a reflecting body is called an echo.
Example
If you have to address a small gathering inside a room everyone can hear you at your normal voice. However, if you address the same gathering in the open air, you have to speak loudly so that every one can hear you.
It is because, when you speak inside a room, the sound waves spread through the air in all directions. Some sound waves reach our ears directly and some strike the walls of the room and get reflected. These reflected waves also reach our ear almost at the same time. As more waves reach the ear, therefore, everyone hear, even if you speak softly.
However, in the open, only direct sound waves reach the ear, as there are no reflected waves, Thus, the ear receives less energy, and hence, you have to speak loudly.
Note
It has been found that an echo can be heard only, if:

  1. There is a high rise building or hill.

  2. Minimum distance between source of sound and reflecting body is 17 m.

  3. The loudness of the sound should be sufficient so that it can be heard after reflection.

Sonar

A device fitted in a ship to find the depth of sea is called sonar. It is based on the principle of reflection of sound. From the point A, ultrasonic vibrations are sent. These vibrations after striking the sea bed, are reflected upward and are picked up at point B. The time in which reflected sound is received is recorded.

Knowing the speed of sound in water \( (1500 \ m \ s^{-1} ) \) and the time taken for the reflected sound to reach the sonar, the depth can be calculated as illustrated by the example given below.
Let us suppose that it takes 2.4 s to receive the reflected sound. It is clear that for 1.2 s the sound must have travelled downward and for another 1.2 s it must have travelled upward.
Now,
Speed of sound in water \( \frac{\text{Distance covered by sound}}{Time} \)
\( 1500 \frac{m}{s} = \frac{\text{Distance covered by sound}}{1.2 s} \)
\( \therefore \) Distance covered by sound = 1500 \( \times \) 1.2 = 1800 m
\( \therefore \) Depth of sea = 1800 m.

Characteristics of sound

The human ear distinguishes the characteristics of sound. These are loudness, pitch and quality or timbre where each refers to a sensation in the consciousness of the listener.

Loudness

It is, easy to distinguish among loud and soft sounds. The term loudness of a sound is self-descriptive. It depends primarily on the amplitude of the sound wave.

The larger the amplitude, the louder the sound (relative).
Note:
The standard unit of sound level is decibel. A sound is judged to be twice as loud as another, if its sound level is about 10 decibels higher.

Pitch

The pitch of a sound refers to whether it is high (sharp), like the sound of a violin, or low, like the sound of a bass drum. The physical quantity that determines pitch is the frequency.
The higher the frequency, the higher the pitch (relative).

Quality or Timbre

The property due to which two notes of same pitch and loudness produced by two different vibrating bodies can be distinguished, is called quality of sound.
This quality of sound is due to the formation of other notes which are produced (hie to the vibration of various parts of musical instruments and their shapes.

t = 15 s, v \( = 330 \ m \ s^{-1} \) , s = ?

A man standing near a cliff with a seconds pendulum fires a gun when the pendulum is at the mean position and hears the echo when it just reaches the extreme position. If the speed of sound is \( 340 \ m \ s^{-1} \) ,calculate his distance from the cliff

Solution:
Let the distance be 'd'. The time taken for one complete oscillation (i.e., the time period) by a seconds pendulum = 2 s
The time taken for it to move from the mean to the extreme position
\( = \frac{1}{4} \times 2s \)
Speed of sound = \( 340 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Distance travelled by sound before the echo is heard = 2 d
2d = speed \( \times \) time
2 d = 340 \( \times \) 0.5 \( \rightarrow \) d = 85 m

Example 4:

A boy standing 70 m from a high wall claps his hands. After how much time is an echo heard? (The speed of sound in air is \( 350 \ m \ s^{-1} \) ).

Solution:
Distance of the boy, from the wall = 70 m.
Distance travelled by sound (before echo is heard) = 2 \( \times \) 70 m.
Speed of sound = \( 350 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Time = \( \frac{distance}{speed} = \frac{2 \times 70}{350} \)
= 0.4 s

of person from nearer cliff, \( d_1 = \frac{v \times t}{2} \ \Rightarrow \ d_1 = \frac{336 \times 0.6}{2} = 100.8 \ m \)

Example 8:

Example 9:

A ship sends a signal and receives it back from the bottom of the sea after 6 s. What is the depth of the sea at that place? (speed of sound in sea water is \( 1450 \ m \ s^{-1} \) )

Solution:

Time interval before the echo is heard = 6 s,
speed of sound = \( 1450 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Distance travelled by sound = 2 d
\( \begin{align} 2 d = speed \times time \\\\[3pt] 2 d = 1450 \times 6 \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow d = 4350 m \Rightarrow d = 4.35 km \end{align} \)
The depth of the sea = 4.35 km.
