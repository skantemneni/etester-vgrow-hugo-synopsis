Common misconception Fact
1 Objects which are not moving are at rest. Objects which are not moving with time and with respect to the observer are at rest. E. g . Earth appears to be at rest to us, but for an observer in the outer space, the earth is in motion.
2 Distance and displacement travelled by the body depends on the path that it follows. Only the distance travelled by the body depends on the path that it follows. It will be equal to the magnitude of the displacement only if the path is straight.

Introduction

In our daily life, we observe vehicles moving on a road, the motion of the birds in the sky, the motion of the blades of a fan etc., In all these examples we observe that the bodies are under motion. In the contrary, a body can also be under rest. To understand rest and motion their basic definition need to be defined. The basic definition of motion and rest are defined in this chapter. Apart from this the physical quantities related to the motion are studied under this topic.

Motion

A body is said to move only when it changes its position in space from one point to another point. In physics, just changing the position is not motion. Change of position with respect to surroundings and time is said to be motion.

Rest

When a body is not changing the position with respect to surroundings and time it is said to be at rest. To make it clear, it can be understood by the following example : When you are standing on a railway platform in front of another standing person, you are at rest (in this surrounding). However, to a person in a moving train, both of you appear to be moving (this surrounding is different). In a moving bus, the passenger who sits along with you is at rest, however to a person on the road, both of you appear to be in motion. Therefore, rest and motion are relative terms. They are not absolute terms.

Different Kinds of Motion

Motion can be classified based on periodicity (repetitiveness) as periodic motion and non-periodic motion.

Periodic motion

A repetitive motion, which repeats itself after a fixed interval (or regular interval) of time is called the periodic motion.

Examples of Periodic motion:

  1. The swinging pendulum of a wall clock.

  2. The piston of a motor car engine running at a constant speed.

  3. Heart beats of a normal healthy person.

  4. The needle of a sewing machine running at constant speed.

Periodic motion can be two types:

  1. Rotatory motion

  2. Oscillatory motion

Rotatory motion

When the moving body describes a curve, the body is under rotatory motion. It is the motion in which the body moves about a fixed axis without changing its position is called the rotatory motion.

Examples of Rotatory motion

  1. Motion of the blades of the ceiling fan.

  2. Motion of the potter's wheel.

  3. Motion of a spinning top.

  4. Motion of planets around the sun.

Oscillatory/Vibratory motion

The 'to-and-fro' or 'back and forth' motion described by an object as a whole, along the same path, without any change in the shape of the object is called oscillatory motion.

Examples of Oscillatory motion

  1. The motion of the bob of the simple pendulum.

  2. The up and down motion of the needle of the sewing machine.

  3. The vertical oscillation of a loaded spring (weight attached to a spring).

  4. Motion of the molecules in a solid.

Vibratory motion

Vibratory motion is another kind of oscillatory motion in which the body does not move as a whole, i.e., the entire body does not move. The moving object undergoes change in shape or size.

Examples of Vibratory motion

Musical instruments such as guitar, violin, sitar, drums, etc., produce vibratory motion.

Non-periodic motion

A motion which is not repetitive or repeats at irregular intervals of time.

Examples of Non-periodic motion

  1. A football player running in the field up and down is performing non-periodic motion as sometimes he runs fast and sometimes slow.

  2. When the brakes are applied to a moving vehicle, the motion described by the wheels is non-periodic.

  3. A cricket ball rolling down the ground gradually slows down and finally stops and hence, is performing non-periodic motion.

Non-periodic motion is also termed as translatory motion.

Translatory motion

The motion in which all the particles of a body move through the same distance in the same time is called translatory motion.

Examples of Translatory motion

  1. A car or a train moving along a road

  2. A ball rolling on the ground.

  3. A girl sliding down a slope.

  4. Pulling out a drawer of a table.

  5. Firing of a bullet from a gun.

  6. A stone hurled from a sling or catapult.

The motion of translation is of two kinds:

  1. Rectilinear motion

  2. Curvilinear motion

Rectilinear motion

When a body moves along a straight line, the motion described by the body is rectilinear motion.

Examples of Rectilinear motion

  1. A train moving on a straight railway track.

  2. A car moving on a straight road.

  3. A freely falling stone.

  4. A coin moving over a carom board.

Curvilinear motion

When a body moves along a curved line, the motion described by the body is called curvilinear motion.

Examples of Curvilinear motion

  1. A ball thrown upwards at an angle

  2. A car or train moving along a curved road or track.

Distance

Distance travelled by a body is the length of the path taken irrespective of the direction of motion. SI unit of distance travelled is meter (m).

Displacement

Displacement of a body is the shortest distance travelled in a direction from initial to final point. SI unit of displacement is the meter (m) while its CGS unit is centimeter (cm).
The following example will elucidate it. If your house is situated far away from your school, different paths (routes) you travel to reach the school constitute distances. On the other hand the straight line path (actual distance) between your house and school can be termed as displacement.

AB is not same as BA (magnitude is same but the direction is opposite).

Few more examples

When a ball is thrown vertically up about 5 m high and catch it again, the total distance the body travels is 10 m and the total displacement is zero, as it returns to the same point.

When a body starts at A and comes to another end at B on semi circle, of radius 'r'. The distance the body travels is \( \pi r \) (semi perimeter) and the displacement is 2 r (diameter).

When the same body completes the entire circle and comes to 'A' again, the distance covered is \( 2 \pi r \) and the displacement is zero.

Speed

The distance covered by a body in unit time. It is the rate at which the body is moving. It means how fast or slow the body is moving.
Mathematically, Speed = \( \begin{align} \frac{Distance \ covered \ by \ the \ body}{Time \ taken \ to \ cover \ the \ same \ distance} \\\\[3pt] V = \frac{s}{t} \end{align} \)
Suppose an insect crawls and covers a distance of 5 m in a time interval of 10 seconds, the speed of the insect
\( speed(V) = \frac{s}{t} = \frac{5}{10} = 0.5 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Speed is measured in S.I. units, as mps (meter per second) or \( m \ s^{-1} \) .
Though we can measure distance in several units, the standard expressions used are as follows:
meter per second \( \rightarrow \ mps \ (m \ s^{-1}) \)
kilometer per hour \( \rightarrow kmph \ (km \ h^{-1}) \)

Kind of Speed

Average Speed

When a body starts with certain speed and changes its speed after some time and completes the journey. Then the average speed implies the overall rapidity or speed of the body.
Mathematically, Average speed \( = \frac{Total \ distance \ covered \ by \ the \ body}{Total \ time \ taken \ by \ the \ body} \)

Uniform speed (constant speed)

If a body covers equal distances in equal intervals of time however small these intervals may be, the body is said to be under uniform speed or constant speed.
For example: A body starts at 7 and travels 20 m in every 5 seconds, then
Uniform speed = \( \frac{20}{5} = \frac{40}{10} = \frac{60}{15} = \frac{80}{20} = \frac{100}{25} = 4 \ m \ s^{-1} \)

Nonuniform speed (Variable speed)

When a body covers either unequal distances in equal intervals of time or equal distances in unequal intervals of time or both, the body is said to be under nonuniform or variable speed. For example A body starts at a point 'p' and travels as shown in figure.

Speeds at different intervals
\( \begin{align} I = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \ m \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] II = \frac{38}{10} = 3.8 \ m \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] III = \frac{61}{15} = 4.07 \ m \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] IV = \frac{89}{20} = 4.45 \ m \ s^{-1} \\\\[3pt] V = \frac{100}{25} = 4 \ m \ s^{-1} \end{align} \)
It can be noticed here, though the average speed is same, the speed itself is not uniform but varying.

Velocity

Rate of change of displacement or rate of change of position of a body in a specific or given direction.
Mathematically, Velocity (V) = \( \frac{Displacement}{Time} \)
Velocity has same magnitude as speed but only direction is different hence its units are same both in S.I. and CGS systems \( (m \ s^{-1}, \ cm \ s^{-1}) \) .
If someone states that speed of a running boy is \( 2 m \ s^{-1} \) , it is very clear that he covers 2 m in one second. Now the question arises in which direction he is running then only it makes a complete sense and meaning. Therefore, it can be stated he is running with 2 m per second from say 'A' to 'B' or say eastwards.

Kind of Velocity

Uniform Velocity

When a body covers equal distances in equal intervals of time in a specified direction, however small these time intervals may be, the body is said to be moving with a uniform velocity.
Imagine a car is moving along a straight road towards east, with a velocity 60 kmph. Here the uniform velocity implies per each minute it covers a distance of 1 km and for each second it covers a distance of \( \frac{1}{60^{th}} \ km \) .

A body will have a uniform velocity only, if:

  1. It covers equal distances in equal intervals of time, i.e., the magnitude does not change.

  2. Its direction remains the same. If any of the two conditions is not fulfilled, then the body will not be moving with a uniform velocity, but with a variable velocity.

Variable Velocity

When a body covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time in a specified direction or equal distances in equal intervals of time, but its direction changes then the body is said to be moving with a variable velocity.
The following figure explains variable velocity clearly.
Now imagine the same car is moving along a circular path with the same velocity, 60 kmph \( (16.67 \ m \ s^{-1}) \) . Here the magnitude of the velocity i.e., 60 kmph is constant but direction is continuously changing. In this case (on circular path) the car is only under uniform speed but not under uniform velocity.

Hence, when the car is covering equal distances is equal intervals of time, in the circular path the car is moving with a variable velocity, as the direction changes continuously.

Average Velocity

The average displacement of a body per unit time, when the body is actually moving with variable velocity, is called average velocity.

In the above figure, the total displacement (total distance travelled) of a body towards east is 3m + 4m + 2m + 5m= 15 m. The total time is 4 s.
\( \therefore \ Average \ velocity = \frac{Total \ displacement}{To \ time} = \frac{14 m}{4s} = 3.5 \ m \ s^{-1} \)
Note:
It is possible to have a moving body with average velocity zero but not with average speed to be zero.

Acceleration

The rate of change of velocity is called the acceleration.
\( Acceleration = \frac{change \ in \ velocity}{time \ interval} \)
In CGS system, the unit of acceleration is \( cm \ s^{-2} \) . In SI system, the unit of acceleration is \( m \ s^{-2} \)
When the velocity changes either increasing or decreasing then the body is said to be accelerating or retarding (decelerating).

Kinds of Acceleration

Uniform acceleration

If equal changes in velocity takes place in equal intervals of time, however small the time intervals may be, the body is said to be travelling with uniform acceleration.
Example : A freely falling body possesses uniform acceleration.
Consider a body moving with an initial velocity u. Let it be travelling along a straight line with uniform acceleration a and let v be its final velocity after time t.
Acceleration (a) = rate of change of velocity
\( \begin{align} = \frac{Final \ velocity - Initial \ velocity}{Time} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{v - u}{t} \end{align} \)
Hence v = u + at

Non - uniform acceleration

If unequal changes in velocity takes place in equal intervals of time, then the body is said to be moving with non-uniform acceleration.

Negative acceleration

If the velocity of the body gradually decreases w.r.t time, the acceleration is said to be negative, which is called deceleration or retardation.

Solved examples

Example 1:

Harish and Mohan start from home at the same time and travel by different routes to school. Harish's house is at a distance of 1500 m from the school while Mohan's house is at 2000 m from the school. Both reach school at the same time. Who travelled faster?

Solution:
Speed depends on both time and distance. In order to decide who travelled faster, Harish or Mohan, we have to compare the distance travelled by each in a fixed interval of time. Let's say both take 20 minutes to reach school.
\( \begin{align} \therefore \ \text{Speed of Harish} = \frac{distance \ travelled}{Time \ taken} = \frac{1500}{20} = 7.5 \ m \ min^{-1} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ \text{Speed of Mohan} = \frac{distance \ travelled}{Time \ taken} = \frac{2000}{20} = 10\ m \ min^{-1} \end{align} \)
Mohan walks 10 m per minute as compared to 7.5 m distance walked by Harish. Thus Mohan travels faster than Harish.

Example 3:

A train starts from station 'A' and reaches station D 150 km away. It has scheduled halts at stations 'B' and 'C' 5 minutes each. The distance between A and B is 50 km and C and D is 60 km. The train starts at 5 P.M. from 'A' and reaches 'D' at 8.30 P.M. What is its average speed in \( m \ s^{-1} \) ?

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Average \ speed = \frac{Total \ distance \ travelled}{Total \ time \ taken} = \frac{150 km}{3.5 h} = 42.8 \ km \ h^{-1} = \frac{42.8 \times 1000}{3600} \\\\[3pt] = 11.94 = 12 \ m \ s^{-1} \end{align} \)
Note:
The other data given in the problem are not required.

Example 4:

A car starts at A with a speed of 40 kmph and reaches B. Then the car returns to the starting point A on the same points with a speed of 60 kmph. What is average speed and average velocity?

Solution:
When the body covers equal distances in different times by different velocity. Average speed is given by the velocity.
\( Average \ speed = \frac{2v_1 v_2}{v_1 + v_2} \)

\( \begin{align} Average \ velocity = \frac{Total \ distance}{Total \ time} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{x + x}{t_1 + t_2} = \frac{2x}{\frac{x}{v_1} + \frac{x}{v_2}} = \frac{2v_1 v_2}{v_1 + v_2} \end{align} \)
In the given problem,
\( \begin{align} Average \ speed = \frac{2v_1 v_2}{v_1 + v_2} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{2 \times 40 \times 60}{40 + 60} \\\\[3pt] = 48 \ kmph \\\\[3pt] Average \ velocity = \frac{Total \ distance}{Total \ time} \\\\[3pt] = 0 \end{align} \)

Example 5:

Imagine, a body is moving along a circular track covering one complete round of 60 m in 10 s. Find the average speed and average velocity of the body.

Solution:
\( Average \ speed = \frac{Distance}{Time} = \frac{60 \ m}{10 \ s} = 6 \ m \ s^{-1} \)

However, as the body has returned to the starting point after one complete round, therefore, the net displacement of body is zero.
\( Average \ velocity = \frac{Distance}{Time} = \frac{0 \ m}{10 \ s} = 0 \)

Example 6:

A plastic ruler is held at the edge of a table, with a part of the ruler sticking out. If the extended part of the ruler is pulled down a little and released, what type of motion will it exhibit?

Solution:
Oscillatory motion is that motion in which a body moves back and forth (to and fro) about a fixed position.
In this activity the extended part of the ruler moves to and fro about a fixed position, i.e. the point at which it is held firmly or fixed.

Example 7:

On a 120 km track, a train travels 30 km at a uniform speed of \( 30 km h^{-1} \) . How fast must the train travel the next 90 km so as to average \( 60 km h^{-1} \) for the entire trip.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Average \ Speed = \frac{total \ distance \ travelled}{total \ time taken} = \frac{s}{t} \\\\[3pt] \Rightarrow \ 60 = \frac{120}{t} \ \Rightarrow \ t = 2h \end{align} \)

  1. For the first 30 km distance, the speed of train = \( 30 km h^{-1} \) . Hence in the 1st hour (in 1 h) it travels only 30 km.

  2. Since, it is required to complete balance distance in 1 h

\( \Rightarrow \) balance distance = 120 - 30 = 90 km
Time = 1 h
\( \therefore \ Speed = \frac{Distance}{time} = \frac{90}{1} = 90 \ km \ h^{-1} \)

Example 8:

A man starts his journey at 8 am at point 'A' which is 40 km away from point 'C'. From 8 am to 8 : 20 am he travelled with certain speed and rested about another 20 minutes at 'B' then he travelled with another speed last 20 minutes, complete the journey by 9 am. Find the average speed of the man for his whole journey.

Solution:
\( \begin{align} Average \ speed \ of \ the \ man = \frac{Total \ distance \ covered \ by \ the \ man}{Total \ time \ taken \ by \ the \ man} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{40 \ km}{20 + 20 + 20 \ min} = \frac{40 \ km}{1 \ hour} = 40 \ kmph \end{align} \)
Here the total includes the time he took rest also.
