Introduction

Air is neither a pure substance nor a single gas. It is a mixture of gases, important among them being nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, water vapor. The other minor constituents include noble gases or inert gases like helium, neon, argon, krypton.

Atmosphere

Air is present all around us. All living things need air to breathe. We breathe in the oxygen that is present in the air.
The blanket of air that surrounds the earth is called atmosphere. It extends hundreds of kilometers above the earth. It acts as a shield for the earth.
The atmosphere is thick near the surface of the earth. It gets thinner as we go higher up. After a few hundred kilometers, the atmosphere ends and space begins. Space has no air.

Layers of the atmosphere

The atmosphere has five layers. These are troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere and exosphere.

Troposphere

The troposphere is the lowermost layer of the atmosphere. It extends upto 15 kilometers above the surface of the earth. All weather changes take place in this layer.

Stratosphere

The stratosphere starts from where the troposphere ends and extends to about 35 kilometers. Compared to the troposphere, this layer of the atmosphere is less dense. The ozone layer, which absorbs the sun's ultra - violet rays, is located in this layer. Jet planes usually fly in this layer.

Mesosphere

Beyond the stratosphere lies the mesosphere. In this layer, temperature begins to fail as we go higher. That it is known as the 'cold layer'.

Thermosphere

The thermosphere is called the 'hot layer' because it is characterized by high temperatures. Space shuttles orbit in this layer.
Another layer, called ionosphere is a part of the thermosphere. The radio signals transmitted from the earth are reflected back to the earth by this layer. So long distance radio communication is possible because of the ionosphere.

Exosphere

This is the outermost layer of the atmosphere. The air here is very thin. Beyond the exosphere is vast, empty space.

Composition of Air

Nitrogen constitutes major percentage of air (78.03%), then oxygen constitutes above 20.9%. The remaining 1.07% is shared by the noble gases, carbon dioxide, water vapour, dust particles etc.,

Proportion and Significance of Various Components of Air

Nitrogen

The percentage of nitrogen in air, by volume, varies from 77.9% to 78.03%.
Significance of nitrogen in atmosphere

  1. It dilutes the activity of oxygen. Thus, the combustion of fuel during burning takes place at moderate rate.

  2. The compounds of nitrogen are of vital importance to plants as they help the plants to manufacture proteins. However, free nitrogen cannot be absorbed by plants. The free nitrogen is converted into its compounds by the following natural methods:

    1. During lightning, an electric spark passes through air. Due to this spark, nitrogen combines with oxygen to form nitric oxide gas.
      \( \underset{Nitrogen}{N_2} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \underset{spark}{\overset{electric}{\rightleftharpoons}} \underset{Nitric \ oxide \ gas}{2 NO} \)
      The nitric oxide gas, further reacts with oxygen and water vapor present in air to form nitric acid vapor.
      \( \begin{align} \underset{Nitric \ oxide \ gas}{2No} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \rightarrow \underset{Nitric \ dioxide \ gas}{2 NO_2} \\\\[3pt] \underset{Nitric \ dioxide \ gas}{2No} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} + \underset{water} \rightarrow \underset{Nitric \ acid}{4 HNO_3} \end{align} \)
      When it rains, the nitric acid in very dilute from is washed down to earth, where it reacts with soil carbonates to from soil nitrates.
      \( \underset{Calcium \ carbonate}{CaCO_3} \rightarrow \underset{Calcium \ nitrate}{Ca(NO_3)_2} + \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} + \underset{Water}{H_2 O} \)
      The soil nitrates, so formed, act as excellent food for the growth of plants

    2. Symbiotic bacteria are present in nodules of pod bearing plants such as peas, grams, sweet peas, etc., The bacteria absorb nitrogen directly from air and convert it into compounds of nitrogen. These compounds are absorbed by the plants for the manufacture of proteins.

    3. Nitrogen being active gas is used in the preservation of foods. The containers containing food are flushed with nitrogen to drive out oxygen and then sealed. Because, of the absence of oxygen, the bacteria do not grow and hence food remains fresh.

Oxygen

The percentage of oxygen in air by volume, varies from 20.8% to 20.9%.
Significance of oxygen in atmosphere

  1. It supports all animal and plant life on the earth, as it is essential for respiration.

  2. It is essential for the combustion of fuels. Man burns an enormous amount of fuels for his energy needs. Oxygen helps in the combustion of fuels to form carbon dioxide and water vapor as principal products.

  3. In nature, it dissolves in water. This, in turn, helps to keep the water fresh and is a source of respiration for water animals.

Carbon Dioxide

The percentage of carbon dioxide in air, by volume, varies from 0.03% to 0.04 %. The areas where a lot of fuel burns have more percentage of carbon dioxide.
Significance of carbon dioxide in atmosphere

  1. It acts as food for plants. During photosynthesis, plants absorb carbon dioxide and water vapor. These and sun are converted into carbohydrates and oxygen in the presence of chlorophyll anlight.
    \( \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{6CO_2} + \underset{Water}{6H_2 O} \underset{Photosynthesis}{\overset{Sunlight}{\rightleftharpoons}} \underset{Glucose}{C_6H_{12} O_6} + \underset{Oxygen}{O_2} \)

  2. Carbon dioxide, dissolved in water, can dissolve rocks containing calcium carbonate (chalk) or magnesium carbonate. The salts, so formed, are calcium bicarbonate and magnesium bicarbonate. These dissolved salts give taste to natural water. Furthermore, they satisfy the needs of plants for growth, as calcium and magnesium ions are needed by the plants.
    \( \begin{align} \underset{Calcium \ carbonate}{CaCO_3} + \underset{Water}{H_2 O} + \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} \rightarrow \underset{Calcium \ bicarbonate}{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \\\\[3pt] \underset{Calcium \ carbonate}{MgCO_3} + \underset{Water}{H_2 O} + \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} \rightarrow \underset{Magnesium \ bicarbonate}{Mg(HCO_3)_2} \end{align} \)

  3. It does not allow the earth to radiate heat energy rapidly at night. It is because the carbon dioxide molecules present in air reflect back heat radiations.

Water vapor

The percentage composition of water vapor in air, by volume, varies from 0.4% to 4%.
Significance of water vapor in atmosphere

  1. The presence of water vapor in air determines climatic conditions. Excessive water vapor causes rain. The amount of rain produced depends upon the geographical location and the surrounding. Depending upon the temperature, the water vapor produces snow, fog, mist, hails and other phenomenon.

  2. The presence of water vapor controls the rate of evaporation from the plants and animals.

  3. The presence of water vapor is very essential for the growth of plants.

  4. The presence of water vapor is very essential forhealth and comfort of animals .Excess or deficient water vapors in air can lead to a number of problems related to good health.

Noble Gasses

A group of gases which do not react chemically with any substance are called noble gases. They are helium, neon, argon, krypton and xenon. Their percentage composition in air by volume is 0.94%.
Significance of noble gasses in atmosphere
Apparently, they do not have any use for the animals or plants. They can be separated from air and put to a number of industrial uses.

  1. Helium gas is very light and is used for filling weather observation balloons.

  2. Argon gas is used for filling electric bulbs at low pressure.

  3. Neon gas is used for making advertising glow tubes, which you see at night on big shops, hotels, etc.

Impurities

The percentage of impurities in air is variable, depending upon the location of place. In general, the air contains a large amount of impurities in industrial areas.
Following are the impurities present in air:

  1. Dust particles
    Small amount of dust particles do no harm. As a matter of fact, they help in the precipitation of water vapor and, hence, in the formation of clouds and rain. However, an excessive amount of these can cause serious respiratory problems.

  2. Smoke particles:
    Smoke particles are present in a large amount where coal is used as fuel. They also act as nucleus for the precipitation of water vapor. However, an excessive amount of these can lead to serious respiratory problems

  3. Sulphur dioxide nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide and hydrogen sulphide:
    These gases are present in variable amounts in big cities, industrial districts, etc., They can cause the following problems:

    1. They can cause serious respiratory problems.

    2. They can destroy ozone layer, which protects us from ultraviolet radiations.

    3. They can cause acid rain, which ruins crops and forests.

Measurement of Pressure

barometer is an instrument that measures the pressure of the atmosphere:
The basic principle of the barometer is to use atmospheric pressure to balance a vertical column of mercury sealed at the top. The height of the mercury indicates the atmospheric pressure.

Carbondioxide

Carbon dioxide was first prepared in 1630 by Van Helmont. It was recognized as an oxide of carbon in 1783 by Lavoisier, who also determined its composition. For a long time, the gas was called 'carbonic acid gas'.

Occurrence

Carbon dioxide is 0.03% of the earth's atmosphere. It is added to the atmosphere by the respiration of plants and animals, and the burning of carbon compounds like wood, fuel, etc., Carbon dioxide is also found dissolved in mineral and spring water. In combination with lime (i.e., in the form of \( CaCO_3 \) ) , it occurs in limestone, marble, chalk, calcite, etc.,

Formation

Complete combustion of carbon and hydrocarbons and organic materials

Carbon dioxide is formed by the complete combustion of carbon, hydrocarbons, organic compounds and organic materials (e.g, wood, vegetable oil, crude oil, natural gas, coal, paraffin wax, etc.)
\( \begin{align} C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 \\\\[3pt] \underset{methane}{CH_4} + 2 O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O \\\\[3pt] \underset{ethanol}{C_2H_5OH} + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2 CO_2 + 3H_2O \end{align} \)

Action of heat on metal carbonates and hydrogen carbonates

Carbonates of most metals (except Na and K) and hydrogen carbonates of all metals decompose on being heated to give \( CO_2 \) .
\( \begin{align} CaCO_3 \xrightarrow{heat} CaO + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] MgCO_3 \xrightarrow{heat} MgO + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] Ca(HCO_3)_2 \xrightarrow{heat} CaCO_3 + H_2 O + CO_2 \end{align} \)

Action of dilute acids on carbonates and hydrogen carbonates

A more volatile acid is displaced from its salts by a less volatile acid.Carbonic acid \( (H_2CO_3) \) is a weak and volatile acid. Treating the salts of carbonic acid, i.e carbonates acid ates and bicarbonates, with dilute HC1 or dilute \( H_2SO_4 \) release \( H_2CO_3 \) , which dissociates into \( CO_2 \) and \( H_2 O \) .
\( \begin{align} Na_2CO_3 + 2HC1 \rightarrow 2NaC1 + H_2O + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] NaHCO_3 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + H_2O + CO_2 \\\\[3pt] CaCO_3 + 2HC1 \rightarrow CaC1_2 + H_2O + CO_2 \end{align} \)

Fermentation

\( CO_2 \) is manufactured as a by - product in the brewery industry. Fermentation of carbohydrates, \( C_2(H_2O_2) \) , in the presence of suitable enzymes results in the forma. tion of ethanol and \( CO_2 \) . The most familiar examples of fermentation are the production of beer, wine and other alcoholic beverages.
\( \underset{ \underset{(Carbohydrate)}Glucose}{C_6H_{12}O_6} \xrightarrow{enzyme} \underset{ethanol \ or \ ethylalcohol}{2 C_2H_5OH} + 2CO_2 \)

Laboratory preparation

Principle

Carbon dioxide is prepared in the laboratory by the action of dilute HC1 (but not H2SO4) on marble \( (CaCO_3) \) .
\( CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2 \)

Procedure

Some marble chips are taken in a Wolfs bottle and dilute HC1 is added to them through a thistle funnel. There is brisk effervescence and the \( CO_2 \) gas that evolves carries some acid also. The acid is removed by passing the gas through water or better still, through a solution of sodium bicarbonate \( ( NaHCO_3 ) \) .
\( HCl + NaHCO_3 \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O + CO_2 \)

Drying

The gas may be dried by passing it over anhydrous \( CaC1_2 \) , concentrated \( H_2SO_4 \) or phosphorus pentoxide \( (P_9O_5) \) . The gas obtained after passing it through an \( NaHCO_3 \) solution and concentrated \( H_2SO_4 \) , is pure and may be collected.

Collection

As \( CO_2 \) is markedly heavier than air, it is collected by the upward displacement of air. It can also be collected over mercury. Being moderately soluble in water, it cannot be collected over water.

Physical properties of \( CO_2 \)

  1. \( CO_2 \) , is colorless and odorless gas.

  2. \( CO_2 \) is 1.53 times heavier than air-its density is \( 1.98 \ g \ L^{-1} \) .

  3. It is easily liquefiable.

  4. \( CO_2 \) can be solidified at low temperature. Solid carbon dioxide sublimes, i.e., vaporizes without melting, at atmospheric pressure. That is why it is also called dry ice.

  5. \( CO_2 \) gas is moderately soluble in water. At 15 Â°C a volume of water dissolves an equal volume of \( CO_2 \) gas. In carbonated beverages (e.g., soda water) the gas is dissolved at high pressure.

Chemical properties of \( CO_2 \)

Combustion

\( CO_2 \) is neither combustible nor a supporter of combustion. However, burning samples of highly active metals like Na, K and Mg continue to burn in \( CO_2 \) gas. The oxygen required for burning is furnished by \( CO_2 \) and hence it behaves as an oxidising agent.
\( \begin{align} \underset{(burning)}{2Mg} + CO_2 \rightarrow 2 MgO + C \\\\[3pt] \underset{(burning)}{2Na} + 2 CO_2 \rightarrow Na_2 CO_3 + CO \\\\[3pt] \underset{(burning)}{4K} + 3 CO_2 \rightarrow 2 K_2CO_3 + C \end{align} \)

Reaction with alkalis

Being acidic, \( CO_2 \) dissolves in alkali solutions, such as those of NaOH, KOH, \( Ca(OH)_2 \) (limewater) and \( Ba(OH)_2 \) (baryta solution) to form the carbonates.
\( \begin{align} 2 NaOh + CO_2 \rightarrow Na_2 CO_3 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] Ca (OH)_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow \underset{white}{CaCO_3(l)} + H_2O \\\\[3pt] 2 KOH + CO_2 \rightarrow K_2CO_3 + H_2O \\\\[3pt] Ba (OH)_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow \underset{white}{BaCO_3(l)} + H_2O \end{align} \)
Since \( CaCO_3 \) and \( BaCO_3 \) are white insoluble solids, limewater and baryta solution turn milky. However, when an excess of \( CO_2 \) gas is passed through the solution, the milkiness disappears due to the formation of soluble hydrogen carbonates \( Ca(HCO_3)_2 \) and \( Ba(HCO_3)_2 \) .
\( \begin{align} CaCO_3 + CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow \underset{(soluble)}{Ca(HCO_3)_2} \\\\[3pt] BaCO_3 + CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow \underset{(soluble)}{Ba(HCO_3)_2} \end{align} \)

Reduction to \( CO_2 \)

When \( CO_2 \) gas is passed over red - hot carbon, zinc or iron, carbon dioxide is reduced to carbon monoxide. Thus, \( CO_2 \) is an oxidising agent.
\( \begin{align} CO_2 + \underset{red \ hot}{C} \rightarrow 2CO \\\\[3pt] CO_2 + \underset{(red \ hot)} \rightarrow CO + ZnO \end{align} \)

Conversion to urea

\( CO_2 \) reacts with \( NH_3 \) gas at \( 185^{\circ} C \) and 200 atm to form \( NH_2CONH_2 \) (urea).
\( CO_2 + 2NH_3 \xrightarrow[200 \ atm]{185^{\circ} C } (NH_2 CO_2 NH_4) \rightarrow NH_2CONH_2 + H_2O \)

Uses of \( CO_2 \)

  1. Carbon dioxide is used as a refrigerant. Dry ice is used to refrigerate ice cream, meat and frozen food during transportation.

  2. The carbonation of soft drinks and beer is a major use of carbon dioxide.

  3. Carbon dioxide gas is used as an inert, protective gas for welding. Metals are welded at temperatures of \( 2500 - 3000^{\circ} C \) . If the welding is carried out in air, the \( O_2 \) of the air may combine with the metals at this high temperature to form metal oxides. To avoid oxidation, welding is often done in an atmosphere of \( CO_2 \) .

  4. Carbon dioxide is employed in large quantities in the manufacture of urea.

  5. Carbon dioxide does not support combustion and is therefore used to extinguish fires.

Fire extinguishers

  1. Basically there are two types of fire extinguishers which make use of \( CO_2 \) :
    The liquid carbon dioxide extinguisher, and
    The water - type extinguisher.

  2. The liquid carbon dioxide extinguisher consists of a bottle containing pressurized carbon dioxide, which is sprayed out through a nozzle. The initial jet that comes out vaporizes and creates such a low temperature that the carbon dioxide solidifies. The fine particles of solidified carbon dioxide are sprayed on to the flames. The particles vaporize and form a dense layer of carbon dioxide gas, which breaks the contact between air and the fire, and also lowers the temperature of the burning material. This helps to extinguish the fire.

  3. A water - type extinguisher is filled with a concentrated solution of sodium carbonate \( (Na_2CO_3) \) in water. Inside the solution is a small bottle containing \( H2_SO_4 \) . By striking the plunger of the extinguisher against a hard surface, the bottle gets shattered. The acid reacts with sodium carbonate to form \( CO_2 \) gas, an the water and the gas rush out of the nozzle. The fire loses contact with air and is extinguished.
    \( Na_2CO_3 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow Na_2SO_4 + H_2O + CO_2 \uparrow \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Why do mountaineers carry oxygen cylinders with them?

Solution:
As we move high up in the atmosphere the air becomes thinner. Hence the amount of oxygen becomes less and with increase in height. It becomes difficult to breathe.

Example 2:

Why glass panels usually appear hazy?

Solution:
Glass panel usually appear hazy because water vapour settles on the tiny dust particles on the panel.

Example 3:

Winds are caused by the unequal heating of different parts of the earth. Justify.

Solution:
When a place gets heated by the sun, the air above it also gets heated. As you have read in the chapter on heat, when air is heated it expands and becomes lighter. The warmer, lighter air rises, and cooler, denser air blows in from the neighbouring areas. This movement of air is what we call wind.

Example 4:

Why is sulphuric acid not used in the preparation of carbon dioxide using calcium carbonate?

Solution:
If dilute sulphuric acid is used instead of dilute hydrochloric acid, initially the reaction starts.
\( underset{Calcium \ carbonate}{CaCO_3} + \underset{sulphuric \ acid}{H_2SO_4(dil)} \rightarrow \underset{Calcium sulphate}{CaSO_4} + \underset{Waste}{H_2O} + \underset{Carbon \ dioxide}{CO_2} \)
However, calcium sulphate formed during reaction is insoluble in water. Thus, calcium sulphate settles over marble chips. As marble chips do not come in contact with dilute sulphuric acid, the reaction stops.

Example 5:

In the activity shown in the figure, a paper kept over the mouth of the glass does not fall although the glass is full of water and its weight is acting downwards.

Solution:
No. It is because the air from below is exerting pressure and this pressure is supporting the paper in spite of the weight of the water on it.

Example 6:

Why is air a mixture but not a compound?

Solution:
The composition of air is not constant but varies slightly from place to place. For in. stance, oxygen is slightly less abundant at higher altitudes that at lower levels. The proportion of water vapour and solid particles too, is not constant. This shows that air is a mixture and not a compound.

Example 7:

Give reasons why helium balloons are safer to use than hydrogen balloons?

Solution:
Helium being a light gas is used for filling balloons instead of hydrogen because it is not inflammable. Helium in the liquid state is also used to obtain very low temperatures.

Example 8:

Why carbondioxide is not prepared by the action of \( H_2SO_4 \) on marble instead dilute HC1 is used?

Solution:
With dilute \( H_2SO_4 \) , the formation of \( CO_2 \) stops very soon because a sparingly soluble crust of \( CaSO_4 \) is formed on the marble which does not allow the marble to come in contact with the acid. As \( CaC1_2 \) is highly soluble, no such problems arise with HCl.

Example 9:

Is it possible to drink with a straw when there is a hole in it as shown in Figure? Give your reason.

Solution:
Not possible. The air pressure inside the straw and the air pressure outside the straw are the same. Therefore, there is no force to push the drink up through the straw.

Example 11:

Explain briefly how a syringe makes use of the principle of air pressure.

Solution:
The piston of the syringe is pulled outwards to increase the volume of the barrel. The pressure inside the barrel decreases and the higher air pressure outside forces the liquid into the barrel.

Example 12:

Explain briefly how you can lift a rectangular - shaped glass plate with two rubber suckers.

Solution:
Place the rubber suckers at each end of the glass plate. Press the rubber suckers to force out the trapped air. Lift the rubber suckers together with the glass plate. Air pressure exerts a farce that holds the glass plate.
