Introduction

Measurement is the comparison of an unknown quantity with a known standard quantity of same kind. Another important thing is to identify a quantity that can be measured (known as physical quantity). Generally most of the measurements are based on physical quantities like length, mass, time, temperature etc.,
In measuring a physical quantity one needs to have an idea of its various units, their interrelationships and also the various devices used for its measurements.

Physical Quantity

  1. A physical quantity is a quantity that can be measured.

  2. Physical quantity is expressed by stating

    1. magnitude of the physical quantity.

    2. the unit employed. Therefore, physical quantity = magnitude x unit.

Standard unit

In measuring a physical quantity one needs to have an idea of its various units, their interrelationship and also the various devices used for its measurement.
The unit plays an important role in the description of a physical quantity. But the unit used for a particular physical quantity is person dependant. Hence, they need to be specified and standardized to maintain uniformity. Under this treaty, an International Bureau of weights and measures was established. This Bureau situated at Serves in Paris has specified standard units, measured more accurately with advanced techniques of science.

Characteristics of Standard unit

  1. It should be of convenient size.

  2. It should not change with respect to space and time.

  3. It should be possible to define, without any doubt or ambiguity.

  4. It should not be perishable

  5. It should be easily reproduced.

System of units

The fundamental units of length, mass and time taken together form a system of units. For measuring various physical quantities following systems are commonly adopted.

Name of the system unit
Length Mass Time
1 FPS foot pound second
2 CGS centimetre gram second
3 MKS meter kilogram second

Types of physical quantities

Fundamental quantities

These quantities do not depend on other physical quantities. Length, mass and time are fundamental quantities.

Derived quantities

These quantities are defined in terms of fundamental quantities. Area, density, volume are derived quantities.

Types of units

Fundamental unit

The units of fundamental quantities are called fundamental units.

Derived unit

The units of derived quantities are called derived units.

Measurement of Length

Length, a fundamental quantity, is used to measure the distance between two points in space. The SI unit of length is metre (m) and its C.G.S unit is centimetre (cm). The various units used to measure length are p[ m, mm, cm, km. Length can be measured using devices like meter scale, vernier callipers, measuring tape etc.,
Using a ruler we can measure the smallest reading accurately upto 1 mm i.e. Least count of ruler is 1 mm. The length of a rod can be measured using a scale by keeping the scale in contact with the object as shown:

Length of rod = y - x = 2.5 cm - 1 cm = 1.5 cm
In case of a curved line the length of curved line is found by using a thread ruler method or divider ruler method.

Similarly, the diameter of a uniform wire is found by winding it over a pencil. The length of wounded wire over pencil is measured by a scale. The measured length of coil is then divided by the number of turns which gives the diameter of wire.
Diametere of the wire = \( \frac{Length \ of \ AB}{Number \ of \ turns} \)

Measurement of Length

Area is a derived quantity, that is obtained by multiplying the two measurements of a regular body. For example the area of a rectangle is calculated as the product of its length and breadth. The SI unit of area is m2 or square metre. The other units of area are \( cm^2, \ mm^2, \ km^2 \) etc.,

The area of an irregular object can be calculated by drawing the outline of its shape on a graph paper. The number of complete squares are calculated, next the squares greater than half are also counted as a square, leaving the squares less than half. The sum of all the squares gives the area of leaf to be that many \( cm^2 \) .

Measurement of Length

Volume is the space occupied by an object. The SI unit of volume is \( m^3 \) or cubic metre. The other units of volume are \( cm^3 \) , ml, / etc.,
\( \begin{align} 1 \ ml = 1 \ cm^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ ml = 10^{-6} \ m^3 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^3 \ cm^3 = 10^3 \ ml \\\\[3pt] 1 \ l = 10^{-3} \ m^3 \end{align} \)
Volume of regular bodies can be calculated by measuring the dimensions necessary for the calculations. The volume of a cube is given by \( (side)^3 \) .

Volume of liquids

Measurement of volume of liquids can be made using the following apparatus.

Measuring jar

It is graduated in ml from bottom to top and used to measure required amount of liquid.

Measuring flask

It has one mark etched on neck and the liquid of only volume of its capacity can be measured.

Pipette

It is filled by sucking the liquid into it. It is used to take up only a fixed amount of liquid.

Burette

It resembles a measuring jar with a pitch cock provided at the bottom. It is used to take desired amount of liquids.

Measurement of Mass

Mass is the measure of matter contained in a body. Common balance, physical balance are the mostly used devices used for measuring it. SI unit of mass is kilogram. Common balance is used to find unknown mass of an object using known standard masses.
Weight is the force with which the earth pulls a body towards its centre. The weight of a body is not a constant quantity, but depends upon the acceleration due to gravity acting on the body. The acceleration due to gravity is not same at all the places on the earth, but changes from place to place.
The weight is generally measured in kilogram - force (kgf) or gram - force (gf) with the help of a spring balance. One kilogram force (kgf) is the force with which the earth attracts a mass of one kilogram. Similarly, one gram force (gf) is the force with which the earth attracts a mass of 1 g.
The SI unit of weight is newton. The amount of force with which the earth attracts a mass of 1 g.
The SI unit of weight is newton. The amount of force acting on a body, when the earth pulls a mass 100 g (98.1 g to be exact) is equal to one newton.

Measurement of Time

Time is the interval between two events. In our daily life, many events are observed like heart beat, blinking of eyes, appearance of comets etc. These events may be either periodic or may not be periodic.
In air lines and railways, a 24 hour clock system is used, for example 3 : 25 P.M. in 12 hour clock system is written as 15 hour 25 minutes in 24 hour clock system.

Simple pendulum

Take a brass bob and connect it to a thread of length 100 cm and suspend it from the nail fixed to a wall and the arrangement is as shown.

The bob is pulled gently towards the left and on releasing the bob swings towards 'B' and `C' and then returns back to a position 'A'. The motion of the bob from the point 'A' to 'C' and back to the point 'A' is called one oscillation and time taken to complete one oscillation is called time period. If time period is 2 seconds then such a pendulum is called 'seconds pendulum'.

Density

It is defined as mass per unit volume of the substance. Density is a derived unit.
Density \( = \frac{mass}{volume} i.e., \ d = \frac{M}{V} \)

Units

S.I unit of density is \( kg \ m^{-3} \)
C.G.S unit of density is \( g \ m^{-3} \)
\( 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = 10^{-3} \ g \ cm^{-3} \)

Relation between the SI and CGS units of density

\( \begin{align} 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1 kg}{1 \ m^3} = \frac{1 kg}{1m \times 1m \times 1m} = \frac{1000 g}{100 \ cm \times 100 \ cm \times 100 \ cm} = \frac{1 \ g}{1000 \ cm^3} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ 1 \ kg \ m^{-3} = \frac{1}{1000} g \ cm^{-3} \ or \ 1 \ g\ cm^{-3} = 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \end{align} \)
Density of a substance in SI system = \( 100 \times \) density of the substance in CGS system.

Relative density

It is the ratio of the density of the substance to the density of water at \( 4^{\circ} C \) . Thus,
Relative density (R.D.) = \( \begin{align} \frac{density \ the \ substance}{density \ of \ water \ at \ 4^{\circ} C} \\\\[3pt] \frac{mass \ of \ the \ substance}{volume \ of \ the \ substance} = \frac{mass \ of \ water \ at\ 4^{\circ} C}{volume \ of \ water \ 4^{\circ} C} = \left [ RD = \frac{M}{V} \right ] \end{align} \)

Solved examples

Example 1:

Manju measures the length of a black board as 6.4 m while her friend Asha measures the length of a table as 280 cm. Identify whether the length of table or length of the black board is greater?

Solution:
Length of black board = 6.4 m
Length of table = 280 cm
= 2 m + 0.8 m
= 2.8 m
Hence, length of blackboard is greater.

Example 2:

A uniform wire is wound 4 turns on a scale such that the length of the wire is 2.4 cm then find the diameter of the wire.

Solution:
Diameter of the wire = \( \frac{length \ of \ the \ wire \ wound}{Number \ of \ turns} = \frac{2.4}{4} = 6 \ mm \)

Example 3:

An artist designed two postage stamps. One was square and measured \( 2 cm \times 2 \ cm \) . The other was rectangular and measured \( 3 cm \times 2 \ cm \) . Before the stamps were issued, it was decided to double the length of each side of the square stamp and triple the lengths of the rectangular stamp. By how many times will the area of paper required for each stamp increase?

Solution:
Initial area of the square stamp = \( 2 cm \times 2 \ cm = 4 \ cm^2 \)
New size of the stamp = \( 2 cm \times 2 \ cm = 4 \ cm \)
New area of the stamp = \( 4 cm \times 4 \ cm = 16 \ cm^2 \)
\( \therefore \) The area of paper required for the square stamp increases by \( \frac{16 \ cm^2}{4 \ cm^2} = 4 \ times \) . Initial area of the rectangular stamp = \( 3 \ cm \times 2 \ cm = 6 \ cm^2 \)
New size of the stamp is, length \( 3 \times 3 \ cm = 9 \ cm \) and breadth = \( 3 \times 2 \ cm = 6 \ cm \) New area of the stamp = \( 9 \ cm \times 6 \ cm = 54 \ cm^2 \)
\( \therefore \) The area of paper required for the rectangular stamp increases by \( \frac{54 \ cm^2}{6 \ cm^2} = 9 \ times \) .

Example 4:

The volume of a solid cylinder is 330 mL. Find the mass of (a) aluminium, (b) tin required to make one such cylinder. (c) What would be the volume of a solid cylinder made from 1 kg 350 g of aluminium? [Density of aluminium = \( 2.7 g cm^{-3} \) , density of tin = \( 7.3 g cm^{-3} \) ].

Solution:
We know that density = \( \frac{mass}{volume} \)
\( \therefore \ Mass = density \times volume \)

  1. Mass of aluminium required = \( 2.7 \ g \ cm^{-3} \times 330 \ cc = 891 \ g \)

  2. Mass of tin required \( 7.3 \ g \ cm^{-3} \times 330 \ cc = 2409 \ g = 2.41 \ kg \)

  3. Mass of aluminium \( 1 \ kg \350 \ g = 1350 \ g \)

\( \begin{align} \therefore \ Volume = \frac{mass}{density} \\\\[3pt] \therefore \ Volume \ of \ the \ cylinder = \frac{1350 \ g}{2.7 \ g \ cm^{-3}} = 500 \ cm^3 \end{align} \)

Example 5:

The length of a school compound is 500 m and breadth is 120 m. Find the area of compound in hectares.

Solution:
We know that, the school compound is in the shape of a rectangle.
Hence area of school compound \( \begin{align} length \times breadth \\\\[3pt] = 500 \ m \times 120 \ m = 60000 \ m^2 \\\\[3pt] 1 \ m^2 = 10000 \ hectares \\\\[3pt] \therefore \end{align} \)
Area of school compound = 6 hectares.

Example 6:

Find the weight of a body of mass 1 tonne.

Solution:
Given mass of the body = 1 tonne.
But, 1 tonne = 1000 kg
\( \therefore \) Mass of the body = 1000 kg Weight of the body = \( m \times g \)
= \( 1000 \ kg \times 9.8 \ m \ s^{-2} = 9800 \ N \)

Example 7:

Density of iron is \( 7.8 \ g \ cm^{-3} \) . Express this value in MKS system.

Solution:
We have Density = \( \frac{Mass}{Volume} \)
Density of iron is \( 7.8 \ g \ cm^{-3} \)
In MKS system we have to find the density in \( kg \ cm^{-3} \)
Thus \( \begin{align} \rho = 7.8 \frac{g}{cm^3} = 7.8 \times \frac{10^{-3} \ kg}{(10^{-2} m)^3} = 7.8 \times \frac{10^{-3}}{10^{-6} m^3} kg \\\\[3pt] = 7.8 \times \frac{10^{-3} \ kg}{10^{-2} m^3} = 7.8 \times 10^3 \ kg \ m^{-3} \end{align} \)
Thus density of iron \( 7800 \ kg \ m^{-3} \)

Example 8:

An iron cylinder is of length 0.4 m and area of cross section \( 0.5 \ m^2 \) . If the density of cylinder is \( 7800 \ kg \ m^{-3} \) , what is the mass of cylinder?

Solution:

Length of cylinder = 0.4 m
Area of cross section = \( 0.5 \ m^2 \)
\( \Rightarrow \) Volume of cylinder (v)
= length \( \times \) area of cross section = \( 0.4 \times 0.5 m^2 = 0.2 m^3 \)
Density of cylinder (d) = \( 7800 \ kg \ m^{-3} \)
Mass of cylinder (m) = ?
We know,
\( \begin{align} d = \frac{m}{v} \ \Rightarrow \ m = v \times d \\\\[3pt] = 0.2 \times 7800 \\\\[3pt] = 1560 kg \end{align} \)

Example 9:

The relative density of mercury is 13.6 what is its density in C.G.S and SI units.

Solution:
We know that
\( R.D = \frac{density \ of \ substance}{density \ of \ water} \)
\( \therefore \) Density of substance in C.G.S. = R.D of the substance \( \times \) density of water
\( \begin{align} = 13.6 \times 1 \ g \ cm^{-3} \\\\[3pt] = 13.6 \ g \ cm^{-3} \end{align} \)
Density of the substance in S.I. unit = R.D of the substance \( \times \) density of water in S.I. unit
\( \begin{align} = 13.6 \times 1000 \ kg \ m^{-3} \\\\[3pt] = 13.6 \times 10^3 \ kg \ m^{-3} \end{align} \)

Example 10:

The density of copper is \( 8900 kg \ m^{-3} \) what would be the value of its relative density?

Solution:
Density of copper = \( 8900 kg \ m^{-3} \)
Relative density = ?
\( \begin{align} R.D = \frac{density \ of \ substance}{density \ of \ water} \\\\[3pt] = \frac{8900 kg \ m^{-3}}{1000 kg \ m^{-3}} = 8.9 \end{align} \)
