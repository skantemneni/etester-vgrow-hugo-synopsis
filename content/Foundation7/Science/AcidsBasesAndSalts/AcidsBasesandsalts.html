Common misconception Fact
1 All acids are corrosive hence, harmful and cannot be consumed by us. Organic acids like citric acid present in lemon are weak and hence not corrosive we consume such acids daily in our food.
2 Litmus is a paper. Litmus is a plant (vegetable) dye that is blue in colour. It changes colour when it comes in contact with certain substance.
3 All bases are alkalis. All hydroxide bases soluble in water are called alkalis. Therefore all alkalis are bases, but vice versa is not true.
4 Salts are available in nature as minerals. Salt can be obtained by reaction between an acid and a base.

Introduction

Chemical compounds can be broadly classified into three categories: Acids, Bases and Salts. The preparation, physical properties, chemical properties and their uses can be studied in this chapter.

Acid

Acids belong to an important class of compounds. The word acid is derived from the Latin word acidus meaning sour. All acids have one property in common - they are sour to taste. Numerous acids occur in nature. Oranges and lemons contain citric acid, apples contain malic acid. Luscious tomatoes are rich in oxalic acid. Tamarind and grapes contain tartaric acid. Lactic acid is found in sour milk. The above acids are organic acids.
In the laboratory we mainly use hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid and nitric acid. These aye mineral acids. These acids are corrosive and must be handled with care.

Preparation of Acids

Acids are formed when nonmetallic oxides are dissolved in water.
\( \begin{align} \underset{(sulphur \ dioxide)}{SO_2} + H_2 O \rightarrow \underset{(sulphurous \ acid)}{H_2 SO_3} \\\\[3pt] \underset{(sulphur \ trioxide)}{SO_3} + H_2 O \rightarrow \underset{(sulphuric \ acid)}{H_2 O_4} \\\\[3pt] \underset{(carbon \ dioxide)}{CO_2} + H_2 O \rightarrow \underset{(carbonic \ acid)}{H_2 CO_3} \end{align} \)

Properties

  1. They are sour to taste.

  2. Acids turn blue litmus red.

  3. Acids turn methyl orange solution to reddish pink.

  4. They are corrosive. They destroy clothes, skin and paper.

  5. All of them contain hydrogen.

  6. Action with metals
    Dilute acids react with metals to produce hydrogen.

  7. Acids react with bases to give salt and water.

  8. Acids liberate carbon dioxide from carbonates and bicarbonates.

Basicity of acids

  1. The number of replaceble hydrogen atoms in a molecule of an acid is called its basicity.

  2. Acids are compounds containing hydrogen atoms, which can be replaced by metals.

Uses of Acids

Hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid and nitric acid are important reagents in thelaboratory. They are very useful in industry.

  1. In the manufacture of chlorine.

  2. In the purification of sodium chloride.

  3. In the manufacture of paper, dyes, artificial silks, rayon, drugs, etc.,

  4. It is used in lead storage batteries.

  5. In the manufacture of explosives like T.N.T (Tri nitro toluene), nitro glycerine etc.,

  6. In the manufacture of artificial silks, plastics.

Bases are metallic oxides or hydroxides, which react with acids to give salt and water.

Preparation

When metals burn in oxygen the respective metallic oxides are formed. These are basic in nature.
Sodium + Oxygen \( \rightarrow \) sodium oxide
Potassium + Oxygen \( \rightarrow \) potassium oxide
Calcium + Oxygen \( \rightarrow \) calcium oxide
The oxides when dissolved in water give the respective hydroxide.
alcium oxide + Water \( \rightarrow \) calcium hydroxide
Sodium oxide + Water \( \rightarrow \) sodium hydroxide
Potassium oxide + Water \( \rightarrow \) potassium hydroxide
But there are other metallic hydroxides like cupric hydroxide, ferric hdroxide etc.,
hese cannot be obtained by the above method.

properties

  1. Bases are bitter to taste.

  2. They are soapy to touch.

  3. All bases turn red litmus blue.

  4. Bases turn colourless phenolpthalein to pink and orange coloured methyl orange indicator to yellow.

  5. Bases react with acids to give salt and water.

  6. Alkalis react with ammonium salts to liberate ammonia.

Solubility in water

Not all bases are soluble in water. Those that are soluble are called alkalies. Sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide and calcium hydroxide are alkalies. All alkalies are bases but all bases are not alkalies.

Uses of Bases

  1. Calcium hydroxide or slaked lime is used to make mortar for building purposes and for white washing.

  2. It is also used in the preparation of bleaching powder. When dry chlorine is passed through slaked lime, bleaching powder is produced.

  3. Lime is used to neutralize the acidity of the soil.

  4. Alkalies are used in making soap. Oils like coconut oil are mixed with alkalies and boiled. Soap is formed along with glycerol. This is called saponification.

  5. They are used in leather industry and glass making.

Differences between an Acid and an Alkali
Acid Alkali
1 It is watery to touch. It is soapy to touch.

Salts

A salt is a substance formed by the neutralization of an acid with a base. It is formed by the replacement of the hydrogen atoms of the acid by metal atoms.
\( \underset{Alkali \ or \ Base}{NaOH} + \underset{Acid}{HCl} \xrightarrow[Neutralisaation \ reaction] \ \underset{Salt}{NaCl} + \underset{Water}{H_2O} \)
A salt may be acidic, basic or neutral in nature.

Uses of Salts

Salts are very useful in our daily life. They find important applications in domestic and industrial purposes.

Domestic purposes

Salt is sodium chloride without which food does not taste good. Sodium bicarbonate is used in baking soda and in the preparation of effervescent drinks. Sodium carbonate known as washing soda is used for washing purpose.

Industrial use

  1. Sodium chloride

    1. In the preparation of chlorine and hydrochloric acid, sodium carbonate etc.,

    2. As a preservative for meat and fish.

    3. Mixed with ice, it is used as a freezing mixture

  2. Potassium nitrate
    In the manufacture of explosives like gunpowder, fertilizers, and crackers in fireworks.

  3. Copper sulphate

    1. As a fungicide under the name Bordeaux Mixture.

    2. As a wood preservative

    3. In the dyeing industry

    4. In electroplating

  4. Ammonium salts
    In the preparation of ammonia

  5. Sodium carbonate
    As washing soda, in laundries.

  6. Alums
    Alums are double sulphates of potassium and aluminium This is useful for :

    1. Purifying drinking water.

    2. As a mordant in dyeing

    3. In the leather industry.

Indicators

An indicator is a substance which shows the presence of a chemical substance by changing color. A commonly used acid - base indicator is litmus paper. Acids turn blue litmus red and bases turn red litmus blue. Methyl orange and phenolphthalein are other indicators. And some of the natural indicators are turmeric powder (haldi), china rose (ghulal) and raw beet root juice. The bright orange yellow turmeric solution changes to a brick red color when comes in contact with an alkali.

Solved examples

Example 1:

How are inorganic acids formed?

Solution:
Non metallic oxides react with water to form inorganic Acids like \( H_2SO_4 \) .
Let's see what are nonmetallic oxides.
Formation of oxides :
An oxide is a compound of an element with oxygen. Elements react with oxygen to form their oxides. Metals react with oxygen to form metallic oxides and Non metals react with oxygen to form nonmetallic oxide.

The nonmetallic oxides react with water to form inorganic acids:
\( SO_2 + H_2O rightarrow H_2SO_3 \) (Sulphurous acid)
\( CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow H_2CO_3 \) , (Carbonic acid)
\( N_2O_5 + H_2O \rightarrow 2HNO_3 \) , (Nitric acid)
\( SO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow H_2SO_4 \) , (Sulphuric acid)

Example 2:

Some of our food contains acids. Are these acids harmful for our body ?

Solution:
Lemons, mangoes, tomatoes, grapes etc. All contain organic acids. These acids are not corrosive in nature and are weak. Hence these are not harmful.
Let's differentiate between a strong acid and a weak acid.
Strong Acid
The acids in which more than 30% of the molecules ionise in water to furnish \( H^{+} \) ions are strong acids. These are corrosive in nature, can cause burns on skin, and can eat away paper, cloth and even metal.
Most of the inorganic acids are strong acids and should never be taaken directly. Not even be touched.
Weak Acids:
The acid whose less than 30 % molecules ionise in water to furnish \( H^{+} \) ions, are called weak acids. organic acids are weak acids like citric acid in lemon and acetic acid in vinegar. These are good and are essential for our body.

Example 3:

Our stomach produces Hydrochloric acid which an inorganic acid for digestion of food.It is a strong acid . Why does this acid not corrode/burn our stomachs?

Solution:
Our stomach lining is protected by a thick layer of mucus. This thick mucus stops the stomach from being burned by the hydrochloric acid.

Example 4:

What are the uses of acids in everyday life?

Solution:

  1. Acids react with metals (except nitric acid) to produce a salt and liberate hydrogen gas.
    Metal + Acid \( \rightarrow \) Salt + Hydrogen
    \( \begin{align} Zn + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow ZnSO_4 + H_2 (gas) \\\\[3pt] Zn + 2HC1 \rightarrow ZnC1_2 + H_2 (gas) \end{align} \)
    This property is used to obtain hydrogen gas.

  2. Acids react with carbonates to release carbon dioxide gas with effervescence. This property of acid is used in fire extinguishers to release \( (CO_2) \)
    \( ZnCO_3 + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow ZnSO_4 + H_2O + CO_2 \)

  3. Vinegar (a liquid containing 3 - 6% acetic acid) is used in pickles and many other food preparations.

  4. Citric acid obtained in lemon and orange juice is used as a food preservative.

  5. Acids have been put to many uses in industry.
    Nitric acid and sulphuric acids are used in manufacture of fertilizers, dyes, Paints, drugs and explosives.

  6. Sulphuric acid is used in batteries of car and vehicles as an electrolyte. Tannic acid is used in manufacture of ink and leather.

  7. Hydrochloric acid is used to make aquaregia which is used to dissolve noble metals like gold.

Example 5:

How are bases formed?

Solution:
The oxides and hydroxides of metals are called bases. The most important bases are NaOH, KOH, \( Ca(OH)_2 \) and \( Mg(OH)_2 \) .

  1. When magnesium ribbon burns in air (oxygen) it forms magnesium oxide (white ash) which is soluble in water, giving rise to magnesium hydroxide.
    \( \begin{align} 2Mg + O_2 \rightarrow 2MgO \\\\[3pt] MgO + H_2O \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2 \end{align} \)

  2. \( Ca(OH)_2 \) is prepared by adding water to calcium oxide (quick lime). A large amount of heat is produced during the reaction. (Exothermic)
    \( CaO + H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + heat \)

Example 6:

What are the common uses of bases?

Solution:

  1. Sodium hydroxide (caustic soda) is used in the manufacture of soap. It is used in petroleum refining, in making medicines, paper, pulp etc. It is also used in making rayon.

  2. \( Ca(OH)_2 \) or slaked lime has the following uses :
    Manufacture of bleaching powder
    Dressing material for acid burns
    Antidote for food poisoning
    Preparation for fungicides

  3. Ammonium hydroxide \( (NH_4OH) \) is used to remove ink spots as it is used as a cleansing agent.

  4. Potassium hydroxide (KOH) is used in alkaline batteries which have a long life.

Example 7:

What is a neutralisation reaction? How are salts formed?

Solution:
When an acid is mixed with a base, both solutions neutralise the effect of each other. The resulting solution is neither acidic nor basic. This is called a neutralisation reaction. In neutralisation reaction, heat is always liberated. This increases the temperature of the mixture.
In neutralisation reaction a new substance is formed. This is called salt. Salt may be acidic, basic on neutral in nature.
\( \begin{align} \underset{Acid}{HCl} + \underset{Base}{NaOH} \rightarrow \underset{Salt}{NaCl} + \underset{Water}{H_2O} \\\\[3pt] H_2SO_4 + 2KOH \rightarrow K_2SO_4 + 2 H_2O \\\\[12pt] HNO_3 + NaOH \rightarrow NaNO_3 + H_2O \end{align} \)

Example 8:

Give examples of some neutralisation reactions you have come across in everyday life:

Solution:
Some of the useful effects of neutralisation are described below:
Indigestion treatment:
Some times when we take too much oily food or food with high acid content it causes indigestion. To cure we should take antacid tablets or solution which contains magnesium hydroxide. This is commonly known as milk of magnesia.
Curing BCC/Ant sting :
The sting of an ant or the ee is very painful, because such insects inject some acidic liquid (formic acid) intoe skin. The effect of the sting can be neutralised by rubbing baking soda, \( (NaHCO_3) \) or calamine solution (zinc carbonate).
Soil Treatment :
Excessive use of chemical fertilisers make the soil acidic. Plants don't grow well on such soil. Such soil needs to be treated with bases like quick lime (CaO) or slaked lime \( (Ca(OH)_2) \) . If the soil is basic, organic matter is added to it which releases acid to neutralise the basic nature of the soil.
Waste Treatment :
Factory wastes containing acids are treated with bases before being released to flow into water bodies. Unless treated such wastes are harmful and may damage the living organisms in water.

Example 9:

What are acidic and basic salts? Give examples.

Solution:
Acidic salts:
When acidic salts are dissolved in water, their solutions behave like acids and turn blue litmus into red.
Sodium bicarbonate \( (NaHCO_3) \) , potassium bicarbonate \( (KHCO_3) \) etc are acidic salts.
Basic salts:
When basic salts are dissolved in water, their solutions behave like bases and turn red litmus into blue.
Magnesium hydroxy chloride Mg(OH)C1 and calcium hydroxy chloride Ca(OH)Cl are examples of basic salts.
